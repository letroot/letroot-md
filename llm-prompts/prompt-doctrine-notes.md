Produce a clean, structured sermon transcript from the source material.

Goal:
Preserve the preacher's actual teaching, doctrine, argument, and meaningful emphasis while removing non-substantive transcript clutter. The result should read like a clean transcript, not like notes, a summary, or a rewritten article.

Instructions:
- Do NOT summarize.
- Do NOT rewrite into article, devotional, study-note, or essay form.
- Do NOT add interpretation or extra sections such as Key Insights, Action Items, Resources, Commentary, Study Notes, Prayer Points, or coaching-style takeaways unless explicitly requested.
- Preserve the speaker's real message and wording as much as possible.
- Keep the transcript substantively verbatim, but clean it for readability.
- Remove transcript noise, timestamps, markup, subtitle artifacts, speaker-label clutter, duplicate lines, and obvious caption/transcription errors.
- Remove non-substantive spoken interaction markers and audience-address phrases when they do not carry doctrinal content, such as `are we together`, `are you there`, `pay attention`, `brothers and sisters`, `all right`, `amen`, `glory to God`, `hallelujah`, `somebody say`, and similar expressions.
- When a missing, broken, or clearly wrong word can be recovered with high confidence from the immediate and broader thematic context, restore or correct it.
- Be conservative with contextual repairs: only insert or replace words when the intended wording is strongly supported by the surrounding thought, argument, doctrine, or sentence structure.
- Be especially alert for Greek words, Greek transliterations, and technical theological terms that may be omitted, split incorrectly, or misspelled in the source transcript; correct them when the intended term is clear from context.
- Do not guess at uncertain words, especially specialized terms, names, or Greek expressions; if the intended wording is not reasonably clear from context, preserve the safest readable form instead of inventing content.
- Remove non-substantive filler and verbal clutter when it does not carry real meaning, including:
  - repeated fillers such as "um," "uh," "you know," "I mean"
  - false starts and repeated fragments where the intended sentence is clear
  - routine audience checks, room-management phrases, and throwaway transitions
  - repeated "Amen," "hallelujah," or similar expressions when used only as cadence, verbal padding, or call-and-response filler
  - audience-response clutter that does not add substantive teaching content
- Remove repeated exhortative restatements when they do not add new doctrinal content, even if they were spoken with emphasis.
- Keep words and phrases that carry doctrinal, explanatory, argumentative, rhetorical, pastoral, or emotional substance.
- If a repeated word or phrase is clearly being used for deliberate emphasis that matters to the sermon, keep it.
- Break the transcript into natural, readable paragraphs.
- Insert descriptive, content-based `## Heading` sections when the topic clearly shifts.
- Prefer more sectional headings where natural, but only when there is a genuine transition in subject, argument, or emphasis.
- Make headings descriptive and specific to the content, not generic.
- Do not invent ideas that are not present in the sermon.
- Do not compress multiple distinct teaching movements into one heading if they are meaningfully separate.
- The final result should feel like a cleaned sermon transcript that is faithful to the preached content, but noticeably tighter than the raw speech where non-substantive spoken clutter has been removed.

Output format:
- Use a main title at the top if a title is available.
- Then present the sermon body as cleaned transcript text.
- Use Markdown `##` headings for major topical shifts.
- Do not append any summary or concluding analysis unless explicitly requested.
