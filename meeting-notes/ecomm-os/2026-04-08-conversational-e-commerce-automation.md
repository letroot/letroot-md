# Conversational E-commerce Automation

## Summary
The meeting reviewed a product plan to build conversational AI that reads a company’s product and knowledge data, confirms availability and pricing, and routes orders to back-office systems for fulfillment and payment. Participants agreed the system must integrate multi-source connectors and be driven by instructional prompts to support real-time inventory, pricing, and cost calculations. Inioluwa warned the approach depends on customers having structured product and process data (Google Sheets, PIMs, or knowledge articles) and flagged that many Nigerian small businesses may lack that documentation, limiting AI effectiveness.

The group then moved to resourcing and finance and recommended hiring at least one responsible backend engineer to accelerate development and deliver an MVP. Daniel summarized the current staged-investment arrangement that buys roughly 30% equity and noted dilution risks and the unconventional installment structure. He proposed creating a new company to isolate prior investors’ claims and to treat Inioluwa’s 800 as either venture debt or capital while establishing a co‑founder equity split. The team favored targeting enterprise verticals—airlines, banks, hospitals, telecoms—using documented knowledge bases and source-of-truth data to automate chat and voice SOPs and suggested pursuing a bespoke mid-sized customer to shape platform requirements and go-to-market. Follow-up items include identifying a backend engineer and continuing the funding conversation, with Inioluwa committing to ping Daniel later that night.

## Chapters & Topics

### Conversational e‑commerce overview
Inioluwa outlined a conversational e‑commerce flow where an AI consults product data, confirms availability and pricing, prompts the user to pay, and then routes orders to back‑office systems for delivery and fulfillment. He framed the architecture as front office (conversation) and back office (routing and delivery) components.
* The product vision is an AI that reads a merchant’s product data and handles ordering, pricing, payment, and routing to fulfillment systems.
* The solution’s effectiveness depends on merchants having structured product and process data (sheets, PIM, or knowledge base).

### Data requirements and Nigerian market feasibility
The discussion focused on the dependency of conversational AI on structured merchant data and documentation, with Inioluwa repeatedly warning that many Nigerian businesses may not capture products and processes in accessible systems like Google Sheets or PIMs. They observed that if data is captured and kept current the AI can check availability and pricing in real time, but absent that the solution will fail to provide accurate responses.
* Many Nigerian small businesses may lack adequate documentation, which could block AI automation for those customers.
* The system should connect to multiple data sources (e‑commerce, sheets, knowledge bases) and use instruction prompts to produce availability and cost outputs.

### Technical approach and prompts
Participants described a technical approach where the AI connects to multiple sources (WhatsApp input, e‑commerce inventory, knowledge articles) and executes instructional prompts to reply, calculate costs, and guide payments. Inioluwa asserted the work is feasible and suggested they can build connectors and prompt templates to automate responses.

### Team size and hiring needs
Inioluwa recommended adding one or two engineers to expand capacity and handle product responsibilities, emphasizing the value of a responsible engineer even if not a "10x" hire. Daniel acknowledged he would need extra hands when shifting toward marketing and sales and said he had begun informal conversations with potential hires.
* Inioluwa recommended hiring at least one additional engineer to speed development and share responsibility.

### Current financing and investor terms
Daniel recounted the existing investment: staged disbursements totaling an initial 3k that correspond to roughly 10% equity, with further installments tied to additional shares and an overall 30% stake held by the investors as a block. Both characterized the arrangement as unconventional and disadvantageous, with concerns about dilution in future rounds.

### Dilution mechanics and future fundraising impact
The participants explained that future funding rounds will dilute early holders and that part of new round proceeds may be used to provide liquidity to prior investors, reducing net funds available for operations. They discussed how liquidating prior investors in a later round lowers the cash retained by the company in that round.

### Structuring a new company and roles
Daniel apologized for prior handling of the business and proposed incorporating a new company where Inioluwa’s 800 could be treated as venture debt or capital, enabling a clean co‑founder split and clearer obligations for e‑commerce growth and revenue. Inioluwa raised legal and operational concerns about investor claims but was open to structuring options while stressing the required intensity and commitment for the venture.
* Daniel proposed spinning up a new company and treating existing capital as venture debt or reallocated capital to enable a proper co‑founder split.

### Product focus and scope
The conversation opens with agreement that a new product should be focused on conversational growth rather than social-commerce alone. Participants acknowledge social media comments are only one input among many and emphasize a multi-channel conversational capability.

### Feasibility and architecture
Speakers agreed the conversational system is buildable but requires understanding of structure and architecture and a platform that accepts inputs from multiple sources. The need for a configurable UI that lets customers define intents and multiple agents was highlighted as central to product design.
* The product focus should be on conversational AI agents that ingest multiple input channels and are configurable for intents
* The team prefers a B2B approach of building for a mid-sized enterprise customer to discover platform requirements
* The offering should target enterprise verticals such as airlines, banks, hospitals, and telecoms where documented procedures exist

### Market and use cases
The team mapped use cases across industries including retail, telecoms, travel, airlines, and banking, emphasizing scenarios where automated access to knowledge bases and transaction flows (search, authenticate, payment) adds value. They noted similar patterns apply to customer-service workflows and that voice interactions are already being adopted alongside chat.
* Chat and voice are both viable channels for the product and should be supported

### Go-to-market and resourcing
The discussion covered go-to-market challenges of selling into enterprises and the practicality of building a bespoke solution for a paying customer to refine the platform. Participants concluded additional engineering capacity is necessary and agreed to continue conversations, with Inioluwa committing to follow up that night.
* The project requires at least one additional engineer, particularly for backend responsibilities
* Inioluwa will follow up with Daniel later the same night to continue the discussion
