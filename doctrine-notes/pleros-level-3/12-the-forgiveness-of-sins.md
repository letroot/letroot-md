# The Forgiveness of Sins

We are looking at the forgiveness of sins. This teaching is connected to the teaching we did earlier on redemption, and it is important to follow that earlier thought through into this one.

When we say forgiveness of sins, people often have different concepts in mind. Many think of God forgiving sins mainly as his personal interaction with them, as though he simply looks at a situation and chooses whether or not to forgive in that moment. So they say, "Lord, please forgive me," with fear and uncertainty, as though forgiveness is based only on how God feels about them at that time.

We must not assume people understand forgiveness of sins biblically. For many, their paradigm of forgiveness is more relational than redemptive. They think, "I have been doing well; just this one thing I did, forgive me," and then imagine God responding on that basis. That is not the biblical mindset.

So we are going to examine forgiveness of sins as the Bible teaches it, that is, as it is taught in the redemption accomplished in Christ.

## Forgiveness of Sins as an Accomplishment of Redemption

Much like redemption itself, people can create descriptions of forgiveness from the way forgiveness operates, but those descriptions are not always the reality itself. This is a sensitive subject because there are ways forgiveness of sins can be taught that breed licentiousness, and there are ways it can be taught that breed fear. If we are going to have the effect God intends forgiveness of sins to have on us, we must understand it properly.

The forgiveness of sins that is primary to the faith of Christ is an accomplishment of redemption. It is not simply an act of mercy from time to time.

When we talk about forgiving one another as human beings, that is an act of mercy from time to time. If you wrong someone, you can say, "I am sorry, forgive me," and the person shows mercy. That is legitimate. If you have done wrong, you should apologize. The person may be hurt, and you are asking for mercy in that given situation.

But that is not the primary way forgiveness of sins is used in the faith.

In Scripture, the word used is `aphesis`, from `aphiemi`. The idea is to send away completely, or to go out completely. If something is on you, it is sent away completely. If you are in a place, you go out completely.

Those two senses help us understand forgiveness in Christ. If sins are like stains, they have been sent away from us completely. If sin is like a prison or bondage, we have gotten out completely. Scripture describes sin in both ways. Sin is an authority people are under, and people are also producing works that testify to that bondage. By the forgiveness of sins, we have gone out completely from sin, and sins have been sent away from us completely.

## Forgiveness, Salvation, and Damnation

The context here is redemptive and salvific. The sins being sent away, or we being sent out of sin, are being spoken of relative to salvation and damnation.

Sin can no longer hold us to any right to damn us, because the evidence against us has been sent away completely, and we have gone out completely from sin. It cannot hold us on the grounds of being under it, nor on the grounds of the things we have done as proof against us.

So if we are thinking in terms of damnation, sins have no such hold on us.

At the same time, we have to be wholesome in Bible teaching. As much as sins have been dealt with such that we cannot be damned, sins still have weight in the justice of God in relation to works and rewards. Their consequence in judgment is still real. Glory to God, we are not under the hold of sin, and we are not going to suffer the consequence of damnation. But we must still tell ourselves the truth and walk the remaining part of our life the way we ought to walk.

Forgiveness of sins is an accomplishment of redemption, not simply an act of mercy momentarily.

## Forgiveness as a Present Continuous Possession

When a man receives redemption, forgiveness of sins becomes a present continuous possession.

Ephesians 1 is speaking particularly to believers. Paul says, "In whom we have redemption, the forgiveness of sins." He is not merely saying that redemption includes forgiveness of sins in some abstract sense. He is saying forgiveness of sins is a present possession of those who have believed.

Colossians 1:14 says the same: "In whom we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of sins." To have redemption is to have the forgiveness of sins.

So on one hand, we can think about it doctrinally: Christ has accomplished redemption. On the other hand, we can think about it personally: I have forgiveness of sins.

Forgiveness of sins, in this redemptive sense, is not meant to be instrumental and transactional relative to your repentance, as though you only get it each time you successfully perform repentance. Rather, when redemption is received, forgiveness of sins becomes a present continuous reality.

## Justice Texts and the Difference Between Redemption and Relational Forgiveness

We have to distinguish between forgiveness in redemption and forgiveness in the justice of God relative to our dealings with people.

Sometimes people use the Lord's Prayer, "forgive us our trespasses as we forgive those who trespass against us," as though it means the same thing as Ephesians 4, "forgive as you have been forgiven." They are not the same.

"Forgive as you have been forgiven" is taking what God has done in redemption as the example for our conduct toward others. But "forgive us as we forgive" is a justice text.

Matthew 6:12 says, "Forgive us our debts, as we forgive our debtors." There is no sound grammatical or contextual basis to make that mean something else. Then in verses 14-15 the Lord says plainly, "If you forgive men their trespasses, your heavenly Father will also forgive you. But if you do not forgive men their trespasses, neither will your heavenly Father forgive you."

That is not describing the redemptive accomplishment of forgiveness from damnation. It is describing the justice of God relative to how you deal with others.

The same thing appears in Matthew 18 in the parable of the servant who was forgiven a great debt but refused to forgive another. The Lord concludes that the heavenly Father will do likewise to each person who does not forgive his brother from his heart.

Luke 6 says something similar: judge not, and you shall not be judged; condemn not, and you shall not be condemned; forgive, and you shall be forgiven. That is a relational issue in the justice of God.

So the Christian faith is not saying a person can do whatever he likes and then simply say, "I am forgiven," as though nothing matters. That person will still bear the consequence of the justice of God in relation to his works.

## The Blood of Jesus and Ongoing Cleansing

Another important text is 1 John 1.

John says that if we walk in the light as he is in the light, we have fellowship with one another, "and the blood of Jesus Christ his Son cleanses us from all sin." The tense there is present continuous. The person who is in the light is in a present continuous state of cleansing.

The blood of Jesus is an ever-flowing reality for the believer. That death of the Lord, his bearing the consequence of death for us, remains an ever-present reality for the one who is in the light.

So when a believer sins, the blood of Jesus Christ cleanses him from all sin. He is in a present continuous state of forgiveness.

Hebrews 10 says Christ offered one sacrifice for sins forever, sat down at the right hand of God, and by one offering has perfected forever those who are sanctified. The context there is sins. Sanctified once for all, forgiven forever, perfected forever. He has dealt with every blemish in relation to sins by one work.

From the standpoint of redemption, forgiveness of sins is already an eternal done work. From our personal standpoint, it is a present continuous reality.

## What Forgiveness of Sins Is Meant to Produce

What is forgiveness of sins meant to do? What is the effect of this message on the hearer?

Forgiveness of sins is meant to produce repentance.

If forgiveness of sins is preached in a way that only makes people comfortable, relaxed, and unconcerned, that is not the proper point of the message. Forgiveness of sins is not taught merely to make you feel happy and non-anxious. It is taught so that you can come back to God.

Ordinarily, sin shuts a person out of relating with God. It weakens boldness. It weakens confidence. What forgiveness of sins does is give the person the confidence to come back to God.

The fruit of forgiveness of sins is repentance. I would even say repentance and righteousness are the fruit, effect, or product of forgiveness of sins.

Licentiousness is not a product of forgiveness of sins. If someone hears forgiveness and the effect on his mind is carelessness about sin, then either the teaching was wrong or the person is twisting it through his own fleshly desire.

The reason your sins are forgiven is so that you can come to God. If you use forgiveness of sins to leave God, you should examine whether you have even understood the message properly.

## Repentance and Remission of Sins

Luke 24:46-47 says that Christ would suffer and rise from the dead the third day, "and that repentance and remission of sins should be preached in his name to all nations."

Repentance and remission of sins go together. The message is: despite everything, you can come to God. And that does not happen only once. The reason for the forgiveness of sins is that you can keep turning to God.

Luke 7 gives another angle. Jesus says concerning the woman, "Her sins, which are many, are forgiven, for she loved much." He is not saying she was forgiven because she loved. He is saying her love is the effect of forgiveness. To whom little is forgiven, the same loves little. The point is that the consciousness of forgiveness affects the heart, the conscience, the emotions. It turns the person toward God in gratitude and love.

If someone hears forgiveness of sins and the result is self-indulgence, then he did not hear it rightly.

## Forgiveness Leads to Love, Repentance, and Righteousness

Acts 5:31 says God exalted Christ to give repentance to Israel and forgiveness of sins. Acts 20:21 speaks of repentance toward God and faith toward our Lord Jesus Christ. Acts 26:20 says people should repent, turn to God, and do works befitting repentance.

That is the nature of the message. The forgiveness of sins turns people back to God. It does not send them away from God to continue in sin. It sends them to God again and again.

When you sin, you turn to God. You do not harden your heart. You do not chest it. You do not use forgiveness as an excuse for stubbornness. You turn to God and say, in effect, "Lord, here I am. I still need help. I am still trusting you."

Psalm language says there is forgiveness with God that he may be feared. Forgiveness produces awe, nearness, boldness to return, and reverence.

Romans 2:4 says the goodness of God leads to repentance. Any forgiveness of sins that is not leading to repentance is not the gospel of the Lord Jesus Christ.

So our sins are thoroughly forgiven, and that present continuous reality leads us to repentance. We are able to come to God.

## What Happens When a Believer Sins

If a man is a believer, his sin is forgiven in relation to damnation, but his sin is still judged as a matter of God's justice.

At the same time, a believer cannot live unrepentantly in sin long term. Whatever one wants to make of all the theological debates around perseverance and salvation, the scriptural posture is that a born-again man cannot remain unrepentant long term.

So what do we do when we sin? The answer is clear: repentance.

Repentance happens on three levels.

First, we acknowledge the sin in remorse. That is godly sorrow. It is not wrong to feel sorrow over sin. What is wrong is ungodly sorrow, the kind that crushes a person into despair, rebellion, or death. Godly sorrow regrets the act but remains safe in God's arms and leads to repentance unto life.

Second, we acknowledge forgiveness in Christ. We recognize by faith that the forgiveness we already have in Christ is real.

Third, we acknowledge our new nature for growth. We reckon ourselves dead to sin and alive to God. We affirm that this is not who we are, and we continue trusting the grace of God that has changed our nature.

That is what forgiveness of sins leads to. The response to sin is not denial. The response to sin is repentance.

## 1 John 1:9 and the Question of Confession

People often say 1 John 1:9 is instructing believers to keep listing out their sins in order to be forgiven. But in context, John is addressing people who deny the reality of sin.

When he says, "If we say we have no sin," he is dealing with those who claim there is no such thing as sin or contradiction. When he says, "If we confess our sins," the word means to admit. It is about admitting the reality of sin rather than denying it. In that context, he says God is faithful and just to forgive and to cleanse. The issue is salvation for those who stop denying sin and acknowledge the truth.

That does not mean repentance cannot include speaking honestly to God about what you have done. A person may well say, "Lord, was that wrong? I am sorry. Search my heart." Repentance can have different expressions. But the pattern is not that one must keep trying to secure forgiveness afresh. Rather, the believer repents on the basis of the forgiveness already secured in Christ.

## Walking in Forgiveness, Repentance, and Righteousness by Faith

The Christian life is a call to walk in forgiveness, repentance, and righteousness by faith.

The entry message of the Christian life is redemption and forgiveness, but that forgiveness is so that you may turn to God in repentance and walk in righteousness.

If someone claims to be a believer and yet there is no repentance in his life, we start to question that profession. Repentance is a distinguishing factor. A believer is someone in whose life there is repentance on the premise of redemption in Christ.

Forgiveness of sins is a present continuous reality for the believer in Christ. Therefore, repentance is also a present reality. If he finds himself wrong, he repents. Walking in righteousness is also a present conscious reality.

For some people there will be particular areas, strongholds, or recurring challenges that require continued repentance and faith until victory comes. That too is part of the Christian life. What must not happen is hardness of heart. The Christian life is a life of walking in forgiveness, repentance, and righteousness by faith.

## The Consciousness of Forgiveness

We are meant to have a forgiveness consciousness that makes us repentant and righteous.

In him we have redemption, the forgiveness of sins. If a person lives as though sins are always hanging in the balance, whether daily or monthly or yearly, that person will be deeply sin-conscious. But the believer is called to be forgiveness-conscious.

My sins are forgiven. That consciousness is part of the riches of the newness of life. It is the confidence for healing, the confidence for nearness to God, the confidence for the Christian walk.

We were sinners, forgiven by his grace. We are not sinners now. We are in the light. The blood of Jesus Christ cleanses us from all sins. We have a present continuous reality of forgiveness of sins.
