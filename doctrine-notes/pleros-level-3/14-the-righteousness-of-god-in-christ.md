# The Righteousness of God in Christ

We are looking at the righteousness of God in Christ. That is a very important subject matter, but an even greater concern is that people often misunderstand what the righteousness of God in Christ is.

When this subject comes up, someone may say it is about a righteous nature. Someone else may be cautious because they know people who teach righteousness in a way that is not exactly how the Bible teaches it. Some people's view of righteousness is justification, and they are justified in thinking so, but it is not wholesome enough. So even though this teaching on the righteousness of God in Christ comes under our teaching on the newness of life, I still want to explain the concept of righteousness in a wholesome way before we zoom in to that particular segment.

## 2 Corinthians 5 and the Broad Meaning of Righteousness

Let us start with 2 Corinthians 5, the very popular text for saying, "I am the righteousness of God in Christ." It is very debatable that this text is talking merely about our nature. It seems to be talking in a broad manner about being righteous, and I do not also think it is talking only about justification.

2 Corinthians 5:20-21 says:

"Now then, we are ambassadors for Christ, as though God were pleading through us: we implore you on Christ's behalf, be reconciled to God. For he made him who knew no sin to be sin for us, that we might become the righteousness of God in him."

The real thing that leads to this statement is reconciliation. Verse 18 says God has reconciled us to himself through Jesus Christ. Verse 21 explains how he did that reconciliation.

The concept of righteousness seems to be ontological, legal, and moral. When it says, "He made him to be sin for us," that has an ontological feel to it. It is speaking of a state or status. And when it says, "that we might become the righteousness of God in him," that also carries ontological, legal, and moral implications.

This text is loaded. You can fit in the different ways sin and righteousness are being spoken of here. He made him sin that we might become the righteousness of God in him.

## What It Means for Christ to Be Made Sin

There was a time I used to preach that Jesus took the sin nature. Over time, as I started thinking about the matter more deeply, I realized that was not making sense. Later I tried saying he took the guilt of sin, and after a while I realized that also had serious problems. How could he take guilt in that sense? There are scriptural descriptions you can point to that may work as descriptions, but not as the reality of redemption.

What Jesus became was that he came under the reign of sin, almost as though a slave of sin, on our behalf. By taking that status and experiencing its effect on himself, he could legitimately restore us back to what God wants us to be. So when 2 Corinthians 5:21 says he was made sin for us, it is speaking about being under the reign and bondage of sin in order to complete the redemptive work.

That is why this verse can hold ontological, legal, and moral ideas together.

## Righteousness as a Qualifying Word

The word righteousness is, at its core, a descriptive or qualifying word. It is about something being right. So when you say a thing is righteous, you are saying something about it, not merely naming it. At the core of righteousness is the idea that something is correct.

Correct and not correct are primarily about the state of how things are. `2 + 2 = 4` is correct. `2 + 2 = 5` is not correct. If I say, "That is a chair," and it is in fact a chair, that is correct. If I call it a table, that is not correct.

But this also moves into conduct. Someone can say, "This is the way a human being should be," or, "This is not the way a human being should be." That is righteousness language. So unrighteousness means not correct in judgment.

## The Righteousness of God and the Judgment of Truth

When we say "the righteousness of God," we mean what God says is right, what is right according to God's judgment. You are right as God considers things right in his judgment, and he made you right in Christ.

The way God considers things right is based on truth. That is why our teaching on the truth of morality matters so much here. We are asking: according to God and according to truth, what ought to be? In much of our discussion of righteousness, we are not talking about mere natural descriptions but about moral, legal, and judgment issues. What ought to be in God's view?

What ought to be is what originally is. When we see a contradiction to what originally is, it ought not to be. What originally is, we have said, is truth, life, and order. Falsehood, death, darkness, and disorder ought not to be.

## God Presented Life as What Is Right

From the beginning, God was setting forth to man what ought to be and what he considers right, and he called that life.

In Genesis 2, man was allowed every tree of the garden, but not the tree of the knowledge of good and evil. We can say there is the tree of life and the tree of death, and both of them are about knowledge. Proverbs 3 tells us wisdom is a tree of life, and that wisdom points to truth. So there is a knowledge that is life, and there is a contrary knowledge that is death.

What God is saying ought to be is life.

Deuteronomy 30 makes this very clear:

"See, I have set before you today life and good, death and evil."

He also says, "I have set before you life and death, blessing and cursing; therefore choose life." He calls life good. He calls death evil. He calls life blessing. He calls death cursing.

What God calls right is life. So righteousness is not first a legal issue. Righteousness is first a judgment about ontology. Life is what is right. Death is what is evil.

## Righteousness Is First Ontological

The first thing to understand about righteousness is that it is ontological. There is a state of being that is right, and there is a state of being that is wrong. Even if there had been no sin, if man had chosen life, man would have been called right. In the very possibility that he could have chosen otherwise, choosing life would have been righteousness.

The law itself was trying to depict how the life of God would be expressed. The law was, by instruction, describing what life is and how life functions in different situations.

Romans 8 says:

"For what the law could not do in that it was weak through the flesh, God did by sending his own Son in the likeness of sinful flesh, and on account of sin he condemned sin in the flesh, that the righteous requirement of the law might be fulfilled in us."

The law was stating how the Spirit works, but because people were in the flesh they could not do it. What the law demanded is now fulfilled in us. The righteous requirement of the law is fulfilled in us because we now have the life the law was pointing to.

Galatians 3 says that if there had been a law given which could have given life, righteousness would have been by the law. That proves the issue is ontological. If life could have been given, righteousness would have followed. So righteousness is primarily ontological.

## Righteousness Is Also Legal

The second thing is that righteousness is legal. Even though this is the state of things as God made them, God is judge of it, and there will be a judgment concerning what is supposed to be and what is not supposed to be.

Romans 3 shows this legal dimension clearly. The law speaks so that every mouth may be stopped and the whole world may become guilty before God. By the deeds of the law no flesh will be justified in his sight, for by the law is the knowledge of sin.

The law makes it clear that something is wrong. If the law encounters you, you may realize that you are flawed ontologically. It shows the problem. But the system of the law cannot make you right.

Then Paul says:

"But now the righteousness of God apart from the law is revealed... even the righteousness of God, through faith in Jesus Christ, to all and on all who believe."

That means what God assesses as right has now been revealed through faith in Jesus Christ. All have sinned and fall short of the glory of God, being justified freely by his grace through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus.

Justification is not merely that you are still wrong but now marked right. If that is your idea of justification, you do not understand what redemption entails. Redemption is primarily ontological before it is juridical. Because Jesus has undergone the consequence required by death, we are now found not wanting before death, but we are also made right.

So righteousness is legal, particularly because sin has happened. Humanity and creation together found themselves on the wrong side ontologically. We were under the state of sin and death. In God's judgment, all of us were marked unrighteous, and not only unrighteous in the sense that we needed change, but also in the sense that the consequence had to be dealt with justly.

That is why, if I say I am righteous, there is a sense in which we can speak of justification: the consequence has been dealt with. Christ has borne the consequence legitimately, and that gives the right for us to be restored into the state that is right.

## Righteousness as Nature and Conduct

The third level is righteousness as moral, based on our ontology. Our new nature in Christ now affects our conduct.

So let me put it simply: righteousness is ontological, righteousness is legal, and righteousness is moral. But that moral dimension is based on our new nature.

Romans 4 says Jesus "was delivered up because of our offenses, and was raised because of our justification." His resurrection is proof that the consequence has been fully borne. So when I read justification, I can read it legally, but when I read righteousness, I should also read it ontologically. I am now one who has the life of God.

That changed nature affects conduct. We can now do the works of righteousness. We can now bear the fruits of righteousness.

Philippians 1 speaks of "the fruits of righteousness." That means righteousness is now a nature from which fruits come. Ephesians 4:24 says the new man was created according to God "in righteousness and holiness of the truth." Our nature is a righteousness of truth. Ephesians 5 says, "For you were once darkness, but now you are light in the Lord. Walk as children of light, for the fruit of the light is in all goodness, righteousness, and truth."

So there is the righteousness that we are as a matter of truth, and there are the fruits that come from that. There is righteous conduct and unrighteous conduct. Righteousness is moral based on our nature.

## What It Means to Say, "I Am the Righteousness of God"

When we make the statement, "I am the righteousness of God in Christ," we are saying, "I have the life of God. I have the nature of God."

Romans 6 presents this to us from two standpoints: the old has been destroyed, and the new is now here.

"Do not present your members as instruments of unrighteousness to sin, but present yourselves to God as being alive from the dead, and your members as instruments of righteousness to God."

To present yourself as alive from the dead and as an instrument of righteousness means righteousness is life. I am righteous. My members are instruments of righteousness.

Romans 6:6 says our old man was crucified with him, that the body of sin might be done away with, that we should no longer be slaves of sin. We were once dead in sins and trespasses, but now we are alive. We were once unrighteous, now we are righteous. We were once the old man, now we are the new man created in righteousness and holiness.

So the righteousness of God means I have life. It means I am the new man. It means I am alive.

## The Flesh, Desire, and Ordered Living

Galatians 5:24 says, "Those who are Christ's have crucified the flesh with its passions and desires." The flesh is what is wrong. The flesh is death. The flesh is unrighteousness.

So when we are talking about who we are as the righteousness of God, we start even with our desires. I am the righteousness of God. I have righteous desires, desires of life, desires of truth, desires of order.

If a feeling, thought, attitude, or desire is not aligned with righteousness, not aligned with life, not aligned with order, and not aligned with truth, then that is not who you are in Christ. In the natural you may still say, "I feel like doing that," but that is not your true identity in Christ. You must affirm who you are.

When we say we are the righteousness of God, we are saying we do not have disordered desires, disordered thoughts, or disordered conduct. We have ordered desires. We have ordered thoughts. We have ordered conduct. Life is ordered. Truth is ordered. That is the point.

From our desires to our thoughts to our attitudes to our words to our conduct, we are the righteousness of God. Our desires are desires of life, desires of truth, desires of order.

## Standing by Faith to Walk in Righteousness

What do we do now that we know we are the righteousness of God? We stand by faith to walk in it.

Primarily, in the place of prayer and devotion, we stand by faith to walk in that righteousness. Romans 6:11 says to reckon yourself dead indeed to sin and alive to God. Romans 6:13 says not to present yourself as an instrument of unrighteousness, but to present yourself to God as alive from the dead, and your members as instruments of righteousness.

Romans 12:1 says to present your bodies a living sacrifice, holy and acceptable unto God, which is your reasonable service. You are not dead; you are alive. You stand on God's word and present yourself accordingly.

It is not enough for righteousness to remain a title. In the place of prayer and devotion, you are putting on the new man and putting off the old. As Peter says, you are adorning the hidden man with the incorruptible beauty of who you are in Christ. As Paul says, you put off the old man and put on the new.

That is how we walk in the righteousness that we have.
