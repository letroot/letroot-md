# Discipleship - The Path for Growth

## God's Purpose Requires Growth

So we are looking at discipleship, the path for growth. We are studying this on the back of what we understand about God's purpose. We have this clear understanding that the reason that we exist is for God's purpose. God, who is self-existent, created the world for his own purpose. And we have examined what God's purpose is. Ephesians chapter 1, verse 9, as well as verse 5, has helped us to conclude that God's purpose is sonship.

Now that understanding, that God's purpose is sonship, leads us to something else. Of course, you remember that sonship means we are God's children as well as his heirs. So as his children, we are meant to walk in his nature and character, and as his heirs, we are meant to carry out our natural assignments as well as spiritual assignments.

Now knowing that is fantastic. Imagine you know that you exist just to walk a particular path. You know there is a definite path laid out for you. Maybe it is like a 1,000-meter path, but it has different twists and turns that you are supposed to take. And someone tells you, this is why you exist. You are supposed to go this journey. There is a destination somewhere that you are supposed to get to. When you get to this 100-meter point, you are to take left, not right. When you get to this other point, you are supposed to do this and that.

Now the person has shown you the path that you are meant to follow to fulfill purpose. But if that journey involves you carrying certain loads along the way, maybe you are supposed to carry two heavy loads on each hand, then your fulfillment of that journey is not just dependent on your knowledge of the path. Your fulfillment of that journey is also dependent on your strength, your energy, your endurance, your fitness to bear the burden as you go.

That is precisely what leads us to the study of growth. We are saying that when we know God's purpose for our life, it still necessitates that we have growth. That we know about God's purpose, that we know how it is supposed to turn out, that we know the way the journey is, does not excuse us from the need for growth. It is good that you know God's purpose, but you need to grow. Without growth, you cannot fulfill God's purpose for your life. So the knowledge of God's purpose is not enough. There is the necessity of growth.

## The Church Was Given for Growth

One of the texts that makes this obvious is Ephesians chapter 4. In Ephesians chapter 4 and verse 11, a text that we often quote, Paul tells us the reason for the church. He says that Jesus gave the apostles, prophets, evangelists, and pastor-teachers. He gave the leaders of the church. What did he give them for? He tells us that he gave the leaders of the church for the perfecting of the saints. That word there, `katartismos`, means they are supposed to bring the saints to a particular end point.

What I want us to see is that end point these people are meant to bring the saints to. Look at verse 13. He says, till we all come to the unity of the faith. At that point you might think the church is just for knowledge. Then he says the knowledge of the Son of God, still about knowledge. But look at the next phrase: the perfect man. Perfect man means fully developed, completely developed. So he is no longer just talking about knowledge. He is talking about the implication on a man's humanity. Then look at the fourth one: the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ. By the time you see the word stature there, there is no debate that what is being discussed is growth.

So he is saying the work of the church, or the work of the leaders of the church, is to ensure our growth. They are to give us knowledge, quite all right, but that knowledge is not enough. It is supposed to turn out to the end that we grow till we come to the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ. And what makes it clearer that growth is really what the church is meant to do in us is verse 14 and verse 15. Verse 14 says we should no longer be children tossed to and fro. God does not want us to remain in a childish state, an undeveloped state. He uses that word `nepios`, someone who cannot yet speak. An infant cannot yet communicate in the language that pertains to his culture or community.

You will find that there are believers who are not fully formed to be able to answer on matters that every believer should be able to answer on. So he says we should no longer be children tossed to and fro by every wind of doctrine, by the trickery of men, in cunning craftiness of deceitful plotting. But verse 15 says, speaking the truth in love, we would grow up into Christ in all things. That is the core work of the church: to ensure our growth into Christ.

## Why Growth Matters So Much

Why does he want us to grow? When Jesus died and rose again from the dead, why did he give the church to make us grow? The reason is simple. The reason why Jesus died and rose again is not merely to forgive our sins, but to restore us back into God's purpose for our life. We have had to repeat that several times: the reason for the death and resurrection of Jesus is not just the forgiveness of our sins, but restoration into God's purpose.

There are some people whose understanding of Christianity ends with the forgiveness of sins, with their redemption from the power of darkness. If you ask them, why did Jesus die for you? They say, so that I will not go to hell. Why are you now going to church? Well, just to thank him for what he has done, thank him for his blessings on my life. That is a fatal mistake. That is a fatal misunderstanding of what Christ has done. And I say fatal because it means the very reason for your life, you are going to miss out on it.

The forgiveness of sins is not an end in itself. It is a means to an end. Your salvation is not an end in itself. It is a means to an end. Except you understand your salvation properly as restoration into God's purpose, that you were saved back into God's purpose, then your understanding of salvation is still incomplete.

So the reason Jesus gave the church, the reason Jesus gave the leaders of the church, is to perfect the saints so that they can grow to fulfill God's purpose for their life. Growth is so important to our existence. If we are going to fulfill God's purpose, which is the very reason why we exist, then we need growth. Fulfilling God's purpose is not just the knowledge of the journey. Fulfilling God's purpose requires that strength, that endurance, that fitness, that growth, to bear the burden as we go on that journey.

You cannot carry out your assignments in God's purpose without spiritual growth. You might say, but I am already doing my natural assignment. I am running my business. I am having my career. But you might be doing it in the flesh, which means you are already losing your rewards. It is possible for someone to be engaged in their natural assignment but not be doing it in the Spirit. God's word is clear that God cannot be mocked. Whatever a man sows, he will reap. If you sow to the flesh, you will reap corruption. If you are engaging your natural assignment from the flesh, you are already doing it to waste.

You can be so productive. You might be so laborious. But if this productivity is not coming from the Spirit, then it is missing what matters. God's word teaches maximum productivity. We are meant to be productive. But what you are doing must come from the Spirit. Growth is not a negligible matter when it comes to the Christian faith. It is the only way you can actually carry out God's purpose. Activity is not the proof that you are doing God's purpose as he wants. Growth is the guarantee that you do it that way. Because when you are growing spiritually, then you are definitely carrying out your walk as a child of God and your walk as an heir of God the exact way that God desires.

## The Great Commission Is a Discipleship Mandate

If growth is this important, how does this growth happen? We know that the most important thing about our existence is God's purpose. We have now resolved another issue: God's purpose can only be fulfilled by growth. If growth is this important, how does it happen?

I have already answered the question, in a sense, by saying the church was given for that growth. But I want to show us the exact point in history where the Lord gave the church to bring about spiritual growth. As at Ephesians 4, it is already clear that Jesus gave the leaders of the church. They are meant to perfect the saints, and it is supposed to bring about growth. But when was that point historically where the Lord spoke to them and gave them that instruction? Matthew chapter 28 and verse 18. Jesus said, "All authority has been given to me in heaven and on earth."

That statement is related to the church. The earlier places in Matthew where Jesus used this heaven-and-earth authority language were about the church. In Matthew 16, he said, "You are Peter, and upon this rock I will build my church," and then, "I give you the keys of the kingdom." What you bind on earth shall be bound in heaven. In Matthew 18, in the matter of discipline and church authority, he again spoke in those terms. So by the time he said in chapter 28, "All authority has been given to me in heaven and on earth," and then said, "Go therefore," they should have understood that he was speaking to the authority he gives to his church.

And what governs how the church exercises that authority is this matter of discipleship. When he says, "Go therefore and make disciples," the KJV says, "teach," but in this instance that is not the most helpful rendering. The word there is not the ordinary word for teach. It is the word for making disciples, `matheteuo`. A disciple is `mathetes`. The point he makes is that the idea is related to a logical passing on of knowledge.

That is why he draws the connection with mathematics. There is nothing more logical than mathematics. In analytical reasoning, you can even reduce arguments to symbols to show there is no contradiction. The point is that discipleship is not imposition. Nobody goes to mathematics and says two plus two is equal to five, and then when you ask how, they say, I am the teacher, you will understand later. That is no longer discipleship. That is assertion. That is imposition.

The nature of discipleship, by the very meaning of the word, is not someone being imposed upon to just accept something. It is someone being convinced on logical grounds. You might almost say on mathematical grounds. So when the person holds that persuasion, the person is doing it with confidence of their own certainty, even though taught by another.

## Discipleship Produces Conviction, Not Manipulation

That is why he warns against a wrong idea of discipleship. Today, the word discipleship has bad PR. There are legitimate fears and concerns people have about discipleship because there are places where what they call discipleship is not really discipleship. It is someone asserting and imposing, and people are just following dogmatically. Sometimes it is manipulative. What is going on there is not conviction.

Very important to recognize that discipleship means there is conviction happening based on evidence that you can independently verify. That is why if someone asks, what is God's purpose, the answer should not be, according to what my teacher said. If we are agreed that the Bible is our authoritative document as believers, then what does the Bible say, and what is the evidence to that effect? If that conviction is not your own, with evidence that you have seen yourself and can present independently of your teacher, then it is more like imposition. You are just being forced to accept it, and that is not God's idea.

So when Jesus says, "Go and make disciples," he is not calling for manipulation. He says, baptizing them into the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit, and then teaching them to observe all things whatsoever I have commanded you. That shows us discipleship requires that they are first saved, but the core of discipleship is teaching toward observance. The first word is make disciples. The second word is the actual word for teach. So he shows us that though teaching is the primary activity in discipleship, discipleship is not mere teaching.

Verse 20 says, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you. That has to do with doing. That has to do with walking. You can teach something without an intention for someone to do it. That is not discipleship. You might just teach for enlightenment. But if you are teaching with the intention that people agree and that it affects their walk, then discipleship is at play.

Another way of thinking about discipleship is apprenticeship. An apprentice is not just there to gather knowledge. An apprentice is there to gain expertise. So the knowledge is the starting point, but practice must happen. The person is being taught to do, taught to observe, taught to act, taught to carry out what they are learning.

## Submission Does Not Come First

Submission does not come in at the beginning of discipleship. That is something we might not want to admit, but it is true. Submission happens as a result of the discipleship process. That is why in the church, people could come who were even unbelievers, and some were unlearned, and they could be accommodated. Their submission would happen over time as they came around, even though what the church was doing was still discipleship.

Otherwise, we make the church look more cultic than churchly. Someone can stroll into church. Someone can be around and not yet have his life together, not yet be committed, and still keep coming. The person is in the process of deciding. That is not strange to the church. In fact, books like Hebrews were written in that light, especially with people who had to come to a firm commitment to salvation and faith in Christ.

So we have to be honest about the face of the church. There is nothing strange about people being in church who are not yet committed. Do not make discipleship difficult for people. Do not make the church difficult for people. If we genuinely want discipleship to happen as Jesus instructed it, it will happen the way it played out in the book of Acts. It will be a journey.

## Discipleship Respects Freedom of Conscience

Many people who could have become strong in the faith have been turned off by certain ideas of discipleship that are not really discipleship. So he clarifies that biblical discipleship fully respects freedom of conscience. The result of discipleship has a lot more to do with the recipient than with the teacher. The teacher can do his best, but discipleship cannot force you. They can do their part, and you might still not be a doer. They are not supposed to bend you. At best, what the teacher of God's word can do is provide knowledge and then inspiration for you to stand on that knowledge.

That is why Paul says in 2 Corinthians 1:24, "Not that we have dominion over your faith, but are fellow workers for your joy; for by faith you stand." The apostolic model of discipleship made it clear: all that we can do is help you. We provide the knowledge and the inspiration. You receive that knowledge by your availability, and it is on you that you respond to that knowledge by faith.

Discipleship does not take away freedom of conscience at all. Acts 5 shows that people were around the church for long sometimes without joining the church. There were people who did not yet dare join them, though believers were increasingly added to the Lord. In 1 Corinthians 14, Paul even assumes that uninformed people and unbelievers may come in. That means weak believers who had not yet gotten their bearing were still counted. They were not yet the committed people, but they were still present, and the church was supposed to be aware of that and help their conscience.

Conscience is a very big issue in the Christian faith. First Corinthians 8 warns that people should not use their knowledge to hinder those who do not have that same knowledge. Knowledge puffs up, but love edifies. So as important as knowledge is in the Christian faith, you must ensure that it is not puffing you up. You must deliberately bring love into what you are doing.

That is why he insists that believers are not supposed to become harsh in handling people. Sharp rebuke has its place in God's word, but it is after longsuffering. Before someone is treated like a tax collector, there has already been repeated correction. There should be a spirit of longsuffering that can endure a wrong for a while before final rebuke and withdrawal.

Biblical discipleship fully respects freedom of conscience. It does not disregard it. In 1 Corinthians 8, Paul did not impose on the weak. He knew what was better, but he did not override the present state of their conscience. He expected those who had knowledge to have that disposition. He says in Romans 14 to receive one who is weak in the faith and not to dispute over doubtful things. He even says clearly that one believes he may eat all things, while the weak one eats only vegetables. He knows who is right, yet he still accommodates the weak conscience.

So discipleship does not override freedom of conscience. It allows people to grow from weakness into strength. Even when the goal is that believers eventually speak the same thing, as in 1 Corinthians 1:10, that sameness is not an imposition. It is an outcome of a process. When they have been `katartizo`-ed, brought to the same mind and the same judgment, then they speak the same thing. Not because they were forced to speak alike, but because they were taught into conviction.

## Discipleship Seeks Knowledge for Conviction

If discipleship is the path of growth, what then does discipleship entail? The first thing is this: discipleship seeks knowledge for conviction. Discipleship begins by seeking knowledge for conviction. It does not begin with commitment, at least not commitment to an institution. At most, it begins with a commitment to know, a commitment to learn.

So you are receiving the knowledge of the word. You are being taught. You are being strengthened. You are trying to confirm: is it really so? Is this the truth they are presenting? On the side of the one discipling, discipleship provides knowledge for conviction. Your work is not to demand conviction. It is to give the word so that the person can enter into conviction.

On the side of the disciple, you are not assuming the rightness of the teacher. You are there to be taught, but you are going to be discerning. You are seeking to be convinced. You are open to it. You are being taught within the confines of the faith of Christ. If someone goes outside that boundary, of course you should run away. But within that boundary, you are confirming whether these things are so.

That is why sometimes, by God's orchestration, more light comes to you, and it may mean you have to change commitment because of that. He points to Apollos as an example. Apollos was teaching according to the light he had, but Priscilla and Aquila taught him the way of the Lord more accurately. From that point, his commitment matched with his knowledge. It can happen.

## Discipleship Exercises Faith-Stand for Growth

The second thing discipleship does is exercise faith-stand for growth. Jesus says, teaching them to observe, and from apostolic teaching we understand that what leads us to observing or doing is faith. If a man gets clarity, knowledge alone will not be enough for any further action. The response of faith must come in.

So discipleship seeks knowledge for conviction, and then it exercises faith-stand for growth. The apostles teach, and they inspire the observing, the doing. They inspire people to stand on the word in prayer. And it is in that process that people now grow to the point where this thing is not just right, but I should commit to it. The strength of commitment comes in the place of growth.

There is a way you can begin to demand commitment too early and jump the gun. As you are providing knowledge of God's word, provide the growth. In the strength of that growth, commitments will come naturally, as growth happens by the knowledge of God's word and the response of faith to the same knowledge.

## Discipleship Makes Commitments to the Local Church

The third thing is that discipleship makes commitments to the local church. In Acts 5, they saw it as joining. There were some who had not yet joined, and there were those who were added. So people do commit. There are people who are just around and have not yet joined, and a church must have room for that. They are still trying to get their bearing.

But discipleship does bring a person to the point of commitment to a local church. At that point you are no longer merely an attendee. You are now a member. He says there should be a standard process for that. He gives the example of a perfecting course, where someone does a first course and is then recognized as having joined, understanding the basics such as the gospel and God's purpose. A further course may indicate that the person is now ready to serve as a worker because they understand the doctrinal framework and are willing to serve others.

## Discipleship Leads to Submission to Spiritual Authority

The fourth thing is that discipleship leads to submission to spiritual authority. As you commit to a local church, you must be aware that it means submission to spiritual authority. But the word submission also has bad PR, and with some justifiable reasons, because people have used it in very funny ways.

So he clarifies it carefully. If what we are saying is biblical submission and not subjugation, then submission demands accountability but does not necessitate obedience. It expects obedience, but it does not always guarantee that you should obey. Why? Because your submission is born out of persuasion. You sought knowledge for conviction. Your persuasion led to your commitment. So many things you are told to do, you will naturally obey, even if they are uncomfortable, because they are already in line with what you have become persuaded of.

But if something comes that seems wrong, harmful, or outside the bounds of that persuasion, then biblical submission does not mean you must obey evil. You can seek clarity. If you are unsure and it is harmless, you may still go ahead. But if you are unsure and you think it is harmful, you should not go ahead. If you are sure that it is not right, you should not do it. That is why he gives the extreme example that even in marriage, submission does not mean following someone into evil.

So your submission is born out of persuasion, and your submission is defined by accountability. It does not necessitate obedience to evil. Even where Hebrews 13 says, "Obey those who rule over you, and be submissive," he points out that the sense is rooted in being persuaded. It is not blind subjugation.

## Discipleship Produces Practice and Walk

Lastly, discipleship births practice and walk. Jesus said, teaching them to observe. That means discipleship must be seen in your walk. It should inspire you to walk in your character, your nature, and your assignment, both natural and spiritual.

When Ephesians 4 speaks of the perfecting of the saints and spiritual growth, the next verse says that you should no longer walk as the Gentiles walk. Discipleship is not complete if we do not see it in expression. Something must change about your attitude, your disposition, your words, your conduct. The way you think should change. The way you act should change. One of the first signs of discipleship is that the way you think is different.

He says the way he thinks now is not the way he used to think before he was born again, and not even the way he thought five years after conversion. His thinking has been highly developed by God's word. God's word gives you the framework of your epistemology. You are able to look at matters better.

That change should also affect how you regulate your emotions, how you talk, how you act, and how you carry out your assignments. Even your attitude toward your natural assignment should change. You should become maximally productive, yet not greedy. You should be able to use resources for comfort without guilt and also use them for mission, generosity, the poor, and whatever the Lord may direct. The issue is wisdom and proportion, not false guilt.

That is the effect discipleship is supposed to have on you. It is not cheating you of one thing in order to give you another. It is bringing you into a wiser, more faithful walk.

## Do Not Be Afraid of Discipleship

So discipleship is the path of growth necessary for us to fulfill God's purpose. It does not override freedom of conscience. It seeks knowledge for conviction. It exercises faith-stand for growth. It makes commitments to the local church. It leads to submission to spiritual authority. And it births practice and walk.

So do not be afraid of discipleship. Do not run away from discipleship because of wrong models that are not necessarily biblical or Christian models of discipleship. Discipleship in the Christian model does not override your own responsibility to reason and arrive at conclusions and have your faith as a result. It does not rob you of the individuality of your personality, even though the process should bring us into sameness in our belief.

The point is that you should not be cheated of all that the Lord has for you in the local church and in discipleship because of concerns, even legitimate ones, produced by wrong models.
