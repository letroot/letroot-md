# The Walk of Faith

So, we are looking at a very important teaching: the walk of faith. We are still on level three, the walk of faith. It is so important because whenever we are talking about the subject of faith, we are really at the heart of the Christian faith. We are dealing with something so central to Christianity.

I would like to start with a reminder about God's purpose so that it gives us context for the place faith has in our life. We have often said that the Christian faith is really about God's purpose. I understand that the first contact we make with Christianity is salvation or redemption. When the gospel is preached to you, it is the message of salvation and redemption that comes to you. You do not want to be damned. You do not want to perish. Then you hear the message and realize that God has done a redemptive work to save us from this evil, from the contradiction, from the death and darkness that is in this world.

But at the point you are saved, the question then is: what are you saved to? You realize that you are saved into God's purpose. The reason for the salvation that God has wrought or fulfilled in Christ Jesus is to restore man back into his purpose.

## Saved Into God's Purpose

This is something we can readily see from Ephesians 1. Look at verse 9, which we typically use to introduce God's purpose. Paul said that God has made known unto us the mystery of his will. He has made known to us this hidden secret of his will. When you call something a mystery, it means it cannot be known except it is revealed. It is hidden.

God's purpose is what salvation restores us into. We are not merely saved from something; we are saved into something. We are saved into what God has designed and purposed.

There is a way that, even though I am talking about walking in it and works, we can disqualify ourselves from one part of God's purpose because we feel that we are not worthy based on another part. We can say, "I no longer care about this issue," because we are failing in another issue. Or, "I am not yet ready for this one," because we are failing somewhere else.

But it says that we are created for good works that we should walk in them. It means it does not matter if you are falling short in one regard; you may still be doing well in another, and that is good. That is good testimony. Paul talked about that in 1 Corinthians 3. He said that he was going to come and perfect what is lacking in their faith, but in that very text he first commended the areas where they were doing well.

We must not have the wrong mindset that failure in one part means abandonment of the faith altogether. It is not all or nothing. There is progress across the board. There may be progress in one area greater than another, and yet you are still making progress.

## Walking Is the Goal of the Christian Life

Look at Romans 6:4. I am saying that the essence of being in God's purpose, of being saved, of now being in God's purpose, of being a new creation, is to walk in it. Walk is the essence. You are not in God's purpose just to be shouting it. The knowledge of it is not the goal. The reality of being in it is not the goal. The essence of being in God's purpose is to walk in it so that you can fulfill it.

"Therefore we were buried with him through baptism into death, that just as Christ was raised from the dead by the glory of the Father, even so we also should walk in newness of life." If we have shared in his death and resurrection, the end is that we should walk in what is new, in the newness of life. Walking is the goal of the faith.

At this point, some might argue that all this walk is just about our character, our status, or merely the reality of being a child of God. But again, walking is the goal of the entire Christian life. It includes walking in the reality of your salvation, walking in the reality of being a new creation in terms of character, but it also includes walking in your assignment.

Look at 1 Corinthians 7:17. "As God has distributed to each one, as the Lord has called each one, so let him walk." The moment we are talking about your calling, your purpose, the end of it is that you should walk in it so that you can fulfill it.

It is not enough that you are basking in the beauty of salvation and redemption in Christ. It is not even enough that you are basking in the glory of the new creation or the spirit of sonship in you. It is critical that you are walking to fulfill God's purpose. So walk is the central issue in our faith.

## Continue With the Lord

Look at Acts 11:23. This is when Barnabas went to Antioch to follow up on the folks who had gotten saved there. "When he came and had seen the grace of God, he was glad, and encouraged them all that with purpose of heart they should continue with the Lord." They were saved. They were joyful. They were in the spirit of that salvation. Yet Barnabas encouraged them to continue with the Lord.

Walking is the big deal because there is something for them to fulfill. With purpose of heart, they should continue with the Lord. Say, "I am born again, I am saved, I am redeemed, I am a new creation," so that I can walk. Walking is the big deal of the Christian faith.

You find it in the prayers of the apostles as well. In Colossians 1:9, Paul says he desires that they be filled with the knowledge of God's will in all wisdom and spiritual understanding. You would think that is enough, but it is not. Why? "That you may walk worthy of the Lord." Walking is the goal, fully pleasing him, being fruitful in every good work, and increasing in the knowledge of God.

Colossians 2:6 says, "As you have therefore received Christ Jesus the Lord, so walk in him." Walk, walk, walk.

## The Christian Walk Is the Walk of Faith

At this point you may say, "So we should do the word. It is not enough that we are saved or know we are saved. We should do what new creations should do." Yes. But if you do not know what kind of walk we are talking about, you will have the wrong kind of walk.

Remember what we are looking at: the walk of faith. I will say it this way: the walk in the Christian faith is the walk of faith. No matter what we are talking about, the walk in the Christian faith is the walk of faith.

James 1 says, laying aside all filthiness and overflow of wickedness, receive with meekness the implanted word, which is able to save your souls, and be doers of the word and not hearers only, deceiving yourselves. James is saying: walk in the word; do not just hear the word. We are called to walk.

Galatians 5:16 says, "Walk in the Spirit, and you shall not fulfill the lust of the flesh." So all this walk, how does it happen? What kind of walk are we talking about?

## Walking Worthy of the Calling

Look at Ephesians 4:1. Walk does not pertain only to our character. It pertains to our assignments. It pertains to our calling. Our entire Christian life can be described with that word: walk.

"I therefore, the prisoner of the Lord, beseech you to walk worthy of the calling with which you were called." You have been called out of darkness into light. Walk worthy of that. You are walking in a sense of equality across gender, race, and class. You are walking in lowliness. But that walk also extends to your spiritual assignment. Verse 7 will say that to each one of us grace is given, so you are going to walk in that as well.

After everything he has said from Ephesians 1, 2, and 3, he now says, "Walk worthy of the calling with which you were called, with all lowliness and gentleness, with longsuffering, bearing with one another in love."

So we are called to walk, but it is not enough that we just say it. We must know how to walk. Ephesians keeps using this language: walk no longer as the Gentiles walk, walk in love, walk as children of light, walk circumspectly. How exactly do we walk in the Christian faith? Is it by mere effort? Is it by trying hard?

## Reckoning Who You Are in Christ

Go back to Romans 6, where he spoke about walking in newness of life. One of the things we have studied a lot is that Romans 6 assumes the knowledge of its audience. Verse 3 asks, "Do you not know...?" That is a rhetorical question that takes their knowledge for granted. Verse 6 says, "Knowing this, that our old man was crucified with him." Verse 9 says, "Knowing that Christ, having been raised from the dead, dies no more." So there are things you should know.

If someone finds himself walking in the flesh, there are things he should first know: I am not like this. That should be settled in your mind. We are no longer under the dominion of death. The things that operate under the reign of death are no longer what rule us.

But then in verse 11 Paul gives an instruction: "Likewise you also reckon yourselves to be dead indeed to sin, but alive to God in Christ Jesus our Lord." All the while he has said, "You already know," but now he says, "Reckon."

That word is `logizomai`. It means to take into account. The reality is already there, but you are now intentionally accounting it. You are affirming what is already the case. It is not enough that you know. You are recording it, affirming it: I am dead to sin. I am alive to God.

Verse 13 describes the same act in another way: "Do not present your members as instruments of unrighteousness to sin, but present yourselves to God as being alive from the dead." The word "present" is `paristemi`, to stand beside. Paul is saying: stand beside yourself as who you are in Christ.

So the way we walk is by reckoning ourselves, affirming ourselves, standing beside ourselves as who we are in Christ.

## We Walk by Faith, Not by Sight

Now look at 2 Corinthians 5:7: "For we walk by faith, not by sight." This is an interesting use because the Bible does not always separate sight from faith. For example, Romans 1 says the invisible things of God are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made. So sight can sometimes be part of the basis of faith.

In 2 Corinthians 5, the context is that Paul is telling them that their body is perishing, but there is a hope in the redemption accomplished in Christ that the body will be glorified. So he is saying that we are not focused on present circumstances. We are focused on something we know in the message. We have believed a revelatory truth. The victory of God over death and darkness has already been won. We are walking by that faith and not by the present prevailing circumstance.

Faith is not always anti-natural evidence. Sometimes natural evidence can lead to faith. You can observe a contingent world and think toward God. People witnessed the death and resurrection of Jesus, and Paul referenced their testimony as a ground of our faith. So when he says, "We walk by faith, not by sight," sight here is not general sensory observation. It is seeing something that appears to negate a truth we have already concluded in Christ.

That means we can apply this principle in other cases. Someone knows he is a new creation, but presently sees himself walking in a contrary way. He says, "I walk by faith, not by sight. I am not like this." He is not denying what he sees. He is saying there is a superior truth in Christ.

If a person sees sickness or disease, he knows there is such a thing present, but he also knows there is victory over it in what Christ has done. That victory is already working in him. So he says, "The power of God is at work in my body because of what redemption has accomplished. I am the healed of God."

When we say we walk by faith and not by sight, we are not denying what exists in the natural. We are saying that we are not walking by the default state of sin and death in this world. We are walking by the victory that has been obtained by Christ.

So, by faith I walk in the redemption accomplished in Christ, not by sight, which is the default state of sin and death that is in the world. Very important.

## By Faith You Stand

The Christian faith is not just for knowing. The Christian faith is for walking, and this walk is by faith.

Look at 2 Corinthians 1:24. Paul says, "Not that we have dominion over your faith, but are fellow workers for your joy; for by faith you stand." Preachers do not have dominion over people's faith. We are helpers of your joy. We are supplying all that we can for your faith because we know it is by faith you are going to stand.

Recall that we have already seen that how we walk is by reckoning or presenting, which is affirming or standing beside ourselves. So that word "stand" is not strange. Standing is what we do so that we can walk. Romans 6:13 says, "Present yourselves to God." Stand beside yourself. How do we stand? By faith we stand. It is that faith-stand that leads to our walk.

From Romans 6 and from 2 Corinthians 1 and 5, we can establish that faith is how we walk.

## As You Received Christ, So Walk in Him

Look at Colossians 2:6-7. "As you therefore have received Christ Jesus the Lord, so walk in him, rooted and built up in him and established in the faith, as you have been taught, abounding in it with thanksgiving."

How did we receive the Lord? Romans 10 tells us. "The word is near you, in your mouth and in your heart, that is, the word of faith which we preach." If you confess with your mouth the Lord Jesus and believe in your heart that God raised him from the dead, you will be saved. With the heart one believes unto righteousness, and with the mouth confession is made unto salvation.

So we receive salvation through the mechanics of the heart and the mouth. Our heart believes. Our mouth confesses. That word "confess," `homologeo`, means to affirm. It is not mere recitation. It is the affirmation of what is already believed in the heart.

If we walk by faith, then the first thing we need to understand is the mechanics of faith. Faith begins with hearing, or with the eyes as well. Romans 10:17 says faith comes by hearing, and hearing by the word of God. The Scriptures are the authoritative document for truth given to us by God.

The mechanics of faith begin with the ears, then go to the heart. The heart here is conviction. You agree that this is actually the case. Then it goes to the mouth. Your mouth affirms what your heart agrees with.

## The Persistence of Faith

Now, for salvation, immediately you agree in your heart and affirm with your mouth, you are saved. But in our walk, it does not work that way. When we affirm with our mouth relative to our walk, it brings about growth. That faith-stand causes us to grow, and from that growth we have the experiential walk.

So there is an area where faith increases, grows, and gets stronger. This is not describing salvation faith. Salvation is received once and for all. But this other faith, the faith that pertains to our walk, has a seed principle to it.

That brings us to the second thing we must understand about faith: the core principle of faith is the persistence of faith. That is what Paul was saying when he said, "Reckon yourselves." You already know and agree, but you need to reckon. That reckoning is with your mouth. In this case, you are persisting.

When Romans 12:1-2 says, "Present your bodies a living sacrifice," that presenting is not a one-off thing. It is your reasonable service, your `logikos latreia`, your logical worship. Presenting yourself is something you do continuously. Again, this shows us the persistence of faith.

James says, "Receive the implanted word." The Lord Jesus says faith is as a mustard seed. The idea of growth, increase, and strength applies to this kind of faith, the faith related to our walk: your walk in status, your walk in character, your walk in the supernatural, your walk in your assignments.

Faith is meant to persist until it bears fruit, until it produces result. If you allow something else to cut short the persistence of faith, you may affect the result.

## The Lord Jesus on Continuing Faith

Look at Luke 11. After teaching on prayer, the Lord begins to teach about persisting for what you are praying about. He gives the example of the friend at midnight. Though the man will not rise and give to him because he is his friend, yet because of his persistence he will rise and give him as many as he needs.

The core principle of faith is persistence. That is where faith grows, increases, and gets stronger unto results.

Then in Luke 18:1, "Men always ought to pray and not lose heart." He tells the parable of the widow who kept coming to the unjust judge. At the end he says, "Nevertheless, when the Son of Man comes, will he really find faith on the earth?" In other words, will he find people persisting like that?

When the disciples said, "Increase our faith," the Lord spoke about faith as a mustard seed. The issue is not merely whether you have faith or not; it is that faith persists like a seed. You stay there. You remain rooted, built up, and abounding with thanksgiving.

That is why where people are given to prayer, where they are in faith in prayer for the supernatural, they tend to see more of the supernatural.

## The Effect of Faith on the Inner Man

The third thing about faith is the effect of faith. The effect is seen in our inner man, our consciousness. The person's consciousness becomes stronger. "I am the healed of God. I am the righteousness of God. I am a love-being in Christ." If you allow the word access into your ears and heart, and it keeps reinforcing itself there, over time your inner man is molded that way.

This is why, if you are not walking in love as you should, or righteousness as you should, or consecration as you should, or your assignment with the fervency and discipline you should, you have to be careful. The same principle of faith that applies to our walk in God's purpose also applies to walking in the flesh. There can be a misdirected reinforcement of the flesh.

Faith can grow. 2 Thessalonians 1:3 says, "Your faith grows exceedingly." 2 Corinthians 10 speaks of faith increasing. There is such a thing as faith growing or increasing. There is such a thing as degrees or measures of faith.

Romans 4 says Abraham was not weak in faith by considering his own body. That means he was not walking by sight. Not walking by sight is not a denial of what is there; it is that you know the default state, but you are not stopping there. You are affirming the victory of redemption over it.

## The Fruit of Faith in Daily Life

The fourth thing we are going to see about faith is the fruit of faith. The fruit of faith is the life of God or the nature of God manifest in any given area of our life. The work of faith is a product of persistent faith-stand.

Look at 1 Thessalonians 3. Paul says, "I sent to know your faith, lest by some means the tempter had tempted you, and our labor might be in vain." Then he says Timothy brought good news of their faith and love. This is not talking about salvific faith. It is the ongoing state of their walk-faith, the faith that pertains to their walk.

Then he says, "Now we live, if you stand fast in the Lord." That is persistence. Yet he also says he wants to perfect what is lacking in their faith. So they are doing well, but something is still lacking. By 2 Thessalonians he can say their faith grows exceedingly.

The result of faith is seen in the walk, in the fruit, in the outcomes. We can see the fruit of faith in one area of a person's life while there is still lack in another area.

## Prayer and Maintaining Faith-Stand

If you are going to grow spiritually after you have been discipled in local church, everything you have been taught boils down to your response in faith. Prayer is the shortcut for maintaining persistent faith-stand.

You may respond in faith at a given moment when something shows up. But if you are going to maintain persistent faith-stand, as Romans 12:1-2 shows us, prayer is the ground that guarantees it. If you start missing your prayer times, you start to get weaker. Once you start to lose your persistence of faith, it will begin to show in the effect of faith. Your inner man, your consciousness, gets more fleshy than spiritual. Then the fruit begins to show that way.

You have to be intentional and active in times of prayer. It is our reasonable worship to present ourselves. If you are not present with your mind, affirming as you are speaking with your mouth, something is wrong. Prayer is work. Otherwise, you are deceiving yourself and losing out on growth.

The things that the Lord would have corrected and addressed in you, leading to you walking more fully in character or assignment, are not being allowed to happen if you are careless in prayer.

## Insisting on the Superior Reality of Redemption

The walk of faith is the walk of the believer. Doing the word is by faith. Walking in God's purpose for our lives is by faith. We are called to walk, and that walk is by faith.

The mechanics of faith are of the ears, heart, and mouth. The core principle of faith is persistence. The effect is on our consciousness, and the result is seen in our life, producing our walk.

Pick an area of your life where you want to stand on God's power. For example, healing: God's healing power is always at work in my body. My body rejects sickness and disease. It cannot entertain it. There cannot be any sickness or disease in my body. There cannot be any malfunction in any part of my body. My body is healed of God, completely. Every single part, every little detail, is perfected by God.

Or preservation: I am safe and secure and preserved and delivered by God at all times, in all places, under all situations, under all circumstances.

Or character: I am a new creation. My character is righteous. The only things I desire to do are the things of righteousness and love. It is natural to me to walk in righteousness and love. I do not struggle to walk in righteousness. I am self-constrained and self-controlled in speech, in attitude, and in conduct. I am born of God. I have his life and his nature.

These are the things that make up our affirmation, our faith-stand. We can do it with understanding. We can do it in the Spirit. We can do it in worship and in singing. All of those times are opportunities for faith-stand.

No matter what we see in the natural state of the world, we must insist on the faith of redemption. We are working on the superior reality of what has been accomplished in Christ, not in denial of what is in the natural, but in the superior reality.
