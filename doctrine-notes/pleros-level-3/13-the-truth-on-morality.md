# The Truth on Morality

We are looking at the truth of morality. Morality is a highly contentious subject matter. You find many people have different things to say about it, and I am not just talking about contentions within the faith of Christ. It is also highly contentious outside the faith of Christ.

Particularly in our age, you find different areas of morality where people are contending. There are issues like homosexuality, where people debate whether the Bible says it is right or wrong. There were times people even debated racism. You hear people say strange things like, "That is our culture," when something is very obviously evil and immoral. That is how contentious morality is. There will always be someone willing to say something is still okay, still morally right, and who are you to tell them it is wrong. Someone will say, "That is my own truth. You cannot dictate to me. Why are you trying to impose your own morality on me? I have my truth; you have your truth. To you it is wrong, but to me it is not."

So both within the faith and outside the faith, morality is a very contentious issue. What we are seeking to do in this teaching is to establish the truth of morality, and not merely in a way that is applicable only within the faith of Christ. On this subject of morality, we have a response to give to anybody and everybody, no matter the philosophy they hold or the worldview they have.

## The Core Question: Subjective or Objective Morality?

One of the main contentions on this subject is whether morality is subjective or objective. That is really the core issue. Is morality subjective, or is morality objective? People say, "To you that was right; to me this was right. There is your truth and there is my truth." In some cases, these issues have even taken on political tones.

Then even where a person accepts some kind of objective moral framework, the next question is on what grounds he accepts it. There are those who hold a divine command theory of moral objectivity. If you ask them, "How do you know what is right?" they say, "Whatever God says is what is right." But then another question arises: is what God said right because he said it, or did God say it because it was already right?

Scripture does not leave us in the dark on this matter. It presents a clear submission about it.

## Ephesians 5 and Truth-Based Morality

I personally consider Ephesians 5:8-10 one of the cardinal texts on morality:

"For you were once darkness, but now you are light in the Lord. Walk as children of light. For the fruit of the light is in all goodness, righteousness, and truth, finding out what is acceptable to the Lord."

Paul is talking about conduct, actions, activities, and practices. He is saying that what we actively do, our walk, is to be judged in a certain way. The law itself could never exhaustively comment on every possible scenario, so there had to be judgment and discernment. Why? Because the law was supposed to be an embodiment of truth, and for the sake of this teaching, an embodiment of moral truth.

Notice that Paul says the fruit of the light is in goodness, righteousness, and truth. The words good and right do not help us by themselves, because those are the very things people are arguing over. If you say something is good, someone can ask, "According to whose standard?" The thing that helps us here is truth. Paul is saying that we know what is good and what is right by truth.

There are places where we use right in a way that is not according to anyone's preference. If someone says `2 + 2 = 4`, we say that is right, not because of anybody's standard, but because it is true. If someone says there is gravity on earth, we say that is right because it is true. So the real issue is truth determining right, and as we are going to see, good as well.

Paul is saying there is a way to know how to walk, the conduct to have, based on truth. Truth determines what is good and what is righteous. It is by that truth that we prove or find out what is acceptable to the Lord. So Paul is speaking of a truth-based walk, truth-based conduct, truth-based morality.

## Truth, Reason, and the Christian Faith

You can never divorce philosophical theology from biblical theology. This issue of truth was already a concern for man even before salvation, and it does not disappear when we receive the revelation of Scripture. We still need logic, sound reasoning, and right epistemology in how we handle biblical revelation.

That is why it is dangerous when people speak against logic or reason. People's lives have been damaged because they were told to ignore reason and logic. Why would God want to fight what he gave you? If the fruit of the Spirit is based on truth, how can someone think God would be against sound medical advice? The exercise of faith is not against sound medical truth.

This same point appears again in Ephesians 4:24. Paul says the new man is created according to righteousness and holiness that is of truth. There is a truth-based righteousness and a truth-based holiness. So it is not only Ephesians 5 that says this. Ephesians 4 also says there is a way by truth to judge what is right, what is good, and what is holy.

## Revelation: God's Ways and Judgments Are True and Right

The book of Revelation also points in this direction. In Revelation 15:3: "Just and true are your ways." In Revelation 16:7: "True and righteous are your judgments." In Revelation 19:2: "True and righteous are his judgments." In Revelation 19:11, speaking of Christ: "In righteousness he judges and makes war."

The point is not that whatever God judges automatically becomes true by sheer fiat. The point is that God judges according to truth, and therefore what he judges is right. His ways are right and true. His judgments are true and righteous. There is a way to judge what is right by truth, and God is consistent with that.

It is easy to make that conclusion ontologically. If someone says something true about the world, we can say it is right because it aligns with truth. The challenge is how to make that conclusion morally. How do we know a conduct is right? The moment we move into the realm of conduct, people ask, "By what standard?"

## The Is-Ought Problem

This is where the so-called is-ought problem appears. It is easy to say, "This is a car," or, "There is gravity on earth," and be right. But when you say, "This is the right thing to do," people ask where that ought comes from. What makes something what ought to be done?

Some therefore argue that there is no objective morality, only moral emotivism, that things just do or do not feel right to us. But I believe there is an objectivity to morality. If morality is a matter of truth, then the discussion must begin where every discussion of truth begins: ontology, the truth of existence, the truth of how things are.

## Truth Begins With Ontology

Any discussion of truth begins with ontology. In Romans 1, Paul says men suppress the truth in unrighteousness. He is not talking merely about biblical truth there. He is speaking about ontological, philosophical, existential truth available to all men.

What may be known of God is manifest in them and to them. Since the creation of the world, his invisible attributes are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made. By observation and reason, the first conclusion we arrive at is the reality of the self-existent Creator God.

Romans 1 also says we can know his eternal power and godhead, that is, his divinity, his nature. If we can know God's nature from the world he made, then that matters for morality.

## God's Nature as Original and First

If God is self-existent, then his nature is what is original and first. The nature of God will be anything that is original and first. When we see in the world things that are original and things that are contrary to the original, we already have a conceptual framework for understanding what is of God and what is a contradiction.

The primary conceptual example Scripture uses for this is light and darkness. Darkness cannot be first, because darkness is the absence of light. You can speak of counterfeit only because there is first an original. You cannot really conceive darkness without reference to light, because darkness is defined as the withdrawal or absence of light.

This is why 1 John 1:5 says, "God is light and in him is no darkness at all." God's nature, as that which is original and first, can conceptually be described as light. If we now speak of darkness, we are speaking of something contrary to the original.

## Life, Light, Death, and Darkness

The conceptual description is light, but the actual reality can be described as life. Another way to speak of it is being. He is "I AM." In the natural world, that reality is often best described as life. I am not speaking of biological life alone. I mean the reality of being, of what properly is, in contrast to corruption, degeneration, waste, destruction, and non-being.

That is why Scripture joins life and light together. John 1:4 says, "In him was life, and the life was the light of men." John 8:12 says, "I am the light of the world... he who follows me shall not walk in darkness, but have the light of life." John 14:6 says, "I am the way, the truth, and the life."

So we can say God's nature is the nature of life and light, and the contradiction to his nature is death and darkness. Before creation there was no darkness in God. But once a world is created in which creatures have freedom, there is at least the possibility of darkness, even if not yet the actuality of it.

## Bridging Ontology and Morality

If we can establish an objective ontology, namely that there is God who made the world and whose nature is life and light, then we have the beginnings of objective morality. But there is still a problem: there cannot be objective morality without an axiom, because morality concerns what ought to be done.

The axiom I consider primary and necessary is this: what is original ontologically is what ought to be morally. We bridge the is-ought gap by this primary and arguably necessary axiom. What is original ontologically is what ought to be morally.

You can say it this way: life should be and be done; death should not be and should not be done. You can call this existentially demanded, ontologically imposed, or divinely imposed. But you cannot escape the burden of such a basic axiom if you are going to speak meaningfully about morality at all.

That is why even people who speak as moral emotivists still act as though life is right and death is wrong. You do not see random killing and say it is normal. Your conscience is shaped by the ontological state of things. There is an objective moral framework founded on objective ontology.

So I will put it this way: life and light are what is morally right. Death and darkness are what is morally wrong.

## Finding What Is Acceptable to the Lord

When I make a judgment about what I am to do in any situation, I am looking at whether what I am doing is in furtherance of life and light, or in furtherance of death and darkness. That is how Ephesians 5 works: by truth, knowing what originally is life, I can find out what is acceptable to the Lord.

This is where moral dilemmas come in. Lying is ordinarily wrong. Truth and order belong to what is original. But there can be situations in which, because the higher concern is life, a lie becomes morally right in that situation. If someone intends to murder a person and asks you where that person is, that is not the same as ordinary falsehood for selfish gain.

In the same way, killing is ordinarily not of life. But there are cases, such as the restraint of evil, where in furtherance of life it may be judged right. This is why ethics is not always neat at the level of moral expression, even when moral truth is objective.

## The Five Parts of the Moral Framework

There are five things at play in morality.

First, moral truth. This is what we have spent our time establishing: life is right, death is wrong; life is good, death is evil. What is contrary to the original is evil.

Second, moral intention. Our intention must always be to do life and to bring about life. We intend to express what is original and to bring about what is original.

Third, moral instruction. This is where laws, codes, and directives come in. Whether in Scripture, in a nation, in a church, or in a home, instructions are formed out of moral truth and moral intention. "Do this" or "do not do this" arises from the aim of preserving and promoting life.

Fourth, moral expression. Moral expression should primarily be life. But in practice there are instances where an action that is not itself the ideal expression of life may be employed for the sake of bringing about life. That is where ethical difficulty often lies.

Fifth, moral outcome. We are trying to bring about the right moral outcome. Sometimes we may discover that a certain expression did not serve the intended outcome, and then we correct it because the intention remains to do life and bring about life.

This is why intention is so important. If the intention is right, a person can later recognize that a particular course of action was mistaken and can correct it.

## Applying the Framework to Human Sexuality

Paul gives one of the clearest applications of this in Romans 1:26 and following. He speaks of women exchanging natural use for what is against nature, and likewise men leaving the natural use of the woman and burning in lust for one another.

At that point, what is in view is not only life in the broad sense, but also order, the truth-order built into nature. What is original by nature is what is right. What is against nature is a contradiction to that original order.

So when Paul speaks that way, he is saying that what is original by nature is what is morally right: life, order, truth. And what is contrary to it is morally wrong: death, disorder, falsehood.

This teaching is necessary because of what we are teaching concerning the newness of life and the righteousness of God. Love, too, has to be explained from within this framework. Love is not just being nice.
