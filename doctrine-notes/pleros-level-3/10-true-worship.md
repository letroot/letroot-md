# True Worship

We are looking at true worship. It can seem close to what we have already looked at in the life of prayer, because prayer and worship are closely connected, yet there is still a distinction worth paying attention to.

When people talk about prayer, they often limit it to speech. We are talking to God, and that makes sense. But worship seems to encompass more. Worship is a more dynamic and more encompassing interaction with God beyond speech.

For example, when Abraham went to sacrifice, the Bible speaks of it as worship. I know people have built extra layers of theology around that, but my present concern is simpler. I am trying to help us see why the things we will look at under worship go beyond prayer. Even if prayer and worship can be equalized at the core, worship gives us room to discuss sacrifices, music, and actions like dancing and clapping, and whether they have any role in our engagement with God.

The reason prayer and worship are closely related is that the very words point us in the same direction. The word worship, `proskuneo`, carries the idea of affection, nearness, and submission. So there is an affectionate, relational sense to worship. In one sense, someone may even want to say worship is a subset of prayer, and the person may not be wrong. But usually we use prayer for our speech engagement with God, while worship is used for a more all-encompassing engagement with God.

## True Worship in John 4

In John 4, when the Lord spoke to the woman at the well, she raised the issue of place: "Our fathers worshiped on this mountain, and you Jews say that in Jerusalem is the place where one ought to worship."

That question already shows how encompassing worship was. It referred to the whole structure of engaging with God. There were altars, sacrifices, places, and inherited patterns of approach. Jesus answered by saying that the hour was coming when they would neither worship on that mountain nor in Jerusalem.

That alone tells us that something fundamental was about to change.

Then he said, "You worship what you do not know; we know what we worship, for salvation is of the Jews." That means God's redemptive revelation and covenantal disclosure had been entrusted to the Jews. But then the Lord goes further: "The hour is coming, and now is, when the true worshippers will worship the Father in spirit and in truth."

Notice first that "in spirit" answers the question of place. The woman asked where worship should happen. The Lord answered by shifting worship away from a physical mountain and toward the spirit.

Notice also the use of "truth." In this context, truth is not chiefly truth versus falsehood. It is reality versus depiction. People were truly worshiping the true God, but much of their worship was still in types, shadows, symbols, and depictions. They used incense, blood, sanctifications, and physical structures that pointed to something deeper. The Lord is saying that through redemption there would now be direct worship in reality.

So when we speak of true worship, we are talking about interacting with God in actuality, in the direct reality that redemption has made possible.

## Redemption Changes the Place and Manner of Worship

Through the work of Christ, two major things change. The first is the place of worship. The second is the mode or manner of worship.

Before, someone could think, "As long as I am in the temple, bowing, sacrificing, and doing the right actions, I have worshiped." But in true worship the mind becomes highly critical. So not only does the place of worship change, the manner of worship changes as well.

This also helps us see the link between prayer and worship. As we study worship, we are moving beyond merely what we do to how we do it, together with the broader realities that accompany our interaction with God.

## The Place of Worship Is in the Spirit

What does it mean that true worship is in the spirit?

The first thing to note is that God created the world as both physical and metaphysical, natural and spiritual. The world is not merely material. Human existence itself presses metaphysical questions upon everyone.

In that broad sense, the world is both natural and spiritual. But when the Lord says we worship in the spirit, he is not merely talking about that broad reality. He is speaking of a specific place in the spirit that pertains to those who have believed.

Ephesians 2 is central here. We are no longer strangers and foreigners, but fellow citizens with the saints and members of the household of God. We have been built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets, Jesus Christ himself being the chief cornerstone. The whole building grows into a holy temple in the Lord, a dwelling place of God in the Spirit.

That means believers make up a specific dwelling place of God in the spirit. There is a general spiritual realm, but there is also this distinct dwelling place of God composed of those who believe.

In 2 Corinthians 6 Paul says, "You are the temple of the living God." So when we talk about temple, we are not talking first about a physical building. A church building is still a natural building. What gives definition to worship is not the structure in the natural but the reality of who we are in the spirit.

Hebrews 12 says, "You have come to Mount Zion and to the city of the living God, the heavenly Jerusalem." It does not say you are coming; it says you have come. So right now, together with all believers, we make up that dwelling place of God. The church, the angels of God, the spirits of just men made perfect, Jesus the mediator of the new covenant, all are spoken of in that heavenly reality.

That is the first thing about true worship: it is happening in the spirit. Right here and right now, wherever you are, you are in that dwelling place of God.

## The Father's House and the Believer

John 14 helps personalize this reality.

When Jesus said, "In my Father's house are many mansions," the word translated "mansions" is better understood as dwelling places or spaces. He is speaking about the Father's house as the congregation of believers, the house God is building.

The physical temple was a depiction. The real dwelling place of God is among his people. Collectively, we make up the Father's house.

Then in John 14:21-23 the Lord shows the personal implication of that collective reality. If anyone loves him and keeps his word, the Father will love him, and the Father and the Son will come and make their home with him.

So collectively we make up the house of God, and individually the Father and the Son are at home with each one of us.

That means our place of worship is in the spirit, in the dwelling place of God, which is the congregation of believers, and in that same reality the Father and the Son are at home with us personally.

## The Manner of Worship: The Heart Must Be Near

Having settled the place of worship, we must ask how true worship is engaged.

Matthew 15 is crucial here: "These people draw near to me with their mouth, and honor me with their lips, but their heart is far from me. And in vain they worship me."

That means someone can be active in worship and yet not be near to God in worship. The person may be doing all the visible activities, yet the Lord says the heart is far away.

The core issue in true worship is the heart, and by heart here we are dealing essentially with the mind. People often overcomplicate such terms, but the practical point is plain: in true worship, what counts most is the state and presence of the mind before God.

We are already one spirit with the Lord. But just as the body is used through the brain, the inward life is functionally engaged through the mind. So even though we are spirit beings and even though God is Spirit, the manner of worship is a matter of the mind.

Someone may already be in the dwelling place of God, and yet because the mind is disengaged, the person can be shut out in experience from what is already true in reality. It is like having money available that you do not know is there. The provision is real, but ignorance prevents participation.

So if your mind is not present in worship, whether you are praying, singing, bowing, or doing anything else, your heart is far from the Lord. True worship is not any activity you do in itself. It is the presence of your mind toward God in the spirit.

## What the Mind Does in True Worship

If the mind is central, then what exactly matters about the mind in true worship?

The first thing is attention. "Their heart is far from me" means their attention is not on me. They are not mindful of me. They are not aware of where they are.

The second thing is consciousness. In true worship we must be conscious of redemption and the new creation. If someone comes before God with sin-consciousness rather than son-consciousness, the person's mode of engagement will be different from what redemption has granted. Scripture repeatedly calls us to come boldly through the blood. So in worship we must be conscious that God is our Father, that we are sons, and that redemption has granted us access.

The third thing is faith. We persist in faith as we engage with God. We stay with the truth. The Father and the Son have made their home with me. I am in the dwelling place of God. We persist in faith for fellowship, for growth, for results, and for anything we are standing on before God.

The fourth thing is that true worship involves being filled. Ephesians 5 says, "Do not be drunk with wine... but be filled with the Spirit, speaking to yourselves in psalms and hymns and spiritual songs." The language of being filled carries the idea of intoxication or transcendence. So true worship can involve degrees of transcendence, stronger knowings in the spirit, and even visions or revelations.

The fifth thing is that true worship affects the emotions. Sometimes there is joy. Sometimes there is burden. Sometimes there is deep reverence. In all of this, the center remains the same: we are engaging with our spirit via our mind.

## Music as an Aid, Not the Essence

At this point, an obvious question arises: if true worship is in the spirit and the mind is central, what place do natural things still have? Can we use music?

Yes, we can. Music is an aid to true worship.

When Scripture speaks of psalms, singing, and making melody to the Lord, it opens the door to music and musical instruments. Ephesians 5, Colossians 3, and 1 Corinthians 14 all show that singing belongs within the worship of God. The use of instruments is not strange to worship.

The key, however, is order. Music is not itself the essence of true worship. It aids the engagement of our spirit via our mind. It can help reverence, focus, and spiritual responsiveness. Our bodies may also express reverence through lifted hands or bowed knees, but those outward expressions flow from the heart.

The Scriptures also show this aid-function in examples like Elisha asking for a musician in 2 Kings 3. As the musician played, the hand of the Lord came upon him. Likewise in 1 Samuel 10 there were prophets with instruments while prophesying.

So music and musical instruments may be a vital aid through the ages, but they are not the core of true worship. True worship remains our engagement with God in the spirit by the mind.

## Worship Is About God, Not Our Enjoyment

This is where wisdom is needed. Once we see that music and instruments are allowable, someone may quickly move to questions about fast songs, energetic praise, and other forms of expression.

The issue is not always that such things are automatically wrong. The issue is that they must be rightly situated. True worship is about God, not about our enjoyment. If something that should be an aid becomes dominant in a way that distracts the mind from engaging God, then the order of worship has been disturbed.

In many congregations, what should have been an effect of worship or an aid to worship becomes performative. Bodies are busy, lips are busy, mouths are busy, but hearts are far away. That is one reason the flow of the Spirit may be diminished.

So the real concern is not merely whether a practice exists, but whether it preserves the priority of true worship.

## Rejoicing and Laughter as Effects of Worship

True worship affects the emotions, and one clear effect is joy.

Luke 10 says that Jesus rejoiced in the Spirit. The word there carries the sense of leaping or exuberant rejoicing. The important point is that the joy came out of the spirit. It was not mere performance. Something provoked in the spirit expressed itself outwardly.

So rejoicing is an effect of true worship. Laughter can also be an effect of true worship.

We must be careful here. These things are effects, not performances. If they are turned into something imposed or artificial, the beauty of them is lost. Someone can rejoice spontaneously. A congregation can also rejoice together, especially where the atmosphere is already full of joy because of the gospel or because of what God has done. Philippians even says, "Rejoice in the Lord always." So rejoicing may be instructed in a fitting sense.

Still, it should not become performative or coercive. One person may be rejoicing while another is under a burden of the Spirit. We must leave room for the genuine flow of the Spirit.

## Dancing and Clapping in Their Right Place

Then what about dancing and clapping?

We need wisdom here as well. There are valid concerns about fleshly, distracting, or purely performative dancing in church. There are atmospheres where people are so busy with outward celebration that the mind is no longer engaging God, and in such settings the flow of the Spirit is hindered.

That concern is real and should not be dismissed.

At the same time, I do not think it is honest to completely demonize dancing or clapping in relation to worship. Scripture shows celebratory dancing in various places. Exodus 15 shows Miriam and the women with tambourines and dancing after the Lord's victory. David danced before the Lord. The Psalms speak of praising God with dance. Other texts show dancing as a common expression of joy and celebration.

That means dancing is often an expression of joy, celebration, or festivity. It is not always the same thing as the direct inner engagement of true worship, but it may happen on account of what God has done. In that sense it can be accommodated as an aftermath or celebratory effect on account of the Lord.

The same applies to clapping. While many biblical instances of clapping can be debated, there are places where clapping plainly appears as joyful celebration rather than mockery.

So I believe we must be able to accommodate self-initiated dancing and clapping as things people may choose to do on account of God, his redemption, and the joy of belonging to him. But they must not gain ascendancy over true worship itself.

## The Order of True Worship

This is the order we must preserve.

True worship is to engage with the Father in the spirit. We engage God, who is Spirit, with our spirit by our mind. Our attention is on him. Our consciousness is shaped by redemption and the new creation. We persist in faith. We may be filled with the Spirit in degrees of transcendence. Our emotions may be affected.

Music can aid that worship. Rejoicing and laughter can arise as effects of it. Dancing and clapping can, in some cases, be accommodated as celebratory responses on account of God and his work.

But none of those things should replace or overshadow the actual heart of true worship.

There is an honest case to make on both sides of the discussion. On one hand, people may ignore true worship and become preoccupied with outward enjoyment. On the other hand, it is also possible to deny legitimate celebratory expressions that Scripture does seem to allow. So wisdom is critical.

The most wholesome submission we can make from the Bible is this: true worship is in the spirit, and it is centered in the mind's engagement with God. It has effects in the natural, and some of those effects may also be self-determined celebratory responses. They are fine when they are kept in their rightful place and do not disrupt the priority of genuine fellowship with God.
