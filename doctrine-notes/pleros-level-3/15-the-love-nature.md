# The Love Nature

## Why Love Needs Definition

We are looking at the love nature. The concept of love cannot be properly understood without the understanding of the truth of morality.

When people try to define love, they often end up in a circular definition. Love becomes affection, affection becomes care, care becomes concern, and concern circles back again. That is a dicey way of understanding anything, because if one person's idea of care or affection is different from another person's, then nothing has really been defined.

The same problem happens with words like righteousness, goodness, and love. People act as though everybody just knows what these things mean by intuition, but our conscience and moral intuitions are not arbitrary. They are formed by the state of things in nature, by the ontology of our world. If we do not define these things properly, we will keep struggling to understand concepts such as love and righteousness.

In a very general sense, love is wanting the good of another. Whether it is parental love, friendship, sibling love, or romantic love, the common idea is that you want what is good, right, or best for another person. But that still leaves the real question: what is good?

## Truth Defines Goodness and Righteousness

Ephesians 5 says the fruit of the light, or the Spirit, is in all goodness, righteousness, and truth. The point is that truth defines the righteousness. Ephesians 4:24 says, "the righteousness and holiness of the truth." That means what is right is known by truth.

Truth tells us first that God is primary. God is first, self-existent, and necessary. When we describe the nature of that which is first, we say God is light and life. The contradiction to God is darkness and death. So when we ask what ought to be, what is right, and what is good, the answer must be that which is of God, that which is first, that which is not the contradiction.

That means life and light are what is right and what is good. What ought to be done is that which is existentially primary. Truth is primary; falsehood is secondary. Life is primary; death is secondary. Order is primary; disorder is the contradiction. So righteousness is doing what is true, what is of order, what is of life.

## Love as Relational Righteousness

At that point it may seem like we are only talking about personal righteousness. But when what is of life is done toward others, or when what is of life is desired for others, that is what we call love.

Love is relational righteousness.

There is righteousness where you do what is right simply because it is right, whether anyone is there or not, and whether it benefits anyone or not. But when we say we have the love nature of God, or that we are walking in love, we are talking about a righteousness that has to do with another person.

You can have personal moral righteousness that is not relational righteousness. You can refuse corruption simply because it is wrong. That is righteousness. But you can also refuse it because you know it will damage people. That is love at work.

Love is part of righteousness, but it is specifically relational righteousness: wanting to do what is right, good, and of God toward another.

## The Difference Between Human Love and the Love Nature in Christ

In ordinary human relationships, love often carries selfish elements. In marital or romantic love, there may be physical attraction, emotional attraction, sensual attraction, or some personal benefit. Even in family and friendship, people often want good for others in ways that still include their own benefit.

That is part of the challenge with human love.

But the love nature in Christ is different. It is not desiring the good of another because of what you gain from them. It is desiring the good of another because that is who you are. There is a love for spouse, children, family, and friends that is real and godly, but there is also the love nature in Christ toward neighbor and people generally. In that love, we want the good of others. We want life for others. We do not want death for them.

## Wanting Life for Others

If we are going to resolve what love, care, concern, and affection mean in a definitive way, we can say they are talking about wanting life for others.

Love is desiring the good of others. The good being referred to is life.

That is the nature of love: to desire good, to desire life for others. Whether we are talking about the Hebrew word for love or the Greek word `agape`, the idea is this desire for good, this positive desire, this emotion toward life for another person.

If you are not desiring life for other people genuinely, then you are not walking in love. That is why Jesus says, "Love your enemies." Imagine a believer desiring death for someone else. That is not love.

There is a place for justice, and desiring life for others does not mean justice should not be done. But justice is different from personally desiring evil for others. When people are no longer desiring life for others, what they want to happen to those people is often not about justice at all; it is selfish bitterness.

Life is not just physical. If someone is depressed, mentally tortured, discouraged, or in despondency, that person is not walking in life. So when you speak to people, when you deal with people, your concern should be that they are found walking in life. One of the highest expressions of wanting good for people is wanting them to fulfill God's purpose.

## Love for God and Faith

This same logic also helps explain loving God. To love God is to want what He wants, to accept Him as He is, to agree with Him, and to receive His purpose and redemption.

The expression of our love for God is faith.

We see His purpose, we see redemption, and we want what He wants. "You want us to be your family. Lord, we love you. We want what you want." Faith in God is loving God.

## Love Is the Most Emphasized Part of Righteousness

Love is an emphasized part of righteousness. More than that, love is the most emphasized part of righteousness.

That is why it is strange when a believer is so serious about personal uprightness and yet not serious about relational righteousness. A person can be big on personal righteousness and still fail in love. That is a major problem.

If you are not walking in relational righteousness, you are not walking in all of righteousness. Love is the most emphasized part.

## Love Must Show in Expression

You can know that love is relational righteousness and still not know how to express that wanting life for others. Wanting life for others must show up not only in your intentions, but also in your tone, your words, and your outward expression.

Someone may genuinely want life for people in intention and yet express it wrongly. Your verbal expression can be wrong. Your tone can be wrong. There may be a time for firmness, but it still has to be right for that situation.

If in your thoughts you do not love people, you are already wrong at the heart level. Your business in life is to desire the good of people, to desire life for them, to want it to go well with them.

## Healing From Hurt to Walk in Love

This becomes especially important when people have hurt you. There are times people hurt us and we have not fully healed from what they have done.

You must learn to heal. You must detach yourself emotionally from the hurt. You must become free from guilt, shame, anger, bitterness, and the wound itself. Some people remain tied to what was done to them because the offender did not apologize properly or did not repent properly. But you cannot waste your life on that.

You cannot be carrying wounds around and still claim to be walking in love. If you are bitter toward someone, you cannot genuinely wish the person well. That is not relational righteousness. That is not love.

Love will cause you to heal because you want to walk in love. If I am going to walk in love, I am not going to carry hate or wounds toward anyone. I detach myself emotionally.

## Cain and Abel as the First Moral Warning After the Fall

Right after man's sin, the first moral correction God had to make was about relational righteousness. It was about love.

That is why Cain and Abel become such a model in Scripture for love versus hate.

Cain's countenance fell. His attitude changed. His shame, anger, and bitterness became tangible. Attitude is real. A negative spirit, a negative disposition, and a fallen countenance are real things. Cain did not stop it at that stage. Instead, he allowed it to continue until it produced murder.

The Lord asked him, "Why are you angry? Why is your countenance fallen? If you do well, will you not be accepted? And if you do not do well, sin lies at the door." The warning came before the act. The moral issue had already begun at the level of attitude, bitterness, and anger.

That is why believers must guard their hearts. You must not live in guilt, shame, hurt, bitterness, or anger. Those things can move toward destruction. Relational righteousness is the first major moral emphasis we see addressed after the fall.

## Love in the Law

The same emphasis appears in the law of Moses. The first commandments concern loving God, and the remaining commandments concern loving people.

Honor your father and mother. Do not murder. Do not commit adultery. Do not steal. Do not bear false witness. Do not covet what belongs to your neighbor. These are all matters of relational righteousness.

Covetousness is especially revealing, because it shows that love operates at the level of desire. To love is to desire other people's good for them. To be able to say, "Lord, let all the good possible for this person be theirs," is a powerful place of love.

The problem with many people is not simply that they want more, but that they want more than others. Love frames desire properly. Even in ministry, the question is whether your heart is to reach people for God's glory, or whether you simply want to be greater than others.

Leviticus 19 spells this out clearly: do not steal, lie, cheat your neighbor, hold back wages, act unjustly, slander, or take vengeance. Do not bear a grudge. "You shall love your neighbor as yourself." Even rebuke must be done in a right spirit, not out of anger.

## The Love Nature Promised in the Prophets

This love nature was promised in the prophets. Ezekiel 36 says God will cleanse His people, give them a new heart, put a new spirit within them, and cause them to walk in His statutes. Jeremiah 31 says He will put His law in their minds and write it on their hearts.

The point is that this law of love, this relational righteousness, would become our nature.

Under the former covenant, people had to be instructed from outside. Under the new covenant, the law is written within. The nature itself is changed.

## The Teaching of Jesus

By the time we come to the teaching of Jesus, the place of love remains emphatic. "Whatever you want men to do to you, do also to them, for this is the law and the prophets."

That is love. Do life to others. Desire life for others.

Do you desire life for all people, particularly those who have hurt you, and those who are against you? There is still a place for justice, but desiring life for them does not mean justice should not be done. Love is still relational righteousness. It is still wanting what is right, what is good, and what is of life for others.
