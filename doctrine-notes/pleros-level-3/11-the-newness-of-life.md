# The Newness of Life

We are looking at a very important teaching: the newness of life. This is a teaching on the new creation, but with particular attention to the life that the new creation has. The center is still the believer in Christ, yet the emphasis here is on the kind of life we have entered into.

One thing we know is that the reason for the church is tied to the reason we exist. We exist because of God's purpose. The church exists to help us fulfill that purpose. You are here on earth because of God's purpose, and the church exists to ensure that you fulfill that purpose.

## Why the Church Was Given

In Ephesians 4, Paul says that the Lord Jesus gave apostles, prophets, evangelists, pastors, and teachers for the perfecting of the saints. The point is simple: Jesus gave the leaders of the church so that the saints may be brought to the end he has in mind for them.

That end is explained in Ephesians 4:13. First, the saints are to come to the unity of the faith and of the knowledge of the Son of God. So there is an end point in knowledge. There is a level of enlightenment and clarity in God's word that the Lord expects every saint to have.

Second, Paul speaks of the perfect man and the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ. That is no longer only about knowledge. That is about growth. So the leaders of the church are given to bring us to an end point in knowledge and an end point in growth.

Colossians 1 helps explain this further. Paul says he labors for the sake of Christ's body, the church, to make full the word of God. Christ has accomplished an exhaustive work in salvation, but the believer does not automatically possess exhaustive knowledge of what has been accomplished. At salvation, we are restored into God's purpose and receive the nature that makes fulfilling that purpose possible, but we still need the knowledge that makes our walk possible. That is why the church must teach.

## Knowledge, Growth, and Walk

After speaking about the church's work in Ephesians 4, Paul then says that we should no longer walk as the Gentiles walk. That means the goal is not information alone. The goal is walk. What is taught in church is meant to affect practical life.

Paul describes the Gentiles as walking in the futility of their mind, with their understanding darkened, alienated from the life of God because of the ignorance in them. The repeated emphasis is on the mind. Their old walk was bound up with futility, darkness, ignorance, and blindness in the mind.

For the believer, the decisive problem can no longer be the spirit. He that is joined to the Lord is one spirit with him. We are partakers of the divine nature. So if a believer is still walking as before, the issue is the mind. The church therefore gives knowledge and growth that affect the mind so that the believer can walk as God intends.

The Lord Jesus did not die and rise merely to forgive sins. He did so to restore us into God's purpose. Redemption is not an end in itself. Redemption is the means by which God brings man back into what man was created for. So salvation is the fixing of what went wrong with God's purpose for man.

## The Core of Christian Growth Is Learning the New Man

Ephesians 4 presses further. Paul says, "You have not so learned Christ," and then explains what that learning is: that you put off the old man, be renewed in the spirit of your mind, and put on the new man, which after God is created in righteousness and true holiness.

This means the central truth of the Christian faith for growth is the truth of the new creation. Every other area of doctrine gives stability and certainty, but what especially makes for growth is knowing who you are in Christ. The focus of Christian teaching must keep returning to the reality of the new man.

That is why this subject matters. Growth into God's purpose happens as the old is put off and the new is put on. The attention of our study must therefore rest on this reality that is now new in us.

## Buried and Raised to Walk in Newness of Life

Romans 6 gives one of Paul's clearest explanations of this reality. He asks, "Shall we continue in sin that grace may abound?" and answers, "Certainly not." Grace does not merely leave us in the mess while covering it. Grace changes us out of that mess.

Paul says that as many as were baptized into Christ were baptized into his death. We were buried with him through baptism into death, so that just as Christ was raised from the dead by the glory of the Father, even so we also should walk in newness of life.

Christ has dealt with the realm of death and satisfied all that death demands. Having been raised with him, we are now in the realm of life. There is one matter still present in this age, namely mortality, because this present age has not yet closed. But apart from that, we have entered the realm of life.

Two things must be noticed. First, we are in the newness of life. Second, it is our responsibility to walk in the newness of life. Romans 7:6 says similarly that we should serve in newness of spirit and not in oldness of letter. So there is a new realm, a new condition, and a new way of serving.

Believers may know these things and still say, "It does not seem to be working." Paul himself speaks as though the Romans already know that they died with Christ and that the old man was crucified. Yet there can still seem to be a gap between what is known and what is walked in. What bridges that gap is faith.

## What Life Means in This Teaching

When Paul speaks of newness of life, the word newness contrasts with the former reality. There was an old man, a former condition, a state of death. Now there is a new condition. We are alive in Christ.

But what does life mean here? Life is a state of vitality and animation that has expression. It is not merely abstract existence. It is life as an active, expressive reality. If something is alive, there are expressions proper to that life.

There is also another way the word life can be used. Sometimes people speak of life as the set of conditions in which one exists. In that sense, life can refer not only to inner vitality but also to the surrounding order or realm in which a person functions.

Both ideas matter here. The newness of life includes internal realities and external conditions. That is why the image of an embassy is helpful. An embassy is physically located in one nation, but it carries the legal standing and conditions of another. In the same way, believers are in this world, yet belong to another order. We are an embassy of Christ on earth.

## Eternal Life as a Present Reality

In the Gospels, eternal life is often spoken of as the reality of the age to come. But in John, the same life is also brought forward as a present possession. Whoever believes has everlasting life. He has passed from death to life.

That means the life of the age to come has already begun in the believer. We have passed from the conditions of death into the conditions of life. So when we speak of eternal life, we are not speaking only of something internal and hidden. We are speaking of a present reality with both internal and external implications.

The Lord said, "I have come that they may have life, and that they may have it more abundantly." The thief comes to steal, kill, and destroy. That belongs to the reign of death and darkness. Christ came to change our condition. He came to bring life.

This is why the life of God must not be reduced only to character, as vital as character is. The life of God also includes preservation, protection, safety, and the realities that belong to God's order. We are not yet walking in immortality, because that will be consummated at the Lord's coming, but the life we now have already bears real conditions that belong to God's kingdom.

## The Realities Active in the Newness of Life

Second Peter says that we are partakers of the divine nature. That means the inherent condition of what is in us is divine. What is natural to this new life is the animation of divinity.

This life is always active. The realities of the newness of life are not dormant until we try to apply them. The life of God does not switch on and off. It is ever-present and active. Faith does not create the life. Faith aligns us with what is already present and active.

Part of this life is status. We are forgiven. We are delivered. We have authority. We are in communion with God. God has made his home with us.

Part of this life is internal animation for character. We have the character of love, righteousness, and courage.

Part of this life is internal animation for supernatural ability. We can see, hear, and know in the Spirit. We can speak and act by the Spirit.

Part of this life is external condition. There is safety, preservation, deliverance, and the healing power of God at work in our bodies. The healing power of God is not something to think about only when sickness appears. It is part of the present reality of the newness of life.

So the life of God may be described as God's power at work in us, over us, around us, and for us. The conditions of this life are not against our good. They are part of the believer's present inheritance in Christ.

## Walking in the Newness of Life by Faith

Romans 6 also tells us how to walk in this life. After laying out what is true, Paul gives the instruction: reckon yourselves to be dead indeed to sin, but alive to God in Christ Jesus our Lord.

To reckon is to take into account what God has made true. It is the language of faith. Paul also says not to present ourselves as instruments of unrighteousness, but to present ourselves to God as those who are alive from the dead. So walking in the newness of life happens by faith-stand, by reckoning, by presenting, by affirming what is true in Christ.

This means standing with the reality of the new life. I am alive to God. I am in the newness of life. The realities of life, not death, are my portion. The power of God is at work in me, around me, and over me. I am an embassy of life on the earth.

That is how the believer walks in what has already been given. The life is present, but the walk is by faith. The practical encouragement, therefore, is to be consistent in a life of faith and prayer, because prayer is one of the primary places where this faith-stand is exercised and sustained.
