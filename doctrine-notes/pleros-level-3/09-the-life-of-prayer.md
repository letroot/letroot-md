# The Life of Prayer

We are looking at the life of prayer. The substance of this teaching is that the life of purpose is inherently, essentially, a life of prayer. This is not just a fancy way to connect prayer to purpose because we want to teach on prayer. It is actually the reality of God's purpose for our lives.

Another reason we are calling it the life of prayer is to take prayer away from the idea of being a mere activity or event in the believer's life. Prayer is meant to be an ongoing reality of our life. There is a way we engage with our parents that is not merely an activity. It is part of life because our life is sourced from them, and that interaction is just part of how our life is. In the same way, when we think about God's purpose, prayer plays into it that way.

## God's Purpose Is Sonship

To understand the life of prayer, we have to establish again what God's purpose is. Ephesians 1:9 says that God has made known to us the mystery of his will. That means his will is not something we could have discovered by observation. It had to be revealed.

The text says he made known to us the mystery of his will according to his good pleasure which he purposed in himself. So there is what God likes, there is what God wants, and there is what he has determined to have. God's purpose is what he likes and wants, what he has purposed in himself.

Nothing is more important in our lives than purpose. If a person does not know why he exists, he has not really begun to live. If there is anything you should take seriously, it is your understanding of God's purpose.

Ephesians 1:5 tells us what that purpose is: he has predestined us to adoption as sons by Jesus Christ to himself, according to the good pleasure of his will. From that verse we can tell that God's good pleasure, God's will, and God's purpose is adoption as sons. The clearest way to say it is sonship.

That word can throw us off if we hear "adoption" in a modern sense. The point is not that we are not really God's children and he only took us in externally. The point is sonship. God's purpose is that we are his children and his heirs.

## The Spirit of Sonship Cries Abba Father

Romans 8:15 says we have not received the spirit of bondage again to fear, but the Spirit of adoption whereby we cry, "Abba, Father." The same word translated there is the same idea of sonship. We have received the Spirit of sonship.

The first thing we are told about that Spirit is that by him we cry, "Abba, Father." At the core of sonship is interaction with God as Father. We relate with him as Father.

Romans 8:16-17 then shows the realities of sonship. First, we are children of God. Second, if children, then heirs of God and joint-heirs with Christ. So sonship means child plus heir. In the Christian faith, if you are a child of God, you are also an heir of God.

As a child of God, you have been reproduced by God. As an heir of God, you have inheritance or assignments given to you by God. There is a spiritual inheritance and a natural inheritance, or a spiritual assignment and a natural assignment. God's purpose is that you are his child and his heir.

## Prayer Is Fellowship With the Father

What do we do as children of God? Romans 8:15 already shows it. The primary thing we do as those who have received the Spirit of sonship is that we cry, "Abba, Father." We interact with God. We fellowship with him. We engage with him.

This is what prayer is. Sometimes we think prayer is only a dedicated manner of engaging God, and certainly there is such a thing as devotion. But prayer in its simplest form is interaction, communion, fellowship, longing toward another.

The Greek word for prayer is `proseuche`, and the verb "to pray" is `proseuchomai`. The idea is directed toward another. Prayer is not something you do toward yourself. It is engagement toward another. In its mildest form, it is desire toward, longing toward, wishing toward another. It can be stronger, like asking or supplication, but ordinarily it is fellowship and interaction.

If by the Spirit of sonship we cry, "Abba, Father," then the Spirit we have received is a Spirit that leads to prayer. That means the life of purpose is a life of prayer.

If God has called us to be his children, and the reason he wants us as his children is primarily to fellowship with him, then God made us for prayer. Another way to say that, without being wrong, is that God's purpose is prayer. What that means is that God's purpose is for us to fellowship with him as children with their Father.

## Prayer Begins With the Father Reality

When the disciples asked Jesus to teach them to pray in Luke 11, he answered, "When you pray, say: Our Father in heaven, hallowed be your name." Prayer does not begin with asking for things. Prayer begins with fellowship with the Father.

The father reality is foundational to prayer. God made us so that we can interact with him as children with their Father. Even when the Lord later speaks about daily bread, forgiveness, and other requests, prayer starts with, "Our Father."

The same thing shows up in Paul's prayers. In Ephesians 3:14 he says, "For this reason I bow my knees to the Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, of whom the whole family in heaven and earth is named." That is family language. He is not just filling space with the word "Father." He is praying with the consciousness that he is speaking to his Father.

John 16:26-27 also shows this. Jesus said, "I do not say to you that I shall pray the Father for you; for the Father himself loves you." In Christ, we have entered a relationship where the Father's love is personalized to us as his children. The Father himself loves us, and we now directly relate with him.

To enter into God's purpose is to enter into a life of prayer. Once you become a child of God, you have entered into fellowship with the Father. You must be father-minded.

## Prayer Is Not Merely an Event

Prayer is not an event. Prayer is not merely an activity. Prayer is part of the life of God's purpose that we have. If we exist because God wants us in his family, then prayer is part of what we were born to do.

That does not mean all prayer is vigorous and intense. If someone hears that prayer is what we are born to do and immediately thinks only of strenuous public prayer, he is looking at prayer with the wrong lens. Prayer in its ordinary form is fellowship with the Father.

There is a place for intensity and persistence in prayer, and we will get there. But prayer first is fellowship. In fact, it is by locking into fellowship with the Father that one arrives at the proper intensity of prayer; otherwise, it becomes performative.

If a believer has no desire to fellowship with God, something is wrong and needs to be checked. The Spirit of sonship cries, "Abba, Father." That longing for fellowship is primary.

## Prayer Includes Different Engagements

Prayer is primarily fellowship with the Father, but it unfolds into other engagements. That is why scripture can speak about "all prayer and supplication" in Ephesians 6:18.

In 1 Timothy 2:1, Paul mentions supplications, prayers, intercessions, and giving of thanks. These are all engagements within prayer. In Colossians 1, Paul says, "We give thanks to God and the Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, praying always for you," and then shortly after he says, "We do not cease to pray for you and to ask." So in the same prayer there is thanksgiving and there is asking.

Prayer remains fellowship with the Father, yet within that fellowship there are different movements: thanksgiving, supplication, intercession, asking.

## Prayer and Walking in God's Nature

As children of God, we are not only called to fellowship with God. We are called to walk in his nature and character. We have the divine nature. We are to walk in love, righteousness, and the new life we have in Christ. That also requires prayer.

Romans 12:1-2 shows this. Paul says to present your bodies a living sacrifice, holy and acceptable to God, which is your reasonable service. He is talking about worship, and in this context we can see prayer there as well. In prayer and worship, we present ourselves to God.

The word "present" carries the idea of standing beside yourself. One of the things we do in prayer is stand beside who we are in Christ. We say, "I am a new creation. I am dead to sin. I am alive to God." We acknowledge and affirm who we are in Christ.

Then verse 2 gives the effect: we are transformed by the renewing of our mind. So prayer is not only fellowship. Prayer is also a place where we stand beside who we are, affirm who we are, and are transformed to walk in our nature and character.

Prayer is a place of fellowship. Prayer is a place of transformation and edification.

## Prayer Builds and Edifies the Inner Man

Jude 20 says, "Building up yourselves on your most holy faith, praying in the Holy Ghost." Prayer brings about personal transformation and edification.

In 1 Corinthians 14, Paul says that the one who speaks in a tongue speaks not to men but to God, and in the Spirit he speaks mysteries. He also says that the one who speaks in a tongue edifies himself. So when a person prays in tongues, he is interacting with God and at the same time being personally edified.

This edification is not merely mental information. It affects the inner man, the conscience, the consciousness. In that sense, prayer strengthens a person inwardly. The more you pray concerning who you are in Christ, the more your inner man is emboldened toward walking in that reality.

So the life of prayer is the life of purpose. Purpose is for us to fellowship with God, and purpose is for us to walk in his nature and character. Therefore the life of prayer edifies us to walk in our nature.

Everything in God's purpose is done by prayer. Some things are directly done in prayer, like fellowship with the Father. Some things are caused by prayer, like the transformation and edification that empower our walk.

## Prayer and Our Assignment as Heirs

We are not only children of God. We are also heirs of God. As heirs, we have assignments. We have a natural assignment, what we do with our hands, and we have a spiritual assignment, our part in God's mission.

It is impossible to carry out natural and spiritual assignments faithfully and effectively without prayer. A man who is not praying cannot carry out his natural assignment out of a godly heart. He will be overwhelmed by the cares of this world, greed, selfishness, personal glory, and ambition.

There is a difference between doing your natural assignment as a steward of God and doing it for applause, self-glory, or selfish ambition. Worldly and godly people can both be highly driven, but what fuels the drive is different. Without prayer, the wrong things will fuel your drive.

That is why Ephesians 6 says servants should obey their masters according to the flesh with sincerity of heart, as to Christ, not with eye-service as men-pleasers, but as servants of Christ, doing the will of God from the heart. The point is that even natural work is to be done as unto the Lord, and that can only happen out of spiritual growth.

Colossians 3 says the same thing: whatever you do, do it heartily, as to the Lord and not unto men. That means natural assignment must be carried out from the state of a strengthened inner life. If the soul is disordered, the work will be carried out wrongly.

There is no way to carry out the assignments and inheritance God has given us, both natural and spiritual, without growth and strength. That strength happens in prayer.

## Prayer Aligns Us With What God Is Working In Us

Philippians 2:12-13 says, "Work out your own salvation with fear and trembling, for it is God who works in you both to will and to do of his good pleasure." There is something in us to work out because God is at work in us.

God is at work in us both to will and to do his good pleasure. That includes our life as heirs of God. So when a believer says, "I do not feel like doing what God has called me to do," that does not mean the desire is absent in the deepest sense. By the new nature, God is already at work in him. But there can be weariness or misalignment in the state of his mind and inner man, and that has to be addressed in prayer.

By standing in prayer, by standing on God's word, we are transformed into alignment with what God is already working in us. The same thing is true for ministry and spiritual assignment. If someone says, "Sometimes I just feel weak about ministry," that weakness must be addressed in the place of prayer.

In prayer, you stand on God's word and say, "I want to do this. I am born of God. God is at work in me both to will and to do of his good pleasure."
