# Reality of Redemption

This morning we are starting a very important teaching: the reality of redemption.

There are many descriptions people give to redemption. Another way people term those descriptions is what is called theories of atonement. So when we say the reality of redemption, we are asking: what does redemption actually entail? What is the actual event pertaining to redemption? What did redemption actually do?

That one reality may then be described in many ways because of the audience you are speaking to. If you are speaking to an audience that is more legally inclined, you may speak about redemption from the point of justification. There are places where the idea of guilt and the idea of someone guilty before a judge being exonerated is used to describe redemption. But that description of redemption is definitely not the reality of redemption itself.

## Descriptions of Redemption and Their Limits

Descriptions of redemption usually hit a roadblock. They always have a limitation. It is like using a parable to explain something. A parable can help you explain, but there are limits to what that parable can do.

Even when Scripture uses a metaphor, the fact that the communicators of the message chose certain metaphors to explain the event of redemption does not mean they are saying that metaphor is itself the reality of redemption. These descriptions of redemption are many in God's word. If you pay attention to the entire teaching of God's word on redemption, you will understand why a particular description is being used. But that description is not the reality of redemption.

There is the reality of redemption, and there are descriptions of redemption. We need to know what the reality of redemption itself is, and then know what the descriptions are, how they work, and the limits of those descriptions.

We looked at redemption a bit in earlier levels, but here in level three we need to establish the core issue in redemption: what is the reality of redemption? Before you go on to a more exhaustive explanation, you must know the core reality.

## Redemption as the Central Truth of the Christian Faith

Redemption is the central truth of the Christian faith. God's purpose is the primary truth of the Christian faith, but redemption is the central truth. Redemption distinguishes the Christian faith from every other faith. When we get to the issue of salvation or redemption accomplished in Christ Jesus, we are at the center of the Christian message.

In Ephesians 1:7 it says, "In him we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of sins, according to the riches of his grace." In Ephesians 1 Paul is giving a summary of his doctrine, and at the center of that summary is the reality of redemption.

From verse 3 to verse 6 he shows the reality of God's purpose. Our existence begins with God, who had a particular purpose, and for that reason he did a work to restore us back into that purpose. By the time he gets to verse 7, he settles on that particular thing God did by Jesus Christ and terms it redemption.

How did we come back to God's purpose? What did God do by Christ to bring us back into sonship with him? In him we have redemption. Redemption is what he did.

The Greek word there is `apolytrosis`, used ten times in the New Testament. Ordinarily, `lutrosis` means to set free. Sometimes that freedom is by a tool or a ransom price. But this word itself has become the basis of some theories of redemption, especially the ransom theory, and if it is understood wrongly it can corrupt the reality of redemption.

The moment someone says freedom comes by a ransom price, the next question is: who is the price paid to? Some have said it is paid to the devil. Others have said it is paid to God. But neither of those may be the right way to think about the matter. This ransom issue can also lead to another theory, `Christus Victor`, the victory theory.

There are many descriptions: ransom theory, `Christus Victor`, penal substitution, satisfaction theory, healing or therapeutic approaches, even theosis language. Some of them can say something useful, but all of them are descriptions. The real issue is to identify the reality. What exactly is this redemption that was done?

When you say redemption, you have essentially said Christianity, because you are saying there was an initial plan and purpose, something went wrong, and redemption is the fixing of what went wrong that will be consummated. So redemption encapsulates the whole Christian faith.

This is important because if you do not have a clear understanding of what redemption entails, you can be unsettled and tossed to and fro. There are people who believe in the Lord Jesus but still face crises in their hearts because the descriptions they have heard pose serious challenges to them.

For example, if redemption is painted as though God simply became angry, then decided instead of judging us he would kill his Son so his mind could calm down, that description can create a serious problem if you do not know the reality of redemption. There are texts that use certain descriptions to get a point across, but if you are unaware of the reality of redemption, those descriptions may trouble you.

## Redemption Requires Understanding the Origin of Sin

Ephesians 1:7 says, "In him we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of sins." If redemption is freedom, then it is freedom from something. We need to know what we are being made free from and how. If we are going to understand the reality of redemption, we have to look at the origin of sin itself.

Romans 5:12 says, "Therefore, just as through one man sin entered the world, and death through sin, and thus death spread to all men..." Paul tells us how sin entered the world: through one man. So sin's origin is by one man, and from this sin we need redemption.

If we are going to understand redemption, we must understand what that sin was. When someone says sin, `hamartia`, the idea is to miss the mark. Sin implies that there is a specific target, goal, or thing that is right. There is one truth, but there are many lies. In that sense, sin does not have to be one specific lie; sin can be anything except the one point that is true. To miss the mark means there was a mark to miss.

So if there is going to be any proper understanding of sin, there must first be an understanding of the mark, the target, the goal, so that we can appreciate what it means to miss it.

## The Two Trees and What God Offered Man

We are told sin entered the world by one man, but what exactly did he miss? What was offered to him?

Genesis 2 tells us that the Lord God formed man and placed him in the garden. We are told of every tree of the garden that is pleasant to the sight and good for food. But besides the natural trees, we also see the tree of life and the tree of the knowledge of good and evil.

There is no such physical thing as life that makes for a literal tree, and there is no such physical tree that produces knowledge. So when we see the tree of life and the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, we know the account has moved into figurative language. The tree of knowledge of good and evil leads to death, so it could also be called the tree of death. And if one tree brings death and the other brings life, then both trees are about knowledge: one knowledge brings life and the other brings death.

Genesis 2:16-17 says, "Of every tree of the garden you may freely eat; but of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil you shall not eat, for in the day that you eat of it you shall surely die." That means man could partake of the natural trees and also of the tree of life, but not of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil.

So what exactly is the tree of life?

## The Tree of Life as Wisdom and Truth

Proverbs 3:13-18 speaks about wisdom and then says, "She is a tree of life to those who take hold of her." That means wisdom is the tree of life. Proverbs 23:23 says, "Buy the truth, and do not sell wisdom and instruction and understanding." When someone has wisdom, what did the person receive? Truth. So wisdom is rooted in truth.

What, then, did God sow on man's heart? Truth. The truth of existence. That truth planted on man's heart is wisdom, the tree of life.

But man did not receive of that. He considered another option better. The temptation was the suggestion that there was something better than sonship with God. But the only thing supposedly better than being a son of God would be being God, and that is foolishness. The moment you find out you are existing and did not bring yourself into existence, it already shows you cannot be God.

The enemy's pattern is always to tell you there is something better, even when you already have the best in God's purpose. That is how sin works.

## Sin as the Rejection of Life

If sin is to miss the mark, then we need to know what the mark was. What was the offer? Life. How was it offered? By showing man the truth. By receiving that truth, he would partake of life.

So what did God offer? Life.

What did man do? He rejected life and partook of another knowledge.

What was the effect of what man did? Death.

That death plays out in phases. The day man ate of it, he died spiritually, which is why Scripture can say we were dead in sins and trespasses. Then there is physical death. And beyond those, Scripture speaks of the second death. Spiritual death and physical death are all classified under the category of the first death, and there is still the second death.

So when we say what man did is sin and what sin caused is death, we are saying the natural consequence of sin is death, not merely a punishment proclaimed for sin. That is very important to the theory of redemption.

## Death as the Natural Consequence of Sin

The death issue that goes on to the second death, what people call hell, damnation, or eternal condemnation, was not simply a judgment arbitrarily passed against man. It was the natural consequence of what man did.

That consequence can be described as judgment, and such language may work for some people as a description. But the real issue is consequence. If someone behaves in a destructive way and ruin follows, people may describe it as though God gave the person judgment, but in reality it is the natural consequence of what the person did.

If you do not understand the exact events leading up to redemption, you will misinterpret the work of redemption and think the descriptions are the reality. If you think the problem is simply that God passed a judgment against us, then you already have a problem. The consequence can be described as the wrath of God, because it is displeasing to God and stands as an enemy to God's purpose, but that is not the actual structure of the matter.

Revelation 20 speaks of death and Hades being cast into the lake of fire, and says, "This is the second death." That means the second death is the final destination of sin. So we have established three things:

God offered life.

Man rejected God's life and purpose. That is sin.

The effect of what man did is death, moving toward the second death.

## The Only Possible Resolution

If that is the problem, then what is the resolution?

First, if the entire cosmos is affected by sin and death, then everything except God is involved in the problem. Man, angels, matter, the whole cosmos is death-affected, death-bound, under the bondage of sin, death, darkness, and evil. That means the only possible resolution must come from the one who is not part of the fallen cosmos: God.

Second, life must enter into death, and light must enter into darkness.

Third, if legitimacy and justice matter, then sin and death must be allowed to take their full course. The issue is not someone paying a price to God or to the devil. The issue is that the consequence of sin and death must fully play out, while the cosmos, and particularly humanity, is still saved.

So the possible resolution comes down to this: only God can resolve it, life and light must enter the darkness, and the consequence of sin and death must be legitimately exhausted.

## The Necessity of the Incarnation

What does that mean? It means we need an incarnation. God has to enter into the cosmos, and enter it as humanity, which is the highest state in the cosmos and also the very sphere in which the problem is centered. He must enter into humanity, allow sin and death to take their course legitimately, and in doing so bring back life and light.

That is the exact narrative of redemption. It is the philosophically necessary resolution of the contradiction in the world of sin. It is also prophetically promised, prophetically patterned, and eventually historically fulfilled.

The problem is not said to be fully resolved merely because someone dies and returns to the same mortal condition. The resolution requires rising above death, rising as immortal. When that happens in Christ, then we know the resolution has truly happened and will be consummated.

So the full movement is this: what God offered, what man did, the effect of what man did, the possible resolution, the promised resolution, the accomplished resolution, and its implications.

## What Christ Actually Did in Redemption

What would be necessary to happen? Life must enter into a world of death. But before life can be legitimately restored to that world, sin and death must be allowed to take their course in a salvific manner.

So Christ comes into the world, dies for us, goes to the place of the dead, and undergoes that second-death reality on our behalf. The second death that consumes the cosmos is exhausted on him. There is a legitimate allowing of sin and death to take their course.

That means someone in the cosmos has fully dealt with sin and death. If anything in the cosmos still experiences it, it is because there is a rejection of what that person has done.

You can describe this with a `Christus Victor` drama and say he fought death and fought Satan and won, but the real issue is that he allowed sin and death to take their course on him, and by that legitimately brought life and light to the world, particularly to humanity.

Acts 2:24 says God raised him from the dead, having loosed the pains of death. He exhausted the pains of death. Other texts say he suffered death for every man. That is not the language of fighting so much as the language of undergoing the full course of death.

So the reality of what he did is that he underwent the course of sin and death so that he could legitimately bring back life to us. He exhausted the demands of death in his sufferings.

The exact work he did was a legitimate exhaustion of sin and death, and a legitimate restoration of life, light, and righteousness.

## Freedom Rather Than Negotiation

This is why other descriptions can make some sense. You can see healing language there. You can see theosis language there. You can see why satisfaction or ransom language may be used. You can even see why substitution language may be used. But the reality is simpler than those descriptions sometimes make it seem.

What Christ did was not a negotiation with a person. It was not a payment to God or to Satan. It was the accomplishing of freedom from sin, death, and its consequence by legitimately bearing and exhausting that consequence.

If we call redemption anything, we can call it freedom. `Apolytrosis` reflects complete freedom. It does not necessarily require the idea of a ransom paid to someone else. That is often an imposition placed into the word. The central idea is complete freedom.

We are completely free from sin and death by what Christ has done. It was a legitimate exhaustion of sin and death by bearing it. If you want a more precise descriptive label, you could call it a consequence-bearing theory. What did Jesus do? He bore the consequence of what man did. Not something imposed arbitrarily by God, but the consequence of what humanity brought into the cosmos.

So if you want to keep it simple, call it a freedom theory. If you want to explain it more fully, call it consequence-bearing freedom.

## The Implications of Redemption

The first implication of redemption is freedom from death. Sin, death, and darkness have lost their right because in the person of Jesus the cosmos has already had that consequence exhausted.

The second implication of redemption is the individual restoration of humanity to life. We now have the life of God in us, the light of God in us, the nature of God in us.

The third implication of redemption is the eventual restoration of the cosmos to life: the new heavens and the new earth, the glorified body, the full restoration of all things.

So knowing the actual reality of redemption helps us make sense of the other descriptions, angles, and theories. Redemption is freedom from sin, death, and its consequence. It is not a negotiation with a person, and not a payment to God or Satan. It is the legitimate accomplishment of freedom from sin, death, and darkness.

## Responding to Redemption by Faith

What are we supposed to do about redemption? We are supposed to respond to redemption with faith and with faith-stand. When I realize what Christ has done, I receive it by faith for my salvation, and I walk in it by faith for the fulfillment of God's purpose.

Many songs use several descriptions of redemption, and that is okay. We just have to understand what they are doing. Some present salvation judicially, some relationally, but at its core the issue is more ontological. The Christian faith addresses an ontological and cosmic problem, not by a cosmic battle in the simplistic sense, but by a consequence-bearing work that resolves the problem of the cosmos.

That brings us to the end of the teaching. Redemption bears the consequence of sin, death, and darkness, and therefore we have complete freedom.
