# Reality of Redemption

This morning we are starting a very important teaching: the reality of redemption. The reality of redemption.

The central point for which we have titled this "the reality of redemption" is that there are many descriptions people give to redemption. Another way people term the descriptions given to redemption is what is called theories of atonement. So there are many theories of atonement, or I will call them descriptions that people give to redemption.

When we say the reality of redemption, we are saying: what does redemption actually entail? What is the actual event pertaining to redemption? What did redemption actually do?

That may now go on to be described in many ways because of the audience you are speaking to. If you are speaking to an audience that is more legally inclined, you want to speak about redemption from the point of justification. That can come across as a law term. Now there is justification as making something right, which we will see. But you will see that there were places where the idea of guilt, and the idea of someone guilty before a judge that is being exonerated, is used to describe redemption. But this description of redemption is definitely not the reality of redemption.

If someone says that the problem the Lord Jesus was solving in the work of redemption is that when we stand before God we are guilty, and then he did not want us to be guilty before his court, so he wanted to justify us and now paid the price for us, that is still not justice, because we are not the ones that paid the price. This is the challenge with descriptions of redemption. Descriptions of redemption usually hit a roadblock. They always have a limitation.

## Descriptions and Their Limits

It is like using a parable. You can use a parable to explain something, but there are limits to what that parable can do.

Let me give an example. There is a parable the Lord Jesus told about prayer. He said that if a friend has another friend that comes to visit him, and then he has a friend that is a neighbor close to him, because he does not have anything in his house, he goes to that friend to knock on his door at three in the morning, middle of the night, for the friend to give him something to give his other friend. Then he says because of the fact that the friend has his children sleeping there, and he does not want him to disturb him, he is going to rise up because of his insistence, persistence, or importunity, and give him those things.

Now let me be honest with you. There are some people that will not rise up and give you anything. Even if their children are there, they will call the cops on you and they might arrest you. So imagine you now start going into the details in the parable. You start stretching it so far. That is how people do with the descriptions of redemption.

Even when Scripture uses such metaphor, the fact that the communicators of the message chose certain metaphors, as they have the right to explain the event of redemption, which you have the entire book to understand, does not mean they are saying that metaphor is itself the reality of redemption. These descriptions of redemption are a lot in God's word. You will just see them. In fact, the Lord himself did it sometimes with things like forgiveness. He will say that he who is forgiven much loved much, and it just looks like God just let what you did go. But if you pay attention to the entire teaching of God's word on redemption, you will understand why that description is being used. But know that description is not the reality of redemption.

There is the reality of redemption. When you say reality, that is how the thing actually is. Someone may be pointing to a chair and trying to tell you how a chair is, and he sees a dog and says, "That dog has four legs. That thing has four legs." He is using a description for a particular reality. We need to know what the reality of redemption itself is, and know what the descriptions are, the descriptions that can work, as well as the limits of those descriptions.

That is what we are trying to examine here in the reality of redemption. We have looked at redemption a bit in level two. Probably under gospel issues it was already inferred. But in level three we need to establish the core issue in redemption. What is the reality of redemption? Before you go to level five and see an exhaustive explanation of redemption, what is the reality of redemption? That is what we are going to be looking at in this study.

## Redemption as the Central Truth of the Christian Faith

The first point I want to make, to establish the importance of this study, is that undoubtedly redemption is the central truth of the Christian faith. I know for us now we talk a lot about God's purpose being the primary truth, and that is still correct. But I am telling you that the central truth of the Christian faith, that already presupposes God's purpose and even projects the restoration, the full restoration of all that God wants, is redemption. It is the central truth of the Christian faith.

God's purpose is the primary truth of the Christian faith, but redemption is the central truth. At the end of the day, when I am making the real distinction, it is a redemption distinction. It is really redemption that distinguishes the Christian faith from every other faith. When we get to the issue of salvation or redemption accomplished in Christ Jesus, we are at the center of the Christian message.

You see in Ephesians 1:7, "In him we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of sins, according to the riches of his grace." In this particular text there are a couple of things to mention. You remember that Ephesians 1 from verse 3 to verse 14 is Paul giving a summary of his doctrine, according to what he told us in Ephesians 3:3-4. Paul is giving a summary of his doctrine, and at the center of the summary of his doctrine is the reality of redemption.

In Ephesians 1:3-6 he was showing us the reality of God's purpose and had already spoken about redemption there as well. He said he brought us to adoption as sons by Jesus Christ to himself according to the good pleasure of his will. The essence of Ephesians 1:3-6 is to show us this fact: our existence begins with God, who had this particular purpose. Our existence is about God having a particular purpose, and for that reason he did a work to restore us back into that purpose.

So by the time he gets to verse 7, he now settles and focuses on that particular thing that God did by Jesus Christ, and he terms it here redemption. In him we have redemption. So how did we come back to God's purpose, where he said he has predestined us to adoption as sons, or predestined us to sonship by Jesus Christ to himself? How? What did he do by Christ to bring us back into sonship with him? He says, in him we have redemption. Redemption is what he did.

The word redemption there is the Greek word `apolutrosis`, used ten times in the New Testament. Ordinarily the word `lutrosis` means to set free, to loose. Sometimes that freedom is usually by a tool or a ransom price. This word itself is the basis of some theories of redemption. In fact, one of the primary and most popular ones, and I think it can corrupt what should be the reality of redemption if we understand it wrongly.

Whenever you see that word `lutrosis`, from whence we have `lutron`, which is like a tool or a price used to set someone free, otherwise called a ransom, that is the basis of the ransom theory of redemption. In fact, the Lord Jesus himself said the Son of Man is given as a ransom. But if someone is in a condition and the person needs freedom, particularly if there is a superimposed bondage, something or someone needs to set the person free, and it is not always the case that what sets the person free is a ransom price in the way we understand ransom price today.

The moment you say the way someone gets free, `lutron`, is a ransom price, the next question, and it is the next logical question indeed, is: who is the price paid to? In those ransom theories, which I will just call descriptions of redemption, there are people who have gone as far as saying it is paid to the devil. Others have gone ahead to say it is paid to God. I think it is better to say it is paid to the devil, that is the bad guy holding people, than to say it is paid to God, that it is God standing in the way of what he says he wants to do. But none of those may be the right way to think about the matter.

It is also this ransom issue that sometimes leads to another theory of redemption, `Christus Victor`, the victory theory. There is a lot to say in that theory as well. But as I am telling you, these are theories of redemption, or we can call them redemption descriptions. So the real issue is to identify the reality. What is this redemption exactly that was done?

Why is it the central truth of the Christian faith? Because it presupposes the very beginning, inception of the faith, as well as its end. So it is all-encompassing. It encapsulates every truth in the Christian faith. The moment you say redemption, you have essentially said Christianity, because you are saying there was an initial plan and purpose, something has gone wrong, and redemption is fixing what has gone wrong, which will be consummated. So in saying redemption you have described the entire Christian faith.

Doing the work to know the reality of redemption is essential. For the same reason Ephesians 4 says God does not want us to be tossed to and fro, there will usually be an unsettled state you would be in when you do not have a very clear understanding of what redemption entails. There are people indeed that believe in the Lord Jesus, but from time to time they face some doubt and crisis in their heart about what they believe. Sometimes they just push it aside and ignore it in their mind to save themselves from the anxiety and distress. But that is not the best for someone's walk. You need to be settled and stable so that you can carry on with God's purpose.

There will be times where maybe the assumption of redemption is like this: God made us, we committed a sin, God was supposed to judge us and deal with us, but he now said instead of judging us and dealing with us, he is going to send his Son to die, and when he sees the blood his mind will now calm down. But why is God so hard? The moment you paint this description of redemption, and there will be some texts of Scripture that use that description for the sake of an audience, just to get across the point of salvation and the point that you need to be saved, those texts are functioning with a paradigm when they use that description. That paradigm is the sovereignty of God. He is responsible for everything, and so in that light they bring in that manner of description. But if you are unaware of the reality of redemption, that description may pose a serious challenge to you.

So redemption is central to the faith, but there are different descriptions, parables, and angles from which this truth can be presented or explained. There is the ransom theory. There is the `Christus Victor` theory. There is one that is very popular, in fact for some people it is the primary one, the penal substitution theory. Jesus became our substitute. There are people that will say you must believe in this substitutionary atonement or sacrifice of Christ. I used to like it as well, because some of my heroes were strong on it. Particularly, if I must confess, W. K. was really strong on substitutionary sacrifice, but also on `Christus Victor`, because he had quite some `Christus Victor` approach to redemption.

For most people, there is almost nobody you will find that has just one theory. There is usually this combination that happens, because there is also the reconciliation theory. That is why I am saying we need to look at the reality. In looking at the reality of redemption, what we really need to look at are the events. Look at the events and what the events actually did. There are all sorts of theories. Some of them are flat-out unworthy, or should be secondary and an addendum. I have said we have the ransom theory, the `Christus Victor` theory, the penal substitution theory, the satisfaction theory. You know that one: God's wrath is satisfied. That one is even seen in the song "In Christ Alone". People like that part, "the wrath of God was satisfied." And they are right, because that wrath of God is a description. When you understand the actual reality of redemption, you can still know what you mean by the wrath of God.

There is another one I kind of like for some reason, but I also think it is fraught with some challenges: the healing therapeutic, also used together with the theosis theory. Theosis is just saying that we are partaking of divinity. So that sin is like a disease or a sickness and then God is healing us. That is a description, and it actually is one that makes sense. But if you are using healing as an ongoing work, not as a one-off thing, it becomes challenging. Maybe you are talking about spiritual growth. It is very common particularly amongst the Eastern church or the Eastern Orthodox church.

Having made that point, that here are different descriptions, parables, and angles of explaining redemption, and we are seeking to know what the exact reality of redemption is, we want to now look at it.

## Redemption Means Freedom From Something

Ephesians 1:7 says, "In him we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of sins." We know that redemption, because the moment you are saying redemption or freedom, is freedom from something. We need to know what we are being made free from, and how we are being made free. Is it by paying a price? What exactly needs to be done for that redemption? In the first place, what happened, and what needs to be done?

So if at all we are going to understand the reality of redemption, we have to go and take a look at the origin of sin itself.

Look at Romans 5:12. Paul says, "Therefore, just as through one man sin entered the world, and death through sin, and thus death spread to all men..." He tells us how sin entered the world. He said it is through one man. Sin entered the world, and death through sin, and thus death spread to all men. "For that all have sinned" is debatable. There is a very good argument that instead of "for that all have sinned" or "all sinned," it can be "upon which all sinned." There is a good argument to say that would be a very good way to think about it. But the main point is that sin came in by one man, and he tells us that one man in verse 14. According to the biblical account, that man is Adam.

So through one man sin entered the world, and death through sin, and death spread upon all men. We can see sin's origin is by one man. And now it is from this sin that we need redemption. So if we are going to understand this redemption and what exactly the reality is, we must understand what that sin was.

When someone says the word sin, `hamartia`, or even the Hebrew word, which I forgot now, I think it starts with something like `chaka` or `chata`, when you say that word sin it means to miss the mark. What sin implies is that there is a specific target, or a specific goal, or a specific thing that is right.

Let me give you an example. I like using the example of truth. On any subject matter there is one truth, but there are many lies. So sin does not have to be one specific lie. Sin can be anything except the one point that is true. If someone should ask, "What is the color of the suit I am wearing?" there is only one right answer to the color of the suit I am wearing. But there are many other colors that can be mentioned, and they will all be lies. That is the way sin operates. Sin is to miss the mark.

That means if there is going to be any proper understanding of sin, there must first be the understanding of the mark, the target, the goal, so that we can now appreciate that if you miss that goal, then you will be lacking or deprived of what that goal is. Sin is to miss the mark.

We are told that it is via this one man that sin entered the world. What exactly did he miss? What was offered? We already have a sense of it from the text when it says, "and death through sin." But we need to look at it from the exact narrative told at the beginning.

## The Two Trees and the Offer Given to Man

Genesis 2:7 says, "And the Lord God formed man of the dust of the ground, and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life; and man became a living being." Then, "the Lord God planted a garden eastward in Eden, and there he put the man whom he had formed." Out of the ground the Lord God made every tree grow that is pleasant to the sight and good for food. So all natural trees were there.

But aside from the natural trees we see two other trees: the tree of life was also in the midst of the garden, and the tree of the knowledge of good and evil. There is no such physical thing as the tree of life. There is no such matter made up called life that can make for a tree. There is no such tree that produces knowledge. Knowledge is a thing of the mind. So the moment we are seeing tree of life and tree of knowledge of good and evil, we know that this account is not literal at that point. There is the reference to natural trees, but now it is going figurative.

The tree of knowledge of good and evil brings death. That means that tree could as well have been called the tree of death. And it will imply that the tree of life itself must be about knowledge. But the result of it is that it brings life. So the two trees are about knowledge. One brings life and the other one brings death.

Genesis 2:16-17 says, "Of every tree of the garden you may freely eat; but of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil you shall not eat, for in the day that you eat of it you shall surely die." What are the trees in the garden? Natural trees. Then there are two figurative trees, non-natural trees, spiritual trees if we can say it that way. And he is saying you can eat all the trees in the garden. That means you can eat all the natural trees and you can also eat the tree of life. But of the tree of knowledge of good and evil he says, the day you eat it, you will surely die.

These two trees are not natural. What exactly is this tree of life? Remember what we are looking at. I know we have gotten into the weeds now because we are looking at the reality of redemption aside from descriptions of redemption. We want to see the reality of redemption, and we said to see the reality of redemption we must look at the events, so that the events will tell us themselves what precisely and exactly happened. That will help us define the reality of redemption and the exact theory of redemption that encapsulates what redemption is all about.

## The Tree of Life as Wisdom and Truth

What is the tree of life exactly? Proverbs 3:13-18. "Happy is the man who finds wisdom, and the man who gains understanding." He begins to talk about wisdom and understanding from that verse all the way to verse 18. He says her proceeds are better than the profits of silver and her gain than fine gold. He keeps talking about wisdom even up to verse 17: "Her ways are ways of pleasantness, and all her paths are peace." Then verse 18 says, "She is a tree of life to those who take hold of her." That means they will partake of the life thereof. Wisdom is a tree of life to those who take hold of her.

If you reject wisdom, which is partaking of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, then you are partaking of death. So what is the tree of life? The knowledge of God's truth planted on man's heart.

How do we know? Because wisdom is rooted in truth. Proverbs 23:23 says, "Buy the truth, and do not sell wisdom and instruction and understanding." When someone has wisdom, what did the person get in order to have wisdom? You got truth. When you buy truth, what you now have is wisdom, instruction, and understanding.

So what did God sow on man's heart? Truth. The truth of existence. So the truth was planted on his heart. That is wisdom. The tree of life.

What did he do? Did he receive of that? No. Because he was considering other options that he considered better. You know the only thing better than being a son of God? Being God. But that is dumb, because the moment you find out that you are existing and you did not bring yourself here, it already shows that you can never be God. You are not self-existent.

Praise God. Do not be fooled about something better. It is like people having God's purpose and looking for something more. No way. It is the same thing the enemy always does. You can have the best, but he will be telling you there is something better. The Lord Jesus already had the best, for goodness' sake, and Satan was showing him all the glories of the world. There is something better? And you will be looking for in the world what God has already given to you in his purpose. Be careful.

You may say, "Then I will become successful." Is it not in his purpose already that you would prosper, flourish, and succeed in your natural assignment and spiritual assignment? What are you looking for? You now have less than the best, looking for something better than the best. It is the way of man's reasoning to seek the best. But it now becomes a problem when you have the best and you are still seeking something better than that. You are about to enter into sin.

## What Man Did and What Sin Produced

So when we say what happened when man sinned, if sin is to miss the mark, we need to know what the mark, the target, the offer was. What was the offer? Life. How was it offered? By showing man the truth. By receiving that truth, he partakes of life.

So what did God offer? Life.

What did man do? He rejected life, therefore partook of death.

What was the effect of what man did, rejecting life and partaking of another knowledge? Death.

We are trying to see the reality, the exact theory of redemption. What did God offer? Life. What did man do? He rejected it, partook of another knowledge. And therefore what is the effect of that? Death.

That death effect we can see across Scripture. We might not have the time to get into every text. It is in phases. The day you eat of it you shall surely die. Man died spiritually, and that is reckoned scripturally: you were dead in sins and trespasses. Yet it took him some years and then there was physical death. And yet the Bible speaks about second death, because that spiritual death and the physical death are all classified as first death in Scripture. Everything happening in this world, the corruption of matter, the mortality of our body, are all classified under the category of first death. And there is still the second death, which the first death is going to die in.

So when we say what man did is sin and what sin caused is death, we are saying that the natural consequence of sin is death, not the punishment proclaimed for sin. This is so important to the theory of redemption.

## Death as Consequence, Not Arbitrary Punishment

This death issue that goes to the second death, what we call hell, damnation, eternal condemnation, was not a judgment passed against man. It was the natural consequence of what he did. You can use judgment language as a description, and that description will work for some people to make it understandable to them. Sometimes even people do that today. If someone is drinking heavily and destroying his body, some people might say, "God will just give you now." But what is God's own there? They are speaking from a particular paradigm, a sovereignty paradigm, as though God is the one passing a judgment. But it is actually a natural consequence.

If you do not understand the exact events that lead up to the work of redemption the Lord Jesus did, then you are going to misinterpret that event, or you will think the descriptions that you can legitimately use for the sake of understanding happen to be the reality of that redemption. If you think the problem we have is that God passed a judgment against us, you already have a problem.

That consequence can be described as the wrath of God, because indeed it is a displeasure to God. It is an enemy of God. But that does not mean it is the actual way things are. The real issue is the consequence of what man did.

Revelation 20:13-14 says, "The sea gave up the dead who were in it, and Death and Hades delivered up the dead who were in them... Then Death and Hades were cast into the lake of fire. This is the second death." Can you imagine? Death was cast into death. He is showing us again that this is a description. He is saying that the eventual consequence of everything that is playing out is that even this first death will become extinguished in the second death. The second death is the final destination of sin.

So if I understand what God offered, which was life, and I understand what man did, which is to reject that by accepting another knowledge, and I understand the effect or consequence of what man did as death that plays out in phases unto the second death, then now we can start to discuss possible resolution.

Because the problem is not with God. It is not a case of saying, "Why is he not just merciful and he can wave his hand against it? How is he going to decide to give his own Son and then make him die for other people that he wants to be his sons? What kind of reasoning is that?" It is not that. The problem is a consequence that is legitimate because he has so allowed and ordered reality, and so it can only be resolved legitimately as well, if justice is to be done.

You know what I find funny? The same people who usually say, "All this one you are saying, God had to do a redemption in Christ, can't he just by his own mercy wave it away?" are the same people that will say they do not believe it is just for someone else to die for our sins, that they would prefer that they pay for their sins, that God cannot do something unjust. But they still expect that God would unjustly wave away a legitimate setup that he himself made. So they are not really concerned about justice. They are concerned about defending a position.

If you do not want God to be unjust by telling someone else to die, which of course I do not think that is what he did, that is not the theory of redemption I hold to, then if you reject that one on the ground of justice, you cannot then say all we need is his mercy, even if something has gone wrong, and he should just show mercy and no longer let justice play out. The legitimate consequence of sin is playing out, and God is allowing it to take its course out of justice.

If we say, "I understand the fact that sin has happened and death is supposed to take its course, but can't God just say, 'I am still God, I can do whatever I like, no need for all this redemptive drama, I will just let it go'?" that would be unjust and illegitimate based on the fact that he has set up his cosmos in a particular order. If you think he could just wave it away and out of his power go against the legitimate and just order that he has laid down, why would you then think it is strange if someone says Jesus paid for our sins? We were supposed to go to jail but did not go to jail. Which, to me too, I know is unjust. You were supposed to go to jail, go to jail. If you do not go to jail, you have not learned any lesson. If you have a problem with that injustice, you must also have a problem with this injustice. You cannot say God should simply wave away the legitimate setup he has made just because he is God. Then that means you can also accept the other one too. I am just saying that as a minor point for a particular audience.

The reality of redemption is what we are still trying to establish. Death and Hades were cast into the lake of fire, and that lake of fire is second death. Anyone not found written in the book of life was cast into second death. So anything of sin and death has eventual consequence being second death.

So we have established three things. Number one, what God offered: life. Number two, what man did: rejection of God's purpose or life, and that is what is called sin. Number three, the effect of what man did: death.

## The Only Possible Resolution

So if this is the case, that death is set in motion and that death has its natural consequence playing out in a particular way, what is the resolution to the problem? Now we are about to see what the reality of redemption can be.

Will there be options, because we are saying what is the possible resolution, or will there only end up being one option? The first thing that limits us to one option is that if the entire cosmos is affected by death, remember sin entered into the cosmos and death by sin, then everything except God himself, the self-existent Creator, is death-affected, death-bound. It is under the bondage of sin and death and darkness and evil.

What is the only way redemption can happen? If something is dead, what you need is life. If something is in darkness, under the bondage of darkness, what you need is light. So if we are looking for the possible resolution of the sin problem man has caused, the option first goes down to one person. The only person that is not part of the cosmos is God. Everything else is in the cosmos: man, angels, matter, all of it. So the possible resolution first comes down to one point. It can only be resolved by God.

Now we come down to another point. Light has to enter into that darkness and dispel the darkness. But this is where you also need to pay attention. If legitimacy and justice are important to us, and clearly they should be, because if God is going to be God and he is unjust then we are already denying him, then if sin and death have been set in motion, they must take their full course. They must exercise themselves fully and legitimately.

So in possible resolution now we have looked at a couple of factors. The first factor is who can resolve this. It comes down to a who: only the self-existent God can resolve it. The second thing is how can it be resolved? Light must enter into darkness. But the third thing is how can it be legitimately resolved? It is a simple thing to restore life into death or light into darkness. But the problem is legitimacy.

Notice that it is not a case of someone paying a price to someone. It is a case of someone allowing the consequence of sin and death fully to take its course. It is neither a price paid to God nor to the devil. It is a case of seeing to it that sin and death fully take their course, that their consequence is allowed to fully play out, while still saving the cosmos and particularly humanity therein.

If you ask me what description is closest in describing it, I would likely have said a ransom one. But the problem is that word ransom has some challenges. That is why I am going to give another word that I believe will best reflect the reality of redemption, which I believe the word `apolutrosis` intends to do.

So with all we have seen, the three things we have seen about the resolution of redemption are these: sin and death must take their course, light has to enter into darkness to dispel it, and it can only be done by God.

What does that mean? It means, number one, we need an incarnation. God has to enter into the cosmos, and of course as the highest state in the cosmos, which is humanity, the very sphere also responsible for the problem. He must see to sin and death taking their course legitimately, and in doing that bring back life and light.

That is exactly the narrative of redemption. The only way it can be resolved is this. This is the philosophically necessary resolution of the contradiction in the world of sin. And it is not only philosophically necessary; it was prophetically promised. It is seen in prophetic patterns, and eventually historically fulfilled.

The problem set in with death has not been fully resolved merely because someone rises back into the same mortal condition. Rise above death means rise as immortal, not rise as mortal. If I did not see that with any of the other persons, then the problem had not been resolved. When I see it there, I know that the resolution has happened and will be consummated. The philosophically necessary resolution is also prophetically promised and patterned, and eventually historically fulfilled.

So if we say what is the exact event of redemption, it starts with what God offered, what man did, the effect of what man did, the possible resolution, the promised resolution, the accomplished resolution, and its implications.

## The Accomplished Resolution in Christ

What exactly would be necessary to happen? It means life must enter into a world of death. And before it can legitimately allow life or light to come to that world, it must legitimately allow sin and death to take their course in a salvific manner.

So he comes into the world and he dies for us, and goes to that place of the dead and suffers that second death on our behalf. The second death that consumes the cosmos, on him he exhausts it despite his sufferings. So the second death that consumes the cosmos is exhausted on him. There is a legitimate allowing of sin and death to take its course.

So it means someone in the cosmos has fully dealt with sin and death. If anything in the cosmos still experiences it, it is because they have the will to reject what that person has done. Someone has legitimately allowed sin and death to take its course. That is why the material world, which does not have a will to reject the work that has been done, has granted the right to the person who has accomplished this work.

You can have a `Christus Victor` drama to explain it and say he went to fight death and fought Satan and he won and all that. But the real issue is that he simply allowed it to take its course on him and by that legitimately brings life and light to the world, particularly humanity. That is the work that he did. So when he died, he went to the place of the dead and exhausted death.

Acts 2:24 says, "whom God raised from the dead, having loosed the pains of death." That is, he exhausted the pains of death. He loosed the pains of death. He exhausted it. Some other texts say he suffered death for every man. So he allowed death to take its course. He suffered death for every man. That is not fighting. That is suffering. That is not even paying a price to someone. That is just undergoing the course of something.

So the reality of what he did is that he underwent the course of sin and death so that he can legitimately bring back life to us. Therefore we are free from the power of death. He loosed the pains of death. He exhausted the demands of death in his sufferings. That is exactly what he did: a legitimate exhaustion of sin and death, and a legitimate restoration of righteousness, life, and light.

So he legitimately exhausted sin, death, and darkness, and he legitimately restored righteousness, life, and light.

## Freedom, Not Negotiation

You can see here that you can choose to see healing therapy or a therapeutic solution. You can see theosis if you want to. There is a way you can reason ransom. It is just that it becomes challenging if you think about ransom the way we do now, that there must be another person on the other side that is being paid. It is simply a consequence taking its course, a reality and its consequence taking its course.

Sometimes just ask: what did Jesus actually do? He bore the second death for the world. That is the actual work he did. He went through that experience. Not someone did the experience to him. The experience was already on ground. He went through legitimate exhaustion of sin, death, and darkness.

You can see why it is easy to use a satisfaction theory to picture it, or a ransom theory, even a penal substitution theory. You can describe it in those ways. But the reality is that what he did is simply a work of freedom. He just did the work of freedom from a situation that is on ground. He worked out our freedom from a situation that is on ground.

I think if we simply call redemption freedom, and we allow it to just be freedom from sin and death on its own without adding, "you have to pay this or that," but by Christ going through that work, we can encapsulate all that it entails. Redemption is freedom from sin and death.

If at all we call it anything, we say it is a freedom theory. That is what `apolutrosis` seeks to reflect. It means complete freedom. Whether it was by paying a price or it was by doing a work, the idea of `apolutrosis` is simply complete freedom. It does not necessitate ransom. That is an imputation into the word. But it means complete freedom.

We are completely free from sin and death by what Christ has done. It was a legitimate exhaustion of sin and death by bearing it. We can also call it, if you like, a consequence-bearing theory. What did Jesus do? He bore the consequence of what man did. Not something imposed by God, but the consequence of what we brought in. It is consequence-bearing, freedom accomplished by consequence-bearing.

You can say, "He won the victory over them," but that can make it look like it was a fight they were fighting, and it is not fully reflective of the legitimate way he did the work. He suffered death for us. Hebrews 2:10 and 14. It was bearing consequence.

Someone may say he was the sin-bearer, but sometimes that can cause confusion. Is he bearing the guilt? Is he bearing the nature of sin? It was consequence-bearing. If you say he was the death-bearer, you might be right. The second-death bearer. If you say it was substitution, it might make it look like it was a matter of some injustice: we did wrong, we were supposed to go and suffer, now someone else is suffering for us. That is unjust. It was that the cosmos was going to have this natural consequence to bear, and someone in the cosmos has borne it. Cosmos consequence-bearing.

So if you want to keep it simple, we can just force the name freedom theory. But if you want to explain it, we say it is a consequence-bearing theory. And that is the reality of redemption.

## The Implications of Redemption

What is the first implication of what he did that accomplished redemption? The defeat or exhaustion of sin, death, and darkness. It has completely lost its right, since the cosmos in the person of Jesus has already experienced this. It is only if someone rejects that work that the person still has to go there, but the dues have been paid in the sense that the course required by the problem we caused has been legitimately exhausted.

So the first implication is freedom from death.

The second implication of redemption is the individual restoration to life. We are talking about restoration of humanity to life.

The third implication of redemption is the eventual consummation of redemption, which is the eventual restoration of the cosmos to life. New heavens and new earth. Our glorified body, and so on.

Let us put it simply.

The first implication of redemption is freedom from death. We are completely free from death's hold. He has brought us out of the kingdom of darkness into the kingdom of the Son of his love, out of the authority of darkness into the kingdom of the Son of his love.

The second implication is individual restoration to life. Now we have the life of God in us, the light of God in us, the nature of God in us.

The third one is the eventual restoration of the cosmos to life: the new heavens and the new earth, our glorified body, and the full restoration that is to come.

So at this juncture I will just say it is easy for us to make sense of other descriptions, angles, or theories of redemption, knowing the actual reality of redemption. Redemption is freedom from sin, death, and its consequence. It is about the natural consequence of sin, which is sin, death, and its consequence, that we are freed from. Not a negotiation with a person. Not a payment to God nor to Satan. It is simply accomplishing legitimately a freedom from sin, death, and its consequence. That is redemption.

## Responding to Redemption by Faith

What are we supposed to do about redemption? We are supposed to respond to redemption with faith and faith-stand. So when I realize all this Christ has done, I receive it by faith for my salvation, and I walk in it by faith-stand for the fulfillment of God's purpose.

Please, when you sing many songs, many songs use several descriptions of redemption, and it is okay. We just have to understand what they are doing. I was even trying to remember one now: "Precious Jesus, I thank you for your blood, I thank you for your body broken for me." You see that? "I remember what you've done for me." This is using substitutionary language, which is still true because he did it for us, so he did not have to do it. But if in your mind you are thinking penal substitution, and someone hears it and is thinking, "penal, your own penalty you were supposed to bear, someone now took it for you," not so. He did it for me by consequence-bearing for the cosmos.

People would have things to point out as flaws there. A lot of the faith is very ontological. Many people, when they present salvation, usually present a judicial salvation or a relational salvation. The restoration can be relational, now we are back to God. You can use a judicial description, like there was a law laid down, you disobeyed this, and the consequence is the penalty of the law. But it is not really like that. It is more ontological.

There is something that if you do it has an ontological effect. The ontological effect goes all the way to this: it has to be experienced. That is why I have told you I am coming up with one theory called ontological truthism. I am really working on it. In fact, always remind me of it, no matter what I tell you. It is a very serious theory that I might even write a book on. Ontological truthism.

Let us leave it. Do not bother. It might not be that interesting. It is one of these boring types of books, but I think it is important.

The Christian faith addresses an ontological problem, a cosmic problem, not by a cosmic battle, but by a consequence-bearing of the problem that the cosmos has.

And that brings us to the end of the teaching. The reality of redemption: redemption bears the consequence of sin, death, and darkness, and therefore we have complete freedom.
