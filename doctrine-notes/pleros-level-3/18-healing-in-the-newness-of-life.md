# Healing in the Newness of Life

Healing is such an important subject matter. Other than the preaching of salvation and the teaching of the gospel, there is hardly anything more emphasized in the person and ministry of the Lord Jesus than healing. That is because healing addresses such a pressing human need. Someone may not yet believe the gospel and still need healing, and God does not mind healing such a person because healing reflects his nature and character. God wants us well. That must be an important standpoint from which we understand this subject.

There are people who would rather prove a point about what counts as truly supernatural than care for people who are suffering, but that is not the heart of God. God wants to do good. He wants us healed. So if we are going to talk about healing in the newness of life, we must first understand the newness of life itself.

## The Church's Emphasis Is the Newness of Life

Ephesians 4 shows that the Lord gave apostles, prophets, evangelists, pastors, and teachers for the perfecting of the saints. That perfection has to do with a change in knowledge, a change in person, and a change in walk. We come to church so that our knowledge changes, our person changes, and our walk changes.

Paul says that, because of this, we should no longer walk as the Gentiles walk. That means the outcome of church life is not only that we know something new, but that our experience changes. There are things in the life of God that, through the mind, should affect our walk.

So the issue is not only that we have received life. The issue is that our walk must change because of that life. The reality of the life of God is already ours, but something has to happen in our mind and consciousness so that the life affects our experience.

When Paul explains what we are taught in Christ, he sums it up in the reality of the old man being put off and the new man being put on. That is the emphasis of the church's teaching. The conclusion of doctrine is the reality of the new creation, or, preferably, the reality of the newness of life.

## External and Internal Conditions of Life

When we speak of the newness of life, we are talking about two things at once. We are in that life, and that life is in us. There are external conditions of the life of God and internal conditions of the life of God.

Romans 6:4 says that, just as Christ was raised from the dead by the glory of the Father, even so we should walk in newness of life. The point is not only that we are in the newness of life, but that we should walk in it. Walk refers to activities and experiences. It includes conduct and character, but by extension it also includes healing. There are experiences that belong to the newness of life, and healing is one of them.

This is not merely a charismatic claim. It is a biblical claim. There are grounds in Scripture to expect not only a new character, but also certain experiences in the newness of life.

## Life Was God's Offer at the Beginning and Humanity's Hope Thereafter

The first thing to know about this life is that it is what God intended for man at the beginning and what will be fully effected at the end. God offered man the tree of life at origin. After sin, that same life became the hope of humanity in faith.

Titus speaks of the hope of eternal life promised before time began. That means this life was God's offer at origin, but now, in the present age, it is still spoken of as a hope because it will be fully effected at Christ's coming.

The Lord Jesus also spoke of entering into life as a future reality. Yet he also spoke in John 5:24 of someone who hears and believes as already having everlasting life and as having passed from death to life. So the life is both possessed internally and entered into externally.

First John 3 shows the same pattern. We have passed from death to life, which sounds like location, and eternal life abides in us, which sounds internal. So when God promised life, it was not just life in us. It was also life that we would be in.

## Life, Immortality, and What Is Not Yet Effected

Second Timothy 1 says that Christ has abolished death and brought life and immortality to light through the gospel. Yet we still see mortality, corruption, sickness, disease, and physical death. So Christ's work is complete, but not everything in that work has yet been effected in present experience.

First Corinthians 15 explains that there is an order in God's plan. Christ the firstfruits has already received immortality. Those who are Christ's will receive it at his coming. So immortality is available by redemption, but it is not yet effected in the saints' bodies. It will be effected by the Lord at his own choosing, at his coming.

That means it is error to say believers are now walking in bodily immortality as a present effect. The Scriptures say immortality will be effected by Christ at his coming.

Romans 8:11 and 2 Corinthians 5 show that the Spirit is the means by which this will happen. The Spirit has the power to affect immortality in our mortal bodies and incorruption in the cosmos. So the question becomes: if that same Spirit has power over body and cosmos, what is available to our bodies today by that power?

## What the Spirit Makes Available Today Is Healing

Matthew 12 shows that when Jesus cast out demons by the Spirit of God, the kingdom of God had come. In that same context, the person was not only delivered from a demon but healed of blindness and muteness. That means the same Spirit by whom the kingdom is manifested now makes healing available today.

Luke 4 and Luke 5 show that the power of the Lord was present to heal. The power that will one day effect immortality is also the power that heals now. Luke 8 shows the same thing, where Jesus perceived power go out from him.

Today, the power that will make the believer immortal at the resurrection is already in the believer. So the primary paradigm should not only be healing through another person's ministry, though that also has its place, but the healing power of God in you healing you.

Healing in the newness of life is not presented as a matter of divine whim or discretion. We are called to walk in the newness of life, not to wait passively for a discretionary moment. If something belongs to life, we are called to walk in it.

## Healing Is Available but Not Automatically Effected

Healing is available in the Spirit, available by redemption, and available in the newness of life, but it is not automatically effected. That distinction matters.

There are things redemption effects automatically. Forgiveness of sins is one of them. Being made a new creation is another. But walking in what has been made available is not automatic. Our walk in the new nature is something we must effect. In the same way, healing is available, but not automatically effected.

This is why certain common healing texts must be handled carefully.

First Peter 2:24 is not, in its direct discussion, about physical healing. The context is about sin, righteousness, and reconciliation to God. By his stripes we were healed refers there to a change from the condition of sin into the condition of righteousness. Verse 25 confirms this by saying that those who had gone astray have now returned to the Shepherd and Overseer of their souls.

Matthew 8:16-17 also should not be used carelessly to say physical healing has already been automatically effected in the same way forgiveness has. When Matthew says Jesus healed the sick so that what was spoken by Isaiah might be fulfilled, the point is that Jesus is the one who bears the redemptive burden. His healing ministry points to him as the one who would bear sin and its consequences and effect redemption. The passage has a clear physical-healing connection, but it is not teaching that redemption has already automatically effected every instance of bodily healing in the same way it automatically effects forgiveness.

So healing is available, but it must still be effected.

## The Place of Natural Healing

At this point, the question naturally arises: what about natural healing? Does healing in the newness of life mean believers should reject natural means?

The answer is no. Scripture gives us no reason to be averse to natural healing. Genesis 1 places the cosmos under man's dominion for good and for life. Psalm 104 shows God causing vegetation to grow for the service of man. The implication is that man is to harness the world God made for good and for life.

So there is nothing unrighteous about natural healing. The problem is not using natural means; the problem is idolizing anything, including nature itself. Believers should fully embrace natural means of healing and preservation while remaining dependent on God's power.

That includes ordinary common-sense care. Rest, food, medicine, prevention, and even things like vaccines fall under the proper use of the created order for life and good. There is no virtue in rejecting such helps.

## The Purpose of Supernatural Healing

The first purpose of supernatural healing in the newness of life is defense. The first revelation of God as healer in Exodus 15 is framed in defensive terms: he will not put those diseases on his people. Healing power works as defense, as a kind of immunity, repelling and expelling sickness and disease.

The second purpose is response when there is a condition that needs to be addressed. And even here the response is often joint. We believe God, but we also do what is naturally fitting. Jesus could raise Jairus's daughter and still tell them to give her something to eat. That means a believer responding in faith should also rest, eat properly, and address obvious natural factors.

The third purpose is that there are times when the power of God is the only solution left. In such cases, after all natural means have been exhausted or where they are insufficient, the power of God remains the answer.

## Healing Is Effected by Faith

The Gospels do not merely tell us that Jesus healed. In the individual cases, they also show us how healing was effected. It was effected by faith.

The centurion said that Jesus only needed to speak the word, and Jesus called it great faith. In the case of the paralytic, Jesus saw their faith. To the woman with the issue of blood, he said, "Your faith has made you well." To the blind men, he said, "According to your faith let it be to you." To the Syrophoenician woman, he said, "Great is your faith."

When the disciples failed in Matthew 17, Jesus called them faithless and connected their failure to unbelief. That means the issue was not that healing happens without a given means. The issue was that healing was being affected by faith, and their failure was tied to a problem of faith.

Even Matthew 13:58 says he did not do many mighty works there because of their unbelief. So if we are going to be honest with the text of Scripture, healing is consistently presented as being effected by faith.

This must be said carefully and graciously, because some presentations of faith have been harsh and harmful. But it would still be dishonest to the biblical witness to deny that healing is affected by faith.

## Faith, Little Faith, Doubt, and Unbelief

Mark 5 helps us see how this faith works. Jairus had come in faith for his daughter, but then he heard that she had died. Jesus immediately said, "Do not be afraid; only believe." That shows that fear can be added to faith in a way that weakens it. Jesus was guarding his faith from being displaced.

Matthew 14 gives a similar pattern in Peter walking on the water. Peter clearly had faith or he would never have stepped out of the boat. Yet Jesus called it little faith and asked, "Why did you doubt?" So little faith is not the same as no faith at all. It is weak faith.

This shows that faith is being spoken of in Scripture as something that can be strong or weak, steady or wavering. There is faith, little faith, doubt, unbelief, and strengthening in faith. That is why Romans 4 says Abraham, not being weak in faith, did not consider his own body or the deadness of Sarah's womb. He did not waver at the promise of God in unbelief, but was strengthened in faith.

James 1 says to ask in faith, with no wavering. So the issue is not merely whether a person has ever had a conviction. The issue is whether that conviction is being maintained and strengthened or whether it is wavering into unbelief.

If someone stays in faith, that person moves toward strength. If someone keeps wavering, that person can slide into unbelief. That is why Jesus said, "Only believe," and why he rebuked unbelief in the disciples. The charge is to stand in faith.

## Stay Strong in Faith Until Healing Is Effected

So the instruction is to stay strong in faith for instant healing or until healing is fully effected. Healing has not been automatically effected by redemption the way forgiveness has, but it is available and is to be effected by faith.

There are two things to have faith for in healing: the process and the result. The process is that the healing power of God is at work in the body, expelling and repelling sickness and disease. The result is that one is healed.

That is why the confession at the end of the teaching is framed that way: the healing power of God works in my body from the top of my head to the soles of my feet; it is expelling every sickness and disease; therefore I am healed now and always by God's power.

We are to be full of faith, full of power, and consistent in ministering healing by words, actions, material means, and the laying on of hands. If there is no instant report, one should not collapse in unbelief. Minister, stay in faith, and continue to stand.

The final charge is that believers should be strong in faith, careful not to waver into unbelief, and full of confidence that the healing power of God is available in the newness of life and can be ministered both in us and through us.
