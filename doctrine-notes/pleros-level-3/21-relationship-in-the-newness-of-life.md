# Relationship in the Newness of Life

Relationship in the newness of life is an important subject. When we say relationships, we are not speaking only about marriage. That is often where people's minds go, especially now, but the subject is broader than that. It includes marriage, but it is really about relationships in their fullest extent.

The reason for that title is connected to what Paul says in Ephesians 4:11. The Lord Jesus gave leaders to the church for the perfecting of the saints, or the equipping, training, and transformation of the saints. That shows us that salvation was not only about the forgiveness of sins. Christ died and rose again not merely to save us from sins, but to restore us to God's purpose. If all that mattered was the forgiveness of sins, there would be no need for discipleship. The very fact that there is a call to discipleship and the perfecting of the saints tells us that there is more in view than pardon alone. God's purpose is in view.

## Growth in Christ Includes Our Relationships

The reason God made us is the same reason Christ saved us. So when we come into God's purpose, what we are taught in church is meant to transform us. Ephesians 4 says that the leaders of the church are given for the perfecting of the saints until we all come to the unity of the faith and of the knowledge of the Son of God, unto a perfect man, unto the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ. That is growth. The church is meant to transform us into the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ.

That means that after we come into God's purpose, there is still a need for growth if we are going to fulfill that purpose. If I do not grow, I can hinder my pursuit and fulfillment of God's purpose. If I do not grow in healing, that can affect my walk in God's purpose. If I do not grow in preservation, that can affect my walk. If I do not grow in character, in love, in righteousness, in the realities of the new creation, those things can affect my pursuit and fulfillment of God's purpose. So we need growth in the light of who we are in Christ.

Paul shows us what that growth looks like in Ephesians 4:17-24. He says that we should no longer walk as the Gentiles walk. That means there is a way the world handles life and relationships, and there is a different way the new creation is meant to walk.

## Not Just the Deed, but the Motive

There are two ways the world can work, but there is only one way the new creation can work. Sometimes the problem is obvious. A person may not be giving at all, not walking in love at all, not being kind at all, and you can tell the person is walking in the flesh. But there is another issue: someone may seem to be doing the right thing and still not be walking in the Spirit because the motive is wrong.

People can talk about kindness, love, or good relationships in a strategic way, as if they are investing in people merely to get returns. Many of the things they say may work, but that is still not the walk of the Spirit. When we talk about walking in the Spirit, both the heart and the deed matter. So when Paul says we should not walk as the Gentiles walk, it is not only that we should do what they fail to do. It is also that even where they seem to do something right, we must not do it the way they do it.

That is why self-righteousness is as much a sin as unrighteousness. A person may be doing the right thing, yet doing it to impress God, to justify himself, or to deserve commendation. That is not the righteousness that is born out of God's nature in us. So in relationships, the issue is not only whether you are doing well externally. The issue is whether it is being done with the heart of Christ.

Walking in relationship in the newness of life is not a strategy for getting ahead. It is not a technique for managing men so that you can rise. We do what is right out of the purity of who we are.

## Renewing the Mind for the New Man

Paul says that the Gentiles walk the way they do because of the futility of their mind and the darkness of their understanding. Their nature and their mind affect their walk. For the believer, the nature has already been changed, so what now requires renewal is the mind.

That is why Ephesians 4:20 says, "You have not so learned Christ," and then calls us to put off the old man, be renewed in the spirit of our mind, and put on the new man, which after God is created in righteousness and holiness of truth. That renewal is not merely receiving information. It is a change in reckoning, consciousness, and inward disposition.

Some people have a fixed spirit of mind about relationships. They may think, "That is just how I am. I do not really relate with people. People just relate with me." But that may be a place where the mind needs renewal. Something may have become so subconscious that it feels natural, when in fact it is not the way the new man should function.

Paul then moves immediately into practical outcomes. He says to put away lying. He speaks about anger and sin. He speaks about love. So the new creation is called to put off the old and put on the new in character. We need to change in our love walk, in righteousness, in truthfulness, and in the way we handle displeasure. There is such a thing as righteous displeasure, but there is also unrighteous rage. "Be angry and do not sin" means our displeasure must not become sin.

## From Character to the Supernatural to Relationships

From Ephesians 4 into Ephesians 5, Paul keeps speaking about character. He speaks about walking in light, putting away darkness, and being imitators of God as dear children. He also speaks about the supernatural: "Be not drunk with wine, wherein is excess; but be filled with the Spirit," speaking to yourselves in psalms and hymns and spiritual songs.

So in the newness of life we are taught to walk in godly character and to walk in the supernatural. But Paul does not stop there. In Ephesians 5:21 he says, "Submitting yourselves one to another in the fear of God." At that point he moves directly into relationships.

He starts with our relationships among believers. The church is not only a place where people minister the supernatural to one another. It is also a place where believers must learn to relate well with one another. "Submitting to one another in the fear of God" means there should be accountability among the saints.

## Accountability Among Believers

There should be a system of accountability in the church. There is leadership and followership, but there must also be a way that leadership is held accountable. Members are accountable to leaders, but leaders should also be accountable in an orderly and righteous way. There should be structures that protect everybody, because nobody is God.

There must be leaders who can check a leader if necessary. There should be rules by which wrongdoing can be addressed. That protects the people who have invested their time, energy, and trust into a church community. Paul says in Galatians 1 that if even we preach another gospel, we should be rejected. That means there must be defined standards by which accountability can actually function.

So Ephesians 5 first speaks about church relationships. Then it moves into marriage: "Wives, submit yourselves unto your own husbands." Then Ephesians 6 speaks of children and parents. Then it speaks of servants and masters, which we can understand today in the category of employment relationships. Scripture is very clear that the believer is taught how to relate in different kinds of relationships: among believers, in marriage, in parental relationships, and in work relationships.

## Relating to the Unrepentant and to Unbelievers

There are also other relationship instructions outside Ephesians. In 1 Corinthians 5, Paul distinguishes between unrepentant believers and unbelievers. He says he wrote not to keep company with sexually immoral people, but then he clarifies that he did not mean the immoral people of this world, because then we would have to go out of the world. He was speaking about someone who identifies as a believer yet is unrepentant.

That distinction matters. This is not about someone who is standing by faith, growing, and fighting the flesh. It is about someone who has accepted as right what is clearly wrong. In such a case, Scripture teaches a withdrawal of a certain level of familiarity.

This is consistent with Matthew 18. If someone sins, you speak to the person. If the person refuses, you take one or two more. If the person still refuses, you tell it to the church. If the person still refuses, the Lord says to treat him like a heathen or a tax collector. That means the kind of familiarity proper among believers is withdrawn. So the Bible does teach that there is a way the new creation relates even in difficult cases.

## Resolving Disputes Among Saints

In 1 Corinthians 6, Paul addresses disputes between believers. He asks why a believer would take a matter before the unrighteous rather than before the saints. His point is that believers should prioritize spiritual resolution first. There are matters that should first be taken to spiritual leadership so that they can be judged and resolved in righteousness.

This should not be abused. The point is not that people have no rights. The point is that Matthew 18 should come first. If a person refuses spiritual authority and proves unyielding, then he is acting like one outside the faith and may be handled accordingly. But the first instinct of the new creation should be to seek righteous judgment among the saints.

Paul's concern is that believers should trust that spiritual authority, when functioning properly, can judge matters in righteousness. Even if the decision ends up similar to what a secular court might decide, the saints should still value the order God has given among them.

## Biblical Estimation in Relationships

Scripture also teaches us how to esteem people in relationship. There is a righteous order of estimation. This is not derogatory toward anybody; it is simply truthful about obligations.

For example, a man must esteem and care for his family in a way that differs from others. If a person does not take care of his own household, he is worse than an unbeliever. That means family obligations are real and primary in their proper place.

This also helps with the question of how to relate to spiritual authority. A wife should not be honoring a pastor relationally in a way that displaces the honor due to her husband. Positionally, spiritual authority has its place, especially in teaching and in the resolution of issues. But relationally, the husband is the head of the union. The husband's own submission to spiritual authority is part of what keeps the order sound. So these distinctions must not be confused.

The same principle applies more generally. People should not create unnecessary tension in their homes by misplacing honor and affection. Positionally, one may recognize authority. Relationally, one must still walk properly in the obligations of marriage and family.

## Relating to Secular Authority

The new creation is not left without instruction regarding secular or governmental authority. Romans 13 says, "Let every soul be subject unto the higher powers. For there is no power but of God: the powers that be are ordained of God." That means God has instituted order in the world, and believers should regard rightful authority as something that serves that order.

So if you live in a place with agreed rules and dues, or under civic obligations such as taxes, there is a righteous way to respond. Disorder is not of God. The believer should not be the one creating lawless confusion in matters where order is legitimate and due.

## Different Kinds of Relationships

There are different kinds of relationships, and there is also a rough hierarchy in how we think about them. First is our relationship with God. Then there is spiritual authority, though spiritual authority has limits: its responsibility is to teach and to judge. After that come family relationships, especially marital and parental relationships. Then there are core friendships, church relationships, general friendships, employment relationships, governmental relationships, and other acquaintances.

The exact ordering of some of those later categories may require a more detailed treatment, but the main point is clear: not every relationship is the same, and not every relationship carries the same obligations. There is a way we owe fellow church members. There is a stronger way we owe core friends. There is a primary way we owe family. The new creation must learn to recognize those differences.

That also affects affection. All of us have an affection bank, and affection must be allotted wisely. A person who enters marriage has taken on obligations of affection that are no longer optional. Those obligations must be honored. Family and covenant relationships should not be neglected under the excuse of other loyalties.

Likewise, no one should be serving spiritual authority more than family. Spiritual authority already receives the collective service of many people. A person should not be giving more time, affection, or practical devotion there than to the family for which he is directly responsible. Spiritual authority should be honored in the light of its role, but affection and duty must be rightly ordered.

## God's Example Is the Pattern for Relationship

The consistent teaching of Scripture is that we should follow God's example in relationship. Ephesians 5:1-2 says, "Be imitators of God, as dear children, and walk in love, as Christ also has loved us and has given himself for us." That is our pattern.

The same principle appears in marriage. Wives are told to submit to their own husbands as unto the Lord, and husbands are called to love in the pattern of Christ and the church. So the standard is not merely social wisdom. The standard is God's own example.

## Guarding the Mind in Relationship Matters

There is also a serious need to guard the mind. People can feed their minds on negative relationship stories, scandals, and destructive narratives until their imagination becomes trained in fear, suspicion, and corruption. If you keep listening to people say men are like this, women are like that, and keep consuming stories of betrayal and manipulation, you can begin to train your mind in directions you do not want.

The mind is not a dumping ground. A believer should not feed on the death and disorder of the world as if that will produce life. There may be proper moments to address problems, but you cannot use your mind to contain all the evil of the world and expect to remain sound. Your life is for God's purpose. Your mind should be fed with life.

That is especially important in relationships, because what you continually entertain can become the lens through which you begin to view people. If you train your mind in corruption, fear, and scandal, you create tension and suspicion for yourself. But if you guard your mind and feed it with life, you are better positioned to walk in relationships as a new creation.
