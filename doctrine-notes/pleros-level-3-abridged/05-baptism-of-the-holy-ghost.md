# Baptism of the Holy Ghost

This is still level three, and we are looking at the baptism of the Holy Ghost. This subject is important because people have many questions about it, many concerns about it, and they want certainty on it.

But when we are dealing with revelatory truths, certainty only extends as far as what has been revealed. We must reason faithfully from what Scripture has made available, not grandstand beyond what is written. On this subject especially, we are fully in the domain of revelation. The matter of the Holy Ghost is not something you arrive at by natural observation. Our authority here is the Bible, and we must trust God's providence in preserving it for us and reason from it carefully.

And let me say this early: you cannot talk honestly about the baptism of the Holy Ghost without talking about tongues. But even there we must stay within the bounds of what the text actually reveals.

## The First Contacts With the Phrase

Our first contact with the idea of the baptism of the Holy Ghost is in the Gospels. They do not immediately explain it fully, but they introduce the reality.

Matthew 3:11: John says, "I indeed baptize you with water unto repentance... He will baptize you with the Holy Ghost and fire." Mark 1:8 repeats it: "I indeed baptized you with water, but he will baptize you with the Holy Ghost." Luke 3:16 gives the same account, and John 1:33 says, "This is he who baptizes with the Holy Ghost."

The word `baptize`, `baptizo`, means to dip or immerse. John was baptizing people into water. That part is plain enough. But then he says there is another reality: people will be baptized with the Holy Ghost. He also says, "and fire," and the next verse shows that the fire there is judgment: "He will burn up the chaff with unquenchable fire." So whether it is water, Holy Ghost, or fire, the underlying idea is immersion.

At the same time, I want you to notice that baptism can also be thought of from the angle of something brought upon people. With water we naturally imagine dipping into it. But with fire, judgment, the sense can also be that he is bringing something down upon people. Keep that in mind, because that way of thinking becomes helpful later when Acts begins to describe this reality in terms of the Spirit coming upon people and being poured out.

At this first stage we only know that there is such a thing as the baptism of the Holy Ghost. We should not assume more than the text has yet shown.

So already we are learning an important habit. Do not rush ahead of revelation. Let the texts introduce the reality first, and then let later texts explain what the earlier texts announced.

## Acts 1 and the Coming Reality

Our next contact is Acts 1:5. Jesus says, "John truly baptized with water, but you shall be baptized with the Holy Ghost not many days from now." So the reality John spoke about was about to become functional. Whatever this baptism of the Holy Ghost means, Jesus says it is about to happen.

Then in Acts 1:8 he says, "You shall receive power after that the Holy Ghost has come upon you, and you shall be witnesses unto me in Jerusalem, and in all Judea and Samaria, and unto the uttermost part of the earth." Here we meet another description of the same reality: the Holy Ghost coming upon.

That is why I said earlier that baptism here should not be limited in our minds to only the picture of dipping into something. There is also the sense of the Spirit being poured out, coming upon, drenching, clothing, and thereby making people baptized in that reality. So Acts 1 does not merely repeat the phrase; it starts to describe its manner and purpose. The Spirit comes upon you. You receive power. You become a witness.

And that point about purpose matters. Their immediate instinct in Acts 1 was to ask about the restoration of the kingdom to Israel, something visibly political and earthly. But Jesus directs them to another kind of expectation first: the coming of the Holy Ghost upon them, power, and witness. So even in the flow of Acts 1, the baptism of the Holy Ghost is being framed as part of God's order for mission.

## Acts 11 and Peter's Memory

The next use of the phrase is Acts 11. Peter says in verse 16, "Then remembered I the word of the Lord, how that he said, John indeed baptized with water; but ye shall be baptized with the Holy Ghost." What made him remember? Verse 15 tells us: "As I began to speak, the Holy Ghost fell on them, as on us at the beginning."

So when Peter saw the Holy Ghost fall upon the Gentiles, he identified that event as what Jesus had meant by baptism of the Holy Ghost. That is crucial. Peter is doing the interpretive work for us. The Holy Ghost fell upon them, and Peter said, This is that word of the Lord.

Verse 17 then says, "If therefore God gave them the like gift as he did unto us, who believed on the Lord Jesus Christ; what was I, that I could withstand God?" Now another description has entered the picture: the same gift given when we believed. So the reality is being described as baptism of the Holy Ghost, the Holy Ghost falling upon, and the gift of the Holy Ghost.

Notice again how careful the argument must be. Peter does not merely report an emotional moment. He interprets what happened by the Lord's own prior words. That is how we build certainty in doctrine: not by mere testimony, but by testimony interpreted through Scripture.

## Acts 19 and the Question of Receiving

Acts 19 gives another very important angle. Paul asks in verse 2, "Did you receive the Holy Ghost when you believed?" They answer that they had not even heard whether there be any Holy Ghost. Then Paul asks, "Into what then were you baptized?"

That means Paul's question about receiving the Holy Ghost is directly tied to a baptism experience. "Did you receive the Holy Ghost when you believed?" and then, "Into what then were you baptized?" They "Unto John's baptism." Paul explains John's baptism, ministers Christ to them, baptizes them in the name of the Lord Jesus, lays hands on them, and verse 6 says, "The Holy Ghost came on them; and they spake with tongues, and prophesied."

So there is no doubt from Scripture that there is a reality called the baptism of the Holy Ghost. If someone denies that such a reality exists, the person is not being faithful to the text. The question is not whether the reality exists. The question is what exactly it means and how Scripture describes it.

And Acts 19 also gives us our first explicit narrated connection between this baptismal reality and tongues in the same scene. That will become very important later.

## Different Descriptions of the Same Reality

From the passages we have seen, several descriptions belong to the same reality.

One, baptism of the Holy Ghost.

Two, the Holy Ghost coming upon. Acts 1:8 and Acts 19:6 show that clearly.

Three, the Holy Ghost falling upon. Acts 11 makes that connection explicit.

Four, receiving the Holy Ghost. Acts 19:2 uses that wording directly.

Five, gift language. Acts 11:17 speaks of "the like gift," and Acts 2 also uses gift language.

Six, being filled with the Holy Ghost.

Now, being filled is slightly broader, because Scripture can use it for people under the Spirit's influence even before the post-resurrection order. Zacharias was filled with the Holy Ghost and prophesied in Luke 1:67. So being filled with the Holy Ghost is a more general description of someone acting under the Spirit's influence. Still, after Christ's resurrection, it is regularly tied to this baptismal reality. When people enter this reality, they are also acting under the Spirit's influence, so the language of being filled can properly be used.

That is why being filled may not always be the best stand-alone label if we want maximum precision, because it can be used more generally. But once we keep the biblical distinctions in view, we can still see why the term legitimately attaches to this experience. If someone is under the active influence of the Spirit externally, the language of filling makes sense.

So we have seen two things clearly already. First, there is such a reality as the baptism of the Holy Ghost. Second, Scripture describes that one reality with several related expressions.

## What the Baptism of the Holy Ghost Means

What then does it mean to be baptized in the Holy Ghost? I prefer to describe rather than over-define it, but this is the clearest way I can put it: the baptism of the Holy Ghost is the state of full expression of the Holy Ghost, specifically the external expression of the Holy Ghost.

Why say it that way? Because there is an internal reality or expression of the Spirit, and there is an external expression of the Spirit. When Scripture discusses this experience called the baptism of the Holy Ghost, it always does so on the premise that the people in question have believed on the Lord Jesus Christ. Acts 19:2 says, "Did you receive the Holy Ghost when you believed?" Acts 11:17 says God gave them the same gift as he gave us when we believed on the Lord Jesus Christ. Faith in Christ is the premise.

Now, analytically, Paul's question in Acts 19 shows that someone may have believed and yet still be lacking this reality in the specific sense under discussion. But that does not mean the person lacks the Spirit absolutely. This is where many people think wrongly.

If we miss that distinction, we fall into avoidable confusion. Some will If a person believed, then the person must already have all that is being discussed here in every sense, so this baptism cannot be a meaningful category. Others will If a person lacks this experience, then perhaps the person does not have the Spirit at all. Both conclusions fail because they collapse internal reality and external expression into one undifferentiated idea.

## The Internal Expression and the External Expression

Ephesians 1:13 says that upon believing we are sealed with the Holy Spirit of promise. Romans 8:9 says, "If any man have not the Spirit of Christ, he is none of his." John 14:16-17 speaks of the Spirit who shall be in believers. So at the point of faith there is an immediate internal reality of the Spirit. The believer belongs to Christ because the Spirit is in him.

Therefore, when we talk about the baptism of the Holy Ghost, we are not first debating whether the Spirit is in the believer salvifically. We are discussing the full expression of the Spirit, where the internal reality is joined by external expression.

The internal expression is seen in the inward work of the Spirit that produces transformed character. Love, joy, peace, longsuffering, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, meekness, temperance, self-control. That is real. That is precious. That is the Spirit at work internally.

But the things tied to baptism of the Holy Ghost are especially about external expression. Tongues, prophecy, miracles, gifts of the Spirit, bold witness, manifestations that assist our work in God's purpose. So a person may truly be saved, may have the Spirit internally, may even be walking beautifully in love, joy, peace, kindness, meekness, and self-control, yet may not have stepped into this external expression. Such a person's experience is real, but it is limited relative to what has been offered.

If God offers something and we do not partake of it, there is no doubt that our experience of what is possible through us is limited. In the same way that a believer may not be walking fully in all the inward fruit available to him, a believer may also not have stepped into the outward expressions available to him.

That does not mean we insult the internal reality. It means we honor the fullness of what God has provided. If someone walks in genuine love and patience and kindness, that is glorious. But if God has also ordained external empowerment for witness, gifts, and manifestation, we should not act as if the inward work alone exhausts the whole provision.

Now, someone could object: people had external operations of the Spirit before Christ came. That is true. Zacharias could be filled with the Holy Ghost and prophesy. Others before the resurrection experienced the Spirit's activity. So why this special new-covenant reality called the baptism of the Holy Ghost?

The answer is that with the new creation a new order has been established. The way the new creation steps into the external expression of the Spirit is this reality called the baptism of the Holy Ghost. It is God's ordained order for sons of God not only to possess the Spirit internally by redemption, but also to enter the external expression of the Spirit by Christ's outpoured gift.

So the point is not that there were no prior operations of the Spirit in history. The point is that after the resurrection, the new creation has a defined order of participation in the Spirit's external expression. That is why this teaching belongs so centrally to the post-resurrection life of the church.

## Empowerment for Purpose

Acts 1:8 tells us why this matters: "You shall receive power... and you shall be witnesses unto me." So the baptism of the Holy Ghost is empowerment for purpose, especially for spiritual assignment, though it also aids our walk in God's purpose generally.

Can a man see a vision or experience a miracle that is not narrowly tied to public ministry? Certainly. But the point remains that this empowerment has a particularly critical role in witness and spiritual assignment. The Lord did not intend to leave the believer with internal regeneration alone. He also intended to clothe the believer with power.

That is why 1 Corinthians 12 says the manifestation of the Spirit is given for the profit of all. The gifts are given to help, to assist, to carry the load with us. Whether in preservation, guidance, service, or ministry, they are aids in the outworking of God's purpose. That is also why Jesus told the disciples in Luke 24 to tarry until they were endued with power from on high. The language of being clothed upon again fits the same outpoured, upon-you sense.

So the baptism of the Holy Ghost is the believer's entry into the full expression of the Spirit, particularly the external expression, for empowerment in purpose.

And that is why we must not reduce this teaching to a dispute about terminology. God's concern in giving this is practical and purposeful. There is work to be done, witness to be borne, burdens to be helped by the manifestation of the Spirit, and assignments for which divine empowerment is fitting.

## Speaking With Tongues as the Primary Mark

How do we know a person has entered this reality? The primary, essential, initial mark in Scripture is speaking with tongues.

Speaking with new tongues, other tongues, tongues: this is the consistent mark we see in the places where Scripture directly narrates the reception of the baptism of the Holy Ghost. The reason is not far-fetched. By the time we read 1 Corinthians 12 to 14, tongues functions as a leading gift into other expressions of the Spirit in the new-creation order. That does not mean every operation of the Spirit requires a fresh speaking in tongues at that same moment. It means that, by order, tongues stands as the primary initial mark of entering this external expression.

Acts 2:4 says, "They were all filled with the Holy Ghost, and began to speak with other tongues, as the Spirit gave them utterance." Acts 10 says the Holy Ghost fell on Cornelius's house, and they knew because they heard them speak with tongues and magnify God. Acts 19 says the Holy Ghost came upon those disciples, and they spoke with tongues and prophesied.

There may be other passages like Acts 8 or Acts 9 where the narrative does not restate the detail the same way. But where Scripture directly shows us the reception in clear terms, the primary initial mark is speaking with tongues. That much is indisputable from the text.

So when Jesus said, "You shall receive power after that the Holy Ghost is come upon you," what do we actually see happening in Acts? Tongues. When Peter said God gave them the same gift, what was the narrated evidence? When Paul asked whether they had received the Holy Ghost and then ministered to them, what happened? Tongues and prophecy. The pattern is not weak. It is strong.

That is why this subject cannot be handled faithfully while trying to avoid the subject of tongues. The texts keep bringing the two matters together.

## What Tongues Is

What then is tongues? This is not a full teaching on tongues, but we need enough clarity to think properly.

Some people think tongues is simply a human language. Understandably, they think that way because Acts 2 can lend itself strongly to that thought. In fact, one thing is beyond debate if we are honest with the text: Acts 2 includes supernatural speaking in actual human languages understood by hearers. That happened.

But we must not make the first narrative automatically determinative in a simplistic way. We must examine the whole biblical witness. And when we do that, 1 Corinthians 14:2 gives the clearer doctrinal explanation: "He that speaketh in an unknown tongue speaketh not unto men, but unto God: for no man understandeth him; howbeit in the spirit he speaketh mysteries."

That is strong language. He does not speak to men, but to God. No one understands him. In the Spirit he speaks mysteries. That points us to the clearest general conclusion: tongues is a non-human language supernaturally granted by the Holy Ghost.

I would not be unnecessarily combative with those who want to leave room for human-language phenomena under the larger umbrella. But the cleanest doctrinal conclusion is that tongues, as Paul explains it, is not ordinary human speech. It is Spirit-enabled utterance beyond the natural human mind.

And that is one reason tongues so directly tests whether we will walk by faith in this matter. The mind wants advance comprehension and prior control. Tongues does not work that way. It is utterance given by the Spirit and voiced by the believer.

## Who Can Speak With Tongues, and When

Who can speak with tongues? Every believer can.

Mark 16 says these signs shall follow them that believe, though people debate the textual history there. Even if you set that aside, Acts 2, Acts 10, Acts 19, and 1 Corinthians 12 to 14 together point in the same direction. The picture is not that this is for a tiny elite. It is open to believers.

When can believers receive? Any time since Jesus has poured out the Spirit. The first disciples had to wait for Pentecost because that was the historical launch of the outpouring. But after that, Acts 2:39 says the promise is to you, and to your children, and to all that are far off, even as many as the Lord our God shall call.

Where can they receive? Anywhere. Peter's language in Acts 2:39 removes the idea that this is locked to one geography or one generation. Since Christ has poured out the Spirit, the gift stands open to those who believe.

So we have clarity on the basic questions. What is tongues? A Spirit-given utterance beyond ordinary human speech. Who can speak? Believers. When? Since the outpouring of Christ. Where? Anywhere the believer is.

So it is not reserved for a special class of Christians, or for one apostolic generation only, or for one sacred location. The promise stands where the gospel stands.

## Why Tongues

Why tongues? There is one central answer: it takes off the limits of natural human language in our engagement with God and in the supernatural.

In fellowship, natural language reaches its limit. You want to commune with God, but your vocabulary runs thin. In self-edification, you want to keep building up yourself in God, and tongues gives liberty beyond the limitations of ordinary speech. In supplication, you often do not know what details to pray, yet by tongues you continue in prayer beyond the narrow range of what your natural mind can formulate.

So the essential reason for tongues is to remove the limit of natural language in fellowship, edification, supplication, and supernatural engagement with God.

That is why we should not treat it as a trivial side issue. It belongs to the Lord's practical provision for the believer's life with God.

## How We Receive the Baptism of the Holy Ghost

That brings us to how the baptism of the Holy Ghost is received. We receive it.

What do I mean by that? It has already been given. Acts 2:33 says Christ, having received from the Father the promise of the Holy Ghost, "hath shed forth this, which ye now see and hear." Acts 10 uses the same outpouring language about the Gentiles. And Titus 3:4-6 uses the language of the Spirit being poured out abundantly on us through Jesus Christ our Saviour.

So the outpouring of the Spirit is something Christ accomplished in his resurrection, ascension, and exaltation. Pentecost was the launch date in history, but the accomplishment belongs to the finished work and present authority of Christ. He has poured out the Spirit.

That outpouring explains both the internal and external sides. By faith in Christ, the believer receives the Spirit internally for salvation. By faith in this outpoured gift, the believer also receives the Spirit for external expression, which is the baptism of the Holy Ghost.

So when we ask, How does the baptism of the Holy Ghost happen? The answer is: by receiving the outpouring of the Spirit for its external expression.

The internal reality came at the point where you believed in Christ. But here you are intentionally receiving the outpouring for external expression. You are saying, in effect: Christ has given this. I accept it. I receive the Holy Ghost for his external expression.

And it is important to say it that way because some people speak as though the baptism of the Holy Ghost were a future event God might or might not decide to create on the spot for you. But the New Testament language is outpouring language. Christ has poured out the Spirit. The issue now is not whether Christ will become willing. The issue is whether the believer will receive what has been given.

That is also why gift language helps. A gift can truly be given and yet not be actively received or used. So when Scripture speaks about receiving the Holy Ghost in this sense, we are not trying to persuade Christ to become generous. We are accepting what his generosity has already supplied.

## Receiving, Faith, and Acting on the Word

But we must be more precise. What does it mean to "I receive"? It does not mean merely saying words while waiting passively for something else to happen apart from you. It means receiving for external expression by faith to do the external expression.

If I receive the baptism of the Holy Ghost, then I receive to speak. I receive to do. I receive to enter those external expressions that Christ has supplied. Primarily, I receive to speak with tongues, and that becomes the leading entrance into other gifts.

The logic of faith here is not, "I receive, and perhaps later I may have it." The logic of faith is, "I believe I receive, and I have." So if I I have received the baptism of the Holy Ghost, that means I have received the external expression. It cannot stop at a bare confession with no corresponding action.

That is why merely waiting passively can become a mistake. If by faith I say I receive the baptism of the Holy Ghost, then by that same faith I must recognize that I have received to speak. I have received to enter that expression. Faith here is not vague religious expectation. It is a response to a gift already supplied in Christ.

This is why acting on the word matters. First, there is the knowledge from Scripture. Second, there is faith that accepts the gift. Third, there is acting on the word, which is not pretending but actually doing what the word implies.

Acts 2:4 is very clear: "They were all filled with the Holy Ghost, and began to speak with other tongues, as the Spirit gave them utterance." The Spirit gives utterance. They do the speaking. If they do not begin to speak, nothing speaks. The Spirit does not speak instead of them.

So when a believer receives the baptism of the Holy Ghost, the believer should not remain waiting for God to force the mouth. The believer receives the gift, yields to the Spirit's utterance, and begins to speak.

That is the pattern: the gift is there, the influence of the Spirit is there, the utterance is given, and the believer responds. The believer is not acting like the word is true in some theatrical sense. The believer is acting on the word by doing what the word warrants.

## On Utterance, the Mouth, and Fear

This is where many people stumble. They But this is a non-human language, how will I know what to say? The answer is that none of us knows ahead of time by natural analysis. The Spirit gives utterance. You do not pre-approve it by the natural mind before you obey.

Some people sense what is about to come and then resist it because it does not sound how they expected. But that is not your business. You are not the one generating the utterance. The Spirit gives utterance; you supply the speaking. As long as you are in faith, receiving what Christ has given, you are not in danger of doing something alien to God.

Believers are often held back by fear, suspicion, or over-analysis. Some even talk about devil's tongues. But if a believer is receiving by faith what Christ has poured out for his people, that fear is misplaced. The same Lord who saved you is the one who poured out the Spirit. This is not a realm where the believer must approach God's gift with panic.

The scriptural pattern is simple. Christ has poured out the Spirit. The gift is available. The believer receives. The Spirit gives utterance. The believer begins to speak.

And once that is understood, many anxieties lose their force. The believer does not need to wait until the natural mind feels fully comfortable. The believer needs to trust the giver, receive the gift, and cooperate with the Spirit's utterance.

And that cooperation should not sound strange to us. If the word of God tells you to act, then acting is not interference with God; it is obedience to God. In the same way, beginning to speak as the Spirit gives utterance is not the believer replacing the Spirit. It is the believer responding rightly to the Spirit.

## Further Clarifications on the Flow

Let us restate the textual flow plainly. John says there is such a reality: he will baptize you with the Holy Ghost. Jesus in Acts 1 says that same reality is about to happen and describes it in terms of the Holy Ghost coming upon them and giving power for witness. Peter later sees the Holy Ghost fall upon the Gentiles and immediately remembers the Lord's word about baptism of the Holy Ghost. Paul then asks disciples whether they received the Holy Ghost when they believed and ties the matter directly to baptism.

So the flow is not fragile. It is strong and coherent. The New Testament gives us a real category, a cluster of descriptions for it, and narrated examples that show its manifestation.

## More on the Outpouring Language

The outpouring language is not a small detail. Acts 2:33 says Christ "hath shed forth this, which ye now see and hear." In Acts 10 the Holy Ghost is said to be poured out on the Gentiles also. Titus 3 says he poured out the Spirit abundantly on us through Jesus Christ our Saviour. This means the post-resurrection gift of the Spirit is explained by the apostles in terms of something Christ has already poured out.

And because of that, receiving the baptism of the Holy Ghost is not like asking whether God might someday decide to invent a new Pentecost for you. Pentecost was the historical launch of a gift Christ secured and poured out. Believers now receive what has already been poured out.

That also helps us understand the relation between the two sides we have been discussing. The same outpouring underlies the Spirit's indwelling in salvation and the Spirit's external expression in this baptismal reality. In salvation, by faith in Christ, the Spirit is in you. In the baptism of the Holy Ghost, by faith in this outpoured provision, the Spirit is upon you in external expression.

So if someone says, But I already have the Spirit because I am saved, the answer is yes. And precisely because of that, we are not talking about another gospel or another saviour. We are talking about another aspect of the Spirit's ministry that belongs to Christ's provision. The internal reality is not denied, and the external expression is not denied. Scripture gives us both, and we should keep both.

## More on Being Filled

Since many believers use the language of being filled, let us keep the distinction clear. Being filled with the Holy Ghost can be used broadly for Spirit-influenced action. But in the new-creation order, when someone enters this external expression under Christ's outpoured gift, the language of being filled can rightly describe that state too.

So when Acts 2 says, "They were all filled with the Holy Ghost, and began to speak," the filling is not floating in abstraction. It is tied to expression. That is why the categories overlap without becoming identical in every historical instance.

## More on Why This Matters Practically

If the baptism of the Holy Ghost is empowerment for purpose, then neglecting it is not a small loss. The believer's spiritual assignment benefits from divine assistance in witness, utterance, gifts, boldness, and other manifestations of the Spirit. Even more generally, the believer's life in God's purpose is aided by what the Spirit supplies.

That is why 1 Corinthians 12 says the manifestation of the Spirit is given for profit. The gifts are not decorative. They are not there merely to satisfy curiosity. They are helps. They assist. They strengthen the believer's participation in God's purpose.

So the burden of this teaching is not merely that you know the phrase "baptism of the Holy Ghost" exists in the New Testament. The burden is that you understand what God is offering in it, how Scripture describes it, what mark attends its reception, and why it matters for your life and assignment.

That way the doctrine is not left as a label. It becomes a revealed provision to be understood, received, and used with faith, sobriety, confidence, and steady obedience in Christ.

## Final Exhortation and Prayer

So right now, if you believe, you can speak. And if you have already spoken, you should speak, because the baptism of the Holy Ghost is empowerment for purpose.

I spoke about why tongues matters: it takes off the limits of natural language. But why the baptism of the Holy Ghost? Empowerment for purpose. "You shall receive power." "You shall be witnesses." There is a need for empowerment in the believer's life, in witness, in assignment, in the outworking of God's purpose.

Please do not set a limit on what God can do through you by neglecting what he has supplied. Jesus died and rose again and poured out the Spirit not just for internal regeneration but also for external expressions of the Holy Ghost.

So the right response is faith. I accept the baptism of the Holy Ghost. The Holy Ghost is upon me. I step into external expressions of the Holy Ghost by speaking with tongues and other gifts of the Spirit. I receive boldly in the name of the Lord Jesus.

And if you have already entered this, then use it. Speak. Pray. Edify yourself. Walk in the empowerment God has supplied. Do not treat the baptism of the Holy Ghost merely as a doctrine to affirm mentally or an event to remember historically. It is provision to be received and walked in.
