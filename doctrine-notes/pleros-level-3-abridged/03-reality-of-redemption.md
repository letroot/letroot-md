# Reality of Redemption

This morning we are starting a very important teaching: the reality of redemption. The reason for that title is that there are many descriptions people give to redemption, what many would call theories of atonement. But when we say the reality of redemption, we are asking something more basic. What does redemption actually entail? What is the actual event pertaining to redemption?

That event can then be described in many ways depending on the audience. If you are speaking to an audience that is more legally inclined, you may explain redemption from the angle of justification. That can come across as a law term. There is a sense in which justification is making something right, and we will see that. But you will also notice that there are places where guilt, a guilty person before a judge, and exoneration are used to describe redemption. Still, that description is not the reality of redemption.

If someone says the problem Jesus was solving is that when we stand before God we are guilty, and he did not want us to be guilty before his court, so he paid the price for us and now we are justified, that description still hits a wall if pressed too far. It raises the question of justice, because we are not the ones who paid. This is the challenge with descriptions of redemption. They usually have a limitation. They can be useful, but they are not the thing itself.

## Descriptions and Their Limits

It is like using a parable. A parable can explain something, but there are limits to what a parable can do.

Let me give an example. The Lord Jesus told that parable about prayer: a friend has another friend arrive at midnight, has nothing to set before him, and then goes to a neighbor to knock and ask for bread. The point is that the neighbor rises because of persistence. But if you stretch the details too far, the parable breaks down. There are some people who will not rise up and give you anything. They may call the police on you. So once you start pressing every detail of the picture, you have moved beyond what the parable was meant to do. That is how people often use descriptions of redemption.

Even when Scripture itself uses metaphor, the fact that the communicators of the message chose certain metaphors does not mean they are saying the metaphor is the reality. Scripture can speak of forgiveness in a way that sounds like God simply let what you did go. It can speak of wrath, ransom, sacrifice, reconciliation, victory, healing. But if you pay attention to the whole teaching of God's word on redemption, you realize those are descriptions functioning within a larger reality. The description is not the reality itself.

When we say reality, we mean how the thing actually is. A person may point to a chair and then point to a dog and "That dog has four legs, and that thing has four legs," but he is only using a comparison to describe a reality. We need to know what the reality of redemption itself is, and then know what the descriptions are, where they work, and where they stop working.

That is what we are trying to examine here. We have looked at redemption before, but in this level we need to establish the core issue in redemption. Before getting to exhaustive treatment, what is the reality of redemption?

## Redemption as the Central Truth of the Christian Faith

The first point to establish is the importance of this study. Undoubtedly redemption is the central truth of the Christian faith. We often speak, rightly, of God's purpose as the primary truth of the Christian faith. That is still correct. But redemption is the central truth because it presupposes the beginning and projects the end. It gathers into itself the whole movement of the faith.

At the end of the day, the real distinction of Christianity is a redemption distinction. It is redemption accomplished in Christ Jesus that marks the center of the Christian message. Ephesians 1:7 says, "In him we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of sins, according to the riches of his grace."

In that text, remember that Ephesians 1:3-14 is Paul giving a summary of his doctrine. In verses 3-6 he shows us God's purpose: he predestined us to adoption as sons by Jesus Christ to himself according to the good pleasure of his will. The essence there is that our existence begins with God having a purpose. Then in verse 7 Paul settles on what God did by Christ to bring us back into that purpose. He terms it redemption. In him we have redemption. So redemption is what God did to bring us back into sonship.

The word there is `apolutrosis`, used ten times in the New Testament. Ordinarily `lutrosis` means to set free, to loose. Sometimes that freedom is associated with a tool or with what we call a ransom price. That association is the basis of some theories of redemption, especially the ransom theory. The Lord Jesus himself said the Son of Man was given as a ransom.

But once you force the word into the modern sense of ransom price, the next question is unavoidable: who is the price paid to? Some have said to the devil. Others have said to God. It is almost easier to imagine it paid to the devil, the bad guy holding people, than to say it is paid to God, as though God himself is standing in the way of what he says he wants to do. But neither way of pressing the language may be right.

The same issue also leads some people into `Christus Victor`, the victory theory. Then there is penal substitution, satisfaction, reconciliation, therapeutic healing, and even theosis language. Usually no one holds only one description. There is often a mixture. That itself shows that we are dealing with descriptions and angles of explanation.

So the real issue is to identify the reality. What is this redemption exactly that was done? Why is it the central truth of the Christian faith? Because once you say redemption, you have essentially said Christianity. You are saying there was an initial plan and purpose, something went wrong, redemption addresses what went wrong, and that work will be consummated. So redemption encapsulates the whole Christian faith.

It is important to understand this clearly because if you do not, you can remain unsettled. There are people who believe in the Lord Jesus and yet from time to time face doubts and crisis because the redemption framework in their mind creates internal problems. For example, if redemption is imagined like this: God made us, we sinned, God was ready to judge and deal with us, but then he decided to send his Son to die, and when he sees blood his mind calms down. The question that arises is, why is God so hard? That description may be used in some texts for the sake of an audience, but if you mistake it for the underlying reality, it can become a serious challenge to your faith.

So redemption is central to the faith, but there are different descriptions, parables, and angles from which the truth can be presented. We need the event itself.

## Redemption Means Freedom From Something

Ephesians 1:7 says, "In him we have redemption through his blood, the forgiveness of sins." If redemption means freedom, then freedom from what? What are we being made free from? Is it by paying a price? What exactly needs to be done?

If we are going to understand the reality of redemption, we have to take a look at the origin of sin itself.

Romans 5:12 says, "Therefore, just as through one man sin entered the world, and death through sin, and thus death spread to all men..." Paul tells us how sin entered the world. It came through one man, and in verse 14 he identifies that man as Adam. So through one man sin entered the world, and death through sin, and death spread upon all men.

Now if redemption is from sin, and sin entered through one man, we need to understand what that sin was. When someone says sin, `hamartia`, the idea is to miss the mark. Sin implies there is a target, a goal, a right point. There is one truth, but many lies. So sin does not have to be one specific lie. Sin is anything other than the one true mark. If someone asks the color of a suit, there is one right answer and many wrong ones. That is how sin works. To miss the mark is to miss the true point.

So if we are going to understand sin properly, we must first understand the mark that was missed. What exactly did Adam miss? What was offered? The text already gives us a clue because death came through sin, but we need to look at the actual narrative.

## The Two Trees and the Offer Given to Man

Genesis 2:7 says the Lord God formed man of the dust of the ground, breathed into his nostrils the breath of life, and man became a living being. Then the Lord planted a garden eastward in Eden and put the man there. Out of the ground the Lord made every tree grow that is pleasant to the sight and good for food. So all the natural trees are there.

But then we are told of two other trees: the tree of life in the midst of the garden, and the tree of the knowledge of good and evil. At that point we should already see that the account is not merely talking about ordinary physical botany. There is no physical substance called life that produces a tree, and knowledge is not fruit hanging on a branch. So the account mentions natural trees, but with these two trees it is clearly moving into figurative, spiritual language.

The tree of the knowledge of good and evil brings death. In that sense it could as well be called the tree of death. And if one tree brings death through knowledge, then the tree of life must also be about knowledge, but knowledge whose result is life. So the two trees are about knowledge. One knowledge brings life, the other brings death.

Genesis 2:16-17 says, "Of every tree of the garden you may freely eat; but of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil you shall not eat, for in the day that you eat of it you shall surely die." So the natural trees are there, but there are also these two figurative trees, and the prohibition falls on the death-bringing knowledge.

Why are we tracing all this? Because if we want the reality of redemption, we need the events themselves. The events will tell us what happened, and that will define redemption better than any later metaphor.

## The Tree of Life as Wisdom and Truth

So what exactly is the tree of life? Proverbs 3:13-18 speaks about wisdom, and verse 18 says, "She is a tree of life to those who take hold of her." Wisdom is a tree of life. That means one receives life by taking hold of wisdom.

If wisdom is the tree of life, then rejecting wisdom and taking another knowledge is partaking of death. But wisdom itself is rooted in truth. Proverbs 23:23 says, "Buy the truth, and do not sell wisdom and instruction and understanding." So what do you receive in order to have wisdom? You receive truth. When you buy truth, what you now have is wisdom, instruction, and understanding.

So what did God plant on man's heart? Truth. The truth of existence. That is wisdom. That is the tree of life.

What happened then? Man did not simply receive that truth. He entertained another option as though there were something better. That is one of the oldest strategies of the enemy: suggesting that there is something better than what God has already given. But if you already have the best, to go seeking something better than the best is precisely how you enter into sin.

That is why this is still relevant pastorally. People can already have God's purpose and still be looking for something more, something beyond what God has given, as though the enemy can offer a better reality. It is the same move. The Lord Jesus already had the best, and yet Satan was showing him the glories of the world as though there were something higher. So what man did at the beginning is not alien to the way temptation still works.

## What Man Did and What Sin Produced

So if sin is to miss the mark, what was the mark? Life.

How was life offered? By truth planted on man's heart.

What did God offer? Life.

What did man do? He rejected life by receiving another knowledge.

What was the effect of what man did? Death.

That death plays out in phases. "In the day you eat of it you shall surely die." Man died spiritually, and that is why Scripture can speak of us as dead in sins and trespasses. Yet physical death unfolds later. And then beyond that, Scripture speaks of second death. Spiritual death and physical death both belong to the category of first death, the entire present order of mortality and corruption. Then there is the second death, which is the final destination of that whole regime.

So when we say man sinned and death came, we are saying something very important for the theory of redemption: the natural consequence of sin is death. Not first an arbitrary punishment added from the outside, but the consequence of what was done.

## Death as Consequence, Not Arbitrary Punishment

This matters a lot. What we call hell, damnation, eternal condemnation, was not first a judgment dreamed up against man as though God simply decided to impose something external. It is the natural consequence of rejecting life. You can still use judgment language as a description, and that description may help certain audiences understand. In ordinary speech even today, someone may "God will judge you," when what is really meant is that a destructive course has consequences. But if we mistake that description for the reality, we distort redemption.

The consequence can also be described as the wrath of God, because death and corruption are indeed displeasing to God and stand as his enemy. But that does not mean the issue is best conceived as God merely venting anger. The real issue is the consequence of what man did.

Revelation 20:13-14 says, "The sea gave up the dead who were in it, and Death and Hades delivered up the dead who were in them... Then Death and Hades were cast into the lake of fire. This is the second death." Death was cast into death. That itself shows us descriptive language at work. It is a way of telling us that even this entire first-death order finds its final extinction in the second death. So the second death is the final destination of sin.

Once you understand what God offered, what man did, and what followed, then resolution can finally be discussed clearly. The problem is not that God is somehow reluctant to be merciful. It is not a case of asking, "Why can't he just wave his hand and let it go?" The issue is that a legitimate consequence has been set in motion within the order of reality as God has made it. So if justice matters, that consequence must be resolved legitimately.

This is where some objections become inconsistent. Some people reject substitutionary formulations because they say it would be unjust for someone else to die for our sins. Yet the same people then say God should simply wave away the whole matter by mercy, even though that too would ignore the legitimate order he himself established. If you object to one supposed injustice, you cannot then demand another kind of injustice. Whatever the resolution is, it must be legitimate.

So far we have established three things very clearly: what God offered, life; what man did, rejection of life, which is sin; and the effect of what man did, death.

## The Only Possible Resolution

Now if that is the case, what is the resolution? At this point we are about to see what the reality of redemption can be.

The first thing that narrows the answer is that if the entire cosmos is affected by death, then everything within the cosmos except God himself is death-affected. Man, angels, matter, all of it is within the cosmos. So the one who can resolve the problem must be God. The possible resolution first comes down to a who.

The second point is straightforward. If something is dead, what it needs is life. If something is under darkness, what it needs is light. So the resolution requires life or light entering into death or darkness.

But the third point is the decisive one: legitimacy. If justice is important, and of course it is, then sin and death must be allowed to take their full course. They must exercise themselves fully and legitimately. This is not first a matter of someone paying a price to another person. It is a matter of allowing the consequence of sin and death to take its proper course while still saving the cosmos, and especially humanity, from that consequence.

So the resolution comes down to three things: only God can do it; life and light must enter into death and darkness; and sin and death must be exhausted legitimately.

What does that mean? It means incarnation is necessary. God has to enter into the cosmos, and of course as the highest state in the cosmos, humanity, the very sphere also bound up with the problem. He must enter in such a way that sin and death are allowed to take their course legitimately, and in that same act life and light are brought back.

That is exactly the narrative of redemption. This is the philosophically necessary resolution of the contradiction introduced by sin. And it is not only philosophically necessary. It is prophetically promised, seen in prophetic patterns, and eventually historically fulfilled.

Notice another important thing: the problem is not fully resolved if someone simply rises again into the same mortal condition. To rise above death means to rise immortal, not merely to return to mortality. So when Scripture presents the accomplished resolution, it is not just resuscitation. It is the defeat of death by passing through it and coming out beyond it.

So if we ask what the exact event of redemption is, we must trace: what God offered, what man did, the effect of what man did, the possible resolution, the promised resolution, the accomplished resolution, and then its implications.

## The Accomplished Resolution in Christ

What exactly had to happen? Life had to enter a world of death. But before life could be restored legitimately, sin and death had to be allowed to take their course in a salvific manner.

So Christ comes into the world and dies for us. He goes to the place of the dead and bears that death for our sake. The point is not that a fight scene is the fundamental reality, though victory language can describe it. The point is that he allowed sin and death to take their course on him and by that act legitimately brought life and light back to the world, especially to humanity.

That is why Acts 2:24 says, "whom God raised from the dead, having loosed the pains of death." That is, he exhausted the pains of death. Other texts say he suffered death for every man. That is not the language of bargaining with another party. It is the language of undergoing the course of something. He suffered death. He allowed death to run its course.

So the reality of what he did is that he underwent the course of sin and death so that he could legitimately bring back life to us. Therefore we are free from the power of death. He exhausted the demands of death in his sufferings. He legitimately exhausted sin, death, and darkness, and legitimately restored righteousness, life, and light.

So someone in the cosmos has now fully dealt with sin and death. If anyone still experiences that order finally, it is because they reject what has been accomplished. But the work itself has been done. That is also why the material world, which has no will by which to reject the work, is destined for restoration on the basis of what has been accomplished in Christ.

You can see why people may use `Christus Victor` language and say he fought death and won. That can function as description. But the real issue is that he allowed death to take its course and thereby legitimately brought life and light into the world. He went through the experience of death and exhausted it.

## Freedom, Not Negotiation

At this point you can understand why several descriptions have some usefulness. You can see why healing or therapeutic language can make sense. You can see why theosis language may arise. You can even see how ransom language can partly function. The challenge is when ransom is pressed to mean there must be another person on the other side being paid. The reality is not negotiation. It is consequence taking its course.

Sometimes the best question is simply: what did Jesus actually do? He bore the consequence of what man brought into the cosmos. He underwent the death experience. Not because someone needed to be bribed or paid off, but because the consequence was already on ground and had to be exhausted legitimately.

That is why descriptions like satisfaction, ransom, victory, or even penal substitution can each capture some angle, but none should be mistaken for the full reality. The reality is a work of freedom. He worked out our freedom from a situation already on ground.

If we want a simple expression, redemption is freedom from sin and death. `Apolutrosis` is complete freedom. Whether freedom comes by a tool, a work, or what people picture as ransom, the core idea is not that the word itself demands a price paid to someone. The core idea is complete freedom.

So we are completely free from sin and death by what Christ has done. It was a legitimate exhaustion of sin and death by bearing it. If one wants a more explanatory label, one could call it consequence-bearing. What did Jesus do? He bore the consequence of what man did. Not something externally imposed in abstraction, but the consequence of what was brought in. It is freedom accomplished by consequence-bearing.

If you say victory, that may make it look like a fight is the center. If you say substitution without care, it may make it sound like an injustice where one unrelated person is arbitrarily punished instead of another. But if you say consequence-bearing, you are closer to the actual structure of the event. Someone in the cosmos has borne the consequence legitimately and thereby opened freedom.

So if you want to keep it simple, you can call it a freedom theory. If you want to explain it more fully, it is consequence-bearing freedom. That is the reality of redemption.

## The Implications of Redemption

What is the first implication of what Christ did? Freedom from death. Sin, death, and darkness have lost their right because in the person of Jesus the cosmos has already experienced and exhausted them legitimately.

The second implication is the individual restoration of humanity to life. We now have the life of God in us, the light of God in us, the nature of God in us.

The third implication is the eventual consummation of redemption, the restoration of the cosmos to life: new heavens and new earth, glorified bodies, full restoration.

So redemption is freedom from sin, death, and its consequence. It is not negotiation with a person. It is not payment to God or payment to Satan. It is the legitimate accomplishment of freedom from the consequence of what man brought in.

Once you know that reality, other biblical descriptions start making sense. You can hear wrath language, ransom language, victory language, reconciliation language, even therapeutic language, and understand what each is trying to express without flattening everything into one inadequate metaphor.

## Responding to Redemption by Faith

What are we supposed to do about redemption? We respond with faith and faith-stand. When I realize what Christ has done, I receive it by faith for salvation, and I walk in it by faith-stand for the fulfillment of God's purpose.

That is why many songs can use several descriptions of redemption and still be useful. Some songs speak in substitutionary language: "your body broken for me," "your blood shed for me." That is true in the sense that he did it for us. But if someone hears that and imports a crude penal scheme into it, confusion follows. The clearer way to say it is that he did it for us by consequence-bearing for the cosmos.

This also shows why the Christian faith is dealing with an ontological problem, a cosmic problem. People often present salvation judicially or relationally. There is a sense in which relational restoration is true, because we are brought back to God. There is a sense in which judicial description may also function, because there is order, lawfulness, and legitimacy. But the problem itself is more ontological. Something happened in reality. A consequence entered the cosmos, and that consequence had to be dealt with in reality.

So the Christian faith addresses an ontological problem, a cosmic problem, not merely by a courtroom fiction and not merely by a battle image, but by Christ's consequence-bearing work. He bears the consequence of sin, death, and darkness, and therefore we have complete freedom.

That brings us to the end of the teaching. The reality of redemption is that Christ legitimately bore the consequence of sin, death, and darkness, exhausted that order in his sufferings, and therefore accomplished our complete freedom.
