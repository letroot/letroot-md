# Your Place in the Local Church

We are looking at a very important teaching: your place in the local church, on discipleship. The long and short of this teaching is to make it clear that the place where discipleship happens is the local church. Of course, that came up in our study on discipleship, where one of the key things we saw is that we commit to the local church. But this teaching has another gist to it. It is like being in a family.

When you are in your family, there is a place you have, and your place changes over time. When you were first born, everybody was attending to you. Everybody put their attention on you. But there is a point where the place of that child changes. The person is now a participant. In fact, there is a time when the child is expected to be contributing to the family. So you can see that in a family your place changes.

That is what we are looking at here. We are not looking only at what the church does to you, though that will come up. We are seeing the scope of your own journey in the church, your place in the local church.

## The Church as Spiritual Reality and Natural Gathering

The first thing I want to do is reestablish what we mean by the local church. Let us go to Matthew 28, and then Matthew 16 and Matthew 18, because there is an important movement there.

In Matthew 28:18 the Lord Jesus said, "All authority has been given to Me in heaven and on earth. Go therefore..." I told us that in this material this concept of heaven and on earth is a church concept. Every time the Lord used that concept in these passages it was relative to the authority He was giving to the church.

Now let us see Matthew 16:18. He said to Peter, "You are Peter, and upon this rock I will build My church, and the gates of Hades will not prevail against it."

I do not want to delve too much into all the debates about Peter here. Peter clearly had primary leadership in the early church. There is abundant evidence that Peter had a place of primary leadership. But that is not the same thing as saying the church was built on Peter in the ultimate sense.

When Jesus said, "Upon this rock I will build My church," it was not about Peter's primary leadership as the foundation. Rather, it was about Peter's identification with Him who was the Rock upon which the church is built, something Peter himself later teaches. In 1 Peter 2:4 he speaks of Jesus as a living stone rejected by men but chosen by God and precious, and then says, "You also, as living stones..." So Peter understood that the Lord was teaching that He Himself is the foundation, and His people are part of the building.

So yes, Peter is being used in the moment as the primary leader, even as an example in what the Lord is building, but he is not the one the church is built on. Peter being the primary leader is not the same thing as something being built on him.

Now when the Lord says, "the gates of Hades will not prevail against it," this is about what will happen in the redemptive work He was going to do, in His death and resurrection. In the death and resurrection of the Lord Jesus, death could not prevail against Him. Peter himself taught this in Acts 2:24, that it was not possible for Him to be held by death.

Out of that, the church is undefeatable by death. This is not something we need to keep hoping will become true. It is settled in the past. Once you are saved, death has no hold on you and Hades has no hold on you. It is not mainly about whether somebody can die physically. It is that once you are part of the church, you are out of the domain of death and now in the domain of life.

I do believe there is also a guarantee in God's word that the church on earth will not be defeated until the coming of Christ, and there are other Scriptures we can use for that. But this particular text in Matthew 16 is primarily about the victory over death secured in Christ's work.

Then look at verse 19: "I will give to you the keys of the kingdom of heaven, and whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven." Interestingly there it is singular, again showing Peter as having primary authority.

But by the time we get to Matthew 18, the Lord Jesus uses the church in another way. In Matthew 16 the church is being used as a spiritual reality, without the emphasis yet being on it as a natural gathering. I will do a work in the spiritual, Christ is saying, and the church will defeat Hades and death permanently. So church there is a spiritual reality.

In Matthew 18 the church is used as a natural reality, that is, a reality that subsists in the natural, not just one that exists in the spiritual.

Matthew 18:15: "If your brother sins against you, go and tell him his fault between you and him alone." Do not do silent treatment. Go and tell him his fault between you and him alone. "If he hears you, you have gained your brother. But if he will not hear you, take with you one or two more... And if he refuses to hear them, tell it to the church. But if he refuses even to hear the church, let him be to you like a heathen and a tax collector."

That is where the idea of withdrawing fellowship could even possibly come from. Then in verse 18 He says again, "Whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven."

Do you see that the issue of binding and loosing comes up again, and the issue of heaven and earth comes up as well? But now it is plural. You may not notice because English just says "you," but if you check it you will see that the "you" in chapter 16 was singular to Peter, whereas here the same authority is plural. That shows that this is a matter of church leadership as a whole having that authority, Peter having primary leadership, the other apostles having that same authority, and that authority now devolving functionally from there.

So there is ecclesial authority. That is the authority that the church, particularly leadership, can wield. Leadership in partnership with the congregation, yes, but leadership usually decisively can wield it.

Why do I say that? Because some people like to press a purely congregational model. But if you really square everything with Scripture, it is not as simple as that. For example, when Paul instructed them in 1 Corinthians to withdraw fellowship from the man who took his father's wife, not everybody obeyed. In the second letter he speaks in a way that suggests that some obeyed and some did not, but leadership still took the initiative and went on.

So when Jesus says, "Tell it to the church," in this kind of case it is very likely the church's episcopal authority saying, this matter has been considered, this person has been warned, there has been no change, and the church withdraws fellowship from the person. This obviously presumes the person is committed to that body.

Then look at verse 19 again: "If two of you agree on earth concerning anything that they ask, it will be done for them by My Father in heaven. For where two or three are gathered together in My name, there I am in the midst of them." Clearly this is about a natural gathering, where two or three are gathered together.

So there is church as this spiritual reality brought about by the work that Christ has done, and there is church as the natural gathering of people that are of that spiritual reality. What we often call the local church is that assembly, that gathering. So when we talk about your place in the local church, we are talking about that community of believers that you belong to.

## God Sets the Solitary in Families

What is that community of believers you belong to? I like the way God's word says it in Psalm 68. God wants all of us to belong to a community of believers. He puts us in families.

Psalm 68:5 says, "A father of the fatherless, a defender of widows, is God in His holy habitation," and verse 6 says, "God sets the solitary in families; He brings out those who are bound into prosperity; but the rebellious dwell in a dry land."

If you are isolated, it is not normal. It is not normal for you to be isolated and alone. No matter where you are in your discipleship phase, even if you are still seeking God's word on things, go to church. If you do not agree, eventually you will not commit. If you now see somewhere else that matches your persuasion better, you go there. But it is not God's idea that you just stay alone and isolated. He sets the solitary in families.

So the local church is something that God wants for every believer once you have believed the gospel. If you refuse that, there is a sense in which the text even names it as rebellion.

I am definitely of the persuasion that people should commit to a local church that matches their persuasion. It is impossible to obey God's word and not be a hypocrite if you are committed to a local church that does not match your persuasion. Also, if you have more light in God's word, you should not act as though that light is accidental. If you have more light in God's word, it is God's orchestration to you. It cannot be the devil giving you more light in God's word. And if God has given you that light, then it matters where you commit.

I think this is a basic Luke 12:47-48 principle. He that knows his master's will and does not prepare himself nor do according to his will shall be beaten with many stripes. Preparation happens in the local church. So if you know the will of God and you do not commit so that you are prepared accordingly, that is serious. Verse 48 shows that God is aware that some people will not have as much light as others. But when people do have more light and then refuse to align their commitment with that light, it is wrong.

Now I am not saying people should leave a local church in a dishonorable manner. Sometimes even having more light may not automatically mean leaving. There may be ways to share what you know in that local church, and if they accept it, that church might itself be transformed to the degree of what you now know. But there are situations where it becomes clear, either before saying anything or after saying it, that it will not work. In that case the most honorable thing is to leave honorably.

Do not ever do anything in an unruly manner. That is not God's Spirit. If it is of the Spirit that you now know more, then it should also be of the Spirit that you make the decision rightly: honorably, lovingly, peaceably.

And even if authorities themselves do not want to do things peaceably, the Bible says, as much as it is up to you, be at peace with all men. On your own part, do everything you should do, giving it the time it requires before you rightly transition. If you have responsibility somewhere, you do not just drop it. There is an honorable way to do it. You hand it over. We are talking about people's lives.

## Discipleship Is the Basis of the Natural Gathering

So the first thing I wanted to establish again is that there is the reality of the local church, the natural gathering of people that belong to the spiritual church. What is the basis of that natural gathering? Matthew 28. Discipleship is the basis of that natural gathering.

Matthew 28 is where the Lord gave the instruction that would be the basis of the church as a natural gathering. It was discipleship that led up to it. He says, "All authority has been given to Me in heaven and on earth." Do what? "Go therefore and make disciples."

Do not forget that this authority in heaven and on earth is church authority according to Matthew 16 and Matthew 18. So in giving that authority, He says, go and make disciples. Discipleship is what births the natural gathering.

It is very important to say that Jesus never said, "Go and start church." He said, "Go and make disciples." But the natural result is church, people gathered together to be discipled.

Is discipleship the only thing we are supposed to do? No. If discipleship means teaching people to observe all that He commanded, then everything that comes out of discipleship, we are also supposed to do. For example, one of the things that comes out of discipleship is fellowshipping with one another in love.

One of the emphatic instructions in the New Testament is, "Greet one another with a holy kiss." It is in Romans, in 1 Corinthians 16, in several places. For them that was their culture, but it was showing you the kind of welcome believers are supposed to have. There is a way you welcome somebody from afar, and there is a way you welcome somebody very warmly.

So church life is not meant to be cold. When Scripture says greet one another with a holy kiss, it is showing that part of church is that we have a warm welcome for one another. Even when the church grows large, and there are practical limits, there should still be intentional warmth, friendliness, phileo spirit. That also is part of discipleship. So discipleship is what led to the church, and discipleship continues to shape the life of the church.

## The Church at the Core Is the Family of God

Why does He want us to make disciples? Because of God's purpose. We can say that the church is really the home of God's purpose. God wanted a family. That is what the church is. At the core, the church is the family of God.

That is why I started out using the family idea to describe your place in the local church. He sets the solitary in families. What is the church at the core? God's family. Every individual there is in God's purpose and in His sonship, which means they are children of God. If we ask for the most definitive way to say it, the church is the family of God.

Why then did He give instruction on discipleship? Because that family needs to be trained to fulfill God's purpose, to function as children and heirs. Being a child of God and being an heir of God are family issues. Even our assignments are family issues. As members of God's family, we have work to do.

So when Jesus gave the instruction on discipleship, it is because discipleship is how the family purpose that God has will be fulfilled. That is what makes discipleship the core of the local church, because the local church at its core is about family.

Sometimes people want to speak as though it is really about mission and training, and family is secondary. But the mission is a family affair. The training is a family affair. In the Greco-Roman world, training was itself a family matter. You took your child through a definite training. The church is a place of discipleship for the sake of family.

If I can get that into you, then the major thing is done. When we talk about your place in the local church, we are talking about your place in your most important family, your core family.

Even our natural families should reflect the church. Imagine if homes functioned like church units, where fellowship activities go on, where parents are pastoring children, and families are learning to pastor other families. If we appreciate family within God's purpose well, it will help the mission greatly. The church should reflect that family.

## Your First Place: Saint, Believer, Learner

So what is your place in this family, the local church? The very first place you have is the place of a saint, a believer, or a learner. That means you are saved. You are a believer. You are a learner.

You will see very commonly that the apostles speak to believers as saints. First Corinthians 1:2 says, "To the church of God which is at Corinth, to those who are sanctified in Christ Jesus, called saints." The very least you can be in the church is a saint. Except somebody wants to start talking about your place as an unbeliever, but then you are not really yet in the church.

The first and primary place of any person in the local church is the place of a saint. That is why Ephesians 4:11 says the leaders of the church are given for the perfecting of the saints. It does not matter how far back you are. You are a saint, and you are there to be perfected.

This is why our understanding of saint matters. There is a concept of saint that can mislead people into thinking saint is an accomplishment. No. Being a saint is Christ's accomplishment for you, on your behalf. He has sanctified you. A saint is not an accomplishment of your deeds.

So the church is a community of saints. That means the church must be embracing of saints at their different stages. This is so powerful: the church is not a community only of committed people, or only of people already looking strong. The church is a community of believers, saints, people who have believed.

One of the biggest errors people can make is demanding some fundamental transformation before people can even really be in church. I am not saying there are no practical corrections to make where something is going to affect others. But your first place in the church is the place of a saint or believer.

You notice that in Acts 5 as well. The believers were increasingly added to the Lord, multitudes both of men and women. Or in Acts 2:47: "The Lord added to the church daily those who were being saved." Not those who had first changed every visible conduct issue, but those who were being saved. In verse 41, those who gladly received his word were baptized. The first thing you are is just a saint or a believer.

## Your Second Place: Member

Your second place in the church is a member. You are saved. You are coming to church. At that point you are an attendee. You are learning. But the second place you should have in the local church is that of a member.

Member means you have now joined the church. The church may have different ways of measuring that, but there is such a thing as joining a church. You have been around, you are a saint, you have been coming around, but you have now joined.

Some people think that language is strange. They say people are just joining church today and not joining the kingdom. But you can join church. The word join is in the Bible.

Acts 5:13 says, "Yet none of the rest dared join them." So you can join the church. There is such a thing as commitment. Acts 17 says that when Paul preached in Mars Hill, many did not believe, but some who believed joined him. So there is such a thing as joining the local church.

At this stage you are accepting this as your church family. The Lord sets the solitary in families. You are accepting that church family, and that means you are also submitting to the spiritual authority there. That now makes you available for further training. So the second place in the local church is member.

## Your Third Place: Worker

The third place in the local church is worker. The interesting thing is that all these realities are already resident in you from salvation, even to the last thing we are going to see, yet you are supposed to grow and unfold into them.

The third place is that of a worker. In 2 Corinthians 5 it is clear that when we get saved and become a new creation, we are meant to be workers. We are meant to serve. It is also clear that believers can be weak and may not yet be functioning in their role, so we are showing how this unfolds.

Some people can unfold speedily. Someone can get saved and join and even start serving in one day. It is possible. For another person it can be a journey. What do we do in both cases? We make available everything to help them grow. Sometimes because we are seeing someone grow speedily, we put pressure on the person who is not like that, and someone who might have become strong collapses under the pressure. That is not the best way.

But we are called to be workers. We are already designed as workers from the point of salvation.

Look at 2 Corinthians 5:17: "If anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation." Then he says all things are of God, who has reconciled us to Himself through Jesus Christ and has given to us the ministry of reconciliation. Very clear: from salvation you already have that reality.

Verse 19 says He has committed to us the word of reconciliation. Then he describes that reality by saying, "Now then, we are ambassadors for Christ, as though God were pleading through us." Then chapter 6 says, "We then, as workers together with Him..."

Do you notice the tone of the ministry? We plead. We implore. We warn where needed, yes, but the natural disposition in our preaching and ministry is not to be forceful, imposing, prideful, dismissive, obnoxious, or standoffish. We are workers together with Him, pleading with people.

He says we plead with people not to receive the grace of God in vain. So the third place in the local church is the place of a worker. You are saved, you have committed, and now you are serving.

So let us recap quickly. First place: saint. You are saved, a believer, and a learner. Second place: member. You have committed to the church. Third place: worker.

## Your Fourth Place: Deacon

The fourth place in the local church is deacon. Yes, deacon. You are supposed to serve as an assistant leader. The word deacon just means servant. The idea of a deacon is that the deacon is serving a person, assisting a person in a work. That is why one of the best ways to describe a deacon is to say assistant leader.

There is the argument from Acts 6 that those men were deacons, even though the passage does not use the formal label there. They were called to come and serve tables. That is why we have that idea. They were assisting those who were doing the primary duty of ministering the word at that oversight level.

You hear of Elisha pouring water on Elijah's hands. You hear of Joshua serving Moses. So the idea of deacon is that you are serving a person doing a work. You are assisting the person.

Look at Acts 6. When the number of disciples was multiplying, the apostles did not have the structure to carry the growing demand, so people were getting neglected. This is why structure is very important, and structure involves people.

Verse 2: "It is not desirable that we should leave the word of God and serve tables. Therefore, brethren, seek out from among you seven men of good reputation, full of the Holy Spirit and wisdom, whom we may appoint over this business." In other words, something we have been doing, let them help us do it. "But we will give ourselves continually to prayer and to the ministry of the word."

So there is the role of deacons, which is actually a training ground for unfolding into your leadership. The fourth place in a local church is that of a deacon.

Go to 1 Timothy 3. It is a training ground for unfolding into the leadership that God has for you. Let me say it unequivocally, though with some caution: if a person does not end up as a leader in the local church in some measure, it is very likely they did not fully fulfill God's plan, though there can be exceptions and circumstances. Natural assignment is part of God's will and will be judged, but this matter of spiritual assignment also matters greatly.

First Timothy 3:8: "Likewise deacons must be reverent..." So it means servant, yes, but you can tell this is a leadership position in servant form. Once you are in the church, you are already a worker. You are serving in natural affairs, helping in basic things, being available. Availability is a key issue. Then when leadership has more burdens, they pass responsibility to you. You are now a deacon.

He says, "Let these also first be tested, then let them serve as deacons, being found blameless." That means there is a point where they were just workers before you gave them that leadership role as deacons. So being a deacon is a leadership role, and there is a test to be a deacon. But being a deacon is also a test to become something else.

And he says that those who have served well as deacons obtain for themselves a good standing and great boldness in the faith which is in Christ Jesus. That means they have now shown themselves fit for what may come next.

## Your Fifth Place: Bishop, Leader, Pastor, Elder

The fifth place in the local church is bishop, leader, pastor, elder. That is how we will summarize it for now. You start as a saint. You become a member. You become a worker, a deacon, and then a leader, a bishop.

Anyone that does not function in leadership in some measure, brothers and sisters, I am not sure that the person has fulfilled God's purpose in the ordinary sense. I say that with the allowance that there can be exceptions because of circumstances and time, but the general trajectory of our spiritual assignment and our participation in the church ought to terminate in leadership.

Before coming back to 1 Timothy 3, let us see Ephesians 4:7, that this is already designed by God for believers.

Ephesians 4:7 says, "But to each one of us grace was given according to the measure of Christ's gift." How many people did He say grace was given to? Each one of us. There is nobody here that does not have grace according to the measure of the gift of Christ.

Verse 8 says that when He ascended on high, He led captivity captive and gave gifts to men. The gift of Christ is the gift Christ gives to humanity. So if each of us has grace according to that gift, then each of us is implicated in that gifting. In that sense, each of us is given to humanity in some measure for their salvation, establishment in, and fulfillment of God's purpose.

Then verse 11 explains the gift in ministry terms: apostles, prophets, evangelists, pastors, teachers. He gave people as people He sends to speak His word, evangelize it, and oversee those who believe it. You are God's gift to humanity in your own measure, and you have grace for that measure.

That means your place in the local church cannot remain, "I am just attending, hearing the word, wonderful." You must unfold. You must move from saint to member, from member to worker, from worker to deacon, and from deacon to bishop or elder or pastor, where you are now serving and leading people.

To show that leadership is open to believers in this trajectory, look at what Paul told Timothy. Because all believers have that grace and because this unfolding is part of the natural doctrine of the church, he tells Timothy that it is not strange for anyone to desire it. If there were an exemption of the kind people sometimes imagine, or if the caveat were that nobody should desire it and only a few should somehow wait for a special feeling, he would have said so. But he did not.

First Timothy 3:1 says, "If a man desires the position of a bishop, he desires a good work." Very clear, very open. As desirable as it is, if you do not have the character for it, then it is not right to be dabbling in it. You should first deal with the character.

Then he gives the qualifications: blameless, husband of one wife, temperate, sober-minded, good behavior, hospitable, able to teach, not greedy, not proud, not a novice, with a good testimony among those who are outside. These qualifications matter because these are the very kinds of things that can damage the church if neglected.

This is your place in the local church. It is like a person born into a house. At first everybody is taking care of the person. Then over time the person grows, starts serving, and eventually starts carrying responsibility for others. So your place in the local church begins with the place of a saint, then member, worker, deacon, and then leader.

## What Determines How You Unfold

It is my desire for you that you unfold unto that end, that you grow until you start to function in that place in the local church. So you must be able to know how that happens. There are two things, or maybe three, that determine that unfolding.

Number one is your own growth, your own personal journey of growth. Number two is your ministry. Number three is orchestrations and leadings.

Your growth matters because you cannot qualify for leadership without the requisite knowledge, growth, stamina, conduct, and capacity to bear it. In leadership you have to teach. If you do not know what to teach, that is a problem. Growth in knowledge and conduct is very key.

Number two is your ministry, because your ministry is what will bring people into your hands. Because of the way ministry often works now, it may be people you directly disciple, or people who trickle in by the joint work of ministry and are then put under your oversight because of your capacity.

Number three is orchestrations and leadings. God can orchestrate that you are supposed to go somewhere, and that is why your work will be done there. God can lead you that you now have to do something in a particular place, and that is why your work is to be done there.

You have two things in your control immediately: your growth and your service. Plug in. If you cannot do visible service because you are weak, start with your growth. There is nobody that cannot do growth. Once you are growing, you will get to the point where you can now serve. And once that is happening, God is always faithful to His orchestration and His leading.

Sometimes there will be no dramatic leading. It is just normal orchestration. Everything is being arranged because God wants you to lead some people, and it just happens over time. We just have these people and say, ah, brother so-and-so should lead this team, this unit, maybe even a virtual unit. With that, you are leading them, and before you know it you are becoming stronger.

And then before you know it, God can orchestrate that you are supposed to go to another place and perhaps start or continue a work there. Or there may be a direct leading: I want you to go to this particular place and do something there.

That is how it works in your place in the local church. From just being an attendee, you should grow into a functional leader that can now be specifically led on different things to do. Timothy grew to the point where he was now being told, "Stay in Ephesus, Timothy." That is how we should think about our life in God's purpose.

We have spiritual assignment and natural assignment to fulfill, and there is so much ahead of us to do in God's purpose.
