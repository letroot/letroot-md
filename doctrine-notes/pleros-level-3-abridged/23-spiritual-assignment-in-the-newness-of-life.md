# Spiritual Assignment in the Newness of Life

We want to start looking at spiritual assignment in God's purpose.

We have already seen natural assignment in God's purpose. Now we are asking again, why are we here? Once the truth of existence is settled, that there is a Creator and the world is not self-explanatory, then the question of why becomes the question of God's purpose. What did God want? What did he propose in himself?

The clearest answer is in Ephesians 1. Paul says in Ephesians 1:9 that God has made known the mystery of his will according to his good pleasure which he purposed in himself. So if we are going to know God's purpose, we must know his will, his good pleasure, what he wanted in himself. Then Ephesians 1:5 explains it: having predestined us unto adoption as sons by Jesus Christ to himself, according to the good pleasure of his will. If redemption is unto sonship, then sonship is the good pleasure of his will. We can answer definitively that God's purpose is sonship.

That leads to the next question: what does sonship mean? We have used the word already, but it is important not to leave it undefined. If God's purpose is sonship, then we must not fill the word with sentiment only. We have to let scripture explain it.

## Sonship Means Child and Heir

Romans 8:15 says we have not received the spirit of bondage again to fear, but the Spirit of sonship whereby we cry, Abba, Father. At salvation, the Spirit we receive is the Spirit of God's purpose. The Spirit bears witness with our spirit that we are children of God. So the first reality of sonship is that we are children of God.

Then Paul goes on: if children, then heirs, heirs of God and joint-heirs with Christ. So sonship has two realities. First, you are a child. Second, you are an heir.

That is consistent with Galatians 4. Paul says the heir, as long as he is a child, differs nothing from a servant, though he is lord of all, but is under guardians and stewards until the time appointed by the father. Then he says that when the fullness of time had come, God sent forth his Son to redeem those under the law, that we might receive sonship. So sonship means we are not merely children in the sense of relationship; we are heirs in the sense of inheritance and function.

That is why Paul stresses that because you are sons, God has sent forth the Spirit of his Son into your hearts, crying, Abba, Father. Therefore you are no longer a slave but a son, and if a son, then an heir of God through Christ. The point is not only that you can call God Father. The point is that sonship carries inheritance. If scripture only wanted to say child, it could have said child. But the language of sonship makes the heirship point very clear.

So the believer must think this way: I am not merely brought near; I am brought into inheritance. I am not merely received; I am also entrusted. The Spirit of sonship is not simply about emotional intimacy, though it certainly includes intimacy with God. It is also about the life of one who belongs in the Father's house as one meant to inherit and function there.

So when we say God's purpose is sonship, we are not saying only fellowship, worship, or affection. We are saying child and heir. That means purpose includes inheritance, function, and assignment.

## Purpose Includes Assignment

When people speak about God's purpose, they sometimes reduce it to being with God, loving God, or enjoying God. Those things are true, but you cannot remove assignment from God's purpose. Human beings are designed with pursuit and fulfillment in view. There is something to carry out, something to steward, something to fulfill.

That is why Barnabas in Acts 11:23 encouraged the believers that with purpose of heart they should continue with the Lord. Purpose is for pursuit and fulfillment. It is not just a reason for existing in the abstract. It carries a direction of life.

In other words, purpose is not merely something you discover so that you can feel settled. Purpose is something you discover so that you can walk. There is a race to run. There is a stewardship to handle. There is something the believer is meant to continue in with deliberate intent.

Paul also speaks that way in Acts 20:24: none of these things move me, neither count I my life dear unto myself, that I may finish my race with joy and the ministry which I received from the Lord Jesus, to testify the gospel of the grace of God. Again in 2 Timothy 4:7 he says, I have fought the good fight, I have finished the race, I have kept the faith. In those places he is speaking very directly about spiritual assignment.

That does not mean natural assignment is unreal or unimportant. Paul himself labored with his own hands and spoke in Acts 20:33-35 about not coveting anyone's silver or gold and about laboring to meet needs. Natural assignment mattered in his life. But it is impossible to read the New Testament honestly and pretend spiritual assignment is not taken with utmost seriousness in God's purpose.

There are some things people would prefer to tone down because they do not want to sound intense, but the text itself is intense on this matter. Paul can speak of ministry as a race to finish, a stewardship received from the Lord Jesus, and a good fight to complete. That should tell us that spiritual assignment is not decorative language in Christian teaching. It is built into the way the apostles thought about life.

It also means we must be realistic about weight and capacity. There are cases where natural assignment and spiritual assignment are both carried meaningfully, and there are cases where major spiritual leadership requires more concentrated focus, delegation, and sacrifice. The point is not to create a rule for every circumstance. The point is to say that scripture treats spiritual assignment as real work, real stewardship, and real inheritance.

Sometimes people speak as though the work of ministry should always fit conveniently into the margins of life. But there are phases and measures of ministry that do not fit the margins. That is not a complaint; it is simply reality. It is one of the reasons the church must think soberly about service, labor, support, delegation, and responsibility. The teaching is not that everyone must abandon ordinary work immediately. The teaching is that ministry is a serious labor in God's purpose and should be thought of honestly.

God's purpose is that you are a child and an heir. Therefore there is both relationship and responsibility in the design.

## Natural and Spiritual Inheritance

We have seen already that there is such a thing as natural assignment. First Corinthians 7:17 says, as God has distributed to each one, as the Lord has called each one, so let him walk. There is a patterned starting point in life, and you begin your walk from there.

But there is also spiritual inheritance. The language of part, lot, and inheritance is used not only for natural realities but also for ministry.

Acts 1 is one of the clearest places. Peter says concerning Judas that he was numbered with us and obtained a part in this ministry. That word is the language of lot or portion. In verse 24 they pray, You, Lord, who know the hearts of all, show which of these two you have chosen to take part in this ministry and apostleship from which Judas by transgression fell. Twice in the passage ministry is spoken of as a part, a lot, an allotted inheritance.

That is an important textual move because it gives us more than a vague impression. Ministry is not only activity; it is spoken of as a received part. Judas had a part in this ministry. Another would take a part in this ministry and apostleship. The apostles are not using inheritance language accidentally. They are thinking of ministry as something allotted within God's ordering.

That matters because it shows something about spiritual assignment. In natural assignment, scripture gives wide room for the righteous use of human will, wise choice, and ordinary stewardship. But in spiritual assignment, there is also choosing, election, allotment, and divine restriction. The Lord chooses where a person takes part in ministry. That is not a restriction on pursuit itself. It is not saying the one not selected becomes inactive. It is a restriction on place, role, and allotment within the mission.

Acts 1 is useful because the church prays plainly, show which of these two you have chosen. That means they did not assume that every sincere candidate occupies every ministerial place interchangeably. The issue was not whether the men should care about God's work. The issue was which part in that apostolic ministry the Lord had chosen. So when we speak about spiritual assignment, we must leave room for divine choosing without turning it into passivity or fatalism.

The point needs to be handled carefully. It does not mean people should become idle, waiting for a mystical guarantee before doing anything. It means the pursuit of God's work is one thing, but the location and allotment of a specific part can still belong to God's choosing. Barsabbas not being the one selected did not mean he had no place in God's work. It meant that particular apostolic part was not his. So spiritual assignment includes both pursuit and divine allotment.

So there is no doubt that there is natural inheritance, but there is also spiritual inheritance, spiritual lot, spiritual assignment.

Through church history people have often gone to extremes. At some times natural work was undervalued, as though only explicitly religious work counted. At other times vocation in the natural became the dominant emphasis. But the scriptural balance is that as heirs of God we have both natural and spiritual inheritance, and both unfold under God's orchestration. You may find your natural assignment developing in one direction and your spiritual assignment developing in another, but both belong within God's purpose.

So the believer should refuse both errors. Do not despise natural work as though it were outside God's concern. But do not collapse everything into natural vocation either, as though ministry were only for a select few while the rest simply live ordinary productive lives. Scripture presents both natural and spiritual inheritance as real.

## Every Believer Is a Part With a Measure

Ephesians 4 helps us explain this further. Verse 7 says, to each one of us grace was given according to the measure of Christ's gift. That is distribution. Christ is giving grace according to measure.

Then verse 16 says that the whole body is joined and knit together by what every joint supplies, according to the effective working in the measure of every part. So each part has a measure, and the grace given works effectively in that measure.

That means every believer is a part. First Corinthians 12:27 says, you are the body of Christ, and members individually. Each person is a distinct part in the body. Each part has a measure. Each part has grace for that measure. So the idea of allotment applies also to spiritual assignment. God distributes grace for our measure as a part in the body.

This is very important because many believers think in only two categories: either I am one of the important people in ministry, or I simply attend. But Ephesians 4 and 1 Corinthians 12 do not allow that way of thinking. The body grows by what every joint supplies, according to the effective working in the measure of every part. Every part means every part. Grace is not given only to those with public visibility. Grace is distributed to each one according to measure.

Paul's wording is also deliberate in another way. He does not say grace is given merely in general. He ties grace to measure. Then he ties effective working to part. So the measure is not random and the grace is not abstract. Grace is working in the measure of each part. That is why the believer should think, I am a part, I have a measure, and I have grace for that measure. That way of thinking resists both insecurity and pride. You do not have to imitate another person's measure, and you do not despise your own.

Then Ephesians 4:11-12 clarifies the purpose: he gave apostles, prophets, evangelists, pastors and teachers for the equipping of the saints for the work of ministry, for the edifying of the body of Christ. That means every believer is being equipped for ministry. Spiritual assignment is not a niche reality for a few special people. Every saint has a part, a measure, grace for that measure, and a share in the work of ministry.

Notice the order. The ministry gifts are given so that the saints can be prepared. The saints are prepared so that the work of ministry happens. The work of ministry happening causes the building up of the body. That is Paul's structure. So if the saints remain permanently passive while a few people perform ministry in front of them, the Ephesians 4 vision has not matured in that setting.

And that is why the note keeps insisting that every believer has spiritual assignment. The local church is not a place where a few function and the rest admire them. It is a place where the saints are equipped until they can carry their own measure in ministry. Some measures will be more visible and some less visible, but all are meant to supply something to the body.

The leaders of the church do not do ministry instead of the saints. They equip the saints to do the work of ministry so that the whole body grows. In that sense, each believer unfolds into the measure of leadership and service proper to his part.

So if we ask, what exactly is our spiritual assignment, Ephesians 4 answers clearly: it is ministry.

## The Work of Ministry

To be very clear, everybody born again has been given the responsibility of ministry.

Second Corinthians 5:17 says, if any man be in Christ, he is a new creation. Old things are passed away; behold, all things are become new. Then verse 18 says, all things are of God, who has reconciled us to himself by Jesus Christ and has given to us the ministry of reconciliation. It is the new creation that is given the ministry. Paul is not talking there only about a narrow ministerial class. He says God reconciled us and gave to us the ministry of reconciliation.

Then verse 19 explains the ministry: God was in Christ reconciling the world unto himself, not imputing their trespasses unto them, and has committed unto us the word of reconciliation. So the ministry is about a message. Spiritual assignment is the work of serving God's message.

That is why we must be clear what ministry means here. It is not merely helping with natural logistics in church life, though such helps have their place. The ministry being defined here is the service of the gospel message. You are serving the message of reconciliation to the world.

That message includes first the redemptive proclamation itself: reconciliation in Christ, forgiveness, new creation, salvation. Then it includes the truth of the purpose to which redemption restores us: newness of life, sonship, inheritance, victory over sin, and walking in God's design.

If there had been no fall, there would still have been the truth of God's life and purpose to announce and live in. But because sin entered, the message comes to us first as redemptive proclamation. People must be reconciled. Trespasses must no longer be imputed. Men must hear what God has done in Christ. Then the reconciled are taught the life into which they have been brought. So ministry serves both redemption and the purpose redemption restores us to.

So your spiritual assignment is to serve a message. You preach the gospel for salvation. You preach the gospel for discipleship. You call people into reconciliation, and then you call the reconciled to walk in the purpose into which they have been brought.

That is why this assignment is not optional. If you are a part, you have a measure. If you have a measure, grace has been given to you. If grace has been given to you, there is an effective working meant to happen in ministry.

This also helps distinguish spiritual assignment from natural usefulness. In natural assignment you may add value, solve problems, produce things, build systems, or meet social needs. All of that can be righteous and good. But spiritual assignment has a more specific object: serving God's message to men. It is ministry because it serves the word of reconciliation and the discipleship that follows from it.

That clarification matters because church life can easily blur categories. A person may say, I help in church, therefore I am doing the ministry being discussed here. But the ministry of reconciliation is not first defined by natural support activity, useful as those activities may be. It is defined by a message committed to us. So we should honor helps and administration in their place without confusing them with the core meaning of ministry in 2 Corinthians 5.

## The Value of the Kingdom

This spiritual assignment is highest priority as a matter of value.

Matthew 13 gives two parables that make the point. In verse 44 the kingdom of heaven is like treasure hidden in a field, which a man found and hid, and for joy over it he goes and sells all that he has and buys that field. Then in verses 45-46 the kingdom is like a merchant seeking beautiful pearls, who, when he had found one pearl of great price, went and sold all that he had and bought it.

The point is that the kingdom, the reign of God in this redemptive age, is of such worth that a man can rightly order his whole life around it. We give our lives for that mission. Even what we do in the natural is to be brought into alignment with that value.

That alignment is not abstract. It touches money, time, attention, habits, and commitments. It shows up in consistent giving, not merely emotional gestures. It shows up in commitment before occasional generosity, because commitment proves what we truly value. Then generosity and sacrifice can build on that foundation. The exact proportions and circumstances differ, but the point stands: the kingdom claims practical loyalty.

That is why the note spends time on the issue of giving and sacrifice. The point is not to create drama around money but to show that value becomes visible in practice. Commitment comes first. Then generosity. Then, at times, sacrifice. A person may talk greatly about kingdom priority, but actual habits of support, labor, and release of resources are what reveal whether that priority is real. The kingdom is treasure and pearl of great price, and life should be ordered accordingly.

That is why the believer's use of resources, time, labor, and giving must be viewed through kingdom value. The detailed applications will vary, but the principle is clear: commitment to the kingdom is not sentimental. It is practical. It affects what we keep, what we release, how we labor, and how we prioritize.

So when we say spiritual assignment is highest priority, we do not necessarily mean every believer will spend the most hours in a week on overt ministry actions compared with all natural labor. Many believers will still spend much of life in natural work. But as a matter of value, the kingdom and the work of ministry stand first. Natural assignment must not displace that priority.

## How Spiritual Assignment Begins

People ask, what is my spiritual assignment? How do I start?

The first thing to understand is that we are generally called into God's purpose, including our spiritual assignment, by the salvific message. Second Thessalonians 2:13-14 says that God chose for salvation through sanctification of the Spirit and belief of the truth, and then says, whereunto he called you by our gospel. So when the gospel is preached and believed, that is how a person is called into what God has planned.

That means the message of salvation is already a call into God's purpose, including ministry. Even if the one preaching does not yet explain all of that, the call of the gospel has brought the believer into the sphere of God's purpose.

Many people who preach salvation do not consciously realize that they are also summoning hearers into God's broader purpose. They may think in narrower terms: be saved, stay morally upright, and make heaven. But the gospel itself calls men into more than that because the one who is reconciled has also been brought into sonship and ministry.

So the first call is broader than many assume. The man who hears and believes the gospel has not simply been given a private rescue. He has been brought into God's purpose. He has entered new creation, sonship, inheritance, and ministry, whether or not he yet understands the vocabulary for all of it.

Then there is a second thing. We are specifically called into our spiritual assignment by the discipleship message. Ephesians 4 says the saints are equipped for the work of ministry. That equipping, that bringing into readiness, belongs to discipleship. So a person may be called to salvation by the gospel and yet not be specifically called into conscious participation in ministry until the discipleship message reaches him.

In other words, salvation brings you into the purpose, but discipleship intentionally points you into the work of ministry within that purpose.

This distinction matters because it explains why some believers are truly born again and yet remain underdeveloped in their understanding of calling. They have responded to the salvific message but have not yet been thoroughly formed by the discipleship message. When the discipleship message comes, it does not create a brand new purpose that was absent before. It clarifies and activates what salvation had already brought them into.

That is why the note can say someone may be a servant of the Lord and yet not know much of his will. The answer is not to deny the reality of that person's salvation. The answer is to see the need for discipleship, establishment, and training. Once the truth of God's purpose and ministry becomes known, the believer should respond to it and not remain content with vagueness.

That helps explain a real pastoral situation. A person can be genuinely saved and yet not know much of God's will, purpose, or spiritual assignment. But when the truth of discipleship comes, that person is now being called knowingly into preparation for ministry. At that point, the right response is not delay or endless waiting. The right response is to receive the truth and move with it.

So we can say it simply. Number one: we are generally called into God's purpose by the gospel of salvation. Number two: we are specifically called into our spiritual assignment by the message of discipleship.

## Commit to the Local Church

The next phase is commitment. It is one thing to be called; it is another thing to commit.

We are to commit to the establishing local church that calls us by the discipleship message and prepares us for effectiveness in ministry. Jesus sent his apostles to make disciples, not merely converts. Therefore the believer is not meant only to hear the gospel, get saved, and remain vague. The believer is meant to be formed, trained, perfected, and established in a local church context.

That commitment is not an optional extra for unusually serious Christians. It belongs to the way ministry matures. If Ephesians 4 says the saints are perfected for the work of ministry, then there must be an environment where that perfectioning happens. That environment is ordinary church life shaped by the truth.

This is also why the local church should not be treated as a loose accessory. The pattern of the word assumes that believers will be established, formed, corrected, and trained in actual church life. The point is not merely attendance but commitment to a discipling environment where spiritual assignment becomes effective.

That means if a church is truly calling you by the discipleship message into God's purpose, including spiritual assignment, that is not a small thing. Commitment there is part of the ordinary way God's word directs us.

This is not about blind loyalty or ignoring abuse, exploitation, or disorder. If there are serious issues, that must be faced honestly. But the principle remains: the church that calls you into discipleship and ministry is where commitment becomes part of your obedience.

You do not keep saying, I am waiting for God to lead me, when the truth of God's word has already become clear on the matter. The word itself leads. The ordinary order of discipleship is already part of God's leading.

## Start From Where You Are

Then you begin from where you are. First Corinthians 7:17 remains an important principle: as the Lord has distributed to each one, as God has called each one, so let him walk.

Start evangelism and discipleship from where you are. Do not wait for a dramatic title, a spectacular platform, or a special identity before serving the message. If God has allotted something larger later, he knows how to orchestrate your life into it. Your responsibility is not to force an image of ministry. Your responsibility is to be faithful where you are.

That includes realism about character, readiness, and context. Many people want to jump into pioneering roles or highly exposed responsibilities without the formation to sustain them. But if God is truly calling a person into a demanding phase of ministry, the orchestration of life, character, doctrine, and preparation will matter. It is not wisdom to go against the truth of scripture under the claim of zeal.

Sometimes people feel pressure because they see visible examples around them and assume they must reproduce the same pattern immediately. But spiritual assignment is not helped by imitation that outruns preparation. God knows how to lead a person into more responsibility when that is truly his portion. Until then, faithful service where you are is not a lesser life. It is the ordinary way God forms useful servants.

That is especially important when people imagine that only pioneering or highly public ministry counts as serious calling. Scripture does not teach that. There are things a person may not yet be built for, and wisdom is to serve truthfully where you are rather than force yourself into a role that your character, doctrine, or circumstances cannot sustain. Many errors in ministry come from pressure, vanity, or restless comparison more than from genuine obedience.

There is nothing weak about serving faithfully within an existing system, learning, submitting, growing, and carrying out ministry in a stable way. In many cases that is exactly how God's wisdom works. Start where you are. Serve the message where you are. Grow where you are.

## Unfolding Into Further Ministry

From there, spiritual assignment unfolds naturally by divine arrangement and orchestration into more phases of ministry.

This is important because not every transition in ministry comes by a dramatic private revelation. Some things unfold through the visible order of church life. Character becomes evident. Soundness becomes evident. Faithfulness becomes evident. Capacity becomes evident. Then more responsibility is entrusted.

That is why Acts 6 and 1 Timothy 3 are so useful. They show that some developments in ministry are ordinary, orderly, and observable. People are recognized, tested, and entrusted. The church can see whether a person is handling truth, responsibility, and people rightly. That is part of divine orchestration too. Not all leading comes as an inner impression detached from communal discernment.

Acts 6 and 1 Timothy 3 help us see this ordinary order. People begin as members being taught. Then they become active workers, serving both within the church and in outward ministry. Then they may unfold into forms of assisting leadership and later fuller oversight as character, doctrine, ability, and grace become evident.

This means you do not need some artificial sense of greatness before you can be useful. The ordinary order of the church is itself part of God's arrangement. Good church life helps a believer discover measure, receive correction, grow in character, and become effective in leading others.

You can describe it simply. As a member you are learning. As a worker you are actively serving both inside the church and in outward ministry. In assisting leadership you begin to bear responsibility for others more intentionally. Further roles may come after that. The labels may vary across church settings, but the principle of orderly unfolding remains.

And within that unfolding the believer should keep pursuing ministry as hard as possible in the present phase. Start where you are and go as far as faithfulness in that phase allows. Let the system of the church help you carry your measure as a part. If it is a healthy church, it will not only give opportunities; it will also give correction. Both are gifts.

If correction comes, that too is part of God's will. The purpose of correction is not to suppress ministry but to make ministry sound. Refusing truth does not help anyone's calling. Receiving truth helps you unfold rightly.

So there is an ordinary pattern: start where you are, serve as a member, become active as a worker, grow in responsibility, and let ministry unfold by God's arrangement rather than personal pressure.

## Be Discerning for Specific Assignments

Within that ordinary unfolding, always be discerning of the leading of the Spirit in the local church for specific assignments.

That means we avoid two errors. One error is to make everything fixed, mechanical, and merely administrative. The other error is to make everything mystical, detached from the structure and wisdom already provided in scripture. The Spirit leads in a real way, but he leads within the truth and often within the life of the church.

Paul is a helpful example here. There were extraordinary features in the orchestration of his life, but there were also ordinary moments of ministry, learning, labor, and communal recognition. In addition, there were particular leadings for journeys, movements, and assignments. So the believer should remain discerning without becoming unstable. Be open to specific assignments, but do not use the language of leading to escape the ordinary order of discipleship and service.

There is an ordinary structure, but there are also specific leadings. We see that in the life of Paul. There were dimensions of his calling that involved unusual orchestration, and there were also moments where the Spirit guided specific decisions and movements. So we should always remain sensitive.

This does not mean claiming spiritual leading for things contrary to scripture. The Spirit of God does not lead against the truth of God. But within the order of the church and the mission of the gospel, there can be genuine direction regarding tasks, places, timings, and responsibilities.

So righteous ambition is not a problem. Ambition itself is not the enemy. The issue is whether ambition is submitted to truth, love, and divine order. Let ambition be used to pursue the salvation of men, their establishment in the faith, and their fulfillment of God's purpose. Do not channel ambition into self-importance, pressure, or disorder. Let it be sanctified and directed.

## Faithfulness in Every Phase

The final and most important matter is faithfulness.

Whatever a person's measure, stage, or visibility, the decisive question remains whether he is faithful in what has been entrusted to him. Faithfulness matters more than appearance. It matters more than title. It matters more than ambition without steadiness.

In fact, this may be the most searching question in the whole teaching. A person may talk much about calling, grace, and assignment, but if he is not faithful in what is already clear, those words remain cheap. Stewardship is measured by faithfulness.

First Corinthians 4:1-2 says, let a man so account of us as servants of Christ and stewards of the mysteries of God. Moreover it is required in stewards that a man be found faithful. Spiritual assignment is stewardship, and the requirement for stewardship is faithfulness.

Faithfulness can be described as conviction producing commitment producing consistency. When you are convinced of God's purpose, that conviction should lead to commitment. Then commitment should lead to consistency.

That sequence is worth stressing. Conviction alone is not yet faithfulness. Many people agree that something is true. Commitment begins when a person deliberately orders himself around that truth. Consistency is what shows that the commitment was real. So faithfulness is not an emotional moment. It is a sustained response to truth.

That is why actual commitments matter. If you say you believe in church life, ministry, evangelism, discipleship, and spiritual growth, then there should be a pattern in your life that reflects those convictions. Otherwise conviction remains admiration. Commitment makes it concrete, and consistency keeps it concrete over time.

That means you do not merely admire the truth of spiritual assignment. You commit to it. You order your life around it. You create actual patterns of obedience around it. You become available. You stay with the work. You serve over time.

This is how the Spirit's work in us becomes concrete in our lives. If there were no commitments, many people would never become consistent. But once conviction leads to real commitments, consistency becomes possible. In that way faithfulness is not vague. It becomes visible in sustained participation.

We must therefore participate faithfully in God's orchestration. God orders our steps; we are responsible for how we respond. We cannot control his orchestration, but we can respond on the side of truth.

That is why the believer should avoid resisting the truth while talking about calling. God may orchestrate many things in life, but a man still has to participate faithfully. He still has to submit to truth, embrace formation, receive correction, and do the work that is presently in front of him.

This is where discipline and faithfulness meet. The Spirit of God does not make faithfulness unnecessary; he empowers it. There is something in the new man that can commit to truth and remain constant in it. So the believer should not think of faithfulness as merely self-generated rigor. It is also an expression of the Spirit's work in a convinced heart.

So we have a spiritual assignment in God's purpose, and that assignment is the work of ministry. It is to serve the message of reconciliation. It is to preach the gospel for salvation. It is to call men into the church for discipleship. It is to help train and perfect believers so they too can fulfill God's purpose in their own measure.

That is the ordinary line of ministry in the note. Men are preached into salvation. Then they are preached into the church for discipleship. Then, over time, they are perfected to fulfill God's purpose and carry their own measure in ministry. That progression may take time, but it is the shape of the work.

So the practical conclusion is not mysterious. Recognize that you have a spiritual assignment. Understand that it is the work of ministry. Serve the gospel unto salvation. Serve the truth unto discipleship. Commit to the local church where the discipleship message is calling you into effectiveness. Start where you are. Let ministry unfold by God's arrangement. Remain discerning for specific assignments. And above all, be faithful in every phase.

That is the line of argument through the teaching. God's purpose is sonship. Sonship means child and heir. Heirship means inheritance and assignment. That inheritance includes spiritual assignment. Spiritual assignment is ministry. Ministry is the service of the message of reconciliation and discipleship. Every believer is a part with a measure and grace for that measure. Therefore the right response is to commit, serve, grow, and remain faithful until your own measure is effectively supplying what Christ intends in his body.

That is how spiritual assignment belongs to the newness of life and not merely to religious activity.

That means, at the simplest level, you can begin now in three directions. Preach the gospel unto salvation. Preach and call men into the local church for discipleship. Then, as you grow, participate in training others within the church toward their own fulfillment of God's purpose.

You are a part. You have a measure. You have grace for that measure. Therefore recognize your spiritual assignment, commit yourself to God's purpose, and be consistent in it by the Spirit of God. That is how the newness of life expresses itself in ministry.
