# Natural Assignment in the Newness of Life

## Why Are We Here?

We're looking at natural assignment in God's purpose. One of the most important questions a human being must answer is, why are we here? But once we have answered that there is God, the better way to ask that question is, what is God's purpose? If there is a self-existent Creator, then why we are here is determined by him. The only way to know purpose is for the one who has it to reveal it.

That is why we go to Ephesians 1. God tells us what his purpose is. Ephesians 1:9 says he has made known to us the mystery of his will according to his good pleasure which he purposed in himself. So if we want to know God's purpose, we have to know his will, what he wants, and his good pleasure, what he likes.

## God's Purpose Is Sonship

Ephesians 1:5 tells us what that will is: having predestined us to adoption as sons by Jesus Christ to himself, according to the good pleasure of his will. If he predestined us to that because of the good pleasure of his will, then what he predestined us to is the good pleasure of his will. Adoption as sons there is sonship. So when we ask, what is God's purpose, the answer is sonship.

Now we have to ask what sonship means. Romans 8 explains it.

## Children Of God And Heirs Of God

Romans 8:15 says we did not receive the spirit of bondage again to fear, but the Spirit of adoption, the Spirit of sonship, whereby we cry, Abba, Father. That means the moment a man believes the gospel, he has entered into God's purpose because he has received the very Spirit of that purpose. The moment you believe, you enter into God's purpose. Knowing it is another matter. Fulfilling it is another matter. But at the instance of faith in Christ, you have entered into it.

That is why knowing matters. Like Luke 12 says, the servant that knew his master's will and did not do according to it is accountable in one way, and the one that did not know is still accountable in another way. So it is one thing to be in God's purpose because you have believed. It is another thing to know that purpose. It is another thing to fulfill it.

Romans 8:16 says the Spirit bears witness with our spirit. That means God's Spirit and our spirit now share the same testimony. First Corinthians 6:17 says he that is joined to the Lord is one spirit with him. Titus 3 calls it the washing of regeneration and renewing of the Holy Ghost. Ephesians 4:24 says the new man is created after God. So salvation is not merely a change of status. Salvation is a change of nature. There is a real regeneration that happens.

And what is the testimony of the Spirit with our spirit? Romans 8:16-17 says that we are children of God, and if children, then heirs, heirs of God and joint-heirs with Christ. So sonship has these two major realities in view: we are children of God, and we are heirs of God.

## Fellowship With God

What does it mean that we are children of God? It means we have been reproduced by him. We have his nature. The first implication of that is fellowship. We have received the Spirit whereby we cry, Abba, Father. So in God's purpose we fellowship with God, we walk in his nature and character, and we fellowship with one another as members of his family.

At its core, God's purpose is relational. He wants a family. He wants interaction with us. He wants friendship with us. In John 15 the Lord Jesus says, greater love has no man than this, than to lay down his life for his friends. Then he says, no longer do I call you servants, because a servant does not know what his master does, but I have called you friends, because all things I heard from my Father I have made known to you. Friendship is seen in revelation. He reveals things to his friends.

That is worth noting carefully. There is a difference between basic observable information and revelatory information. There are things you can say to many people because they are merely public facts about your life. But there are other things tied to your aims, your burdens, your inward direction, your next steps, your vulnerabilities. Scripture associates that deeper disclosure with friendship. The point here is not to turn the teaching into advice on privacy, but to notice the nature of God's purpose: he does not only want servants doing tasks. He wants friends who know what he is doing.

And in John 16:26-27, when Jesus says the Father himself loves you, the word there is `phileo`. The Father is friendly toward you. So the first thing we must recognize in God's purpose is fellowship with God, friendship with God.

The fact that we are children means we should have familiarity and openness with God. We cry, Abba, Father. There is a vulnerability a child has before a father. At the same time, you must not turn that into self-justification. Don't relate with an idol you made in your own image. Relate with the God of truth. He can console you and still correct you. So fellowship with God includes truthful dealings with God, not using prayer to merely hear what you already wanted to hear.

And that is important. Some people talk to God only in a way that allows them to remain justified in themselves. That is not fellowship with the God of truth. If you really go to him, he may first tell you, do not be anxious. Then he may tell you where you have been foolish, where you have been dishonest with yourself, where you need to repent, where you already know what to do and are refusing to do it. So yes, sonship includes comfort, but it also includes truth. It includes correction. It includes familiarity without falsehood.

## Heirs And Inheritance

But sonship is not only that we are children. If children, then heirs. So if God says he made us for sonship, he did not only make us to be his children. He made us also to be his heirs.

The idea is simple. If a man has children, and one of those children is also his heir, then what belongs to the father is assigned to that child, whether the whole or a portion. In God's case, the answer is both. Jointly, believers are heirs of all that belongs to God in creation. Individually, God gives a portion, an allotment, an assignment.

So if God says he made us for sonship, he is saying more than, I want you to be in my family. He is also saying, I want you to participate in what is mine. I want you to bear responsibility under me. I want you to handle portions under my lordship. That is why the language of inheritance becomes important for natural assignment.

There are three major ways inheritance is used in Scripture here. First, God gives us himself by giving us his Spirit. That is inheritance. Second, God gives us creation. Scripture can speak of the meek inheriting the earth, or Abraham being heir of the world. Third, God gives individual portions or allotments in that created world. That is where natural assignment comes in.

## Natural Assignment Is Part Of God's Purpose

There are two broad assignments we can speak of: spiritual assignment and natural assignment. Spiritual assignment refers to things like the mission of the church, preaching the gospel, discipleship, the work of ministry. Natural assignment refers to what we do in the natural: academics, career, business, craftsmanship, administration, research, social causes, whatever work we are doing in ordinary life.

The point of this teaching is that natural assignment is not outside God's purpose. It is part of God's purpose. The clearest text for that is Colossians 3. Another strong text is 1 Corinthians 7. Ephesians 6 says the same thing plainly.

This matters because some believers quietly imagine that only direct ministry activity counts before God, and everything else is just what they are doing to survive. But the apostles do not speak that way. They do not leave natural work in a morally neutral or spiritually empty zone. They place it under the lordship of Christ and call it part of inheritance, part of the will of God, and part of our service to the Lord.

If you miss that, you can spend huge portions of your life thinking you are merely enduring something secular until you get to what really matters. But that is not how the apostles frame life. They frame life so that natural assignment is not the highest thing, yet it is still a serious thing before God.

## Colossians 3: Serving The Lord Christ

Colossians 3:22 addresses bondservants and their masters according to the flesh. So Paul is speaking about natural work, earthly labor, employer-employee relations, what you do in this world. He tells them not to work with eye-service as men-pleasers, but in sincerity of heart, fearing God, and whatever they do, to do it heartily as to the Lord and not to men.

The important thing is that he is not merely saying, act as though it is unto the Lord. He goes further and says, knowing that from the Lord you will receive the reward of the inheritance, for you serve the Lord Christ. So it is actually unto the Lord. The natural work is truly rendered to Christ. That is why he can call its recompense the reward of the inheritance.

In other words, that portion of work you are doing is part of your allotment before God. Whether you are an employee, an employer, a worker under somebody, or somebody over others, there is a purpose mindset you must have: my service on this earth is unto the Lord Christ.

This should be drilled into the believer. There is a self-serving mindset in work, where a person says, I will be excellent because excellence will speak for me. That can produce some natural results, and in a secondary sense it is fine. But the Christian has a deeper mindset. I am faithful because my reward is from the Lord. I am serving somebody higher than the visible human structure. I am not measuring my integrity by the mood, maturity, or fairness of the human authority over me.

And because your reward is from the Lord, there is also a seriousness to injustice and wrong. Colossians says the one who does wrong will be repaid for the wrong he has done, and there is no partiality. So if you are the worker and you are negligent, Christ sees it. If you are the master and you are exploitative, Christ sees it. If you create toxic conditions, withhold what is fair, abuse authority, or refuse justice in the structures under you, Christ sees it.

This is why unfaithfulness in natural work is not just a matter between you and your boss. If you serve only according to the salary a man pays you, then you are not serving the Lord Christ in that thing. You have put a cap on yourself. There is a way a man may appear adequate before men and still be unfaithful before God because he did not serve according to the measure of intelligence, strength, capacity, and opportunity that God gave him.

God is judging faithfulness, not mere appearances. Another person may seem to have done less than you externally and yet has served maximally with what was given to him. The issue is faithfulness with your actual resources, knowing that you serve the Lord Christ.

That is why natural assignment is not a light matter. If someone wastes his strength, gifts, or intelligence in the natural, heaven does not call that trivial. It goes on God's record. And if someone serves faithfully in what looks ordinary, that too is before God. This is why the believer should not think in terms of bare compliance. Serve with the consciousness that you will answer to Christ for how you worked.

Then Colossians 3 continues, he who does wrong will be repaid for the wrong he has done, and there is no partiality. In chapter 4:1 he addresses masters too: give your bondservants what is just and fair, knowing that you also have a Master in heaven. So no one is exempt from the lordship of Christ in natural assignment. The boss is under Christ. The worker is under Christ. The employer is under Christ. The employee is under Christ.

And by natural assignment we really mean everything you do in ordinary human life that is not the direct mission-work of the church. Your academics, your business, your trade, your professional work, your institutional work, your technical labor. Whatever you are doing there, you are serving the Lord Christ.

This is why Colossians 3 is not merely a character sermon. Character is included, yes, but Paul is also teaching productivity. Not with eye-service means your output must not depend on whether men are watching. Since Christ is the one you serve, the believer is supposed to be productive with the consciousness that the Lord always sees.

So Paul is not only saying, be morally nice at work. He is saying, be maximally faithful in work. You are not as productive as when men are watching. You are as productive as one who knows, I am serving the Lord. That mindset changes diligence, excellence, consistency, and what you count as acceptable output.

That is why the believer should refuse the mentality that says, once nobody is checking, I can reduce the standard. No. The standard is shaped by the One you serve. Productivity before God is part of Christian obedience in the natural.

## Ephesians 6: Doing The Will Of God In Natural Work

Ephesians 6 says the same thing from another angle. Bondservants are told to obey their masters according to the flesh with sincerity of heart, as to Christ, not with eye-service as men-pleasers, but as bondservants of Christ, doing the will of God from the heart, with good will doing service as to the Lord and not to men.

Notice the language. In natural work, Paul says you are a bondservant of Christ and you are doing the will of God. So the apostles do not leave room for the idea that natural assignment is spiritually irrelevant. It is part and parcel of God's purpose.

That does not reduce the value of spiritual assignment. Spiritual assignment remains weightier in value. In fact, natural assignment should often serve spiritual assignment. The man who found the treasure sold all to obtain it. There are sacrifices required for the mission of Christ and the life of discipleship. But none of that cancels the truth that your natural assignment also belongs inside God's purpose.

So there should be no confusion here. To say natural assignment is part of God's purpose is not to say it is equal in value to the mission of the gospel. It is to say it is included under Christ's lordship and evaluated by him. Spiritual assignment is priority in value, and natural assignment should often serve it. But if most of your waking hours are going into natural work, you cannot treat that whole region of life as outside divine purpose. That would be to leave most of your actual life outside your theology.

And practically, in a week of one hundred and sixty-eight hours, a great deal of time will be spent in natural assignment. Often far more than believers realize. So if you imagine that those hours are spiritually empty, you are going to live with a chronic sense that most of your life is not really part of what God is doing. The apostles do not allow that way of thinking.

Most of your hours in a week will be spent in natural assignment. So do not imagine you are merely wasting time there. You were serving the Lord there. The question is whether you were faithful in it.

Ephesians 6 also addresses masters and tells them to stop threatening, knowing that their own Master is in heaven and there is no partiality with him. So if you are the boss, you are serving Christ in how you lead, pay, structure work, and treat people. If you are the worker, you are serving Christ in your character and productivity.

## What The Bible Teaches About Natural Assignment

Now, if natural assignment is really part of God's purpose, what exactly does Scripture teach about how to recognize and handle it? First Corinthians 7 gives a number of foundational ideas.

### Start From Where You Are

First Corinthians 7:17 says, as God has distributed to each one, as the Lord has called each one, so let him walk. That word translated distributed carries the idea of portioning out, dividing, allotting. The point is that what has been parted to you is what you are called to walk in from there.

So one of the first things to understand about natural assignment is this: start from where you are. Look at what has been apportioned to you already. What context are you in? What opportunities, responsibilities, capacities, relationships, skills, and conditions are presently yours? That is where your walk starts.

Paul uses examples from natural status. Were you called circumcised or uncircumcised? Remain there. Then he says, let each one remain in the same calling in which he was called. The point is not fatalism. The point is starting point. God's walk with you in purpose begins from where he met you.

So wherever you find yourself when you come to Christ, begin from there. That does not mean every broken structure in the world is God's ideal design. Of course not. It simply means God already knows where you are and has planned your walk with him from there.

That guards us from two errors. One error is resentment about the starting point, as though God cannot work there. The other error is treating the starting point as final, as though change is disobedience. Scripture gives neither of those. Start where you are. Walk from there with God.

### Take Advantage Of Opportunities That Come To You

Paul then says, were you called while a slave? Do not be concerned about it. But if you can be made free, rather use it. That tells us the second principle. Start from where you are, but take advantage of opportunities that come to you.

Freedom is better than slavery. Improvement is real. So Scripture is not teaching static existence. It is saying: do not despise your starting point, but when a genuine opening for something better comes, use it.

That is an important balance. The Bible is not saying every present condition is ideal. It is saying God's purpose can begin in any present condition. Start there. Then when lawful and wise opportunity appears, step into it. So there is neither despair about where you are nor passivity about where you could go.

And in Paul's actual example, his very wording shows the direction of wisdom. If you can be made free, rather use it. So the believer should not confuse contentment with refusal of improvement. You can be content in God and still take advantage of a better opening when it comes.

That applies broadly. If opportunities come to become freer, more skilled, more resourced, more effective, better positioned, use them. Your starting point is not your prison. It is your point of departure.

And there is no such thing as having nothing to do from where you are. People often are not doing what they can do because they are waiting for some ideal condition. But a person gains by engaging where he is. Discipline is gained. skill is gained. relationships are learned. habits are formed. capacity is increased. Even what looks small is not a waste when it is done as service to the Lord.

Even in very difficult conditions, there is still something to do. There is still a way to serve faithfully from there. And often the person who starts from where he is is already changing. He is gaining discipline, relating better with people, learning how to show up, learning how to keep commitments, learning practical skill, learning endurance. There is progress taking place before obvious promotion ever comes.

Joseph is a clear biblical example. He served faithfully first as a slave and then in prison. Those were not ideal conditions. Yet the blessing of the Lord upon him showed itself in excellent service. He did not wait for ideal freedom before becoming faithful. And because he saw his life before God, he would not take Potiphar's wife's offer. He said it would be sin against God. That tells you his natural work was already being rendered as service to God.

That is one of the clearest pictures of what Paul is teaching later in Colossians and Ephesians. Joseph was in unjust conditions and still did not become careless, cynical, or morally loose. He remained productive and Godward in his work. So when Paul says bondservants are serving the Lord Christ, Scripture already gave us narrative examples of what that looks like.

This is also why it is wrong to think that difficult present conditions make faithfulness meaningless. The slave in Paul's example still serves Christ there. The prisoner Joseph still serves God there. The issue is not whether the condition is ideal. The issue is whether, inside that condition, you know whose servant you are.

### Use The World Without Misusing It

First Corinthians 7 later speaks of those who use this world as not misusing it. That is another major principle. There are people whose natural assignment will not merely be receiving instructions under others. They will use the world more directly: producing, building, innovating, trading, organizing, applying the resources of creation.

Scripture gives genuine room for that. Use the world. Harness the possibilities in creation. Work with matter, systems, markets, institutions, technologies, structures. But do not misuse the world while using it.

That means do not engage in destructive or corrupt forms of work. Do not make your livelihood by harming people, polluting, deceiving, selling what destroys, or building systems that are unjust. There are many legitimate ways to use the world. So do not choose misuse.

There is also liberty here. In some matters God gives you wide room for choice. You still stay within righteousness, but there is real room for human initiative. Natural assignment is one of those areas where you often must choose, act, and build responsibly rather than pretending every ordinary decision must come by extraordinary revelation.

That is why the believer should not become fatalistic. There are places where you really do have the liberty of your will under God's moral boundaries. You can decide what line of work to pursue, what tool to learn, what legitimate value to create, what need to solve. The instruction is not, wait until heaven tells you every tiny step. The instruction is, use the world and do not misuse it.

That same liberty should also remove the habit of blaming God for unwise ordinary choices. There are decisions where Scripture has already given you moral boundaries and then left real room for you to choose. Choose responsibly. If you misuse that liberty, do not pretend the problem was lack of revelation. Often the problem is lack of wisdom.

### Be Subject To The Leading Of The Spirit

At the same time, liberty does not mean independence from God. Always stay subject to the leading of the Spirit. The leading of the Spirit is not the primary mechanism by which every ordinary natural-work decision is made, but the believer must remain discerning.

God can direct in natural matters. We see that in various biblical examples. So maintain a devotional life. Pray. Walk with God. Be open. As you actively take initiative in life, stay sensitive in case the Lord wants to redirect, restrain, emphasize, or open something.

So the pattern is not passivity. It is active responsibility with spiritual discernment.

### Be Maximally Productive

The fifth principle is this: be maximally productive. This comes through strongly in both Colossians 3 and Ephesians 6. If you are doing your work unto the Lord, then laziness, half-heartedness, and selective seriousness cannot be your posture.

You are not simply to be busy. You are to be productive. There is a difference. God's concern is not with motion only but with faithful output according to what has been entrusted to you. So your natural assignment should be approached with the intention to serve maximally with all that God has put at your disposal.

That is why some people are tired without being effective. They are active, but they are blunt. They are sincere, but they are scattered. Scripture is pressing us toward faithful productivity, not mere exhaustion.

The labor of fools can weary them precisely because they do not know how to reach the end effectively. So Christian diligence must include wisdom, method, and sharpened capacity.

## Further Exhortations On Natural Assignment

Beyond those foundational principles, there are several practical exhortations Scripture supports.

### Find One Thing To Do

The first is: find one thing to do. You may have hobbies and peripheral interests, but you cannot spread your main energy over everything and expect excellence. Human life is limited in time and strength. So know the main thing you are doing.

If you are a student, your academics may be that primary thing for that phase. In another phase it may be your trade, your research, your business, your profession, your craft. But know your one major direction.

Pour your energy there. Otherwise you can be busy on many fronts and not effective on any front. Being single-minded in this sense is part of wisdom. The point is not that nothing else may exist around your life. The point is that one major direction must gather the weight of your effort.

In that sense, saying find one thing to do is not condemning every side interest. It is saying that your life should not be dissipated. There should be a central line of labor you can point to and say, this is what I am doing.

### Be The Best At It

Second, be the best at it. Ecclesiastes 10:10 says if the axe is dull and one does not sharpen the edge, then he must use more strength, but wisdom brings success. The lesson is clear: sharpen your tool. Sharpen your skill. Master your craft.

Ecclesiastes 10:15 says the labor of fools wearies them, for they do not know how to go into the city. A man may be very diligent and still foolish in method. He may be working hard and yet not effectively because he is blunt everywhere, spread thin, and excellent nowhere.

So find one thing, and become good at it. If you are doing it unto the Lord, you should not be satisfied with mediocrity. Faithfulness to the Lord includes serious pursuit of mastery.

Whatever is done for the Lord should be done at its best. So sharpen the edge. Learn better methods. Improve the tool. Improve the system. Improve your execution. The believer should not be comfortable remaining dull in his work while claiming sincerity of heart.

There is a difference between humility and low standards. It is not pride to want your work to be well done. If you serve Christ in it, then excellence is part of consecration.

### Count The Cost And Decide Wisely

Another exhortation is to count the cost and decide wisely. Luke 14 uses natural examples while teaching discipleship: a man intending to build a tower sits down first and counts the cost; a king going to war considers whether he can meet the other. The principle applies in natural assignment too.

Do not dabble mindlessly. Decide intelligently. Think through your capacity and your time. Those are two major things within your stewardship. You do not control every event life will throw at you, but you can think carefully about your present capacity and the use of your time.

When you choose a course, profession, trade, or long-term direction, count the cost. What will this demand? What habits will it require? What sacrifices will it involve? How will you remain faithful to spiritual assignment while carrying this natural one? How do you begin from where you are and still move toward finishing well?

That means you do not choose casually. If a man says he wants a particular vocation, let him think through the use of time, the developmental path, the cost of competence, the likely demands on schedule, and the way it will interact with discipleship. There are callings that take enormous hours and energy. That may still be legitimate, but you should enter it with open eyes and planned faithfulness, not vague sentiment.

The Lord's examples in Luke 14 are natural examples, and that itself is instructive. He assumes intelligent planning in ordinary human life. So it is not lack of faith to sit down first and think. It is wisdom. It is part of obedience in natural assignment.

The point is not that you will predict everything. Life has many curve balls. The point is that you should not be careless. Have a definable direction. Be able to say where you are going, even if details evolve.

You may begin one way and later refine, adjust, or even redirect in part. That is normal. But when you begin, begin intelligently. Sit down first. Count the cost. Choose one thing you can do well, finish well, and succeed at under God.

### Master The Exchange Of Value

Another exhortation, especially in our age, is to master the exchange of value. In a world of specialization and division of labor, it is not enough to create value privately. You must know how value is exchanged.

That means you must learn how to present, communicate, package, price, distribute, and market what you do. This is not worldliness. It is ordinary wisdom about the kind of economy we live in. You may be skilled and still ineffective if you do not know how that skill reaches those who need it.

The modern world is arranged around specialization. Most people no longer create every value they need by themselves. People exchange value. So if you create value but do not understand exchange, you remain unnecessarily weak. Learn how your work reaches people. Learn how to communicate worth. Learn how to structure the exchange honestly and intelligently.

This means learning how to package, brand, present, and distribute what you do in truthful and useful ways. It is still part of stewardship. You are not being carnal because you learned how to communicate value properly. You are learning how life in this age actually works.

So if you choose one thing and sharpen it, also learn how that value is exchanged in the real world.

### Use The Cosmos Maximally

Another exhortation is to use the cosmos maximally. First Corinthians 7 says those who use this world should do so without misusing it. The world includes the structures and resources of creation: matter, systems, tools, capital, intelligence, social cooperation, and technology.

So leverage what exists. Use tools well. Use machines where machines should be used. Use capital wisely. Work with other people's capacity through fair exchange and wise negotiation. Leverage the intelligence available in your age.

That includes artificial intelligence. There is nothing inherently wrong with using it. It is part of humanity's aggregated intelligence and can be used to think, plan, execute, refine, and accelerate work. The point is to use the cosmos maximally without misusing it, not to romanticize unnecessary inefficiency.

And this can be done in degrees. You can use AI like a simple tool for isolated tasks. You can use it like an assistant to help execute a defined task. You can use it more strategically around short-term goals, long-term goals, planning, synthesis, and refinement. The larger point is that available intelligence in the world should be leveraged wisely. There is no virtue in refusing legitimate help and then calling your inefficiency spirituality.

So use the world maximally. If there are better tools, learn them. If there are systems that make the work more effective, use them. If there are lawful means of multiplying your effort, learn how. The believer should not cling to avoidable inefficiency as though it were piety.

Beyond AI, use capital well. Use machines where machines are appropriate. Use human collaboration fairly. Leverage systems. Leverage distribution. Leverage the available structures of the world. The command is to use the world without misusing it, not to ignore the world while trying to appear holy.

### Be In Faith For Favor And Orchestrations

Finally, be in faith for favor and orchestrations accompanying your work. Natural work is designed to be done naturally. The instruction from creation is that man works in the earth. Yet Scripture is full of God's hand aiding human labor through favor, timing, openings, and orchestration.

So do not become mystical and passive, but also do not become merely naturalistic. Believe God for his help in your work. Believe for favor. Believe for rightly timed meetings, opened doors, preserved steps, and gracious providences that support your labor.

Scripture is full of the supernatural aiding what is still natural work. That does not mean you abandon design, skill, effort, planning, and stewardship. It means you add faith to your labor. You expect the hand of God in timings, relationships, openings, protections, and orchestrations that no mere human scheduling could have produced.

So the posture is this: I work naturally because that is how natural assignment is designed to be done, and I also remain in faith for God's hand upon it. I do not choose between diligence and dependence. I walk in both.

That should not come from greed or desperation. It should be a normal lifestyle of trust. God is your Father. You work naturally, faithfully, diligently, and intelligently, yet you also trust his hand in the affairs of your natural assignment just as in your spiritual assignment.

## Closing Confessions

So the conclusion is straightforward. I am a bondservant of Christ in my natural assignment as much as in my spiritual assignment. My natural assignment is part of God's inheritance to me and part of the will of God for my life. Therefore I must be faithful in it. I must be maximally productive in it. I must serve Christ in it.

That also means I should not spend my working life complaining that I am not really doing what God wants me to do, while neglecting the very thing in which he has placed me to serve him. If I am in a lawful natural assignment and rendering it faithfully to Christ, I am not outside the will of God there. The real question is whether I am serving lazily or faithfully, blindly or knowingly, selfishly or unto the Lord.

So I should take natural assignment seriously. I should enjoy it as part of gospel purpose rather than treat it as dead time. I should be careful not to lose in two places: to perform poorly now because of foolishness, and then still answer to Christ later for the same unfaithfulness. Better to wake up to the seriousness and dignity of labor rendered to the Lord.

And I remain in faith that God's favor and orchestrations accompany my work. My needs are met. My steps are ordered. Events are aligned for good. I fulfill God's purpose in my natural assignment to his glory.

That is the point on natural assignment. The next discussion is spiritual assignment.
