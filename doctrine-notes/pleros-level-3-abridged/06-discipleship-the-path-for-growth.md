# Discipleship - The Path for Growth

## God's Purpose Requires Growth

So we're looking at discipleship, the path for growth. We are studying this on the back of what we understand about God's purpose. We have this clear understanding that the reason that we exist is for God's purpose. God, who is self-existent, created the world, and he made the world for his own purpose. And we have examined what that purpose is. Ephesians chapter 1 and verse 9, as well as verse 5, helped us conclude that God's purpose is sonship.

Now that understanding, that God's purpose is sonship, leads us to something else. You remember that sonship means we are God's children as well as his heirs. So as his children we are meant to walk in his nature and character, and as his heirs we are meant to carry out our natural assignments as well as spiritual assignments.

Now knowing that is fantastic. Imagine you know that you exist just to walk a particular path. You know there is a definite path laid out for you. Maybe it is like a one-thousand-meter path, but it has different twists and turns you are supposed to take. Someone tells you, this is why you exist. You are supposed to go this journey. There is a destination you are meant to get to. When you get to this point you are to take left, not right. When you get to another point you are supposed to do this and that.

Now the person has shown you the path you are meant to follow to fulfill purpose. But if that journey involves carrying certain loads along the way, maybe you are supposed to carry heavy loads in both hands, then your fulfillment of that journey is not just dependent on your knowledge of the path. It is also dependent on your strength, your energy, your endurance, your fitness to bear the burden as you go.

That is precisely what leads us to the study of growth. We are saying that when we know God's purpose for our life, it still necessitates growth. That you know God's purpose, that you know how it is supposed to turn out, that you know the way the journey is, does not excuse you from the need for growth. It is good that you know God's purpose, but you need to grow. Without growth you cannot fulfill God's purpose for your life. So the knowledge of God's purpose is not enough. There is the necessity of growth.

## Why the Church Exists

One of the texts that makes this obvious is Ephesians chapter 4. In Ephesians chapter 4 and verse 11, a text we often quote, Paul tells us the reason for the church. He says Jesus gave the apostles, prophets, evangelists, pastoring teachers. He gave the leaders of the church.

Now what did he give them for? He tells us that he gave the leaders of the church for the perfecting of the saints. Perfecting of the saints simply means that word there, `katartismos`, they are supposed to bring the saints to a particular end point.

What I want us to see is that end point they are meant to bring the saints to. Look at verse 13. He says, till we all come to the unity of the faith. At that point you may think, well, maybe the church is just for knowledge. Then he says, and of the knowledge of the Son of God, and you still think it is only about knowledge. But hold on. The third one says, unto a perfect man. Ephesians 4:13, the perfect man. Perfect man means fully developed man, completely developed man. So he is no longer just talking about knowledge. He is talking about the implication on a man's humanity, the development of the person himself. Then the fourth one says, unto the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ. By the time you see the word stature there, there is really no debate that growth is what is being discussed.

So he is saying that the work of the church, or the work of the leaders of the church, is to ensure our growth. They are to give us knowledge, quite all right, but that knowledge is not enough. It is supposed to turn out to the end that we grow till we come to the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ.

And what makes it even clearer that growth is really what the church is meant to do to us is verse 14 and verse 15. Verse 14 says we should no longer be children tossed to and fro. So God does not want us to remain in a childish state, meaning an undeveloped state. He used that word `nepios`. That word actually means someone that cannot speak. It is rooted in the idea of speech, then negated. So it is not speaking. You know an infant, someone just born, cannot yet speak. And there are believers like that. They cannot yet communicate the truth of God. That means they have not learned the language of God, of his word, of his Spirit.

So if you ask them a question like, what is God's purpose, they cannot speak on it. That is a child, like a child that is not yet fully formed and developed to communicate in the language that pertains to his culture or community. There are believers who are not fully formed to be able to answer on matters that every believer should be able to answer on.

So he says we should no longer be children tossed to and fro by every wind of doctrine, by the trickery of men, in cunning craftiness of deceitful plotting. But I want you to look at verse 15 particularly. He says, but speaking the truth in love, we would grow up into Christ in all things. That is the core work of the church, to ensure our growth into Christ. Speaking the truth in love, we grow up into Christ in all things. Growth is one of the central reasons for the church.

## Why Growth Matters So Much

Why does he want us to grow? That is the point I want to emphasize. When Jesus died and rose again from the dead, why did he give the church to make us grow? The reason is simple. The reason why Jesus died and rose again is not merely to forgive our sins, but to restore us back into God's purpose for our life. We have had to repeat that several times, that the reason for the death and resurrection of Jesus is not just the forgiveness of our sins, but restoration into God's purpose.

There are some people whose understanding of Christianity ends with forgiveness of sins, with redemption from the power of darkness. If you ask them, why did Jesus die for you, they say, so that I will not go to hell. Why are you now going to church? Just to thank him for what he has done, thank him for his blessings on my life. That is a fatal misunderstanding of what Christ has done. And I say fatal because it means the very reason for your life, you are going to miss out on it. It is not a slight mistake.

The reason the Lord died and rose again from the dead is the restoration of humanity back into God's purpose. In fact, I used to say it this way: the forgiveness of sins is not an end in itself. It is a means to an end. Forgiveness of sins, as powerful as it is, as great as it is, that confidence we have that my sins are forgiven, I am saved by the work that Christ has done, it is not an end in itself. Your salvation is not an end in itself. It is a means to an end. Except you understand your salvation properly as restoration into God's purpose, that you were saved back into God's purpose, then you have not understood salvation the way you should.

So the reason Jesus gave the church, the reason Jesus gave the leaders of the church, is to perfect the saints so that they can grow to fulfill God's purpose for their life. That is why growth is so important to our existence. If we are going to fulfill God's purpose, which is the very reason why we exist, then we need growth. Fulfilling God's purpose is not just knowing about God's purpose, knowing the path to take. Fulfilling God's purpose requires strength, endurance, fitness, growth, the capacity to bear the burden as you go on that journey.

You cannot carry out your assignments in God's purpose without spiritual growth. And you cannot say, but I am already doing my natural assignment. I am running my business. I am building my career. The interesting thing is that you might be doing it in the flesh, which means you are already losing your rewards. It is possible that someone is engaged in natural assignment, but is not doing it in the Spirit.

God's word is clear that God cannot be mocked. Whatever a man sows, he will reap. If you sow to the flesh, you will from the flesh reap corruption. So if you are engaging your natural assignment from the flesh, you are already doing it to waste. You can be so productive, so laborious, yet that productivity is not coming from the Spirit. God's word teaches maximum productivity. We are meant to be productive. But activity is not the proof that you are doing God's purpose as he wants. Growth is the guarantee that you do it that way. Because when you are growing spiritually, then you are carrying out your walk as a child of God and your walk as an heir of God the way God desires. Growth is the guarantee that we are carrying out God's will as desired.

## The Great Commission and the Church's Authority

So if growth is this important, then that is what we are now about to examine. How does this growth happen? We know the most important thing about our existence is God's purpose. Then we have resolved another issue: God's purpose can only be fulfilled by growth. If growth is this important, how does it happen?

I have already answered the question in one sense by saying the church was given for that growth. But I want to show us the exact point in history where the Lord gave the church the instruction that would bring about our growth. Ephesians 4 already makes it clear that Jesus gave the leaders of the church and they are meant to perfect the saints, and that leads to growth. But when was that point historically where the Lord spoke to them and gave that instruction? See Matthew chapter 28 and verse 18.

He said, "And Jesus came and spoke to them, saying, All authority has been given to me in heaven and on earth." Let me say something about that statement. That language in verse 18 is related to the church. The earlier uses of that heaven-and-earth authority language in Matthew, especially in Matthew 16 and Matthew 18, are tied to the church's authority to bind and loose and to exercise discipline.

So by the time he now told them in chapter 28, all authority has been given in heaven and on earth, they would know automatically that when he says, go therefore, he is speaking to the authority he gives to his church.

And so by the time he says, go therefore and make disciples, he shows that what governs how the church exercises its authority is this matter of discipleship.

## Discipleship Is Not Mere Imposition

But let me quickly make a clarification here. When he says, go therefore and make disciples, remember that in the KJV it says, go therefore and teach all nations. The KJV does fantastic things many times, but in this instance that wording is not the most helpful, because the word there is not the actual word for teach. It is the word for to make disciples, or to disciple, as a verb. Then later the actual word for teach appears.

And the point is that this idea of making disciples is related to the logical passing on of knowledge. The goal is not that someone says, according to what you people said. The goal is that the person can say, this is what the Bible says, and here is the evidence. If that conviction is not your own, with evidence you have seen yourself and can present independent of your teacher, then it is more like an imposition. You are just being forced to accept it, and that is not God's idea.

That is one of the reasons discipleship has bad PR today. In many places what is called discipleship is not conviction but manipulation, not persuasion but imposition. Very important to recognize that discipleship means conviction happening based on evidence you can independently verify.

Very important to recognize that discipleship means conviction happening based on evidence you can independently verify.

## Teaching Toward Observation and Practice

So Jesus says, go and make disciples, or go and disciple all nations, baptizing them into the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost, and then teaching them. Of course that shows that discipleship requires that they are first saved. But the core of what that discipleship is, is teaching, which is what I was trying to say. The first word was make disciples. The second word now actually uses the word teach. So he shows us that no doubt the primary activity in discipleship is teaching, but discipleship is not just teaching.

In fact, to show you the nature of the teaching that goes on in discipleship, notice what verse 20 says. Verse 20 says, teaching them to do what? To observe, or to keep, all that I have commanded you. That has to do with doing. It has to do with walking. Teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you. At the end of the day, you can teach something without any intention that the person should do it. That is not discipleship. For example, I could face a camera now and start teaching, and I am not teaching toward persuasion. I am just teaching for enlightenment, just to make something clear. But if I am teaching with the intention that I want people to agree and that it will affect their walk, at that point you can already tell that discipleship is at play.

Another way of thinking about discipleship is apprenticeship. An apprentice is not just there to gather knowledge. An apprentice is there to gain expertise. So the knowledge is the starting point, but practice must happen. The person is being taught to do, taught to observe, taught to act, taught to carry out what they are learning. And that is why there is a point where they can graduate. In normal old Nigerian parlance they would say the person has gotten freedom. The person has completed what he is learning.

Now maybe in the faith of Christ there is a point you can say someone has gotten freedom, even though we know that submission, and I am going to come to where submission comes into discipleship, does not come in at the beginning of discipleship. This is something we might not want to admit, but it is the truth.

## Submission Does Not Come First

Submission would happen as a result of the discipleship process. That is something we must recognize. And I will show you. That is why in the church people could come to church who were even unbelievers. Some were unlearned, and we can accommodate them. Their submission over time would happen as they come around, even though what we are doing, notwithstanding, is discipleship. Otherwise, we will make the church look the way the church was not intended to be. It will start looking more cultic than churchly.

Someone can stroll into church. Someone can be around, and he has not gotten his acts together, and the person is just like that. He is not yet committed, and he is still coming to church. The person is in the process of deciding. It is not strange to the church. It is what has always happened. In fact some of the books written, like the book of Hebrews, were written in that light, where there was concern that at a certain point people should know enough to be committed, in that case committed to salvation, to faith in Christ.

So we have to be honest about the face of the church. There is nothing strange about people in church who are not committed. It did not start today. Do not make discipleship difficult for people. Do not make the church difficult for people. If we genuinely want discipleship to happen as Jesus instructed it, it will happen the way it played out in the book of Acts. It will be a journey.

So we said discipleship is the path of growth. That is how growth happens. Many people that could have become strong in the faith have been turned off by wrong ideas of discipleship that are not really discipleship. Very important, we must have the right idea of it.

## Discipleship Respects Freedom of Conscience

So I said today discipleship has bad PR, and there are legitimate fears and concerns people have about discipleship because of the fear that it takes away the freedom of their conscience. That is really the concern. So I want to quickly clarify that based on what we have seen. Discipleship is maturation. But I want to show it in a practical way. Discipleship has a lot to do with the recipient's own will, the recipient's freedom of conscience.

The result of discipleship has a lot more to do with the recipient than with the teacher. The teacher can do his best, but to show you that discipleship cannot force you, it has much more to do with the recipient receiving it than the teacher merely presenting it. Why will you see several times that they say, do not be hearers of the word only but be doers? It is not all on the teacher. They can do their part, and you might still not be a doer. They are not supposed to bend you. At best, what the teacher of God's word can do is provide knowledge and then inspiration for you to stand on that knowledge.

Let me show you that again. Second Corinthians chapter 1, verse 23 and 24. Paul says, "Moreover, I call God as witness against my soul, that to spare you I came no more to Corinth, not that we have dominion over your faith, but are fellow workers for your joy; for by faith you stand." Imagine him saying, we do not have dominion over your faith. It was the model of the apostles in their discipleship to make it clear: all that we can do is help you. We are fellow workers for your joy. We provide the knowledge and the inspiration. You receive that knowledge by your availability. It is on you that you respond to that knowledge by faith when we inspire you to. It still rests on you.

Discipleship does not take away freedom of conscience at all. If you do not allow your conscience to be calm about discipleship so that you can receive growth, you may not even know whether you are growing or whether you are just doing things for people. If you have seen the light of God and you want to learn more, come around and be learning. Over time you would grow into commitment, into submission, into service.

Let me show you that people were around the church for long sometimes without joining the church. See Acts chapter 5. We will see this even more when we get to studying your place in the local church. Acts chapter 5 and verse 12 says, "And through the hands of the apostles, many signs and wonders were done among the people. And they were all with one accord in Solomon's porch. Yet none of the rest dared join them, but the people esteemed them highly. And believers were increasingly added to the Lord, multitudes both of men and women." So you see, certain people were always around, but they did not join.

I want to show you another example, 1 Corinthians chapter 14. The church was that open. If we are going to have biblical discipleship, it must guarantee freedom of conscience. If biblical discipleship is not merely assertive and imposing, it is simply convincing logically. It is analytical. It is logical. Not that I will go there and they will seize my time and I have no control. No. They should spell out their commitments at different phases, and you enter into them one after the other.

First Corinthians 14:23 says, "If the whole church comes together in one place, and all speak with tongues, and there come in those who are uninformed or unbelievers, will they not say you are mad?" Now if those people's coming in was unexpected, it would not come up there. Notice that it was not only unbelievers he mentioned. He mentioned uninformed, meaning weak believers who had not gotten their bearing were still counted. They were not yet the committed people, but they were believers notwithstanding, uninformed, and they were still there. And you are supposed to be aware of that and seek how you can help their conscience on those matters.

Conscience is a very big issue in the Christian faith. First Corinthians chapter 8 warns them not to use their knowledge to hinder those who do not have that same knowledge. Knowledge puffs up, but love edifies. So believers must deliberately bring love into the use of truth. Sharp rebuke has its place, but it is after longsuffering, patient correction, and repeated attempts to restore. Harshness is not normal maturity.

The legitimate fears people have about discipleship because of the bad PR it has today are not supposed to be present in biblical discipleship. Biblical discipleship fully respects freedom of conscience, allowing you to grow into all that you are supposed to do.

## Weak Conscience Must Be Handled Carefully

First Corinthians chapter 8, let me just read verse 9 and 10. "But beware, lest somehow this liberty of yours becomes a stumbling block to those who are weak." Do you know what I find funny about this text? He did not impose on them. He did the same thing in 1 Corinthians 14. He knew what was better, but he did not impose on them. That means he is waiting for their knowledge to catch up. He is not overriding the present state of the conscience of the weak. And he is saying that those who have knowledge will have that disposition.

Do you know there are people that cannot talk in tongues just because of their conscience? Not because they have not understood the explanation, but because something previously said affected them so deeply that even now it takes time for that emotion to wash away. It is not for you to override the person's conscience, but to work on it until it becomes stronger. This is the way of the faith of Christ.

Look at Romans 14. He did not disregard the conscience of the persons that had other ideas. Even though he stated his conviction, which was a matter of truth, he still said respect that conscience and walk through it. Of course we are not talking about someone coming to preach error. That is a separate issue. We are talking about someone whose conscience is still growing.

Romans chapter 14 says, "Receive one who is weak in the faith, but not to dispute over doubtful things." Can you do that? Can you receive one who feels weirded out a bit about tongues? Can you do that, knowing that you still believe it, you still practice it, and you can help them to grow into it because their conscience may be that way for certain reasons? "For one believes he may eat all things, but he who is weak eats only vegetables." See how clear he is. He says one believes he may eat all things, showing you that one is the one that is right. But he says he who is weak eats only vegetables. That means Paul knows the person is wrong, but it is because of weakness in conscience, and yet he says receive him.

He even made it clearer later, around verse 14. He says, "I know and am convinced by the Lord Jesus Christ that there is nothing unclean of itself." So I know what is right. I know the truth, yet I can accommodate a weak conscience. Discipleship does not override freedom of conscience.

## Speaking the Same Thing Is an Outcome, Not an Imposition

You know what I find interesting? In 1 Corinthians chapter 1, he was showing us one of the expectations of discipleship that people usually interpret as if all of you are just being forced to say the same thing. Go to 1 Corinthians chapter 1 and verse 10. He said, "Now I plead with you, brethren, by the name of our Lord Jesus Christ, that you all speak the same thing, and that there be no divisions among you." Now in discipleship some people say, ah, they are just forcing them to speak the same things.

But hold on. That I beseech you to speak the same thing is not an imposition. It is an outcome of a process. He said, I want you to get to the point that you are all speaking the same thing. I want you all to speak the same thing, that there be no division among you, but that you be perfectly joined together. That word perfectly joined together is `katartizo`, the verb form from the same word family as `katartismos` in Ephesians 4:12. So he is saying that you will speak the same things when you have been `katartizo`-ed.

Do not forget that `katartizo` is used for discipleship in Luke 6:40. A disciple is not above his teacher, but when he has been fully trained he will be like his teacher. So Paul is not saying, I want you to speak the same thing because I am forcing you to speak the same thing. He is saying, I want you to speak the same thing by ensuring that you have been brought to the same mind and the same judgment. This is not that all of us must act like we have the same mind. It is that we must have been taught and brought to conviction of the same mind, which means we have accepted by evidence given to us.

So we exist for God's purpose. That purpose requires growth. Growth happens by discipleship. And discipleship does not override freedom of conscience. It fully accommodates and embraces freedom of conscience, allowing people to grow from weakness into strength. On the issue of food, they could not yet speak the same thing. Yet it was a matter of use your knowledge with love to win them. Accommodate for now. On fundamental issues, of course, particularly when people are now committed, they should speak the same things.

## Discipleship Seeks Knowledge for Conviction

So if discipleship is the path of growth, what then does discipleship entail? The very first thing is this: discipleship seeks knowledge for conviction. Discipleship begins by seeking knowledge for conviction. Notice that it does not begin with commitment to an institution. It begins with a commitment to know, to learn.

So you are receiving the knowledge of the word. You are being taught. You are being strengthened. You are trying to confirm, is it really so? Is this the truth, what they are presenting? It seeks knowledge for conviction. And I say this in two ways: if you are a disciple, and if you are discipling people. If you are the one discipling people, discipleship provides knowledge for conviction. For the person that is the recipient, you are seeking knowledge for conviction.

You are not assuming conviction. You are not even assuming the rightness of the teacher simply because he is the teacher. You are there to be taught, to discern, to be convinced. Within the boundaries of the faith of Christ, you are confirming, is this really so?

This is why sometimes, by God's orchestration, more light comes to you, and sometimes it may mean you have to change commitment because of that. Apollos was taught the way of the Lord more accurately by Priscilla and Aquila, and his commitment matched his knowledge more fully. If you are teaching, then you are providing knowledge for conviction. You are clear that the person needs his own persuasion.

## Discipleship Exercises Faith-Stand for Growth

The second thing discipleship does, remember Jesus says, teaching them to observe, and we understand from apostolic teaching, even from the Lord Jesus himself, that what leads us to observing or doing is faith-stand. If a man, upon getting clarity, is going to be able to make commitment or any further action, knowledge alone will not be enough. So the second thing discipleship does is that discipleship exercises faith-stand for growth.

So discipleship seeks knowledge for conviction, because you want conviction. The second thing it does is that it exercises faith-stand for growth. Very important. It was the work of the apostles to teach them to observe. So they are teaching them, inspiring their observing, their doing, which means they inspire them to stand on the word in prayer and response. And it is there that they now grow to the point that this thing is not just right, but I should commit to it. The strength of commitment will come to them in the place of growth.

There is a way after giving people knowledge in God's word you can start demanding commitment, and sometimes you are jumping the gun. As you provide the knowledge of God's word, provide the growth. It is in the strength of that growth that commitments will come. Offer the knowledge, inspire faith-stand, and then commitments will form.

## Discipleship Makes Commitments to the Local Church

So that is the third thing. Discipleship makes commitments to the local church. It makes commitments to the local church. You announce it. Acts 5 said none of the rest did join them. They saw it as joining. But it also says believers were added, which was a way of describing that joining. So people now commit. But there were people who were just around, and they were not yet in that joined state. They had not yet joined. Usually those ones would just be around. A church must have people like that.

So discipleship makes commitments to the local church. At that point you are now a member. You have gone beyond the level of an attendee, someone that just comes around. There should be a standard process for that in the local church, because they told us they continued in the apostles' doctrine.

## Discipleship Leads to Submission to Spiritual Authority

The fourth thing in commitment to the local church, we must now clarify that discipleship leads to submission to spiritual authority. So as you commit to a local church, you must be aware that it means submission to spiritual authority. Now the word submission too has bad PR, and I will not lie, there are some justifiable reasons for it. People have used submission in funny ways.

Listen well now. If what we are saying is biblical submission and not subjugation, submission demands accountability, expects obedience, but does not necessitate obedience. I know that may not be palatable to many people, but it does not change the truth. Submission demands accountability, expects obedience, but does not always necessitate or guarantee that you should obey.

For example, let us say you commit to this church. You are submitted by your commitment. It means you will be accountable. If for some reason you cannot be around, you can tell someone. If major things are going on in your life, you let people know. Just like a family, you are accountable for meaningful matters. It is not that you call your pastor to ask what you want to eat this morning.

I am very careful here because you are submitted because of your persuasion. Do not forget, you are seeking knowledge for conviction. Persuasion. Because your submission is born out of persuasion. We believe the same things. Many things I tell you to do, you would naturally obey, and you will be expected to obey, even if it is uncomfortable sometimes, because it is born out of your own persuasion. Most times you are doing what you are supposed to do, but because it is your persuasion, it is not a matter of imposition, even though it came by instruction.

If persuasion did not make you commit, and it was all imposition in the beginning, one day you will rebel, because you will be feeling like, what is going on? And this is a lot of the challenge we find in many places.

But there might be times I say things and you will be like, ah, which one is this one? If you are unsure, you can seek clarity. Maybe if it is harmless, you might still go ahead. But if you are unsure and you think it is harmful, you should not go ahead. Then if you are sure that it is not right, you should not do it. Submission demands accountability, expects obedience, but does not always necessitate it.

If someone goes out of the bounds of your persuasion and asks for something harmful or clearly wrong, biblical submission does not mean you comply in the name of obedience. If you are unsure and the matter is harmless, you may still yield while seeking clarity. But if it is harmful or plainly wrong, hold on and seek higher clarification. That is not rebellion. That is biblical responsibility.

So the fourth thing, submission. In God's word all these things need explanation. Not explaining them, or trying to exercise them without clarity, is problematic. "Obey those who have the rule over you, and be submissive." The interesting thing is that word obey can also be read as be persuaded, even though it can also be obey. Be persuaded of those who have the rule over you, and be submissive. Yield to them. But it is rooted in that sense of persuasion. They watch out for your soul. So your submission is born out of persuasion, and your submission is defined by accountability. It does not necessitate obedience to evil.

## Discipleship Produces Practice and Walk

Lastly, discipleship births practice and walk, your walk in the word. It births practice and walk. Teaching them to observe. That means they will inspire you to walk in your character, your nature, your assignment, both natural and spiritual. Discipleship must be seen in your walk.

When Ephesians 4 was written and he told us, perfecting of the saints and all, that we are supposed to grow spiritually, the next verse, verse 17, said this: "This I say therefore, and testify in the Lord, that you should no longer walk." Discipleship is not complete if we do not see it in expression. Something must change about your attitude. Something must change about your disposition. Something must change about your words. Something must change about your conduct because of discipleship. The way you think is different. One of the first signs of discipleship is that the way you think is different.

Really, if you are being discipled by God's word, it has to happen. The way I think now is not the way I used to think before I got born again. Even five years after I got born again, it is not the way I think. My thinking has been highly developed by God's word. Judging matters, the way you are looking at issues and judging them, because God's word has given you the framework of your epistemology, you are able to look at the matter better. The way you think will change.

There are times we are emotionally spent, and sometimes it is okay to regulate your emotions. It is of God's Spirit to do that. Nothing has gone fundamentally wrong; you are just tired, strained, and need to regulate rather than misread the whole situation.

So discipleship should change you. It should change the way you are looking at issues, the way you talk, the way you act, your attitude, the way you carry out your assignments. Your attitude toward your natural assignment should change. You should become maximally productive, yet not greedy. And you can still use resources for comfort. Handling resources is many times a percentage issue.

It is one reason I am saying this is Bible wisdom. You are able to live peaceably without false guilt. Resources are for your needs, your wants, the mission, generosity, the poor, and even things the Lord might direct you to do. Discipleship is not cheating you of one thing or the other.

Imagine you go and read where the Lord Jesus said, go and sell all that you have. Joseph of Arimathea was also discipled, and he did not sell all that he had. Not all disciples sold all that they had. Even the people that were giving were giving out of their substance. So we must not miss the actual lesson going on there.

## Do Not Be Afraid of Discipleship

So discipleship is the path of growth necessary for us to fulfill God's purpose. It does not override freedom of our conscience. And we have seen the things that happen in discipleship. It seeks knowledge for conviction. It exercises faith-stand for growth. It makes commitments to the local church. It submits to spiritual authority, and I have explained what that entails. And then it births our practice and our walk.

So my exhortation to you is, do not be afraid of discipleship. Do not run away from it because of wrong models. Discipleship in the Christian model does not override your own responsibility to reason, arrive at conclusions, and have your faith as a result. It does not rob you of your individuality, even though the process should bring us into sameness in our belief.

And I trust that this encourages you not to be cheated of all that the Lord has for you in the local church and in discipleship because of the concerns, even if legitimate, produced by wrong models.
