# Redemption

**00:00**

Looking at a very important teaching, we are still on level two examining doctrinal summaries. In this particular session, we are looking at redemption. I would like us to turn our Bibles to Ephesians chapter one. I will start by saying that I consider, like you would learn when you get to level five, redemption to be at the heart of Bible doctrine. That is to say, I don't think it's the core issue in Bible doctrine, but it is the bridge because it takes us from the point where there is a problem to the resolution of that problem that brings us back to original intention. Paul, in giving us the summary of the doctrine in Ephesians chapter one, put it at the center as a bridge between what was on God's mind and the fulfillment of that which was on His mind.

That appreciation of redemption is necessary because it shows that if there is a weak understanding of redemption, one's walk in what God intends—His purpose—is going to be affected. Your walk in God's purpose can become shaky. The very essence of the doctrinal summaries we have been looking at, or the essence of the entire spectrum of Bible doctrine, is to train us or perfect us to fulfill God's purpose. We have seen that at the point of salvation, when a man receives the gospel, you come out of darkness and enter into light. At that same moment when you are saved, you are now in God's purpose. You become a son of God, which means there is something for you to fulfill as God's child. As His heir, you have two assignments: the natural assignment He wants you to do and the spiritual assignment.

Being set on that path of purpose, knowing that there's something for you to fulfill, you have this goal that I must fulfill God's purpose. The very first burden on you is to grow, to develop, or to be built to such a degree and extent where you are able to carry out that purpose of God for your life. We saw from God's word that what builds us to that stature that the Lord wants us to have is discipleship. The Lord Himself said that in Matthew 28:18 to 20\. He mentioned that He has given the leaders of the church for the discipleship of those who will receive His message. He says, "Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost, and teaching them." Perfecting of the saints is needed for us to fulfill God's purpose. That perfecting is to bring us to that point in growth where we are able to bear the burden of purpose and fulfill it.

Redemption is at the center of that doctrine. Without the proper understanding of redemption, if you don't have that strength of conviction, you will find yourself from time to time easily shaken about different matters that would affect your walk in God's purpose. It can be just by virtue of your humanity and the imperfections that pertain to the same, which means you find yourself in sin. At that juncture, an individual can get so consumed by the fact that they have done a sinful act that they are unable to pick themselves up to walk in God's purpose. There's the very opposite example as well. We can find someone that has a poor reckoning of redemption such that there is no gravity to their life; they are licentious about their conduct. There is no reckoning of their nature in Christ, and as a result, it affects their walk in God's purpose.

**05:00** Understanding of redemption is very key. In Paul's outline of the doctrine, he says in Ephesians 1:7, "In Him we have redemption through His blood, the forgiveness of sins, according to the riches of His grace." What is profound about that is he didn't say, "In Him there is redemption." If he said in Him there is redemption, that would not be wrong, but he's speaking to a particular people—those who have believed the gospel—and he says, "In Him we have redemption." We have studied that part which is God's wisdom towards redemption; this redemption we now have, God worked it out by His wisdom and prudence. Our focus at this juncture is on that redemption itself.

The way we should think about redemption is not just as an event that happened. It's sort of like an asset in our accounts. The implication of redemption is that there's been some value credited to you. The first thing we are going to see is that it tells us we have redemption worked out by God's wisdom, and he called that redemption the forgiveness of sins. What he is doing there is giving us context to redemption. Ordinarily, when we see that word redemption, *lutrosis* or *apolutrosis*, it simply is talking about freedom. To be set free, to be made loose, completely free. The implication of that word is there is something we need freedom from. If he says in Him we have freedom, He quickly points out to us that that freedom is the forgiveness from sins, clearly showing that that which we have freedom from is sin. Both the word redemption and the word forgiveness point to the reality of a bondage that we need freedom from.

The word for forgiveness is the word *aphesis*. It's from two words: *apo*, which can mean off or away, and *hiemi*, which means to send or to go. It means to send away. We are talking about forgiveness of sins; it can be sending away sins from you, clearing off sins away from you. On the other hand, it can be taking you away from sins, sending you off or out of sins. In fulfilled reality, both of them are true about us. On the one hand, sins that are to your account have been cleared off. On the other hand, you have been taken away from the bondage of sin. Both things are true when you say I have forgiveness of sins.

There is a context to redemption, and that context is sin. But there is a context itself to sin. When we talk about redemption, we must start all the way at the origin. If redemption is about sin, and sin itself is not independent of a goal that was set that you did not fulfill or that you missed, then it means redemption is about sin, and sin is about God's purpose. The context to redemption is: God made man for His purpose. God gave man free will to accept or reject His purpose. Sin happened, which means man rejected God's purpose. Man by free will rejected God's purpose. Therefore, we now have sin, and it has become necessary that we are redeemed. Otherwise, we are damned. The context for redemption is the truth of our existence: that there is a God who has a purpose, that He has offered it to man, and man by his free will has rejected the same, plunging into a state of sin, death, and contradiction, which God saves us from.

**10:00** We have studied Christology earlier, where we saw the necessity of the Christ. By virtue of the nature of the problem that we are seeing in the world, only one entity can resolve that problem. That entity must be light, yet must legitimately come into darkness and legitimately deal with that darkness to bring back light. Understanding that means the only way we can have redemption is it must be by God becoming a man. The creation is under darkness; it has to be creation at its highest because it's the very same way sin happened as well. Otherwise, redemption will only happen to the degree of creation that God became. Or to the degree of light that shines. If light shines only on things that do not have a spiritual reality, then it might be a redemption that pertains only to the material world. The only way redemption can happen is that it must be by God who becomes a man and therefore deals with the contradiction of sin in the world.

The first thing in studying redemption is understanding the context of redemption: God's purpose, man's free will, sin, and the necessity of the Christ. The second thing is the narrative of redemption. The narrative of redemption is found squarely in the four gospels. The redemption we need is substantial. There are times people will say, "I agree that there's a contradiction in the world, but it's because man has just forgotten who he is originally." That's not a serious claim. There's a substantial problem that requires a substantial solution; that's the work of the Christ. We must have the record of that substantial solution, the narrative of redemption.

In the narrative of redemption, the first thing we must see is the narrative of the Christ. The second thing is the Christ's recognition of His work of redemption. This is very key. There are people who will say that He was just taken and killed without any intention to die. They challenge the redemptive narrative fulfilled in Christ on that premise. We must see His recognition of the work He is to do. The third thing we must see is His willingness and agreement to do that work of redemption. The fourth thing we'll see is His execution of this redemptive work. The fifth one is the proclamation of the work.

First, we have the narrative of the Christ, the person of the Lord Jesus becoming a man. Second, He has recognition of the redemptive work He is to do. Let me give you the instances in the book of Matthew where we see Him spelling out that this is what He's here to do. He lived to die. He was aware that this is what His work is for. Look at Matthew 16:21. You remember this is after He asked them who He is and Peter said He's the Christ. We mustn't be quick to think their understanding of the Christ was His own understanding. Their understanding of the Christ was very likely political, someone anointed to deliver, to save, and usually to rule or to lead. At this point where they said He's the Christ, and He told them, "I give to you the keys of the kingdom," they would have been excited that they were following the right person. Look at verse 20: "Then He commanded His disciples that they should tell no one that He was Jesus the Christ." Now look at 21: "From that time Jesus began to show to His disciples that He must go to Jerusalem, and suffer many things from the elders and chief priests and scribes, and be killed, and be raised the third day." There was the recognition of the work He was to do as the Christ.

**15:00** Let's see Matthew 17:22. "Now while they were staying in Galilee, Jesus said to them, 'The Son of Man is about to be betrayed into the hands of men, and they will kill Him, and the third day He will be raised up.'" Matthew 20:17-19 says, "Now Jesus, going up to Jerusalem, took the twelve disciples aside on the road and said to them, 'Behold, we are going up to Jerusalem, and the Son of Man will be betrayed to the chief priests and to the scribes; and they will condemn Him to death, and deliver Him to the Gentiles to mock and to scourge and to crucify. And the third day He will rise again.'" We could equally cite Matthew 20:28. This one was more open; it wasn't in private, but it was generic so it's difficult for maybe everybody to have gotten what He said. He said the Son of Man did not come to be served, but to serve, and to give His life a ransom for many. Lastly for Matthew would be Matthew 26:1-2: "Now it came to pass, when Jesus had finished all these sayings, that He said to His disciples, 'You know that after two days is the Passover, and the Son of Man will be delivered up to be crucified.'" He very clearly had a recognition of the work of redemption as something He was to do. You'll find it in other materials as well: Mark 8:31, Mark 9:31-32, Mark 10:30-32, Luke 9:22, 31, 44-45, and Luke 18:31-34.

The third thing is His willingness and agreement to do it. You'll find instances of this in texts like John chapter 10, where He said, "No one takes it from Me, but I lay it down of Myself." It's not something that happened perchance. Beyond that willingness, you have an express account of a conversation to do the work of redemption seen in Matthew 26:37-46, as well as Luke 22:39-46. People will say, "How can you say He's a redeemer, He was chickening out." That only validates His humanity, which is a necessity for redemption. People might bring up others who didn't back down in the face of death, saying Socrates was killed for his beliefs and didn't chicken out. But Socrates is not taking the eternal weight of sin. Christ's physical death in redemption means nothing about the second death. When the Lord said, "My soul is exceedingly sorrowful, even unto death," He wasn't just talking about physical death. The death in question that the Lord was about to experience is known in Scripture as the second death, which is not just physical death. There was good reason to feel that way. It only shows His humanity in light of what He is about to face.

It is a message to us to reckon the gravity of damnation. The gravity of eternal condemnation should dawn on us when we appreciate the reaction of the Lord Himself. In Matthew 26:36, Jesus came with them to a place called Gethsemane, and said to His disciples, "Sit here while I go and pray over there." He took with Him Peter and the two sons of Zebedee, and He began to be sorrowful and deeply distressed. He said to them, "My soul is exceedingly sorrowful, even to death. Stay here and watch with Me." This is where we start to see the issue of willingness. There is definitely grief at the work He is about to do. Look at verse 39: "He went a little farther and fell on His face, and prayed, saying, 'O My Father, if it is possible, let this cup pass from Me; nevertheless, not as I will, but as You will.'" It's very clear that the work of redemption, which He is aware He is about to do, was undesirable to Him. It's what He came to do, yet it's not something readily desired. It's something gruesome, despite the fact that He's going to go through it and fully accomplish that work. He says, "not as I will, but as You will."

**20:00** In the second prayer, He says, "O My Father, if this cup cannot pass away from Me unless I drink it, Your will be done." We see that despite how undesirable it was, He was willing to go through it. If it cannot pass over Him, He says, "Your will be done." We are told that the third prayer was exactly in that way. He accepted to do it. There was a willingness despite undesirability, and an agreement to do it.

Fourth, there's the execution of the work. This includes the fact that He goes to the cross and dies, and the fact that He is raised from the dead. There is also the behind-the-scenes reality of His death and resurrection, which is a separate issue under the doctrinal overview. The last part we should see in the narrative is His proclamation of that redemption. He proclaims: I am the Christ. I know I am going to do it. I am willing to do it. I have done it. Now, I am proclaiming that what I have done is that redemption. See Luke chapter 24\. He proclaims the redemption that He is aware He came to do and that He has accomplished.

Redemption is at the heart of Bible doctrine. Having a bad estimation of redemption affects our walk in God's purpose. We are likely to be shaken in our walk in God's purpose if it is not based on redemption. When we are teaching God's purpose, a person's assignment might be a slave. That person is in God's purpose. Another person might already be in a position of power, might already be wealthy, and is now entering into true knowledge. Sometimes, people think Christianity is meant to upgrade their life, to build a bigger business or start an NGO. A poor person might come into Christianity thinking that they will stop being poor. The truth is Christianity will order your life correctly. There are things you will learn that will repair your life. People might look at someone who was wayward before giving their life to Christ and see how put together they are now, making good decisions and being prudent. Their life is repaired because the knowledge of God orders your steps, guides you, teaches diligence, and teaches respectful interaction. Those things have a positive impact on physical well-being. But that is not God's purpose. All these elements come together, and people often look at this as what they want when interacting with the gospel.

This brings a challenge because a faith that is not properly built is not based on redemption. A properly redemption-centered faith knows why it is saved: "I am delivered from this to do this. There was a reality I was in before. The Son of God came down, suffered, and brought me to another reality. This other reality is His reality." My life cannot be exactly as it was going to be previously. To someone looking outward, it looks like a repaired life where things are moving smoothly, implying the goal is just resolving physical contradictions. But a truly built believer's faith is centered on redemption. It tells them that the life they are now living is a new life entirely. It belongs strictly to God, and they must use it fully to obey. There is a total surrender based on an understanding of redemption. A believer who thinks the word of God just exists to sort out their life is behaving exactly like man before regeneration—seeking principles to get their life running perfectly according to their own will. A truly discipled person, knowing redemption, knows they are fully not their own. The Bible says we must no longer live for ourselves but for Him who died for us and rose again. Why? Because of redemption. That changes a lot. The individual's life completely belongs to God. Let's see Luke 24:44.

**25:00** "Then He said to them, 'These are the words which I spoke to you while I was still with you, that all things must be fulfilled which were written in the Law of Moses and the Prophets and the Psalms concerning Me.'" What are those things? Verse 46: "Then He said to them, 'Thus it is written, and thus it was necessary for the Christ to suffer and to rise from the dead the third day.'" It is not that after He died, He realized it was redemption. From birth to His personal recognition, to His agreement to it, to the execution, and after, the narrative of redemption affirms redemption. The problem was the disciples were confused about how exactly He was going to give them this kingdom, and now He's talking about death. The Christ is supposed to rule the kingdoms forever, but they didn't have a full appreciation of the implication of redemption.

Now we are going to the core issue, which is the doctrinal overview of redemption. There are ten things in the doctrinal overview of redemption. This is the bulk of our teaching when you get to level five; it is one of the high points. It is what we often call the "contents of redemption." We said there is the context that leads up to redemption, and the content of redemption—what is inside this redemption He has done. Sometimes people have a wrong appreciation of redemption. The first thing we see is the need for redemption. What is redemption addressing? Simply, sin. At that point, we open up sin. We see sin as man's rejection of God's purpose, and sin as the authority that is now at play because of man's rejection of God's purpose—death and contradiction have come in. We can see sin as the nature man has as a result, and sin as the conduct man does as a result.

No matter the standpoint you are speaking of redemption from, it is still the same redemption. Someone can say, "I am delivered from sin, from the authority of darkness." Another person can say, "My sins are forgiven." The statement "my sins are forgiven" means a deliverance from sin as appropriate to the context. It's not referring to two different things. If you're looking at redemption and sin from the context of conduct, "I am completely forgiven of all sins" means complete freedom from this whole thing called sin. They are speaking about the same salvation. Paul defined the doctrine of Christ with the word salvation in 2 Timothy 3:15. He said, "From childhood you have known the Holy Scriptures, which are able to make you wise for salvation"—which is again saying redemption. This shows how core redemption is to the doctrine. Despite the fact that redemption is just an event that set you into the life God wanted you to have, it is core in defining the faith of Christ and affects how you live the faith of Christ. The value you think it has affects the way you live out your faith.

The need for redemption is for sin in its full understanding. It has dealt with sin across the board—the rejection of God's purpose, the authority, and the state that has come in as a result. We can speak about sin in five ways: man's rejection of God's purpose, the authority of sin, the state of sin (which is death, darkness, and evil that affects humanity and matter), the nature of man, and the conduct of man. Usually, you will see "sins" used to talk about it a lot because it is the most relatable. People are aware that we are not living perfectly; we do wrong that is meant to be judged. It doesn't matter what standpoint, that is what redemption is addressing: this issue of sin in all its ramifications. The second thing we see under the doctrinal overview is the nature of redemption.

**30:00** The nature of redemption is very major. There are longstanding debates about whether what Christ did was physical, spiritual, or both. Some would say it is the very physical work He did that redeemed us; that it is the physical blood He shed that saved us. The efficacy of physical blood to redemption becomes an issue, and people debate it. The nature of redemption is that it is both physical and spiritual; in fact, it is spiritual at its core. The physical is necessary for the spiritual, otherwise you are saying He didn't have to become a man for that to happen. So the nature of redemption is physical and spiritual.

Number three we look at is the scope of redemption. The scope of redemption is how long it covers in time. Does redemption avail people before Christ came, and how many people after Christ came does it avail? One of the major texts here is Romans 3:25. He said God passed over the sins that were committed before. Another text is in Hebrews 10, showing that redemption availed people all the way from the first man until Christ's coming, and from Christ's coming to the consummation of redemption at His second coming. On that note, we addressed the scope of redemption, how far it covers in time.

The fourth thing we addressed in the doctrinal overview is the extent of redemption. This asks how many people it covers. Here we have to face some of the Calvinist questions about limited atonement. Is it limited in its extent? How many people did He die for? 1 John 2:2 says He is the propitiation for our sins, and not for ours only but also for the whole world. Some argue He died only for those who would believe. Other people hold a view that He died for everybody, even though only some will be beneficiaries of it. The problem is that this view creates theological problems. Are you saying someone is going to burn in hell for sins that have been paid for? These are serious questions. If someone owes a debt and it is paid for fully, why are they being charged for the debt again? If Jesus paid for it completely, yet God demands payment for exactly what Jesus has paid for, the question of God's justice comes into question. To be logically sound without any theological issue, some conclude He did not die for them at all, and that it is just meant for those who will benefit from it. Both of those issues are addressed squarely in level five. But you must know that the extent of redemption deals with who is covered.

**35:00** We addressed this completely with robust theological evidence. We must be careful to handle biblical theology side-by-side with historical theology and philosophical theology. Number five is the longevity of redemption. Is it eternal? Does it expire? Is it like animal sacrifice that happens yearly? Or does it expire if I do certain things as someone that receives redemption? And if it expires, can I be re-redeemed? This borders on eternal security, or preservation of salvation. Can people be saved multiple times or not? All of these are tied to the longevity of the redemptive work. In concluding that part, we saw clearly that the redemptive work brings salvation that is completely eternal to its beneficiaries. But the beneficiaries are proven by the mark of repentance. I don't believe that someone living unrepentantly is saved and redeemed. One of the core reasons why it's eternal is the same way you are a child of your father permanently, you are permanently a human being by nature. If you are disowned, it doesn't change the fact that you are still a child of your father by nature. But if someone is behaving in a manner that clearly cannot be of the nature of God, which is marked by unrepentance rather than mere imperfection, then we need to raise questions. The least expected of a man having the nature of God in him is repentance.

Number six is the accomplishment of redemption. This asks what Christ was really trying to accomplish. Some people say He took away our guilt. Some say He took the nature of sin for us. Neither of those are correct. He dealt with our guilt, but what Christ did was not as much becoming guilty for us. The idea that we were guilty of something and put it on someone else to suffer for our wrong has become an offense to some people's sense of justice. They say, "If someone kills someone, you are saying someone else goes to suffer for him out of love?" They question the sense of justice in that, saying it's not righteous. But that is not what redemption accomplished.

The proper understanding of what Christ accomplished is that we are slaves under sin, and He went under sin as well as a slave. His "becoming sin for us" is not becoming guilty of sin or having the sin nature, but being found under the bondage of sin to disarm sin. He came under the reign of sin in a body that could die. He eventually died and went to Hades, a place of the reign of sin, to disarm sin. The redemption He accomplished was not becoming guilty of sin or having the sin nature. The Bible uses several descriptions of the actual work He did, which is why we have different theories of redemption or salvation, like penal substitution or Christus Victor.

**40:00** Authors and preachers have the liberty to describe the actual work He did in many ways. The question is what was the actual work He did? The actual work is He became a slave under sin to bear the demand that it makes on its slaves, which is the second death. He exhausted that second death and legitimately restored life back. I remember some descriptions of redemption saying that when Jesus got to Hades, He started fighting Satan. I am not saying this to mock anybody; I used to say this as part of my confession. I would describe it like that: Satan can never do anything again, He conquered him, He stripped him of this. Interestingly, Paul uses a description that looks like that, particularly in Colossians 2 and Hebrews 2\. But you will be wrong if you think that's the actual thing He did. When you pay attention to the entire scripture, the actual thing He did is He became a slave under sin so the demands of sin could be put on Him, which is the second death. He bore and exhausted that and legitimately brought life back.

If Jesus went there to start fighting Satan, does Satan own sin? If Jesus beats Satan and takes us away, Jesus has stolen us, legally speaking. If God just wanted to steal us, He didn't need any redemptive plan; God's power is higher than everybody's. He would have just sent an angel to steal all of us from the grasp of Satan. But the matter was legal. It had to be a slave that brings the freedom for slaves. He took upon Himself flesh and blood so He could legally fulfill the demands of death, exhausting it completely by virtue of who He is. By virtue of His light, He completely swallowed up the darkness so that darkness no longer holds, bringing about salvation and liberty for Himself and everyone identified with Him. The actual accomplishment of salvation is the legal fulfillment of sin's demands, thereby bringing our freedom. This is the central thesis. Every other description flows out of this truth.

A preacher can want to say this truth so the person can describe it as penal substitution, because you were under death and He went there instead of you. Someone else might say it's Christus Victor, that He defeated sin in battle. Paul sometimes describes it as a victory battle, saying He spoiled principalities and powers. But how did He spoil them? He made a public spectacle of them, triumphing over them in the cross. He didn't triumph over them in a physical fight. What happened on the cross was a legitimate execution, exhausting the demands of that legal condition. By so doing, because they can no longer hold Him, He completely overthrows whatever hold they had from its foundation.

Number seven is the effect of redemption. The effect of redemption distinguishes what happens after redemption from what happens fully at the consummation. That will be at the end of the age. The immediate effect of redemption is spiritual: the giving of the Spirit, the forgiveness of sins.
