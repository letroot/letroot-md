# God and His Eternal Purpose

## God and His Eternal Purpose in the Doctrinal Outline

All right brothers and sisters, we are on level two. We are looking at God and His eternal purpose. In this subject matter in the systematic theology of different people, they may call it theology proper because theology is taken as the entire subject matter of scripture, the study of God. But the study of God Himself is often called theology proper. We just call it God and His eternal purpose because we are teaching for everybody's understanding, and we are not trying to be overly technical.

In doctrinal summaries, we started out saying that there is a body of knowledge necessary for us to fulfill God's purpose. Right after someone has received the gospel and becomes saved, you have entered into God's purpose. What is next is you want to fulfill God's purpose for your life. The fulfillment of God's purpose requires discipleship. The Lord Jesus said to go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them into the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost, and teaching them. He says to make disciples via teaching them to observe all things whatsoever He commanded. There was a specific body of knowledge to be taught.

That is later called the apostles' doctrine in Acts chapter 2 and verse 42\. By the time we get all the way through to Paul, Paul was saying that what we are built on is the foundation of the apostles and prophets, where he has a central role in the submission of that message. He made that clear in Ephesians chapter 3, verses 1 to 4\. Peter acknowledged Paul's wisdom and grace in the presentation of the message. In Ephesians chapter 3, when Paul was making that statement, he told us how that by revelation, this grace gave him the privilege of revelation such that God made known to him the mystery. That is quite funny because the mystery is already found in the Old Testament text, but he is saying that there is a presentation and an understanding that he has the privilege to give us.

If you have listened to the introduction to doctrinal summaries, you will recall that Paul had written briefly this outline of God's message in Ephesians 1, from verses 3 to 14\. There is another word Paul used in verse 4\. He said by which when you read, you will understand my knowledge in the mystery of Christ. That word knowledge is the word *sunesis*, which is like the parallel word to the word wisdom, *sophia*. *Sunesis* means understanding. It means putting things together. It is like solving a puzzle, taking every piece and putting them in their place. When we read Ephesians chapter 1, we see Paul's presentation, understanding, or putting together of the message. That is the message the Lord Jesus gave for our discipleship.

Ephesians chapter 4 says the leaders of the church are given to perfect the saints so that the saints will fulfill God's purpose. They are to perfect the saints till they arrive at this knowledge that God wants them to have: this unity of the faith, this knowledge of the Son of God, which results in the perfect man, the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ.

## The Authority of Scripture and the Reality of God

We have looked at bibliology because we saw in 2 Timothy chapter 3 that the first thing the student of God's word ought to know is how to defend the source or the authority of the doctrine. The doctrine is from the Old Testament text and the apostles who make up the New Testament text. The student of God's word must be able to say clearly why they respect and hold the Bible as authoritative. Sometimes some of us hold the Bible as authoritative simply because we were brought up to do so out of a dogmatic stance. We hold the Bible as authoritative because of the authority of truth that finds itself in the person of Jesus, who then validated the Old Testament text as well as the New Testament text.

If we go to Paul's outline of doctrine in Ephesians 1 from verses 3 to 14, what do we see? Immediately after the salutation—where he says, "Paul, apostle of Jesus Christ, by the will of God, to the saints who are in Ephesus, faithful in Christ Jesus, grace to you and peace from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ"—he writes, "Blessed be God and Father of our Lord Jesus Christ, who hath blessed us with every spiritual blessing in heavenly places in Christ." The very first subject matter of Paul's discussion is God.

He didn't stop there talking about what God has done to us now in Christ; he took it a step further and said, "Just as He chose us in Him before the foundation of the world." He is taking us into God's mind before He ever made the world. The claim of God itself is not being proven here; he is just asserting it because he does the proving elsewhere. He is simply stating his doctrine. He says there is a God, He has made you like this, and He did this in the light of something He planned before the foundation of the world. He chose you that you should be holy without blame before Him in love, having predestined you to adoption as sons by Jesus Christ to Himself according to the good pleasure of His will.

The doctrine of Paul, which we call the unity of the faith, starts with the reality of God. Once you cross the bridge of the source of the scripture, the first subject matter you will be examining is God, and not just God in His person, but His mind. Later in verse 9, he is going to call it God's purpose. The entirety of Ephesians chapter 1 from verses 3 to 6 is simply talking about God and His purpose and plan. That is the beginning point of the doctrine.

He describes it as the good pleasure of His will. That purpose is called out in Ephesians chapter 3 and verse 9: "And to make all see what is the fellowship of the mystery, which from the beginning of the ages has been hidden in God." The idea of the fellowship of the mystery means everybody is supposed to be part of what we saw with the Israelites. Every kind of human is supposed to see the commonness of the mystery. The mystery is sonship. God created all things through Jesus Christ to the intent that now the manifold wisdom of God might be made known by the church to the principalities and powers in heavenly places, according to the eternal purpose which He has accomplished in Christ Jesus our Lord.

## Defending the Faith and Understanding Evidence

We call this first part of the doctrine "God and His Eternal Purpose." All these things are necessary to build your conviction so that you can have the requisite persuasion to fulfill God's purpose. When your conviction is shaky, your commitment to God's will and purpose will equally be shaky. Imagine having doubts about the Bible because you have not studied bibliology. Someone throws a spanner into your beliefs, calling the Bible a tool of manipulation or claiming someone just sat down somewhere and wrote it. If you are not with firm conviction, your faith can be shaken.

Another area where faith gets shaken is if you lack firm conviction about the reality of God and His eternal purpose. In the systematic theology Paul has given us, there are certain things that must become clear to you. One thing is that the Bible submits to us the reality of God. In fact, this is the very first thing the gospel establishes. In Ephesians 1:13, it says, "In Him you trusted after you heard the word of truth, the gospel of your salvation." The first truth that the gospel seeks to establish is the reality of God. Everything rises and falls on this.

The Bible answers the metaphysical question: Why are we here? How could we even be here at all? Why is there something rather than nothing? Some people assume the Bible is just a book asserting things. It is actually a book providing evidence for conclusions that it says are the truth. The scriptures show us the evidence God gives to persuade us to come to Him. God is calling us.

Go to Romans chapter 1\. "For I am not ashamed of the gospel of Christ, for it is the power of God to salvation to everyone who believes, for the Jew first and also for the Greek. For in it the righteousness of God is revealed from faith to faith." When people believe, the righteousness of God is revealed to them. Look at verse 18: "For the wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness and all unrighteousness of men who suppress the truth in unrighteousness." Why are some people seeing the wrath of God revealed to them? Because of their response. The wrath of God is revealed to those who suppress the truth. This implies that the gospel is the truth, and it is obvious or evident to them. It is something they can see, but they add other things to reject it.

## Observation, Reason, and the Contingent World

Look at verse 19: "Because what may be known of God is manifest in them." It is manifest in their minds. It is manifest in them because God has shown it to them. They know it by observation and reason. Their observation and senses have caught a glimpse of it and gotten the requisite data to arrive at that conclusion. By observation and reason, we can arrive at the conclusion of truth. This is true about any subject matter. If someone asks what color of shirt a person is wearing, you look at what the person is wearing. You think about it because you know colors and labels, and you arrive at a conclusion.

Paul is saying their observation has made available to their reason the conclusion of this truth, which they are suppressing. Go on to verse 20: "For since the creation of the world, His invisible attributes are clearly seen." It uses the eyes as a placeholder for all forms of observation. "His invisible attributes are clearly seen, being understood." That is the mind. Observation and reason. Just the same way you arrive at every other conclusion, whether scientific or metaphysical, you require observation and reason.

He says His invisible attributes are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made. He shows us two things: from the creation of the world, finitude. Understood by the things that are made, contingency. The finite nature of the world—the fact that time is counting—and the contingent nature of the world—it is made, it doesn't have to be, it could have not been—points to the fact that it has a cause equal or greater to it.

The only way we can explain the finite, contingent world is a cause equal or greater to it. By nature, you cannot see the person of God. Sometimes people say God can resolve a matter by just appearing right now. When you say God can appear now, you are assuming He exists in matter, space, and time and should be rendered in matter, space, and time, which He is not. You are demanding of Him something that contradicts His very nature. By nature, He is invisible to the visible world. You cannot demand of Him what He just isn't.

The evidence for who He is is already available. People are simply raising the bar to reject the conclusion they are naturally led to by the evidence. You are requiring the standard of science—empirical evidence—for someone not bound by that. This is a metaphysical issue. If God appeared in a vision, people would say they were hallucinating, because any appearance in time, space, and matter is an apparition, a rendition. The reality of the person cannot be bound by time, space, and matter.

## God's Status and Absolute Sovereignty

The evidence of this natural world leads us to the conclusion that God is the most rational, sound, and probable conclusion about our existence. Something finite and contingent calls for a cause that is equal or greater to it. This must be an infinite, non-contingent cause that has intelligence and character. Once we see an entity with such ability, intelligence, and character, we are talking about a personal being. Thus, the conclusion is God.

This conclusion is obvious to everyone. In Romans 10, Paul says they have all heard the message, quoting Psalm 19, which states that day unto day utters speech, night unto night utters knowledge. The scriptures are clear; by reason, the evidence from nature leads to the metaphysical conclusion that there is a self-existent, necessary being with the ability, intelligence, and character to bring forth this finite and contingent world. If we judge this by the way we do all rational inquiry, our conclusion without debate ought to be God.

The first thing the gospel seeks to establish is the reality of God as a non-finite and non-contingent person. He is not made. There is no way He could have been another way. He is a necessary being, self-existent, not a creation.

The next step is to know His status. We all have status relative to something else. What is God's status relative to everything that exists? We are dividing everything that exists into two categories: the self-existent being, and created, contingent existence. One is self-existence, necessary by His very nature. The other is contingent. Therefore, speaking of status, one is supreme because He is determinant of the other. One is supreme in authority and standing. In existence, only this particular person is sovereign.

Sovereignty is a subject matter in the church that has been an issue. People have different angles and opinions on it. But one thing is undebatable: God is sovereign. We must establish that God is sovereign absolutely. When we talk about the sovereignty of nations today, a supposedly sovereign nation might have another nation intervene and kidnap its president. The United States exercising a form of sovereignty over another nation is an action people sometimes wrongly attribute to God's style of sovereignty. When we talk about the sovereignty of God, He is sovereign absolutely in the sense that He could have determined whatever He wants.

## Free Will and God's Character

By His status, He has the right to do whatever He wants to do. Whatever we find as created existence is His choice. He is self-existent to have done anything. His sovereignty is not just a matter of what He did, but who He is. Because He is self-existent, He could have created a world where there is no free will whatsoever. That is the nature of His sovereignty. He is at liberty. What could have happened is anything possible to Him.

Some people ask, if there is free will in the world, does that mean God is not absolutely sovereign? God is absolutely sovereign in His status. Did He choose to have free will exist in that world? That is a separate issue. But could He have done whatever He wanted to do? There is no debate. As a matter of status, He could. You cannot dictate to Him whether He is going to create a world with free will or not. A world with free will doesn't affect His sovereignty; it is proof of it. A world without free will wouldn't affect His sovereignty either.

The next thing we see under this study is the character of God. This is another vital part of the study. What can we say the character of God is, and what can it not be? From the standpoint of our own existence, we see a contradiction in this world: light and darkness, life and death, good and evil, right and wrong. We must ask what we make of our origin and our Creator. What is His character?

Some people say He has both light and darkness. But He cannot have both, because the very presence of one is the absence of the other. The presence of darkness is simply the absence of light, because light is original. That is why 1 John 1:5 says, "God is light, and in Him is no darkness at all." This is a conceptual, metaphysical claim that we cannot attribute contradiction to Him. You cannot say light and non-light exist in the same place in the same respect. It doesn't make sense. The very presence of light means there is no darkness. Very important to God's character is the emphasis that He is just light. He is just life. He is goodness. He is righteousness.

There are things we can never fully resolve, particularly the problem of evil. But we must be honest and say that evil is not of His character. In His sovereignty, we can attribute everything to Him, even things He did not directly want, simply because He set up the system. But we must have a clear philosophical and biblical theology to make the right conclusions about His character.

## God's Abilities and Eternal Purpose

We also look at the ability of God. In the light of His status and character, His ability is that He can do everything that aligns with His character. We must be careful when we say God is omnipotent and can do all things. Can He lie? Hebrews chapter 6 clearly states it is impossible for God to lie. His ability is bounded by His character. He cannot do what contradicts His nature.

Finally, we look at His mind, or what we call His eternal purpose. Paul focused on this in Ephesians 1 from verses 3 to 6\. In His mind, we speak of order. We can speak of His good pleasure (what He likes), His will (what He wants), His counsel (what He determined), and His purpose. All of this happens in His mind. We also see foreknowledge, predestination, and election in Romans 8:28-30. Because He foreknows, He is able to work all things together.

Foreknowledge, predestination, and election simply mean that God foreknows humanity's condition—sin. He predestined salvation in Christ and elects those who choose to receive salvation. The election is an effect of the setup He made. For example, let us say I want to give one million dollars, and there are a hundred people. I scatter a hundred balls around a location. Whoever finds one ball within twenty-four hours gets the money. I have not limited anyone from finding a ball. Everyone can get one. Some may say they are not interested. Some may follow my directions and find it. Everyone can become part of my chosen people for the money, not because I directly forced them, but because I chose the parameters of who I will choose.

God's election operates on faith in the redemptive work of Christ, not on arbitrarily picking individuals in themselves. He elected based on the conditions He set: faith. This is the understanding of His mind we must have. He foreknew what man would do, predestined the solution in salvation, and elects those who align with the condition of salvation.

All of the things we have seen—the reality of God, His status, character, ability, and mind—produce His activities. Paul says God was like this before the foundation of the world. He already had this entire mind, and therefore He started executing His activities. The activities of God happen on three levels: creation, redemption, and all time. He is interacting with humanity constantly.

This is where we wrap up for now on God and His eternal purpose, doctrinal summary level two, theology proper.
