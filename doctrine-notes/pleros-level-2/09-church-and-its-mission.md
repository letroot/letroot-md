# Church and Its Mission

**00:00**

We are looking at the Church and its mission. Before I go to any text, we will be looking at Ephesians 3 before we look at Ephesians 1\. We speak about God's purpose as we have been studying as sonship, but we can have a very individualistic sense to it that betrays the intention that was on the heart of God. If we say God's purpose is sonship, you could as well say God's purpose is one person being His child and being His heir. But if we look at what was on His heart, it was more than one person. When He said we have the spirit of sonship, the Spirit bears witness with our spirit that we are children. It is very clear that His intention is this plural reality—a collective, a people that have this reality of sonship.

One of the texts that makes this very clear, that God's purpose is not just one person being His son but a people that have that reality, is Ephesians 3:10 and 11\. It says, "To the intent that now the manifold wisdom of God might be made known by the church." That "now" is talking about when Jesus has fulfilled redemption. After redemption, God is able to fully show His wisdom. The manifold wisdom of God might be made known by the church to the principalities and powers in heavenly places.

There are two ways to think about that. You could say God is making known His wisdom by the church saying it, or God is making known His wisdom by how the church is. The second one is the intention of Paul here. He is not saying God's wisdom is made known by the church telling people, "This is God's wisdom." Of course, we could also make known God's wisdom by our preaching, but the reality of who we are reveals what God always wanted. The wisdom of God is made known by the church to the principalities and powers in heavenly places. We are making known what God always wanted to the principalities and powers in heavenly places.

You can think about it as the angels are seeing what God always wanted. But you could also think about it as the world is seeing what God wants. Both the spiritual entities as well as the natural ones, humans, are witnessing God's purposes when they see the church. I like a statement made by Peter in 1 Peter 1:12. He says that we have received in the gospel the things that angels desire to look into. They wanted to see the fulfillment of what this creation and existence is all about, and now they have eventually found that in what Christ has done, which the church is the product of.

I will read verse 10 again, then read to verse 11 to make that simple point: the church is really what God's purpose is. When we say sonship, it is not sonship of an individual; it is sonship of a people that is the church. "To the intent that now the manifold wisdom of God might be made known by the church to the principalities and powers in heavenly places, according to the eternal purpose which He accomplished in Christ Jesus our Lord." What they are seeing of God's wisdom is in the light of His eternal purpose. What is seen in the church is the fulfillment of God's purpose. The church reflects the fulfillment of God's purpose.

**05:00**

To make this clear, let us look at Romans 9:3. Paul says, "For I could wish that I myself were accursed from Christ for my brethren, my countrymen according to the flesh, who are Israelites." He so desires their salvation that even if it is at the cost of his own damnation, he doesn't mind. The Lord gave up Himself for our salvation; Paul is saying, "I wish I could be sacrificed for the sake of their salvation." Verse 4 says, "Who are Israelites, to whom pertain the adoption." That is the same word *huiothesia*, which is sonship. Ephesians 1:5 calls it God's purpose.

He didn't say to one person pertains sonship. He rather says to this people, his countrymen, his brethren after the flesh, pertains sonship. God's clearest depiction of His purpose in the Old Testament was with a people, Israel. And what they were to reflect is sonship. Sonship is not a reality that is about one person saying, "I am a son of God, I am in His purpose." It is really about the community. What God wanted is a community of people that are His children, amongst whom He dwells, and that are also His heirs.

As we start looking at the subject matter of the church, the church is the awesome fulfillment of God's purpose. We could talk about God's purpose from an individual standpoint, but speaking about it awesomely, the church is what God's purpose is. What we see as the people of faith is what God's purpose is. Those people reflect God's purpose. God's intention is for every single person to be in His purpose. 1 Timothy 2:3 says this is good and acceptable in the sight of God our Savior, who desires all men to be saved and to come to the knowledge of truth. He wants every human to be in His family.

There is no human that exists that God did not intend to be in His family. Now they have their choice to receive or reject His will, but every single one of us is made to be in God's family. The entirety of humanity is what ought to embody God's purpose. That the church is today called the fulfillment of God's purpose is because those are the people that have received His will for their lives. They have received the Spirit by receiving redemption.

One could have said humanity is what God wants, and you would almost be correct. He wasn't wanting one man in His life; He wanted a people in His life, a people in His light, a people of His Spirit. That is God's purpose. The fundamental understanding you ought to have about the church is that the church is the reality of God's purpose. When we get to the new creation, we also say this is the fulfillment of God's purpose, speaking more to the individual sense of it. But the church is really the complete, awesome embodiment of God's purpose.

It is a serious matter when we are discussing the church. You can't go it on your own when you are walking in God's will. If God's will is the church, it is not something that can be lived out just personally. It is something that involves this community that He ever wanted. I mentioned in Romans 9 that to Israel pertains sonship, which we have well established as God's purpose. You will find that Israel was also described as a church. To Israel pertains sonship, the glory, the covenants, the giving of the law, the service of God. This Israel is also described as a church.

**10:00**

Let's look at Acts 7:37 for a direct description of Israel as the church. Here, we see Stephen preaching. "This is that Moses who said to the children of Israel, 'The Lord your God will raise up for you a Prophet like me from your brethren. Him you shall hear.' This is he who was in the congregation in the wilderness." Some translations say "the church in the wilderness." Moses was amongst the people of God in the wilderness, and he calls them the church. That word "church" used there is the exact same word used for the church everywhere else. The book of Acts uses "church" for the church from the resurrection. Translators will prefer to change it to "congregation" in this context, but it is the same word, *ecclesia*.

He was with the *ecclesia* in the wilderness. Today we are called God's *ecclesia* by the work that Christ Jesus has done. The church that is fulfilled in Christ was foreshadowed in the Old Testament text. The people that made up the church in the Old Testament is what the writer of Hebrews, in Hebrews 12:22-24, calls the spirits of just men made perfect. They were righteous by faith, they were just by faith, but now they have been made perfect by the indwelling of the Spirit upon the fulfillment of redemption in Christ.

Hebrews 12:22 says, "But you have come to Mount Zion and to the city of the living God, the heavenly Jerusalem, to an innumerable company of angels, to the general assembly and church of the firstborn." What he calls the general assembly there is the church. It is the gathering of all people from different places, the entire congregation. It is describing where we are: God's city, God's house, God's temple. It is the congregation of angels, the assembly of saints, the church of Jesus. What else is it? "To God the Judge of all, to the spirits of just men made perfect."

If you read the earlier portions of the book, you understand what is calling the spirits of just men made perfect. He had talked about the elders who were justified by faith in Hebrews 11\. Hebrews 11:38 says, "Now the just shall live by faith." Then it says faith is the substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen. It was talking about the faith of people before redemption. They lived by faith, they were made just by faith, and it was a faith in the light of what would be fulfilled. By it, the elders obtained a good report.

Despite the report they obtained because of the faith they had that redemption would be fulfilled, verse 40 tells us, "God having provided something better for us, that they should not be made perfect apart from us." And all these, having obtained a good testimony or report through faith, did not receive the promise. They were made just, they were made righteous by faith, but they didn't receive the promise. The actual thing God always wanted us to have—His Spirit—they didn't receive it because redemption had not been fulfilled. So despite the depiction in them, they didn't have the substance of it. They didn't receive the promise, God having provided something better for us, that they without us should not be made perfect.

**15:00**

In that era, they were just, righteous by faith, but they were depicting the reality of what God wants: the church. So we can say the church was foreshadowed by them. Today, we can really say they are part of the church. We are now one body; they are part and parcel of the church, one congregation.

Not only was the church foreshadowed in the Old Testament era, you will see that it was still described by Jesus even during His own time, before He had fulfilled it in redemption. He was going to bring about His own church by the work of redemption, fulfilling God's purpose, but He was still describing a present church during His lifetime in the four gospels.

See Matthew 18:15. The Lord was speaking here about a situation where there is an offense between two brothers. If there is such a situation, you should go and tell the person alone. "Moreover if your brother sins against you, go and tell him his fault between you and him alone. If he hears you, you have gained your brother. But if he will not hear, take with you one or two more, that by the mouth of two or three witnesses every word may be established." The first step is to discuss it personally. Second step, take other people to have a discussion. The third step, verse 17: "If he refuses to hear them, tell it to the church."

His own church had not yet been birthed. He had not yet brought about the reality of the church God wants—a people indwelt by the Spirit. Yet there was what they knew to be the church then, just like Stephen was saying there was a church in the wilderness. They understood the church as a congregation of people called out in the light of something. This is where we need to explain what the word "church" means: *ecclesia*.

There are two words there: *ek*, which means "out of," and the other word is rooted in the verb *kaleo*, "to call." It means "called out of." A people called out of. There are many ways it can be used. It was used by the Greeks for their general assembly. Like when we have the House of Reps today—you have called some people out of the general populace to represent others. Sometimes people try to describe the church as being like that today. I don't think the Bible really presents it that way, that the church is a people that are a governing authority representing the rest of society.

The way the Bible uses *ecclesia* is quite different. It is a people called out of a particular contradiction or bondage. When it was talking about the congregation in the wilderness, these were the people called out of Egypt. A separated people, a holy people. There is a state of sin and death in the world; these ones are separated, called out from that common problem or bondage of humanity. That is the sense in which we speak of the church of Jesus.

By the time He says "tell it to the church here," it is still talking about Israel regarded as God's called-out people. Tell it to the church. At this time, the church was in a synagogue form; there were different pockets of gatherings. That is why later down in Matthew 18, He will talk about His own gathering where people will gather in His name. But they understood that there was already a church based on what happened to them, their redemptive work from Egypt. The Exodus.

He is saying this reality that is applicable now will be applicable when there are gatherings in my name. You will have that same authority. "Whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven." That text actually means what you will be binding on earth is what is already bound in heaven. What is bound in heaven? God has bound or forbidden darkness, death, evil. By the authority you have, you will be opposing the same things. You are not meant to have fellowship with such things. You are supposed to set aside the leaven to not leaven the whole lump. That is exercising the authority of the church.

**20:00**

People sometimes say what you bind on earth will become bound in heaven. That is not the idea. It is the other way around. What you bind is what is bound in heaven, and it is actually pointing to a future reality because it is the work of redemption that will lose life and bind death. It will defeat death in heaven and bring about the reign of life. Long and short, the church is the complete reality of God's purpose. It was foreshadowed in the Old Testament. It was still mentioned by the Lord. It had a futuristic depiction, an incomplete reality in the time of the Lord Jesus.

The third thing we see is that there is the church that Jesus builds. The Lord Jesus fulfills or brings to pass what God always wanted. The people of faith become part of God's one big body because of the redemption that Christ has done. In Matthew 16, there was a conversation that ensued by a question Jesus asked them. "Who do men say that I am?" Eventually, Peter gave the right answer: "You are the Christ, the Son of the living God." Whatever their conception of the Christ is is a different issue. They might think the Christ is supposed to be a national ruler that is going to have international clout like David, or a spiritual ruler whose spiritual rule is affected on the natural. But notwithstanding, they still believed Him to be the Christ, the Son of the living God.

What did Jesus say when Peter said that? "Blessed are you, Simon Bar-Jonah, for flesh and blood has not revealed this to you, but my Father in heaven. And I say to you, you are Peter, and upon this rock I will build my church, and the gates of Hades shall not prevail against it." You are Peter (*Petros*). Upon this rock (*Petra*)—even though we understand that *Petros* is masculine, the word is ordinarily feminine, so the distinction between *Petros* and *Petra* may not mean much here. It is worth pointing out that there can be such a distinction, where *Petros* was regarded as a little rock compared to *Petra* as a big one, but it is argued that such a distinction did not exist at this time. Also, Jesus must have been speaking in Aramaic or Hebrew, so the distinction would not have been an issue in His speech.

He says, "You are Peter, upon this rock I will build my church, and the gates of Hades will not prevail against it." This text has caused a lot of debate. Did Jesus build the church on Peter? The problem with that understanding is what is the very act of Jesus building His church? Whenever you read anything or see anything, you need to answer the questions of truth about that thing: What is this? When was it? Where did it happen? Who did it? How did the person do it?

The subject matter here is "I will build my church." There is a question of when. There is a question of where. How will He build His church? Who is going to build the church? He said, "I will build my church." We can put the "on this rock" to the side while we deal with the other questions of truth. When did He build the church and where? The gates of Hades will not prevail against it. That means His building of the church has something to do with a particular place, Hades, and Hades' authority. Hades is the realm of the dead. The authority of Hades is death. So the gates of Hades will not prevail against it.

**25:00**

Acts 2 in Peter's own teaching tells us that Jesus, in the work of redemption after His death on the cross, His spirit went to Hades. His soul went to Hades. This idea of Hades not prevailing against Him is not future to redemption; it is actually what happened in redemption. Death and Hades did not prevail against Him in redemption. Peter had no business with what was going on there. So the rock upon which the church is built, that Hades fails to prevail against, Peter was not yet in view there. The reason Jesus was talking about Peter in this text is a very important reason.

Look at Acts 2:24. It says that God raised Jesus from the dead, having loosed the pains of death, because it was not possible for Him to be held by it. He could not be held down by death. Death and Hades are one reality. Hades is simply part of the authority of death. That is why in Revelation 1:18, the Lord said, "I have the keys of Death and Hades." The defeat of death is itself the defeat of Hades. When it says the gates of Hades will not prevail against it, Jesus is saying it was not possible for death to hold Him when He went to Hades, the place of the authority of death. He bore fully the demands and consequences of the same and rose again from the dead.

Jesus building His church and Hades not prevailing against it is a specific event that happened in redemption. Debating over the fine details of the words while ignoring the substantial issue being discussed—what is being said, the questions of who, why, how—is not the way to read. The act of building the church was accomplished by Christ in His death and resurrection. The rock upon which the church was built, that Hades failed to prevail against, is Christ Himself. The ongoing sense of Hades never prevailing against the church is because of the fulfilled sense in what Christ did. The Rock is Christ.

He is bringing Peter into the discussion because Peter is the one who recognized Him as the Christ. "You are Peter, and upon this rock \[the reality of Christ\], I will build my church." So the church is built on Him. We are built as living stones on Christ, the chief cornerstone. Ephesians 2:19-22 says we are built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets, Jesus Christ Himself being the chief cornerstone. The church is a product of redemption. Redemption has given the legitimate right for all humans to become part of God's family. That is the church, the people of God. Redemption has made it possible for people to come out of death and darkness into life and light. The work He did has made it possible for people to come out of death and darkness into life. Hades cannot hold people back based on the work He did.

Ephesians 1:15-18 says, "Therefore I also, after I heard of your faith in the Lord Jesus and your love for all the saints, do not cease to give thanks for you, making mention of you in my prayers: that the God of our Lord Jesus Christ, the Father of glory, may give to you the spirit of wisdom and revelation in the knowledge of Him, the eyes of your understanding being enlightened; that you may know what is the hope of His calling, what are the riches of the glory of His inheritance in the saints."

**30:00**

What are the riches of the glory of His inheritance in the saints? The inheritance God has given to us is His Spirit. But that inheritance of the Spirit includes the reality of an inheritance involving creation, the severe inheritance. There are texts where you read inheritance and it is about all of creation, like when it says the meek will inherit the earth. There are texts where the inheritance is the Spirit. You must be aware of that in God's Word.

"That you may know what is the hope of His calling, what are the riches of the glory of His inheritance in the saints, and what is the exceeding greatness of His power toward us who believe, according to the working of His mighty power which He worked in Christ when He raised Him from the dead and seated Him at His right hand in the heavenly places, far above all principality and power and might and dominion, and every name that is named, not only in this age but also in that which is to come. And He put all things under His feet, and gave Him to be head over all things to the church, which is His body, the fullness of Him who fills all in all."

The power at work towards those who believe is the same power that was worked in Christ when He raised Him from the dead. What was worked in Christ brought Him out of the dead, placed Him far above all authorities, put all things under Him. By virtue of the fact that it was done for others, it accrues to them. He gave Him to be head over all things to the church, which is His body, the fullness of Him.

The idea of calling the church the body is that the realities of the head are the realities of the body. Calling the church the body is first an identification term. We share the same things. We are one. The head and body make one person. It is in this sense that He told Simon, "You are Peter, you are part of me." You are part of this building, this rock. You are part of me.

The church is Christ's body, meaning the church is one person with Christ. Marriage is used to describe that reality, where two are now one. One person is head, but they are now one person. There is leadership in that, and the same thing applies with Christ and the church. We are fused to one person, and there is yet leadership in that—He is our Lord.

1 Peter 2:4-5 says, "Coming to Him as to a living stone, rejected indeed by men, but chosen by God and precious, you also, as living stones, are being built up a spiritual house." Jesus is a living stone; you are living stones. We are identified with Him, fused as one with Him. Therefore, the church is called the body of Christ.

**35:00**

The second critical reality about the church as body, coming from that idea of identification, is that we all share the same reality. We are one, one body, one person. The church as a body means a community of people that share the same reality. We share the same reality across differences. That is precisely what the church means: a body across gender, race, and class. A people sharing the same reality across gender, race, and class.

What God has accomplished in the new humanity is that the old humanity struggles to exemplify equality of gender, equality of races, and equality of class. You will see them struggle to exemplify this. It is only in Christ that equality is found. God sought to fix this via the redemptive work of the Lord Jesus in the church. It would be most unfortunate if the church now continues to take on the same identity as the old humanity. We are meant to show people, "This is how things should be." We should reflect that equality of people across gender, race, and class.

Galatians 3:26 says, "For you are all sons of God through faith in Christ Jesus. For as many of you as were baptized into Christ have put on Christ. There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor free, there is neither male nor female; for you are all one in Christ Jesus."

All of us share this reality of sonship by faith in Christ. Being baptized into one body eliminates inequality along the lines of gender, race, or class. You are put on Christ. You are all just Christ. There is neither Jew nor Greek (race), slave nor free (class), male nor female (gender), for you are all one in Christ Jesus. The understanding of the church as a body is that we are one with Christ and we are a community of people that share the same reality across gender, race, and class. The church is one person with Christ.

Now, we proceed. In our study of the church, we reach an important aspect: the local church. When Jesus said, "I will build my church," the next contact we have with the word "church" in the Bible, after Matthew 16 and 18, is Acts 2\. In Acts 2:47, it says, "The Lord added to the church daily those who were being saved." This is not just about the spiritual reality; it was about a physical gathering of people. There is a sense in which we study the church as a spiritual reality—we have come to Mount Zion. But particularly in the book of Acts, the consistent use of the word "church" refers to a physical gathering.

**40:00**

In Acts 5:11, "Great fear came upon all the church." It's talking about the gathering of the people that are part of that spiritual body. This gathering of those people is called the church. We can choose to describe this as the local church. It depends on the terms used. In a denomination, you might say "the Fullness of Christ Church" is the general church, and the different locations are local churches. But in this context, "church" as that spiritual reality now has the intended physical gathering, which we refer to as the local church.

Is this physical gathering a matter of choice? "Well, we are the church spiritually, we can choose if we want to gather or not." Is the physical gathering a necessity or an option? Brothers and sisters, there is a necessity for physical gatherings. Every time the Lord spoke about the church, He said, "I give you the keys of the kingdom." In Matthew 18, He talked about binding and loosing. The authority the church has is meant to be exercised in its gathering.

All authority has been given to Christ in heaven and on earth, and He says, "Go therefore and make disciples." Despite being part of this spiritual body, the essence of being part of it is for you to make disciples. There is an instruction Jesus gave that births the local church. The whole issue of discipleship is rooted in God's purpose. The reason there is a necessity for the local church is God's purpose. The spiritual family that we are requires the actual interaction of a family. You cannot be a family without interaction. If the church reflects God's purpose—a family—there must be commensurate physical interaction that reflects it.

One of the words used to describe the coming together of the church is the same word used to describe a husband and wife coming together. 1 Corinthians 5 talks about when you gather together. There is a necessary physical interaction the church must have to actually function as a people of God's purpose. Family requires physical interaction.

Why do we need this physical interaction? The major reason the Lord gave is discipleship. That is the reason we saw in Acts 2:42. They continued steadfastly in the apostles' doctrine, in fellowship, in the breaking of bread, and in prayers. The physical interaction is for the sake of discipleship. Discipleship is like raising a child. Ephesians 6 says to nurture children, raise them up in the way of the Lord. You can't nurture a child in a non-physical way. We must banish the idea that there can be a purely non-physical gathering system for the church. There can be the application of virtual tools available to us today, but it can never take the place of the necessity of physical gatherings for the full expression of God's intention. We must interact as a family.

**45:00**

There will be meetings. There will be training to perfect the saints. There will be ministry—how it carries out its work. There will be leadership. There will be finances; the order outlines how finances are handled. There will be a natural order. 1 Corinthians 14 says let all things be done decently and in order. In 1 Corinthians 16:1, Paul says, "Now concerning the collection for the saints, as I have given orders to the churches of Galatia, so you must do also." He gave an order to the churches in Corinth, just like he gave to the churches in Galatia. Every church must have order—how it operates, how it is arranged.

We shouldn't have church splintering for no reason. People just say it's based on a "call." That call should be within the operation of the local church, which leads us to the operation of the local church. How does it operate? There will be doctrine that it should be founded on. There will be meetings. There will be training. There will be ministry. There will be leadership.

The next thing we are going to study is the mission of the church. We have to have a proper understanding of what Jesus sent us to do: evangelism and discipleship. There is a wrong understanding of evangelism sometimes. Evangelism is the full publishing or publicity of the Word. There is a value for one-on-one discipleship that we cannot ignore, which is the ability to effectively disciple people. We should factor that into our ministry for the sake of discipleship, but we must not ignore what evangelism actually is: the full publishing of the Word.

Your individual place in the body of Christ is very important. Starting with salvation. Once you are saved, Ephesians 4:7 says to each of us grace is given according to the measure of Christ's gift. You have an individual part and place in the body of Christ. There is a spiritual assignment God gives to everyone. Some people never do it. Some people may never know it. Every single day you waste not taking maximum steps in your spiritual assignment is a day of disobedience to God's purpose for your life.

It unfolds from salvation to discipleship, all the way to leadership. You start doing the work of ministry, then you enter into leadership, assisting in leadership and eventually functioning as an overseer. God wants us to take our place and unfold in our place in the body of Christ. That is the Church and its mission.
