**00:00 \- The Human Burden of Truth**

Brothers and sisters, we are looking at the gospel, the word of truth. I'll say that it is probably the most important subject matter that a believer can resolve or has to understand because every one of us, as human beings, is burdened with this question. We are burdened with the question, "What is the truth?" Interestingly, for some people, they have shoved that question aside, even though it's somewhere underneath on their hearts. But it's a burden nobody can escape. The question sometimes dovetails into things like, "Why am I here?" It sometimes gets into other questions like, "Am I sure there is a God?" It sometimes leads to other concerns like, "Maybe we are just being fooled. Maybe everything is just a lie. Maybe there is really nothing to anything. What's going on?"

There is this raging question that we have been burdened with. Interestingly, when people even have those questions, they start to get into fear like, "Oh, am I losing it? Why am I even thinking like this?" But there is nothing strange about asking those questions. It's actually part of how you are designed. In fact, those questions are meant to lead you into the right conclusion, which the Christian faith is going to claim to itself. They are supposed to lead you to God and to faith in Christ. Don't be in fear that you have the questions.

The reason why people tend to think that way is because before they even had the opportunity of reasoning on those questions by themselves, they had already been taught certain things in Sunday school, in church, or wherever they were going prior. At the point where you now have to question things you have been taught earlier that you have taken as dogma, it seems like you are going against the grain. But it's only natural that you have those questions. From the standpoint of someone who believes in God, he will say God put that burden on you. If you don't believe in God yet, you will say you are existentially designed to have those questions. It's a matter of your existential design. Some even call it existential questions, and they are natural. Why are we here? What is this all about? How did we get to be here? Humanity's greatest burden is the question of the truth of existence.

**05:00 \- The Gospel's Claim to Truth**

We are starting from Ephesians chapter 1, because we are going to see here that to this very question that we are burdened with, the gospel is making a claim that it is the answer. Ephesians 1:13. Paul speaking here says, "In him"—talking about Christ Jesus—"you trusted, after you heard the word of truth." He is saying you believed on the person of Jesus after you heard the word of truth, the gospel of your salvation. He's saying you believed, not because you were cajoled. You believed, not because it was imposed or coerced. He said, "In him you believed after you heard the word of truth, the gospel of your salvation."

He is making this very heavy claim that the gospel, the message of the Christian faith, is the word of truth, implying that it is the right answer to the question of the truth. We only believed it because it showed itself to us to be the truth. Now that will be quite a serious claim, particularly for someone who is yet to believe, or someone who has happened to believe but is getting shaky about his faith. How am I sure that this is the word of truth? That's what this teaching is for. This is the simpler version that can give you the basic and necessary foundation of confidence in the faith of Christ, and for those who don't believe, a solid ground and compelling reason to have faith.

The claim of the gospel is that it is the answer to the truth. But a claim is not enough; it must be proven. And that's precisely what we are facing with the gospel. The very person of the Lord Jesus makes this claim as well. In John chapter 18, he is making the claim that he is bearing witness to the truth. Bearing witness means he is giving evidence. He is not enforcing his own perspective or agenda. He is only showing you the evidence that this happens to be the truth.

John 18:37: "Pilate therefore said to him, 'Are you a king then?' And Jesus answered, 'You say rightly that I am a king. For this cause I was born, and for this cause I have come into the world, that I may bear witness to the truth. Everyone who is of the truth hears my voice.'" Pilate said, "What is truth?" Jesus is giving evidence to truth. And the people who are about the truth, they are going to heed his voice. If you are a person that is seeking the truth, you will arrive at that conclusion.

Another way of saying it—and I say this without intention of offense—is that one who doesn't believe the gospel, having heard it, is likely somewhere being dishonest or inconsistent about their desire for truth. Such is the implication of the claim of the gospel: that if you are really seeking the truth, you are going to agree with what I'm saying because the evidence will show itself so. There are several other texts in that regard. In Matthew 22, the Pharisees say, "We know that you teach the way of God in truth." In John 14:6, Jesus says, "I am the way, the truth, and the life." In John 8:32, "You shall know the truth, the truth shall set you free." There is no question that the gospel is really big on this truth issue.

**12:00 \- Knowing Truth Through Observation and Reason**

We have seen two things now. One, our biggest concern as humans is the truth. Two, the gospel's claim is that it is the answer to the truth. The big deal is, can it prove itself to be so? If the gospel is going to prove itself to be the truth, we need to find out how truth itself is proven in the first place. How do we know what is true?

If something is true, it's already true on its own accord, whether anyone knows that it is so or not. Imagine none of us are here, but the chairs are arranged in this hall, and there is no human being here to witness it. It is still true that there are chairs arranged in this hall. The fact that we don't know about it doesn't change the fact that it is true. There is truth independent of our knowing it, but there is our own process of knowing truth. If there was a phone on this lectern, and someone was asking what brand it is, it is still that brand independent of the fact that you know it. Our real issue here is how would a mind find out that truth.

You must take this disposition: that there is the truth about life and existence independent of me, and I sincerely want to know it. The implication of there being the truth is that I better find out, because without accepting the truth, I might be on the wrong side of it. There is a cost, an implication of not accepting the truth that I must not ignore. In 2 Corinthians 13:8, Paul says, "For we can do nothing against the truth, but for the truth." That is the cost and the weight that truth has. It is so serious that even your actions against it are invariably still actions for it.

Let's assume there is the truth within our earth space that there is gravity. Someone says, "I don't really believe that," and steps off a very tall building. If he acts against gravity, he's still acting for gravity. It's still going to take its toll on him. It just happens to be the case that he will suffer the negative implications of that. Same thing with the question we are asking here. Whatever the truth of existence is, your non-alignment with it will still affect you negatively. Being unaware that you are about to take a poison doesn't mean that the poison is not going to affect you. That's how important truth is, and that's why you must never joke with it. The voice of truth is the voice of God. You cannot afford to ignore it.

The basic, simple way we establish what truth is on any matter is by observation and reason. There is only one truth question that is not established by observation and reason, and that is "Why?". If a human enters a hall, observes it, and reasons about it, he's arriving at something that was true independent of him. Observation and reason can tell him *what*, *how*, *when*, *where*, and *who*. But it cannot tell him *why*. 'Why' is always someone's intention, which means it's on someone's heart and mind. That one requires revelation. Let's set that aside because that's not what we are looking at right now. We are looking at matters that can be established by observation and reason. We know the truth by observation and reason in matters within the physical world, the natural realm that we can directly witness, and relative to questions that are beyond that physical realm.

**22:00 \- The Gospel's Proof in Romans 1**

How does the gospel prove itself to be the truth? I want to show us the central text that examines this matter by Paul, Romans chapter 1\. The things he's going to say here are not unique to him. He's going to tell us that what he's saying about the gospel is available to all humanity.

Romans 1:16 says, "For I am not ashamed of the gospel of Christ, for it is the power of God to salvation for everyone who believes, for the Jew first and also for the Greek. For in it the righteousness of God is revealed from faith to faith; as it is written, 'The just shall live by faith.'" This gospel is making the claim of salvation and righteousness. For him to use the word righteousness, he's claiming there is a wrong. If he's making you right, it's like someone saying you are saved. Saved means there is a danger, a mess, a problem. There is a claim in the gospel about a problem. We have to see whether that claim is correct by observation and reason.

Now, look at Romans 1:18. "For the wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness and unrighteousness of men, who suppress the truth in unrighteousness." Notice what he just did there. He earlier told us that those who believed the gospel experienced salvation and the righteousness of God. But he's saying those who suppress the truth experience the wrath of God. He has replaced the word 'gospel' with 'truth' in verse 18\. For him to say that they are suppressing the truth and will experience the wrath of God, he is making a subtle claim that what they are rejecting should be obvious to them.

Look at verse 19: "because what may be known of God was manifest in them, for God has shown it to them." If something is manifest in them, it's on their mind. Their observation helps them to see God, and their mind should acknowledge him. Proceed to verse 20: "For since the creation of the world His invisible attributes are clearly seen, being understood by the things that are made." "Clearly seen" is observation. "Being understood" is reason. Is Paul saying that the claims of the gospel are just revelatory claims? No. He's saying they don't have a reason to reject the gospel because the things we are claiming are things they can see and think about. The gospel's claims can be proven by observation and reason.

**30:00 \- The Testimony of Creation and the Human Condition**

Are these claims unique to Paul and Jesus? No. There are claims that the gospel has made even before the fulfillment we find in Christ Jesus. See Romans 10:13. "For whoever calls on the name of the LORD shall be saved. How then shall they call on Him in whom they have not believed? And how shall they believe in Him of whom they have not heard?" The major issue that can make you believe is that you must hear it.

The next question is, can you say everybody has heard? Look at verse 18\. "But I say, have they not heard? Yes indeed: 'Their sound has gone out to all the earth, and their words to the ends of the world.'" He is quoting Psalm 19\. Let's see what Psalm 19 says that makes Paul confident that all men have access to the gospel's message.

Psalm 19:1: "The heavens declare the glory of God; and the firmament shows His handiwork." He is saying that the natural world is testifying to the reality of God. For us to witness the heavens and the earth, we are compelled to the conclusion of the divine. How does it do this? The fact that the world is finite—"day unto day utters speech"—when you say "day unto day," you are talking about time counting. If days are counting backward infinitely, there is no way we would have gotten to today. For it to count forward to this point, there must be a point that it began. The finitude of the natural world is testimony to the reality that it must have started. It has not just always been. Something must have caused it.

He is also making the contingency argument. A world that is not necessary in itself, that is finite, implies a cause. And a world with evident contradictions—death, darkness, disease—testifies to a problem. Why do we say there is a deficiency? We are observing the fact that there is a contradiction in the world. We feel that this should not be. There is a sense of privation.

How come there is good and evil? You conceive a contradiction where good and evil are against each other. How come the origin has brought forth a world where there is contradiction? The natural deficiency in the world means you need to rely on salvation, on God himself resolving the problem. Some say there is no God because of all these problems. You are rejecting what is first obvious because you are dissatisfied with what has gone wrong. That is not being honest. All competing worldviews are simply suppressing what their natural trajectory is leading them to.

**40:00 \- The Necessity of the Divine**

If the cause has equal or greater power, intelligence, and character than the effect, then observing a world with intelligence and character means there must be an entity with greater intelligence and character. The moment you are seeing an entity that has intelligence and character, you are moving away from an impersonal entity to a personal one, which leads to the conclusion of God.

Acts 17:28 says, "For in Him we live and move and have our being, as also some of your own poets have said, 'For we are also His offspring.' Therefore, since we are the offspring of God, we ought not to think that the Divine Nature is like gold or silver or stone, something shaped by art and man's devising." You cannot think that the divine nature is without intelligence. If you are his offspring and you have intelligence, he himself must have intelligence.

Second Peter 1:3-4 says, "His divine power has given to us all things that pertain to life and godliness, through the knowledge of Him who called us by glory and virtue, by which have been given to us exceedingly great and precious promises, that through these you may be partakers of the divine nature, having escaped the corruption that is in the world through lust." The divine nature includes power, intelligence, and character.

When as human beings we observe this finite and contingent world, the only rational conclusion is that an equal or greater cause must have brought it forth. A cause with power, intelligence, and character. That is what leads to the idea of a personal God. We are compelled by reason to the conclusion of God.

**48:00 \- The Reality of the Contradiction**

But then we are faced with a second observation. We observe the nature of the world, but we also observe the state of things in the world. We observe a contradiction in the world. Sometimes we call that contradiction suffering or pain. There is a sense that there is deficiency, a privation. We experience this contradiction in beliefs, in morality, and in the nature of the world itself—evils, disasters, sickness, disease. We see a deficiency and say, "This person is not the way he should have been."

How come there is deficiency? Are we saying there is a deficiency in the origin? That cannot be, because it's an absurdity for the origin to have a deficiency in the first place. This reality of contradiction in the world has led people across all cultures to redemptive faith. Every culture has a claim to the divine, and every culture has a framework for redemption. They might interpret it in different ways, and some might even reject the divine because of the contradiction, which is what Paul says in Romans 1: suppressing the truth.

The natural trajectory of observation and reason is to the conclusion of God. You are naturally compelled by truth to the conclusion of God. The first truth we can observe and reason about our world is: there must be a God. Second, there is a contradiction that can only be resolved by God. This contradiction can be conceptually described with light and darkness, life and death, truth and falsehood. You can never have darkness if there is not light first. You can never have death if there is not life first. You can never have falsehood if there is not truth first.

We are living in a world that necessarily begins with truth, begins with life, begins with light, and begins with good, but we are also seeing the contradiction of falsehood, death, and darkness. We are witnessing a contradiction that can only be resolved by the origin, by God.

The historical work done by the Lord Jesus Christ in his death and resurrection is the fulfillment of what was already philosophically necessary and prophetically promised. God, supernaturally dealing with it that way in the death and resurrection of Jesus, is how we are now confident that the matter has been resolved and will be consummated at the end.

Your actions for the truth will yield the positive outcome that God intends: life in his purpose and eternity in his purpose.
