# Commitment: How to Fulfill Purpose

### **00:00 \- The Mystery of God's Will and Purpose**

We are looking at commitments: how to fulfill purpose. I want us to turn our Bibles to Ephesians chapter 1\. No doubt, as we have seen in other teachings as a church, purpose is the most vital question we are likely to have after salvation. Anybody that knows what they are doing, once saved, will find the next question coming up in different ways. You might ask, "What next? What am I supposed to do now? Why all of this? Why did God even make us in the first place? I am grateful for salvation, I see the truth of it, but why did He make us?" The purpose question is bound to come up. There are several teachings we have taught to address that question of purpose, which is why we are starting in Ephesians chapter 1\. The purpose question is one that God answers by revelation. It is not something you can know by observation and reason.

So, what does God reveal His purpose to be? After this text, we will see Romans 8 to explain purpose a little bit, but we are going to a particular text to take off from there. Don't forget we are looking at commitment: how to fulfill purpose. I don't want us to assume when people hear commitment, "What are they about to say? They want to tell me I must commit." We are looking at it from the Word. How exactly does the Word present this issue?

Ephesians chapter 1, verse 9 tells us that God has made known to us the mystery of His will, showing that His will—what He wants—is something that He has to reveal. It is a mystery, meaning it is not something known by observation and reason. He says His will is according to His good pleasure, meaning what He likes. What God wants is based on what He likes. Typically, as we all start out, one day you say, "I want to become this when I grow up. I want to become that." Later you say, "I want to become something else." All these desires are majorly "I like to" at that point. It is more of different good pleasures cropping up. But at a point, it is all going to come together. It is more like, "I would like to become a footballer." That is the person's good pleasure. The person is now very serious at this point. "I like the idea of being a footballer. I want to become a footballer." But if the person goes one step further and says, "I am going to become a footballer," that is now determined. At that point, this issue has moved from good pleasure to will, all the way to purpose. The person is now determined to be a footballer.

### **03:04 \- Predestined to Sonship**

It is the same thing that Paul is saying here. He said, having made known to us the mystery of His will according to His good pleasure which He purposed in Himself. He is saying this thing that He likes, that He wants, is what He has made His purpose. What is this thing that He likes, that He wants, that He has made His purpose? We see in Ephesians 1:5, it says, having predestined us to sonship. The word adoption as sons, or adoption of children, is the word *huiothesia* in the Greek that can be best understood as sonship. He predestined us to sonship by Jesus Christ to Himself according to the good pleasure of His will. His predestining us is based on the good pleasure of His will. That means whatever He predestined us to must be the good pleasure of His will. His act of predestining us was said to be done according to the good pleasure of His will, so what He must have predestined us to will be the good pleasure of His will, which is sonship.

Sonship is that which God likes, that He wants, and that He has purposed in Himself. But what really is sonship? Romans chapter 8 tells us what sonship is. You exist because of sonship. That is God's eternal purpose, which He wants you to reflect in this world. Romans 8:15 says, "For you did not receive the spirit of bondage again to fear, but you received the Spirit of adoption \[sonship\]." The word adoption is *huiothesia* again. "We have received the spirit of sonship, by whom we cry out, 'Abba, Father.'" He is saying that the spirit of sonship causes us to have familiar relations with God as Father. It is by this spirit that we cry, "Abba, Father."

But we want to really see what makes up this purpose of God, which is sonship. Verse 16 says the Spirit Himself bears witness with our spirit that we are children of God. Now, we want to see something that must have been taught by Barnabas. See Acts chapter 11, starting from verse 22: "Then news of these things came to the ears of the church in Jerusalem, and they sent out Barnabas to go as far as Antioch. When he came and had seen the grace of God, he was glad, and encouraged them all that with purpose of heart they should continue with the Lord."

### **06:04 \- The Meaning of "Purpose of Heart"**

Remember, we have established that there is the reality of purpose in the faith of Christ. Barnabas went to Antioch. These people had been saved, they were glad and excited, and the church had been hearing news of them. Barnabas went there and told them, "With purpose of heart, you should continue with the Lord." You need to understand the word "purpose" here to understand what Barnabas said. The word purpose is the word *prothesis* in the Greek. It comes from two words: *pro* and *tithemi*. *Tithemi* is to put or to place. If you put a phone on top of a pulpit, you *tithemi* the phone on that place. *Pro* means before, ahead, in front of. So, it is to put something in front of someone. If people are running a 100-meter race, and you put the tape at the finish line in front of them, you have set the point they are supposed to get to. It is something set before them. It becomes their goal.

If someone says "with purpose of heart," it means with something set before you, you are to continue with the Lord. That means in view of getting to a particular end that has been set before you, you should continue with the Lord. If Barnabas was preaching this message, it is a message that must have been taught in the Jerusalem church: that people have the sense that with a goal in mind, we are continuing with the Lord. This idea that there is an end that has been set before you for which you continue with the Lord is a common theme in God's Word. We need to pay attention to it.

### **08:24 \- The Christian Life as a Race**

Let's go to Hebrews chapter 12\. One of the ways this theme comes up a lot is in the idea of a race. The central text of this teaching is on that matter, 1 Corinthians 9:24-27. We will come back to that. The idea of a race is where this sense of something being set before you is seen. That is the idea of purpose. With the sense that something is set before you, continue with the Lord.

There are ways people walk with God where they are not trying to get anywhere. They are not pursuing anything. There is no goal they are trying to reach. That is not how the Christian life is. The Christian life is a pursuit of His purpose. There is a goal set before you. You cannot just be walking around aimlessly. You cannot just say, "I am just enjoying this, taking it easy. Whatever will be, will be. Just live in the moment." There is a sense in which you can enjoy the little things, after you have established your purpose and you are walking in it. But to think that life is just about living in the moment, enjoying nature—these philosophies are not founded on truth. They are founded on feelings and emotion. They are not getting to the core issue: is there a purpose that God has?

Hebrews chapter 12, verse 1 says, "Therefore we also, since we are surrounded by so great a cloud of witnesses, let us lay aside every weight, and the sin which so easily ensnares us, and let us run with endurance the race that is set before us." Notice the idea of purpose is something set before you. He says let us run the race that is set before us, looking unto Jesus, the author and finisher of our faith, who for the joy that was set before Him endured the cross, despising the shame. He is saying here that there was something set before Him. The word there is *prokeimai*. It is a different word, but with very similar implications. Because something was set before Him, He endured the cross, despising the shame, and sat down at the right hand of God. He is telling us that the same thing is true about us. We will face all kinds of issues, but there is something set before us.

### **11:51 \- Finishing the Course**

When Barnabas was speaking to them, he knew there would be persecutions and oppositions, but they were still to do their assignment because there is a sense of purpose of heart. There is an assignment given unto us to fulfill. That is the nature of the Christian life. We are out to fulfill purpose. There is something we have to get done. Purpose is for fulfilling.

This idea of a race comes up over and over. Let's see another one in 2 Timothy chapter 4, verses 6 to 8\. Paul is speaking here: "For I am already being poured out as a drink offering, and the time of my departure is at hand. I have fought the good fight, I have finished the race, I have kept the faith." Before I finish this text, look at Acts chapter 20, verse 24, just to catch his thoughts. "But none of these things move me; nor do I count my life dear to myself, so that I may finish my race with joy, and the ministry which I received from the Lord Jesus, to testify to the gospel of the grace of God." There is a race set before us. Each and every one of us is born with a race set before us to finish. There is something God has called you to do. Paul talks about finishing his race with joy, referring to the ministry and spiritual assignment that is priority.

I want you to see what the Lord Jesus Himself said in John chapter 4, starting from verse 31: "In the meantime His disciples urged Him, saying, 'Rabbi, eat.' But He said to them, 'I have food to eat of which you do not know.' Therefore the disciples said to one another, 'Has anyone brought Him anything to eat?' Jesus said to them, 'My food is to do the will of Him who sent Me, and to finish His work.'" We can use "purpose" when we say "will," because we have seen in Ephesians 1:9 that purpose, good pleasure, and will are used in the same context. Everyone was living a life of purpose. No one was living with a "whatever will be will be" attitude. There was a purpose set before them, something to get done.

Back to 2 Timothy chapter 4, verse 7: "I have fought the good fight." At this point, Paul had not yet died, but he was about to, and he said, "I have finished." It is very much possible for someone to know when they are done. The Lord Jesus said the same thing in John chapter 17: "I have finished the work which You have given Me to do." Paul says, "I have finished the race, I have kept the faith." Notice the boldness to say that while he is still alive. He said, "Finally, there is laid up for me the crown of righteousness, which the Lord, the righteous Judge, will give to me on that Day, and not to me only but also to all who have loved His appearing." There is a reward. People were living a life intentionally to fulfill purpose.

### **16:14 \- Pressing Toward the Goal**

See Philippians chapter 3, verse 12: "Not that I have already attained, or am already perfected; but I press on, that I may lay hold of that for which Christ Jesus has also laid hold of me. Brethren, I do not count myself to have apprehended; but one thing I do, forgetting those things which are behind and reaching forward to those things which are ahead, I press toward the goal for the prize." That goal is the *skopos*—something set before you that you are looking at. I press toward the goal for the prize—*brabeion*, the reward—of the upward call of God in Christ. The call of God, the purpose of God, has an end goal, a reward.

Look at 2 Timothy 1, starting from verse 8: "Therefore do not be ashamed of the testimony of our Lord, nor of me His prisoner, but share with me in the sufferings for the gospel according to the power of God, who has saved us and called us." Sometimes people will just talk about being saved from damnation. But salvation is salvation into God's purpose. "He has saved us and called us with a holy calling, not according to our works, but according to His own purpose and grace." Our salvation out of darkness into light is a calling into God's purpose.

### **18:44 \- The Athletic Mindset of Discipline**

Paul says there is a prize of the upward call of God in Christ. That call has a goal you are pressing towards. You can read that text and say you are just looking forward to the glorified body. But there is an assignment of pursuit in that text. There is a part where we have something to do, like Peter talks about in 2 Peter 3: hastening the coming of the day of God. We are doing our parts in contributing to the fulfillment of what God wants to do at the end, and we will be judged and rewarded in that light.

Paul tells us how he ran his own race and fulfilled his purpose. In 1 Corinthians chapter 9, verses 24 to 27, Paul gives us an explicit explanation. If we are seeking how to fulfill purpose, we have to find out from Paul. Look at 1 Corinthians chapter 9, verse 24: "Do you not know that those who run in a race all run, but one receives the prize? Run in such a way that you may obtain it." He deliberately uses the metaphor of a competitive race. Even though the race of God's purpose is not a race against one another, he uses it to show that you have to be very intentional about winning. Everyone is competing against the assignment given them and their faithfulness to it. He is using that example to point to the mindset required. "Run in such a way that you may obtain it."

Everyone who competes is temperate in all things. The word is *egkrateuomai*. "Now they do it to obtain a perishable crown, but we for an imperishable crown." An athlete runs, competing with others to get a perishable reward. Paul says we are also competing, but for an imperishable crown. It is an athletic mindset. An athlete has a specific approach and discipline. The word "temperate" is the word for discipline. The person is disciplined in all things. That is the core word Paul used to explain his mindset toward purpose. Therefore, he says, "I run thus: not with uncertainty. Thus I fight: not as one who beats the air." The goal he is trying to reach is clear.

### **23:30 \- Order and Drive**

Verse 27, he says, "But I discipline my body." That word is *hupopiazo*, which means "I beat myself into subjection." The second word he uses is *doulagogeo*. "Lest, when I have preached to others, I myself should become disqualified." I bring myself into discipline so that I do not lose out on winning the race. I stay with what it takes to fulfill purpose.

You might be wondering why we didn't title this teaching "Discipline: How to Fulfill Purpose." You could say that, but we are looking at commitments deliberately. What are the things Paul says in his commitment? Notice in verse 25, he says, "Everyone who competes for the prize is temperate in all things." Then there is a "therefore" in verse 26, showing us the things that make for that discipline, for being temperate (*egkrateuomai*), for the ability to control oneself and submit to an order. Let me show you the second time that verb *egkrateuomai* is used. It is in 1 Corinthians 7:9, talking about containing one's sexual desire, if one chooses not to marry. "But if they cannot exercise self-control"—if one cannot *egkrateuomai*—"let them marry. For it is better to marry than to burn with passion." If a person cannot restrain his appetite, he should marry. If people are godly but do not take the necessary steps to manage this, they might remain volatile.

There is a need for discipline. Discipline requires that you know your goals. Second, it requires that you bring yourself into submission. What are you submitting to? You are submitting to what it takes to fulfill those goals. That is what *egkrateia* is about. I know my goals, therefore I subject myself to what it takes to fulfill them. Someone who does not want to marry knows he has to subject his sexual appetite in order not to marry. So *hupopiazo*, beating yourself, is in view of fulfilling a goal. I run not with uncertainty, I fight not as one who beats the air; therefore, I beat myself into the state necessary to fulfill the goals. After I do that, the second action is "I slave-drive myself." The translation could be clearer, because *hupopiazo* is to beat oneself into submission, and the second word, *doulagogeo*, is to slave-drive oneself.

When we talk about being disciplined, you bring yourself into an order to fulfill the goal of purpose. Two or three things make up this temperance Paul is talking about. Number one is knowing your goals. Number two is submitting yourself to the necessary order to fulfill those goals. He uses an athlete's behavior as an example. An athlete might be denied certain foods or comforts because of the goals they are pursuing. They subject themselves to intense regimens. That is subjection to a strict state just to achieve a goal. That is the idea of *hupopiazo*. And then the third is drive: "I slave-drive myself." A definite goal, a definite order, and drive. Notice that the definite goal is imposed by the purpose itself, but the definite order and drive are determined by the individual. When we talk about *egkrateuomai*, the temperance an individual has, it is a determination they make. I choose to do this to myself because of a goal. It is a commitment you make. You are the one who decides to do that.

### **30:14 \- Discipline as a Fruit of the Spirit**

We have seen thus far that as a believer, we approach the Christian life with a purpose mindset. We are purpose-driven. That is the way we are expected to think about the faith, as seen in Hebrews 12 and in Paul's letters. In that mindset, Paul explains that what makes you fulfill purpose is discipline: a definite goal, definite order, and drive. You are the one who determines the order and the drive. What we are seeing as this order and drive that makes discipline is the commitment of the person. That discipline—your own commitment to order and drive to fulfill the goals of purpose—is a result of growth.

Discipline is one of the things we produce from our spirits. Look at Galatians chapter 5, verses 22 and 23\. Remember, any fruit implies a corresponding seed at the root. A fruit of the Spirit implies a nature of the Spirit that can produce it. When it says the fruit of the Spirit is love, we have the nature of love, then joy, peace, longsuffering, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, and then self-control or temperance. That word is *egkrateia*, which is the noun form.

There is a nature that produces discipline. What is this discipline that is produced? You determine an order and a drive. When someone is disciplined according to 1 Corinthians 9, the person says, "I subject myself to this order, and I slave-drive myself." You become disciplined by the Spirit. When you grow, you commit yourself to an order and a drive to fulfill the goals of purpose.

If someone lacks commitment, it is a problem stemming from growth. If you are growing in Christ and growing in discipline, eventually, even if someone starts out saying, "I am not disciplined," they will recognize it is their nature in Christ to bear that fruit. They will pray and grow. As that purpose-related discipline grows, a commitment emerges: "I will follow this order, and I am driven to do it." There will be a point in growth where an unshakable commitment resolves. The person might start with a weak commitment, but it will grow to a strong commitment that cannot be shaken. They commit themselves to the necessary order to fulfill purpose and are driven to complete it. It is from spiritual growth—by a stand of faith—that discipline is born.

### **35:24 \- The Three Commitments**

The real question is this: if there is a purpose for us to fulfill that has goals, we need to find out what order it demands us to subject ourselves to and to be driven by. What are the things we are meant to be committed to? When Jesus gave us His purpose, the first thing He gave us was the church to disciple us to fulfill His purpose. The first commitment that we ought to have is to the church: commitment to the order of the local church and the drive to stick with one another.

The second thing is commitment to devotion. Devotion is where we are able to grow to fulfill everything in God's purpose. For some, their personal devotional life leads them to commit to the church; for others, it might be the reverse. But there will be a commitment to the local church and a commitment to devotion. Lastly, there will be a commitment to assignments, which is what Paul focused on in 1 Corinthians 9\.

Three commitments will be seen in the life of anyone who will fulfill purpose: a commitment to devotion, to the church, and to assignments. There will be growth to a point where the person commits to the order of devotion, the church, and their assignment, and is driven to do so. If the person is not committed to that order and drive, they have not yet grown in that regard. Perhaps you are not yet committed to your assignments. During the time meant for your assignment, you are frittering it away doing other things instead of dedicating that time. If you lack the drive to finish one task and learn the next, something is lacking in growth, allowing a disorderly walk. Paul speaks in 2 Thessalonians 3 about people walking in disorder, not working with their hands, living a life of disorder. These are people who have not grown in discipline to dedicate their time to their natural and spiritual assignments.

It is commitment that guarantees the fulfillment of purpose. Knowing God's purpose does not guarantee it. Knowing you are a new creation does not guarantee it. It is deciding, based on all you have known, to grow into commitment. It is commitment to the local church, to devotion, and to our assignments in order and drive that causes us to fulfill God's purpose.

When you commit to your devotion—not haphazardly, but following a clear order—and with the drive of the Holy Spirit, then you have started. You commit to the local church in its order. You recognize the meeting days and times, and you commit to the local church in that order and with drive. I see this assignment, and I commit to it in order, driven to complete it. Commitment to purpose in its order and with drive is what fulfills purpose. All that it takes to fulfill purpose is in your inward man. That fruit of the Spirit—discipline—comes from the nature of discipline that is already within you. You can stand by faith and grow to that point of commitment to purpose in its order and with drive.

### **40:36 \- Taking Responsibility for Growth**

If you are not committed, failing to take consistent steps to fulfill God's purpose, it is on you. It means you have not committed to purpose in its order and drive. It means you have not committed to the local church, to devotion, or to your assignments in their respective orders and drives. There is an order to your spiritual assignment that will guarantee fulfilling it. For a natural assignment, there is also a required order. The local church has a straightforward order: prayer meetings, midweek meetings, and Sunday meetings. You commit to the local church in its order. Commitment is not just being aware of it or showing up occasionally; it is committing to the order with drive. It takes growth to get there. When you find you are lacking, you have to assess yourself and ask why. It takes discipline, which is commitment to purpose in its order and with drive. In the place of prayer, as you grow in discipline, it will reach the point of commitment. When that seed of self-control grows to the right point, it manifests as commitment to the necessary order and drive. Then we know you are on the path to fulfill purpose.

We fulfill purpose by committing to it in its order and with drive. We *hupopiazo* ourselves, subjecting ourselves to its order, and we slave-drive ourselves with drive. I want you to pray for yourself concerning God's purpose and the three things He expects you to commit to. Where is the state of growth for this seed of discipline in your life? Has it grown to the point of commitment? Has this seed of discipline relative to the local church grown to the point of commitment? For some people, it has not. They just show up occasionally or watch online, which is fine if they are just starting or going through something difficult. But you cannot stay there. The seed of discipline in your life must grow to the point of commitment to the local church in its order and with drive. The same applies to devotion. Showing up haphazardly for a prayer watch here and there is not how it works. You must ensure that this seed of discipline relative to devotion grows into commitment. The seed of discipline relative to your assignments must also grow into commitment to its order and drive.

Pray for yourself regarding these commitments. Recognize that you have that discipline within you. You are well able to commit to purpose in its order. You are well able to commit to the local church in its order. You are able to commit to your devotional life—prayer, Bible reading, and hearing the Word—in its order. It is not an impossible task. Regarding your natural and spiritual assignments, there is no excuse. You are able to commit to them in their proper order and with drive. You are born of God. You can identify the required order for your natural assignment to accomplish what you are supposed to do, and you commit to it. The same is true for your spiritual assignment. Identify what it takes to get the job done. Paul said, "By all means, save some." You have to commit to it in its order and with drive.

I commit to purpose in its order. I commit to the local church. I commit to devotion. I commit to my assignments in their proper order and with drive. I grow in discipline until it becomes a firm commitment, to the point where I am no longer wavering.
