# How One State Solved Nigeria's 50-Year Power Crisis

## Summary
This documentary covers how Abia State in Nigeria achieved uninterrupted power by bypassing the broken national grid. Spearheaded by [[Professor Bart Nnaji]] and supported by [[Alex Otti]], Geometric Power Limited built a completely vertically integrated power system—from gas extraction to the end-user meter—in the industrial hub of Aba. It proves that Nigeria's chronic electricity problems are a design choice and can be solved by decentralizing control to state-level entities.

## Key Insights
*   **The Cost of Self-Generation**: Before the plant, manufacturers spent 30-40% of operating costs on diesel generators, which cost 47x more than grid power. When a city lacks power, industrial capacity dies. 
*   **[[Vertical Integration]]**: Instead of plugging into a broken national chain (generation -> transmission -> distribution), [[Geometric Power]] built the entire stack themselves: a dedicated gas pipeline, power plant, high and low voltage lines, and smart meters. 
*   **Political Risk & Sabotage**: The project took 20 years, enduring the 2008 financial crisis, banks pulling funding, and a 9-year legal war after the government mistakenly sold their legally binding territory to a different buyer during national privatization.
*   **The Power of Alignment**: The project finally crossed the finish line when the banker who rescued the project's funding ([[Alex Otti]]) became the Governor of Abia State, providing the necessary political authority to clear remaining hurdles.
*   **Decentralization as the Blueprint**: The 2023 Electricity Act allows states to regulate their own power. Abia proved the engineering works and set the legal architecture for other states like Lagos or Kano to replicate the model.

## Coach's Corner
**On Taking Full Ownership (Vertical Integration in Life & Business):**
Notice what [[Professor Bart Nnaji]] did. When he saw the national grid was broken, he didn't try to fix their broken system. He bypassed it entirely by owning the entire value chain. In your SaaS journey, you will encounter broken dependencies—unreliable APIs, flaky contractors, or platforms that change their rules. Sometimes, the only way to guarantee a result is to own the entire stack. Take extreme ownership of the critical paths in your business. 

**On Endurance and Playing Long Games:**
This project took 20 years. Twenty. It survived global financial crises, bank pullouts, and a 9-year legal war where the government literally sold their land out from under them. Most founders quit after 6 months of low traction. Building true enterprise value requires an absurd level of pain tolerance. When things feel impossibly hard with your product, remember that structural change takes time. You have to stay at the table long enough to let luck and timing (like Otti becoming governor) catch up with your preparation.

**On The Cost of Friction:**
Manufacturers in Aba were spending 40% of their operating costs just to run diesel generators. It killed the city's industrial capacity. In your life, what is your "diesel"? What inefficient, high-friction systems are draining 40% of your daily energy? Poor sleep? Doomscrolling? Disorganized workflows? Identify the friction points that are quietly bankrupting your productivity and build a permanent "power plant" to solve them.

## Action Items
*   [ ] **Identify your "diesel"**: Audit your week and find one high-friction, recurring task that is draining your energy, and automate or delegate it.
*   [ ] **Audit critical dependencies**: Look at your SaaS stack. Identify any external dependency that could kill your business if it went down, and map out a backup plan.
