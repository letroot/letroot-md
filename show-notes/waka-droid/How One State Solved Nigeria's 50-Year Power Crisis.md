# How One State Solved Nigeria's 50-Year Power Crisis

### **A Revolution in the Dark: Nigeria’s Power Breakthrough**
Africa's most populous nation, with more than 200 million people, is served by a grid that frequently delivers under 5,000 MW of electricity. For a country of this scale, that level of output is structurally insufficient. When a single state in southeastern Nigeria achieves uninterrupted power for 45 consecutive days, it isn't just news—it is the beginning of a power revolution. This is the story of how a small state solved a problem that has plagued Nigeria for over 50 years, and the $800 million investment that made it possible.

### **Aba: The Japan of Africa**
Aba, in Abia State, is one of Africa's largest manufacturing hubs. Geographically, it is a commercial sweet spot, with major population centers like Port Harcourt, Owerri, and Umuahia all within striking distance. Aba is not just a city; it is a factory. It houses the Ariaria International Market, which spans over 400 hectares and 3,700 stores, supporting up to two million jobs with an annual turnover of $3 billion. From a shoe cluster of 70,000 artisans making boots for soldiers across the continent to textiles, pharmaceuticals, and metalwork, Aba's industrial capacity earned it the nickname "the Japan of Africa."

### **The Crippling Cost of Self-Generation**
For decades, this industrial giant ran on diesel. Before the current power plant existed, Aba received only about 25 MW from the national grid, despite needing 228 MW—an 89% shortfall. When a manufacturing city lacks power, it is forced to manufacture its own. Diesel generators became the "real" grid, costing 47 times more than grid power. Manufacturers were spending 30% to 40% of their total operating costs just to keep the lights on. Consequently, Aba saw its operating factories dwindle from 165 in 2005 to fewer than 30 by 2024. The problem wasn't the skill of the workers or the quality of the products; it was the lack of power.

### **The Broken Chain of the National Grid**
To understand why someone would spend $800 million on a single city, you have to look at why the national system fails. Nigeria’s grid is a broken chain. In 2013, the government privatized the national power company, breaking it into six generation companies (GenCos), one government-owned transmission company (TCN), and 11 distribution companies (DisCos). The system collapsed because DisCos could only collect a fraction of what they were owed due to energy theft and lack of meters. This meant the DisCos couldn't pay the trading company, the trading company couldn't pay the generators, and the generators couldn't pay for gas. This cycle created a debt of over 3 trillion naira. While Nigeria has 13,000 MW of installed capacity, it often generates less than 4,000 MW for 200 million people.

### **The Visionary: Professor Bart Nnaji**
Professor Bart Nnaji looked at this broken chain and decided to bypass it entirely. Born in Enugu State, Nnaji is a world-class academic who studied physics at St. John's University and earned a post-doctorate in robotics from MIT. He founded robotics laboratories and invented the concept of geometric reasoning for computers. In 2000, he returned to Nigeria and saw a country sitting on massive gas reserves but living in the dark. He founded Geometric Power Limited, Nigeria's first homegrown power development company, with the goal of building an integrated system that didn't depend on the national grid.

### **The Integrated Model: From Gas to Meter**
Nnaji’s proposal was radical: one company controlling everything from the gas in the ground to the meter on the wall. The project included a 27-km dedicated gas pipeline from the Owaza gas field, a 141-MW power plant running three General Electric LM6000 turbines, 140 km of high-voltage lines, 1,500 km of low-voltage lines, and over 133,000 prepaid smart meters. This was more meters than all 11 other distribution companies in Nigeria combined. By removing dependence on the national transmission company and government payment systems, Nnaji created a self-sustaining loop.

### **Financial Crisis and the Entrance of Alex Otti**
Construction began in the mid-2000s but was nearly derailed by the 2008 global financial crisis. The primary lender, Diamond Bank, stopped funding mid-construction. In 2010, Nnaji sought a lifeline from First Bank, where he met executive Alex Otti. Otti believed in the project and pushed through an $85 million facility. However, Otti was then hired as the CEO of Diamond Bank—the very bank that had pulled funding. Upon his arrival at Diamond Bank in 2011, Otti’s first order of business was to restructure Geometric’s troubled loan and resume funding. By 2014, the turbines and pipeline were in place.

### **The Nine-Year Legal War**
Just as the project neared completion, it was hit by political sabotage. During national privatization, the government sold the territory Geometric already had a 20-year concession for to a different buyer, Interstate Electrics. This resulted in a nine-year legal battle. Even when courts ruled in Geometric's favor, they were physically blocked from accessing their own substations. The fully built plant sat idle for nearly a decade, costing $3.5 million in monthly bank interest and insurance while generating zero revenue. In February 2022, Geometric finally paid $26 million to settle the dispute and regain control of their territory.

### **The Banker Becomes the Governor**
By 2023, there was one obstacle left: gas supply. The original gas field had changed hands, and extraction had stalled. In May 2023, Alex Otti—the banker who had saved the project's financing years earlier—became the Governor of Abia State. With his new political authority, Otti worked with the NNPC to ensure gas started flowing. On February 25, 2024, twenty years after it was conceived, the plant was finally commissioned. President Tinubu called it a testimony to good leadership.

### **Aba Transformed: 24/7 Power and Industrial Return**
By late 2025, Aba recorded over 45 consecutive days of uninterrupted electricity. The impact was immediate. Major manufacturers like PZ no longer need backup generators, and Neimeth Pharmaceutical is building a brand-new factory in Aba specifically because of the reliable power. Geometric is now expanding, constructing a dedicated 41-km industrial power line and providing electricity to communities that hadn't seen power in over a decade.

### **A New Legal Architecture for Nigeria**
The success in Aba isn't just about engineering; it’s about a new regulatory model. Governor Otti signed a new electricity law in 2025, giving the state the power to regulate its own market through the Abia State Electricity Regulatory Authority (ASEPA). Abia became the first state in Nigeria to fully control its own generation, transmission, and distribution. The state is now acquiring a 60% stake in the distribution company for the rest of Abia, aiming for 22 hours of electricity statewide and exporting surplus power to neighboring states.

### **The Blueprint for a Different Nigeria**
Aba proved two critical things. First, that you can bypass a broken national chain if one company integrates the entire system. Second, that the 2023 Electricity Act allows every state to take control of their own power destiny. If Lagos, Kano, or Onitsha replicate this model, Nigeria's electricity landscape could look fundamentally different within a decade. The Aba Integrated Power project shows that the power crisis isn't inevitable—it is a design choice. Aba simply chose to design something better.

### A Blueprint for the Nation
The system is now expanding. Communities in Rivers State that hadn't had electricity for over 10 years are now receiving power from Geometric. More importantly, Governor Otti built the legal and regulatory architecture to make this model permanent. In March 2025, he signed a new electricity law giving the state power to regulate its own market. Abia became the first state in Nigeria to fully control its own generation, transmission, distribution, and trading.

Aba proved two things. First, the engineering works: you can bypass the broken national chain if one company controls the entire process. Second, the government argument: Nigeria's 2023 Electricity Act allows every state to do what Abia did. The blueprint is there. If Lagos, Kano, or Onitsha replicated this model, Nigeria's electricity landscape could look fundamentally different within a decade. The crisis isn't inevitable—it's a design choice, and Aba just chose differently.
