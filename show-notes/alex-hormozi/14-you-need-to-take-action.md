# You Need to Take Action

## Summary
Alex Hormozi draws a sharp line between parrots (people who repeat what they have heard) and practitioners (people who have derived knowledge through volume and repetition). He explains the difference between declarative knowledge (knowing about) and procedural knowledge (knowing how), arguing that only doing at unreasonable volume produces real competence. He introduces the "unreasonable volume" framework: do enough reps that it would be statistically impossible for you to still suck, then study your top performers and iterate. He also covers the proof-promise-plan-picture-pain content framework and the founder-mode concept from Brian Chesky.

## Key Insights
* **[[Parrots vs. Practitioners]]**: Parrots repeat what they have heard. Practitioners know why something works because they derived it from the ground up through repetition. If you cannot explain why you believe what you believe, it is not your belief -- it is someone else's.
* **[[Declarative vs. Procedural Knowledge]]**: Declarative knowledge is knowing about something (you can explain how private equity works). Procedural knowledge is knowing how to do it (you have actually closed a deal). Watching 10 videos on sales does not make you a salesperson; making 100 sales calls does.
* **[[Unreasonable Volume]]**: To build confidence in any endeavor, do enough volume that it would be unreasonable for you to still suck. If you asked yourself, "How many public speeches would I need to give before it is impossible for me to still be bad?" -- that is your target number.
* **[[Suck 100 Times]]**: Do 100 reps as fast as you can. Find the 10 that sucked least. Ask, "How do I suck least on the next 100?" Repeat until you are actually good.
* **[[Feedback Loop]]**: Volume without review is just sweating. After every 10-20 reps, look at the top performers and study what made them work. That feedback loop is what converts volume into skill.
* **[[Education Is Buying Time]]**: Education is the only way to buy time today. You purchase other people's life experiences so you do not have to pay for them with your own years. Rich people buy education with money; poor people pay with time (driving to 10 gas stations for 10-cent savings).
* **[[Proof-Promise-Plan-Framework]]**: Hormozi's content framework evolved through iteration: first proof-promise-plan, then added picture, then added pain. Each layer discovered through volume and review of what actually worked.
* **[[Founder Mode]]**: The founder always has special powers in the company because they know what built it. They know the conditions, they know how to break the rules, and they know when to bend them. This is the practitioner advantage.

## Coach's Corner

**On Being a Practitioner, Not a Parrot:** Daniel, as a SaaS founder, the temptation to parrot is enormous. You read a blog post about PLG, you watch a video about product-led sales, and you repeat the jargon. But until you have run the experiments yourself, those are someone else's beliefs, not yours. You need your own data.

**On Volume Over Theory:** The fact that you are technical is an advantage and a trap. It means you can build fast, which lets you get to 100 reps faster than most. But if you are spending 90% of your time coding and 10% talking to users, you are building in declarative mode. Flip the ratio for 30 days: 90% user conversations, 10% code. That is where procedural knowledge lives.

**On the Feedback Loop:** After every 10 sales conversations or onboarding calls, stop and write down what the top 2 had in common. This is how you derive your own frameworks instead of borrowing someone else's.

**On Unreasonable Volume for Your SaaS:** What would it look like to do 100 user interviews in 30 days? That is unreasonable volume. It would be nearly impossible to still misunderstand your user after 100 conversations.

## Action Items
*   [ ] **Calculate your unreasonable number:** For your primary skill gap (sales, marketing, product), what number of reps would make it unreasonable for you to still suck? Write it down.
*   [ ] **Do 100 reps in 30 days:** Whether it is sales calls, user interviews, or content posts, commit to unreasonable volume.
*   [ ] **Build a feedback loop:** After every 10 reps, review the top 20% and document what worked. Feed this back into your next batch.
