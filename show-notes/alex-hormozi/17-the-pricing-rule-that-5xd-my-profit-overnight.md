# The Pricing Rule That 5x'd My Profit Overnight

## Summary

Alex Hormozi breaks down a pricing framework rooted in the Pareto principle: 20% of your customers have dramatically more spending power than the rest, and your pricing tiers should reflect that reality. He introduces the "5 to 10x per tier" rule, demonstrates with real math how adding a premium tier can multiply profit rather than just revenue, and shares the actual tiered pricing structure he uses at [[Acquisition.com]] -- from a free hobby plan to $135,000 offers. The core argument: most businesses underprice because they fail to serve the customers who can actually afford to pay more.

## Key Insights

- **[[The 5-10x Tier Rule]]**: For every new pricing tier, you should 5 to 10x the price and expect roughly 20% of customers from the tier below to take it. This matches the natural distribution of spending power across your customer base.
- **[[Pareto Principle in Pricing]]**: Just as 20% of people hold 14x the wealth of the bottom 50%, your top 20% of customers have disproportionately more willingness and ability to pay. Your pricing structure should capture this, not ignore it.
- **[[Profit Multiplication vs Revenue Doubling]]**: Adding a premium tier doesn't just add revenue -- it disproportionately multiplies profit. Hormozi's example: if your base tier generates $80 revenue with $70 in costs ($10 profit), and your premium tier generates $100 revenue with $20 in costs ($80 profit), you go from $10 total profit to $90 -- a 9x increase, not just a revenue double.
- **[[Wealth-Adjusted Pricing Psychology]]**: A $100/month customer paying 10% of their income will be more demanding than a $100,000 customer paying 1% of theirs. Higher-tier customers are often easier to serve despite paying far more.
- **[[Top-Down Branding]]**: Start as high up the pricing ladder as you can. Tesla building down from the Roadster is a stronger brand play than Honda trying to build up to a luxury car. [[Rolls-Royce]] making "a Rolls-Royce light" is an easier sell than [[Honda]] making "a better Honda."
- **[[Start with the Unscalable]]**: Beginning with your highest-tier, most hands-on offer lets you learn what actually delivers value before you try to operationalize it at lower price points with thinner margins.
- **[[Acquisition.com Pricing Structure]]**: Hormozi's actual tiered model:
  - School (free/hobby) -- $0
  - Pro plan -- $100/month
  - L1 (live cohort) -- $5,000
  - L2 (advisory) -- $35,000
  - L3 (portfolio-level engagement) -- $135,000
  Each tier follows the 5-10x rule from the one below it.
- **[[One Tier at a Time]]**: You don't need to build all four tiers on day one. Add them sequentially over years as your operations mature. But knowing the full ladder exists gives you a roadmap.

## Coach's Corner

**On Pricing & Revenue Architecture:**

Daniel, this is directly relevant to where you are right now. As an early-stage SaaS founder, the temptation is to price low to "get customers in the door." Hormozi's framework flips that instinct on its head: your lowest tier likely barely covers costs, which means your profit -- the thing that actually lets you reinvest and grow -- has to come from the minority of customers who can afford and want more. If you only have one tier, you're leaving 80%+ of your potential profit on the table.

Ask yourself: what would a 5-10x version of your current offer look like? Not just "more of the same thing" -- what would genuinely justify 5x the price? More access? Faster results? Done-with-you instead of DIY? That's your next tier.

**On Brand Positioning:**

The top-down branding insight is underrated for SaaS. If you launch at the bottom and try to move up, you're fighting the "budget brand" perception every step of the way. Launching with a premium tier -- even if few people buy it initially -- positions your entire brand differently. It reframes what "normal" looks like for your product.

**On Profit vs Revenue Focus:**

The math in this episode is the wake-up call. Going from $10 profit to $90 profit isn't a tweak -- it's the difference between a side project and a fundable business. Don't optimize for revenue. Optimize for profit contribution per tier. The tier that covers overhead frees every tier above it to be almost entirely profit.

**On Execution:**

Don't build four tiers tomorrow. Build one: your current offer, done well. But know the ladder exists and plan your next tier before you need it. The operational complexity Hormozi mentions is real -- each tier requires its own delivery system. Master one, then expand.
