# Hard Work Won't Make You Rich

## Summary
[[Alex Hormozi]] argues that sheer effort is an amateur metric. Hard work matters at the beginning because you need enough motion to learn, but it stops being a meaningful edge once you hit your personal capacity. From there, wealth comes from [[Leverage]]: choosing higher-impact activities, delegating lower-value work, and building a team whose combined strengths outperform any single hard-working operator. His broader point is that great entrepreneurs evolve from doers into role-players and eventually into coaches who recruit, organize, and multiply talent.

## Key Insights
* **Hard work is necessary but not sufficient**: Hormozi pushes back on the "no one can outwork me" mantra because many people do work harder, and that alone clearly does not explain who wins.
* **Output equals activity multiplied by leverage**: He frames performance as a mix of volume and the leverage of the work itself. Once effort is maxed out, the only remaining lever is improving what you work on.
* **Beginner metrics stop working at higher levels**: Counting hours, early mornings, or number of tasks done may help a beginner get moving, but those metrics become noisy and low-value as the game gets more advanced.
* **High performers upgrade their calendars**: A practical way to grow a business is to inspect the founder's calendar and org chart, remove low-leverage tasks, and either delegate them or hire around them.
* **Entrepreneurship is a team sport**: Great businesses are not built by one superstar doing everything. They are built by combining different strengths across [[Marketing]], [[Sales]], [[Product]], and customer success.
* **Different business functions require different people**: A strong marketer is not automatically a strong salesperson. A product builder is not automatically the same person who can run operations or customer experience.
* **Founders should stop trying to be the star player**: Many entrepreneurs act like they need to score every point themselves. Hormozi argues they should increasingly act like the coach who assembles and aligns the team.
* **Leadership is a leverage function**: Real executive leverage comes from recruiting, culture, standards, and shifting the entire organization upward through better decisions and better people.
* **The CEO should have the highest return on time**: The founder's role is to keep replacing yesterday's tasks with more valuable problems, so the business keeps compounding beyond personal effort.

## Coach's Corner
**On why this matters for you as a founder:**
This is a useful correction because founder culture often glorifies visible suffering. Long hours, early mornings, and constant busyness can feel like proof that you're serious. Sometimes they are. But they can also become a comforting illusion that lets you avoid the harder question: is the work itself the right work?

**On effort versus leverage:**
At the start, brute force matters because you need reps. You need to ship, talk to customers, write copy, make mistakes, and build taste. But after a certain point, simply doing more of the wrong or mediocre thing does not move the business much. The upgrade is not "try harder." The upgrade is "choose better." Better channel, better offer, better hire, better system, better use of your attention.

**On founder delegation:**
A lot of founders cling to tasks because they can do them well. That is not the right test. The test is whether you are the highest-leverage person to keep doing them. If someone else can do the task at 70 to 80 percent of your level and it frees you to work on something materially more important, that trade is often worth making.

**On playing the right role at the right stage:**
This also matters psychologically. Early on, you may need to be operator-heavy. Later, if you keep identifying as the one who "does the most work," you can quietly become the bottleneck. The business then grows only at the speed of your direct effort instead of the speed of your systems and team.

**On building a stronger company, not just a stronger personal work ethic:**
The deeper lesson here is that winning companies are coordinated systems. Marketing, sales, product, delivery, and culture all need the right people in the right seats. If you obsess only over your own output, you may neglect the much bigger opportunity: designing an organization that produces great output without requiring you to carry everything on your back.

## Action Items
* [ ] **Audit your calendar for leverage**: Mark which recurring tasks are high leverage, low leverage, or probably delegatable.
* [ ] **List the functions your business actually needs**: Separate [[Marketing]], [[Sales]], [[Product]], and operations so you can see where one person is overextending.
* [ ] **Delegate one low-leverage responsibility**: Choose one task you should no longer own directly and define who should take it over.
* [ ] **Identify your best founder role**: Decide where you create the most value right now rather than where you simply feel most useful.
* [ ] **Use output, not effort, as the scorecard**: Review one area where you may be rewarding busyness instead of business impact.
