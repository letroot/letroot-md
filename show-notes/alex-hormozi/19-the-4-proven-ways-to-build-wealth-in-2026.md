# The 4 Proven Ways To Build Wealth In 2026

## Summary
Alex Hormozi lays out the four paths to significant wealth using a 2×2 framework: your money vs. other people's money crossed with your business vs. other people's businesses. The four resulting paths are bootstrapping, raising capital, investing, and fund management. He walks through each using real examples from the Forbes top 11, his own experience across all four, and clear analysis of the trade-offs, risks, and ideal candidates for each. The core message: poor people chase shortcuts, but the richest people pick one path, play it for a decade, and let compounding do the work.

## Key Insights
* **The four paths form a clean 2×2**: Your money + your business = bootstrapping. Other people's money + your business = raising capital. Your money + other people's businesses = investing. Other people's money + other people's businesses = fund management. Each combination creates fundamentally different [[Leverage]] dynamics and lifestyle trade-offs.
* **Bootstrapping is the slowest but safest starting point**: You keep 100% equity and control, but you incur every debt except financial — [[Management Debt]], [[Technical Debt]], and [[Talent Debt]] accumulate fast because you can't afford to buy your way out of them. Hormozi's gym, Prestige Labs, and Alan all started this way.
* **Raising capital trades equity for speed**: The biggest advantage is that you can pursue opportunities that are impossible to bootstrap — AI robotics, social networks, anything with years of negative unit economics. The trade-off is serving two masters (customers and investors), equity dilution, and the very real risk of losing control of your company. Steve Jobs got voted out of Apple; the founder tombstones of failed VC-backed ventures vastly outnumber the success stories.
* **Top Forbes wealth is dominated by raised capital**: 7 of the top 11 (Elon, Ellison, Zuckerberg, Bezos, Page, Brin, Huang) built through outside capital. Bootstrapped outliers include Ballmer (Microsoft), Dell, and the Waltons (Walmart). Buffett is the sole investing representative. Fund management appears further down the list.
* **Investing is the easiest lifestyle but the slowest compounding path**: You have no boss, no operational burden, and full diversification — but diversification reduces upside. Warren Buffett made the vast majority of his wealth between ages 80 and 95, and bought his first stock at age 11. The [[Compounding]] runway required makes this path unrealistic as a primary wealth-builder for most people starting in their 30s.
* **Real estate creates millionaires, not billionaires**: Main Street real estate is the most common path to a million dollars, but not the most common path to a billion. It stores wealth well but rarely generates the extreme velocity needed for the top end.
* **Fund management is maximum leverage on minimum personal capital**: Hormozi illustrates with a $5M GP contribution controlling a $100M fund that uses $200M in debt to buy $300M of businesses. At a 2.98x return over 6 years, that $300M becomes ~$900M. After repaying debt, principal, and preferred returns, the GP split generates tens of millions on a $5M check. This is [[Leverage]] on steroids — OPM (other people's money) plus debt plus asset appreciation.
* **Good deals attract capital; capital does not create good deals**: The key constraint in fund management and investing is not access to money — it's access to [[Deal Flow]] that others don't have. Proprietary deal sourcing and a provable edge in improving companies are what separate successful fund managers from failed ones.
* **Each path accrues different kinds of debt**: Bootstrapping accrues non-financial debts (talent, technical, management). Raising capital accrues equity and control debt. Investing accrues opportunity cost. Fund management accrues responsibility and reputation debt. There is no free lunch — you are always paying for something.
* **The right path depends on your stage, not your ambition**: Hormozi recommends bootstrapping for first businesses (pay off ignorance debt first), raising capital only when the opportunity genuinely requires it, investing once you have meaningful excess cash, and fund management only after building a track record and proprietary deal flow. The mistake is choosing the path that sounds prestigious rather than the one that matches your current reality.

## Coach's Corner
**On choosing your path honestly:**
The temptation is to skip ahead — to raise capital before you've learned to sell, to invest before you've earned, to run a fund before you've built anything worth managing. Hormozi has done all four and is explicit about the sequencing: build first, invest second, leverage third. If you're choosing a path based on how it sounds at a dinner party rather than what your current skill set and balance sheet can actually sustain, you're optimizing for ego instead of outcome. The most boring path (bootstrapping a service business) has produced more durable wealth than the most glamorous one (raising a VC round). Pick the path your current reality supports, not the one your future self's fantasy demands.
