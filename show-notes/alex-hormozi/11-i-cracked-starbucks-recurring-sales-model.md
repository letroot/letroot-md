# I cracked Starbucks RECURRING SALES model and built a FORTUNE...

## Summary

Alex Hormozi breaks down the single most important concept for building a fortune in business: selling things that people never stop buying. Drawing from his experience selling an eight-figure software company and now running acquisition.com (portfolio doing ~$85M/year), he explains why recurring revenue and customer retention beat constant new customer acquisition. The core insight is creating massive customer surplus — delivering value so far in excess of price that customers update their credit cards when they expire rather than churning.

## Key Insights

* **The Fortune Formula**: [[Recurring Revenue]] > Constant Acquisition. The only way to build a fortune is selling things people do not stop buying. [[Starbucks]], [[Coca-Cola]], [[Apple]], [[Netflix]] — all sell products customers continue buying indefinitely.
* **Customer Surplus**: [[Value Discrepancy]] between what you deliver and what you charge. If your Wi-Fi goes down, you update your card immediately because the value exceeds price by 10x+. Most businesses try to cut prices to create surplus; winners increase value delivered.
* **Methods vs Models**: [[Business Models]] > [[Business Methods]]. Methods are one-and-done (learn this tactic). Models are frameworks that evolve (franchises sell models, not methods). Media changes; models remain.
* **The Three-Phase Sequence**:
  * **Promotion** ($0-$1M): Make sales to get customers, not customers to get sales. Test product until people don't leave.
  * **Product** ($1M-$10M): Optimize until churn is 1-2% monthly max. Interview customers, survey them, analyze usage patterns.
  * **Scale** ($10M+): Marketing compounds because customers stick. If you never lose a customer, you never stop growing year-over-year.
* **High Value > High Ticket**: [[Pricing Strategy]] should reflect value delivered, not arbitrary "premium" positioning. A $500K house sold for $50K is a great deal regardless of "ticket" size.

## Coach's Corner

**On Building a SaaS That Sticks:**

Daniel, this is directly relevant to your SaaS journey. The SaaS world has quantified everything Hormozi is describing — CAC, LTV, churn, NRR. Read [[SaaS Metrics]] literature because service businesses are just "software doing the job service is supposed to do." The metrics transfer 1:1.

**On Your Current Stage:**

You're likely in the Promotion-to-Product transition ($0-$1M heading toward $1M-$10M). The trap is optimizing for acquisition before retention. Most indie hackers obsess over Stripe dashboards and new signups. But if you're losing 10-20% monthly churn, you're running on a treadmill. Fix the leak before pouring more water in.

**On Customer Surplus for Your Product:**

Ask: If a customer's card expired, would they email support to fix it? Or would they silently churn? If the latter, you don't have surplus yet. Ways to increase it:
- Reduce [[Time-to-Value]] — how fast does a new user get their first win?
- Increase [[Outcome Certainty]] — can you guarantee or at least de-risk the result?
- Layer [[Services]] on top of software — onboarding calls, done-for-you setup, community
- Build [[Network Effects]] or [[Data Moats]] — the longer they use it, the harder to leave

**On Method vs Model:**

Don't sell a tactic ("I help you send cold emails"). Sell a model ("I help you build a predictable customer acquisition system"). Tactics die (cold email → LinkedIn → Twitter → AI). Models persist (systematic outreach → systematic content → systematic partnerships).

**On the 20-Year Horizon:**

Hormozi mentions the consultant who sold the same number of clients but made $300K/month vs his $4M/month. The difference? Retention and monetization. If you sell 100 B2B clients/month, you can build a $100M enterprise — *if* you don't lose them. Play the long game. Do the boring work of making customers stay.

## Action Items

* [ ] **Audit Your Churn**: Calculate monthly churn rate. If >5%, stop all acquisition experiments and fix retention first.
* [ ] **Interview 5 Ex-Customers**: Ask why they left. Look for patterns in the first 7-30 days.
* [ ] **Map Your Value Equation**: For your core customer, quantify: (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay + Effort + Sacrifice). Where can you 10x the numerator or cut the denominator in half?
* [ ] **Design a "Credit Card Test"**: What feature or outcome would make a customer update their expired card rather than churning? Build toward that.
* [ ] **Read** [[The SaaS Playbook]] by Rob Walling or [[From Impossible to Inevitable]] by Aaron Ross — both obsess over retention before scale.
