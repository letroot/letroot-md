# You're Not a Victim of Your Circumstances. Keep Going.

## Summary
Alex Hormozi pushes back hard against the victim mentality, using a striking logic trap: if you blame your circumstances for your lack of progress, what about the people who depend on you -- does that mean you blame your children for the things you cannot achieve? He frames hard work not as suffering but as the thing you do for fun, drawing on his own story of losing everything (including a partner who absconded with his money to Sweden) and making more in the next 12 months than in the previous five years combined. The core message: the outcome of your work is not just what you produce but who you become in the process.

## Key Insights
* **[[The Victim Logic Trap]]**: If you say "must be nice" or blame circumstances for your lack of progress, the logical conclusion is that you blame the people in your life (including your kids) for your limitations. That is a tough weight to put on anyone.
* **[[Feelings Are Temporary]]**: "You don't have to feel good about it. You just have to keep going. The feeling will pass, but you will remain. You are greater than your feelings."
* **[[Working Hard Is Not Wasted Time]]**: Many people associate working hard with not making progress, so working feels like wasting. But whatever you amount to is not what matters -- who you become in the process is what lasts.
* **[[The Works on You More Than You Work on It]]**: A favorite quote. The work transforms you. Even if the specific venture does not produce the expected outcome, you become better through doing it.
* **[[Profit in All Labor]]**: A biblical proverb: "There is profit in all labor." Even if the outcome does not match expectations, the skills and experience compound.
* **[[The Five-Year Compounding Story]]**: Hormozi spent five years building a chain of gyms, sold five locations, got a big payday, then lost everything when his partner filed bankruptcy and sent the money to his girlfriend in Sweden. In the next 12 months, he made more profit than the previous five years times five. The five years were not wasted -- they made him who he needed to be.
* **[[Work Is Fun for Some People]]**: The reason people get confused about your workload is that they do not realize the thing you do for work is the thing you do for fun. Working hard does not feel like wasted time when you genuinely enjoy the craft.
* **[[Small Mountains Lead to Big Mountains]]**: The more able you become, the more able you realize you can become. You get bigger outcomes from the path that led you here than you think possible.

## Coach's Corner

**On Rejecting Victimhood:** Daniel, as an early-stage SaaS founder, there will be weeks where nothing seems to work and it feels like the market, the algorithm, or the competition is against you. That is the moment to remember this: blaming circumstances is a logical dead end. The only productive question is "what do I do next?"

**On Who You Become:** Your first SaaS might not be the one that succeeds. The codebase might get rewritten. The product might pivot. But the skills you build -- shipping under pressure, talking to users, closing a sale, debugging at 2 AM -- those are permanent. They are the compound interest of effort. The five years you spend building something that does not work out is not a waste. It is the prerequisite for the 12 months that changes everything.

**On Working Hard as Fun:** If you are not having fun doing the work itself, you need to seriously question whether you are on the right path. The founders who make it are the ones who would code, sell, and build even if nobody was watching and no money was on the line. Find the version of your work that feels like play.

**On Feelings as Weather:** Going to bed late and waking up early to work for a few days will not kill you. Burnout is real, but most people calling it burnout are just uncomfortable with sustained effort. Sit with the discomfort. It passes. You remain.

## Action Items
*   [ ] **Identify one area where you are blaming circumstances:** Write it down. Now write down what you would do differently if you fully owned the outcome.
*   [ ] **Reframe a "wasted" effort:** Take something you tried that did not work out and list the skills, relationships, or insights you gained from it. That list is the real ROI.
*   [ ] **Audit your relationship with hard work:** Is the thing you are building something you would do for fun even without the money? If not, what needs to change?
