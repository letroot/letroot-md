# Are You Even Trying

## Summary
Alex Hormozi argues that the reason most people stay average is not lack of talent or belief -- it is simple underestimation of volume. He tells the story of putting out 300 flyers, getting one call (about a dinged Mercedes), and thinking marketing did not work, only to learn his mentor tested with 5,000 per batch. He draws a direct line between output volume and outcome quality, making the point that people who seem exceptional are often doing 10,000x the work, not 2-3x. The only way past the volume barrier is to accept that the hard way is the only way.

## Key Insights
* **[[The Volume Gap]]**: People wildly underestimate the amount of work required. The difference between average and exceptional is not talent -- it is that the top performer has done 300x more reps, not 2-3x.
* **[[The Flyer Story]]**: Hormozi put out 300 flyers, got one call, and concluded marketing did not work. His mentor tested with 5,000 per batch and then deployed 5,000 per day once he found a winner. That is 150,000 flyers per month versus 300 total. The method was the same; the volume was the variable.
* **[[Content Volume]]**: Acquisition.com produces 450 pieces of content per week. Someone who has been posting for 90 days has produced 90 posts. Hormozi produces that in two days.
* **[[Sales Volume]]**: A salesperson who has taken 400 live calls over 90 days is being compared to someone who has closed 4,000 deals (not calls -- deals), which required roughly 12,000 calls. That is a 30x gap, not a small difference.
* **[[The Easy Way Does Not Exist]]**: A huge amount of the delay when starting out is the time spent searching for an easier way. People only start the hard way after giving up the search for a shortcut. The hard way is the only way.
* **[[Doing vs. Talking]]**: People do a lot of talking about doing and not a lot of doing. It is the doing that does everything. Lack of motivation, focus, or any other excuse boils down to number of actions taken per unit of time.

## Coach's Corner

**On the Volume Gap for SaaS:** Daniel, if you are feeling stuck, the first question is not "what is wrong with my strategy?" It is "how many reps have I actually done?" If you have done 10 sales conversations, you are at the 300-flyers stage. You do not have enough data to conclude that anything does or does not work. Get to 5,000-equivalent reps first.

**On the Easier-Path Trap:** As a technical founder, the "easier path" often manifests as over-engineering. You build a feature instead of making 10 sales calls. You refactor code instead of shipping and talking to users. The search for the easier way is itself the delay. Accept that the hard, unglamorous work is the work.

**On Content as Volume Practice:** If you are producing content, compare your weekly output to 450 pieces. Not to demoralize yourself, but to calibrate. You are probably at 1-3 pieces per week. Scale it up: 5, then 10, then 20. The data you get from each piece compounds.

**On the Sales Conversation Math:** 400 calls over 90 days sounds like a lot until you realize the top closer has done 12,000. Set a pace that would be unreasonable to sustain and see what happens to your skill in 30 days.

## Action Items
*   [ ] **Count your actual reps:** How many sales calls, user interviews, content pieces, or outreach attempts have you done this month? Write down the real number.
*   [ ] **Multiply by 10:** Whatever volume you think is sufficient, multiply it by 10. That is closer to the actual threshold.
*   [ ] **Stop searching for the shortcut:** For the next 30 days, ban yourself from reading about new strategies, tactics, or frameworks. Instead, do more reps with your current approach.
