# How to Get What You Want

## Summary
[[Alex Hormozi]] argues that goals by themselves are weak predictors of success because winners and losers often want the same things. The real difference is whether someone consistently performs the behaviors that produce the result, and deeper still, whether they become the kind of person who naturally does those behaviors. His practical tool is simple: at every decision point, ask what the person you want to become would do, then act in accordance with that identity. Over time, repeated action becomes evidence, and that evidence reshapes self-concept.

## Key Insights
* **Goals are not the differentiator**: Hormozi points out that high performers and low performers often share the same stated ambitions. The separating factor is not the goal itself but the repeated behaviors underneath it.
* **Results come from behaviors, but behaviors come from identity**: The more durable path is not obsessing over outcomes alone but becoming the kind of person who naturally performs the required actions.
* **Identity is built by repeated action**: Who you are is reinforced by what you repeatedly do. Each action acts like a vote for the kind of person you are becoming.
* **Use identity questions at decision points**: A powerful shortcut is asking questions like "What would a wise man do?" or "What would a billionaire do?" in real situations rather than relying on abstract motivation.
* **Affirmations without evidence are weak**: Simply saying you are disciplined, wealthy, or excellent does little unless your actions provide proof that the statement is becoming true.
* **A simple question can replace giant checklists**: Instead of carrying around a huge system of rules, one identity-based question can guide behavior across many unpredictable scenarios.
* **Most people already know what to do**: The issue is often not lack of information but lack of follow-through on obvious actions.
* **Long-term success comes from internalization**: When the work becomes part of who you are rather than just a means to an external reward, consistency becomes much easier.
* **External rewards can weaken effort if identity is missing**: If someone acts only to hit a short-term outcome, they may ease off once they get it. Identity-based effort sustains momentum beyond one result.

## Coach's Corner
**On why this matters for a founder:**
This is especially useful when you're building something hard because startup life constantly confronts you with small decisions that shape your trajectory. Whether you ship the feature, make the sales call, send the outreach, face the uncomfortable conversation, or hide inside easier work all becomes part of who you are. The company you build is downstream of those repeated choices.

**On identity versus motivation:**
Motivation is unreliable. Some days you will feel clear, aggressive, and focused. Other days you will feel uncertain, tired, or discouraged. If your system depends on feeling ready, you will have long gaps in execution. But if your system depends on identity, the question changes from "Do I feel like doing this?" to "What would the founder I want to become do right now?" That question is much more stable.

**On becoming the kind of founder your company needs:**
At your stage, you probably do not need a thousand productivity tricks. You need a sharper self-concept. The founder who wins is often the one who keeps doing the obvious but uncomfortable things: talking to users, improving the offer, following up leads, clarifying positioning, and shipping important work. A lot of growth comes from becoming someone who does not negotiate with those basics.

**On why this is powerful in practice:**
This also helps when the exact next move is unclear. You may not always know the full roadmap, but you can still ask what a disciplined operator, a thoughtful builder, or a serious businessman would do next. That framing keeps you moving forward even when the path is incomplete.

**On self-respect and momentum:**
One underrated part of this is internal trust. Every time you act in line with the identity you want, you build evidence that you are that person. That matters because founders often lose momentum not only from bad strategy but from eroded self-respect. When you keep promises to yourself, your confidence becomes grounded instead of theatrical.

## Action Items
* [ ] **Pick one identity question**: Choose a prompt you can reuse daily, such as "What would a serious founder do right now?"
* [ ] **Use it at real crossroads**: Apply the question when deciding between deep work and distraction, shipping and delaying, outreach and avoidance.
* [ ] **Define the obvious actions**: Write down the 3 to 5 recurring behaviors that the founder you want to become consistently does.
* [ ] **Replace affirmation with evidence**: Focus less on saying who you want to be and more on collecting proof through repeated action.
* [ ] **Audit where you already know the answer**: Notice one area where the blocker is not confusion but reluctance to do the obvious work.
