# You're Not Behind: My System for Outworking Everyone

## Summary
[[Alex Hormozi]] lays out a blunt system for moving dramatically faster in business by focusing only on the activities that actually grow a company. His argument is that most founders are not behind because they lack talent or intelligence. They are behind because they spend too little time on the few levers that matter and too much time on low-value work, distractions, and self-created obligations. The fix is to identify the true growth inputs, protect deep maker time, and aggressively buy back hours from daily life so those hours can be reinvested into the business.

## Key Insights
* **Business growth comes from four core levers**: The business only grows by getting more customers, increasing [[Conversion]], raising prices, or getting customers to buy more times.
* **Most people are busy, not effective**: A lot of founder effort goes into tasks that feel like work but do not move one of the core levers. Hormozi argues that this kind of work can be almost equivalent to doing nothing.
* **Traffic and conversion dominate early-stage focus**: For smaller businesses, the biggest gains usually come from advertising more or better and from improving the ability to close interested prospects.
* **Skill is built through repetition and feedback**: Whether it is [[Advertising]] or [[Sales]], mastery comes from high-volume practice plus tight feedback loops, not from scattered effort.
* **Every constraint should get concentrated focus**: Once you identify the current bottleneck, you should attack it intensely until another constraint becomes the true limiter.
* **Maker time and manager time are different games**: [[Maker Time]] requires long, uninterrupted blocks for deep work, while manager time is fragmented and optimized for meetings, coordination, and decisions.
* **Interruptions destroy deep work**: A single meeting dropped into a maker block can wipe out most of the value of that whole window.
* **Founders need to buy back time**: Hormozi makes a strong case for outsourcing food prep, cleaning, laundry, and commuting friction if the founder can reinvest those recovered hours into higher-value work.
* **Elimination beats optimization**: He argues that most productivity hacks matter less than simply removing energy drains and low-value responsibilities from your life.
* **Real speed compounds from focused hours**: The founder who consistently spends meaningful hours on the right problems can move vastly faster than someone who feels busy all week but rarely gets true deep work done.

## Coach's Corner
**On why this lands so hard for founders:**
This is one of those messages that sounds obvious until you really audit your week. A founder can feel maxed out, exhausted, and fully committed while still spending too little time on the actual growth engine of the company. That is the trap. Effort alone is emotionally convincing. Output is the thing that tells the truth.

**On the danger of fake work:**
A lot of founder work is disguised avoidance. Tinkering, admin, over-planning, checking tools, refining internal systems, and handling every little loose end can create the feeling of momentum without producing much real movement. The painful part is that fake work often feels responsible. But if it does not meaningfully improve traffic, conversion, pricing, or retention, it is probably secondary right now.

**On maker time as a founder weapon:**
If you are building a SaaS or any early-stage business, uninterrupted time is not a luxury. It is a competitive advantage. You need blocks where you can think clearly, write, build, analyze, and solve one meaningful problem without being scattered. If your whole day is fragmented, you may be present for the business without actually advancing it.

**On buying back your life to reinvest in the business:**
This is a useful mindset shift. A founder should not evaluate outsourced chores only as expenses. They should also evaluate them as recovered strategic capacity. If buying back 10 to 20 hours a week lets you spend those hours on distribution, product, sales, or customer learning, the trade is often excellent. The catch is that the recovered time has to be used intentionally rather than leaking into more distraction.

**On focusing on the actual constraint:**
This may be the sharpest lesson in the whole talk. You do not need to solve every problem right now. You need to solve the problem currently limiting growth. That requires honesty. Some founders obsess over cheaper leads when their real issue is weak close rates. Others keep improving product when the real issue is that not enough people know they exist. Strategy gets clearer when you stop trying to fix everything at once.

## Action Items
* [ ] **Audit last week against the four levers**: Check how much of your time actually went toward traffic, [[Conversion]], pricing, or repeat purchase/retention.
* [ ] **Create protected maker blocks**: Reserve at least a few uninterrupted weekly blocks for the highest-leverage work in the business.
* [ ] **Name the current constraint**: Decide whether your main bottleneck is acquisition, sales, pricing, onboarding, or retention.
* [ ] **Eliminate one recurring time drain**: Outsource or remove one chore or obligation that regularly steals energy from important work.
* [ ] **Match your calendar to your stage**: If the business is small, bias more of your time toward growth activities rather than maintenance tasks.
