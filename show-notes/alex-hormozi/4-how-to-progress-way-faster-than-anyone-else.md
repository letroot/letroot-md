# How To Progress Way Faster Than Anyone Else

## Summary
[[Alex Hormozi]] argues that most people do not fail because they picked the wrong strategy. They fail because they keep switching strategies before giving any sensible path enough time and force to work. Real progress comes less from finding the perfect plan and more from having conviction, sticking with the path, and building an organization that can actually execute. He breaks the execution problem into three practical layers: culture, talent, and training.

## Key Insights
* **Most people overvalue the perfect plan**: Hormozi compares progress to climbing a mountain. There may be multiple valid paths up, but constantly switching paths slows you down more than committing to a good one.
* **Conviction matters more than perfection**: In most cases, you do not need a flawless strategy. You need enough belief and consistency to stay on a sensible strategy long enough for it to compound.
* **The fallacy of the perfect pick keeps people stuck**: Waiting for the perfect choice feels intelligent, but it often becomes a disguised form of avoidance. The cost is lost time and fragmented effort.
* **Focus beats spread-thin ambition**: Hormozi describes a period where he ran too many businesses at once and made little progress. The issue was not lack of opportunity but lack of concentrated execution.
* **Most people quit at confusion, not effort**: The hard part is often not working hard. It is staying in the uncomfortable phase where you do not yet know what to do next.
* **Execution is the real edge**: Many strategies can work. What separates winners is the sharpness of execution behind the chosen strategy.
* **Culture is the rule system of reinforcement**: [[Culture]] is created by what gets rewarded, ignored, and punished. Teams learn what matters from what leaders consistently tolerate.
* **Talent quality is massively underpriced**: Great people often cost modestly more but can produce multiple times the output. Raising the bar on [[Hiring]] can create outsized returns.
* **Obsession predicts role fit**: A useful hiring signal is what people do in their discretionary time. If they naturally spend that time on the craft they are being hired for, they are more likely to compound.
* **Training requires behavioral specificity**: Big labels like "accountability" are too vague to coach. [[Training]] works when abstract values are translated into concrete repeatable actions.

## Coach's Corner
**On why this matters for founders:**
This is one of the more useful founder reminders because it attacks a very common trap: changing direction too early. Early-stage companies often look messy, uncertain, and slow. That does not automatically mean the strategy is wrong. Sometimes it means the founder has not stayed with it long enough, the team cannot execute it well enough, or the systems under it are too weak.

**On strategy-hopping as sophisticated avoidance:**
There is a very seductive kind of procrastination that looks like thinking. You keep revisiting positioning, offers, channels, pricing, product direction, or GTM because it feels like responsible leadership. Sometimes it is. But often it is emotional escape from the harder work of enduring confusion long enough to figure things out. Switching paths resets the pain, but it also resets the progress.

**On your company only moving at the speed of execution:**
For you, this is especially relevant because a small SaaS does not usually die from a lack of ideas. It dies from diffused attention. If you are half-committed to too many bets, nothing gets enough depth. One clear path, pursued with enough intensity, usually beats five loosely held options.

**On culture being more concrete than it sounds:**
A lot of people talk about culture like it is a vibe. Hormozi's framing is better: culture is what gets reinforced. If mediocre work gets ignored, that is culture. If missed deadlines carry no consequence, that is culture. If strong ownership gets praised and repeated, that is culture too. This matters because founder frustration often comes from tolerating behaviors they privately dislike and then acting surprised when those behaviors spread.

**On why better talent changes everything:**
This also lands hard for a founder. The cheapest hire is often the most expensive one. Someone who is merely acceptable can consume management time, create ambiguity, and lower the standard for everyone else. A stronger hire can look expensive on paper but cheap in total effect because they create speed, clarity, and leverage.

**On making training concrete:**
If you want someone to improve, abstract labels are almost useless. "Be proactive," "take ownership," or "be more accountable" sound meaningful but are hard to execute. The better move is to define visible behaviors: send the update by 5 PM, review the call within 24 hours, follow up with the customer the same day, bring two options instead of one problem. Once behaviors are concrete, coaching becomes real.

## Action Items
* [ ] **Pick one strategy and stay with it longer**: Choose one important path in your business that is sensible and resist the urge to reset it too early.
* [ ] **Identify where you are confusing execution problems for strategy problems**: Ask whether the issue is truly the plan or whether the plan is just being carried out poorly.
* [ ] **Audit your reinforcement rules**: Write down what behaviors you currently reward, ignore, and punish in your work or team.
* [ ] **Raise the bar on one role**: Revisit one hiring standard that may be too low and define what exceptional actually looks like.
* [ ] **Translate one vague value into behavior**: Pick a word like ownership, urgency, or accountability and rewrite it as specific actions someone can perform.
