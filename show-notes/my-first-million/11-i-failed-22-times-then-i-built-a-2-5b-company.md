# I failed 22 times... then I built a $2.5B Company

## Summary
Sam Parr interviews [[Christina Cacioppo]], the CEO of [[Vanta]], a $2.5B security and compliance automation company. Christina shares her journey from failing at 22 different startup ideas (bad art) to finally finding product-market fit with Vanta. They discuss the importance of persistence, managing your own psychology as a founder, the "bad art" framework for creativity, and how to validate ideas by selling before building. Christina also touches on her time at Union Square Ventures ([[USV]]) and the transition from VC to founder.

## Key Insights
*   **The "Bad Art" Framework**: Treat early startup ideas as "bad art" to lower the stakes. Just like turning on a tap in an old motel, you have to let the muddy water run out before the clean water flows. Accept that your first few attempts will likely suck, but they are necessary to get to the good stuff.
*   **The "Marshmallow Test" for Startups**: Don't spend months planning and architecting a perfect solution only to fail at the very end (putting the marshmallow on top). Instead, test the riskiest assumption first. If the core concept (the marshmallow) is too heavy, find out immediately, not after building a complex structure.
*   **Validation via Sales, Not Code**: Christina validated [[Vanta]] not by writing code, but by using a spreadsheet and manually doing the work for early customers. If someone isn't willing to use a "concierge MVP" or pay for the outcome, they likely won't care about the software either.
*   **The "Hard No" Rule**: When validating, treat anything other than "Can I have this right now/tomorrow?" as a **hard no**. Polite interest ("maybe next quarter") is a lie to spare your feelings. Real product-market fit looks like desperation for the solution.
*   **The Mom Test**: To avoid false positives, never ask "Would you use this?" (people lie). Instead, ask about their past behaviors and problems. They can lie about the future, but they can't lie about what they did yesterday. Ask about their problems, not your solution.
*   **Managing Founder Psychology**: The hardest part of the journey is managing your own mind. Christina used a weekly survey to check in with herself: "What would make you stop working on this?" When her past self started telling her current self to stop, she knew it was time to kill a project.
*   **Keep What Centers You**: To survive the startup grind, identify the specific habits that make you feel like yourself and **never give them up**, no matter how busy you get. For Christina, this was **running 3-4 times a week** and **reading on the bus**. She still takes the bus to work (instead of a car service) to force 20 minutes of downtime for reading.
*   **Progress = Happiness**: Happiness for founders often comes from a sense of progress in a meaningful direction, not just hitting static goals. Moving the goalposts is fine as long as you enjoy the pursuit of the new target.
*   **High Standards Attract Talent**: Contrary to the advice to lower standards to be more accommodating, high standards can be a talent magnet. A demanding environment signals to A-players that their work matters and they will be surrounded by others who care.
*   **The "USV" Anti-Portfolio**: Working in VC ([[Union Square Ventures]]) taught her that most successful founders aren't geniuses who hit a home run on their first try (the "Mark Zuckerberg" myth). Most try many things before one works. This demystified the process for her.

## Coach's Corner
As a technical SaaS founder, this episode is a crucial reminder to **stop over-engineering before validating**. You have the skills to build anything, which is a trap. Do not build a "Voice Assistant for Biologists" just because you can. Instead:

### 1. The "Hard No" Validation Rule
Stop counting "That sounds interesting" or "Maybe next quarter" as validation. It is noise. In early customer conversations, if they don't say **"Can I buy this now?"** or **"When can I have this?"**, treat it as a **hard NO**.
*   **Why:** People are polite. They will lie to spare your feelings. The only truth is money or time commitment *today*.
*   **Action:** If they say "maybe," ask: "What would stop you from buying this right now?" Push for the no.

### 2. The "What Did You Do?" Litmus Test (Mom Test)
When interviewing potential users, **ban the conditional tense** ("Would you use this?"). People are terrible at predicting their future behavior but great at lying about it.
*   **The Trap:** "Would you use an app that helps you find healthy recipes?" -> "Yes!" (Aspirational lie).
*   **The Truth:** "When was the last time you looked for a healthy recipe?" -> "6 months ago." -> "**What did you do then?**" -> "I just ordered pizza."
*   **The Lesson:** If they haven't tried to solve the problem (Googled it, used a spreadsheet, paid for a hacky solution) in the past, **it is not a painful problem**. Do not build for problems people aren't already trying to solve.

### 3. Manual First (Concierge MVP)
Can you solve the problem with a spreadsheet or a manual process? If yes, sell that first.
*   Christina sold Vanta as a spreadsheet service before writing the automation code.
*   If they won't buy the manual result, they won't buy the software.

### 4. Kill Bad Ideas Fast ("Bad Art")
Use the "bad art" mental model to detach your ego from your code. If a project isn't getting traction (see point #1), kill it and move to the next one. You are just clearing the muddy water.

### 5. High Standards are a Feature
Don't apologize for demanding excellence in your code or your product. It's how you build a world-class team. A-players want to work where the bar is high.

## Action Items
*   [ ] **Audit your current "side projects"**: Are they "bad art" that you're holding onto too long? If they haven't shown traction, kill them or pivot.
*   [ ] **Validate before you code**: For your next feature or product, try to sell it or solve it manually for a customer *before* writing a single line of code.
*   [ ] **Create a "Founder Check-in"**: Set up a weekly recurring task to ask yourself: "What would make me stop working on this?" Be honest with the answer.
*   [ ] **Read "The Art of Fear"**: The source of the "bad art" concept mentioned by Christina.
