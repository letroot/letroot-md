# Forget S&P, Bitcoin & AI, here's where Mohnish Pabrai is putting his money in 2026

## Summary
Mohnish Pabrai returns to [[My First Million]] for his third appearance, this time sharing his 2026 investment outlook and nine counterintuitive principles that guide his decisions. The conversation covers why patience and temperament matter more than IQ, how to avoid the "mistress" trap of chasing shiny new stocks, and why the S&P 500 and AI hype may be dangerous places to park money right now. Mohnish also shares his actual stock picks for 2026, his framework for introducing randomness into life, and the cautionary tale of [[Rick Guerin]] -- the forgotten third partner of Buffett and Munger who got margin-called into oblivion.

## Key Insights

### The 9 Investment Principles
1. **Mental Models of the Top 1%**: Great investors think in frameworks, not formulas. The game is transferring wealth from the active to the inactive.
2. **The Mistress Is Always Hotter Than the Wife**: What you own (the wife) you know deeply. What you don't own (the mistress) looks exciting because you don't know her flaws. This creates a high bar for selling -- you need conviction the new opportunity is *truly* better, not just superficially attractive.
3. **Introduce Randomness in Your Life**: Humans are terrible at copying. The best way to generate novel ideas is to expose yourself to random, unexpected inputs -- books outside your field, conversations with unusual people, travel to unfamiliar places.
4. **Humans Are Poor at Copying**: We see success and try to replicate it, but we inevitably miss the key variables. The best approach is to study great investors deeply, then adapt their principles to your own temperament.
5. **Take a Simple Idea Seriously**: The most powerful concepts (compound interest, patience, circle of competence) are simple but not easy. The edge comes from actually living them.
6. **Be an Inch Wide and a Mile Deep**: Specialization beats generalization. Know one or two things incredibly well rather than many things superficially.
7. **Never Use Excel**: If you need a spreadsheet to justify an investment, you don't understand the business. The thesis should fit on a napkin.
8. **Wait for Fat Pitches**: The stock market is like a church with a casino attached. Most people can't resist gambling. The best investors sit in the church and only enter the casino when the odds are overwhelmingly in their favor.
9. **The Inner Scorecard**: Define success by your own standards, not by comparison to others. Buffett lives by an inner scorecard; Guerin was destroyed by an outer one.

### Market Outlook 2026
*   **On S&P 500**: Bearish. Valuations are stretched and the index is increasingly concentrated in a few names.
*   **On Bitcoin**: Not mentioned as a focus area; Mohnish sticks to businesses he can understand.
*   **On AI**: Invest in the "pickaxe makers" -- the infrastructure companies that benefit regardless of which AI application wins. The [[Alphabet]]s and [[Meta]]s are playing a game they haven't played before, making them uncertain bets.
*   **On Stock Picking**: Well under 1% of Americans who invest in stocks are good at it. Index funds put you ahead of 90%+ of the crowd with zero effort.

### The Rick Guerin Cautionary Tale
[[Rick Guerin]] was the third member of the Buffett-Munger-Guerin trio in the 1970s. He was brilliant, hardworking, and used leverage (margin). When the market dropped 50% in 1973-74, he got margin-called and was forced to sell his [[Berkshire Hathaway]] stock to Buffett at distressed prices. The lesson: leverage can turn temporary price declines into permanent capital impairment. Patience and survival matter more than brilliance.

### Charlie Munger's Final Days
Mohnish shares that he saw [[Charlie Munger]] making investments six days before he died at age 99. The man never stopped learning, thinking, and playing the game. This is life advice disguised as investing advice: stay engaged until the end.

## Coach's Corner

**On Patience & Temperament:**
As a SaaS founder, you are playing a game where nothing happens for 3-5 years. Mohnish's "watching paint dry" principle applies directly to building a startup. The temptation is to pivot, add features, chase new markets -- the "mistress" of business ideas. But your current "wife" (your core product and market) is someone you know deeply. Don't abandon her for a shiny new opportunity unless you have *unequivocal* conviction the new path is better. Most founders would do well to raise their bar for strategic changes.

**On Randomness & Innovation:**
Mohnish's principle of introducing randomness is counterintuitive for a systematic person like you, but it's where breakthrough ideas live. You're deep in the React/Tailwind/Next.js ecosystem -- an inch wide and a mile deep. But what if you read a book on funeral home operations, or Turkish banking, or biological systems? The best product ideas often come from analogies drawn from unrelated fields. Schedule "randomness time" -- one book per month completely outside tech.

**On the Inner Scorecard:**
The startup world is designed to make you compare yourself to others. Funding announcements, revenue milestones, Twitter hype. Mohnish's "inner scorecard" principle means defining what *you* want from this business. Is it $10M ARR and an exit? Or is it a sustainable, profitable company that gives you freedom? Guerin was destroyed by comparing himself to Buffett. Don't let someone else's scorecard dictate your decisions.

**On Fat Pitches:**
You're early in your journey. You don't need to swing at every pitch. The "church with a casino" metaphor applies to the startup ecosystem too -- there's always a new framework, a new channel, a new trend. Sit in the church. Build your product. Talk to customers. Only enter the casino when you see a truly fat pitch: a distribution channel with CAC < LTV by a huge margin, a feature that unlocks a new market, or a partnership that changes the trajectory.

**On Never Using Excel:**
If you can't explain your business model on a napkin, you don't understand it. The same applies to your investment thesis for your own company. What's the one-sentence reason this business wins? For [[Airbnb]], it's "unlocked unused inventory." For [[Uber]], it's "turned car ownership into a service." What's yours? If you need a 20-slide deck to explain it, keep refining.

## Action Items
*   [ ] **Write your "Inner Scorecard"**: Define what success looks like for your SaaS in 12 months, 3 years, and 10 years. Make it personal, not comparative.
*   [ ] **Audit your "mistress" temptations**: List the shiny new ideas/projects you've considered in the last 90 days. Apply the wife vs. mistress test -- do you truly know them better than your current focus?
*   [ ] **Schedule randomness**: Pick one book this month from a completely unrelated field (suggested: *The Dhando Investor* by Mohnish Pabrai, or a biography of a non-tech founder).
*   [ ] **Define your fat pitch criteria**: What would make you "enter the casino" and aggressively invest time/money? Write it down so you don't get FOMO on mediocre opportunities.
*   [ ] **Napkin test your business model**: Can you explain why your SaaS wins in one sentence? If not, refine until you can.
