# More Value, Not Save Money

## Summary
Jason Cohen demonstrates that how you position your product's value proposition dramatically affects what you can charge. Using a hypothetical AdWords tool called DoubleDown, he shows that repositioning from "save money" to "generate growth" lets you charge 8x more for the exact same product. The insight: growth is always 10x more valuable to a business than cost savings, and customers are willing to pay 100% of value created when it's framed as growth rather than clinging to 75% of savings.

## Key Insights
*   **Growth Over Savings**: [[Positioning]] as growth creation is fundamentally more valuable than cost reduction. A CMO who saves 20% gets a pat on the back and a follow-up question about reinvesting those savings. A CMO who grows 20% gets a celebration and a budget increase.
*   **Value-Proportion Multiplier**: In the "save money" pitch, the customer needs to keep 75% of the value created. In the "generate growth" pitch, the customer will happily pay 100% of the value created. You capture both a larger pie *and* a larger slice.
*   **Same Product, Different Pitch**: [[DoubleDown]] is the same product either way -- it doubles cost-efficiency of AdWords. The reframe is not about the feature but about which goal it serves: reducing spend or increasing leads.
*   **Non-Linear Company Value**: Growth increases company value non-linearly. The additional revenue growth more than pays for the additional marketing cost that generated it. This is why CEOs instinctively prioritize growth.
*   **Pricing Is Positioning**: [[Pricing]] is much more about positioning and perceived value than cost-analysis and ROI calculators. The mechanics of what your product does matter less than the story of what outcome it delivers.

## Coach's Corner

**On Pricing Strategy & Self-Worth:** You are almost certainly underpricing your product right now. Not because your costs are wrong, but because your framing is wrong. If you're pitching "save time" or "save money" or "be more efficient," you're anchoring your customer to frugality -- a mindset where every dollar spent is a dollar to be minimized. Flip it. What does your product *enable* that didn't exist before? That's your real value.

**On SaaS Positioning:** As a SaaS founder, the temptation is to build ROI calculators and cost-justification decks. Jason is telling you those are the weak play. The strong play is to position your product in the language your customer's boss already speaks: growth, revenue, expansion. When you sell growth, you're not an expense to be optimized -- you're an investment to be increased.

**On Mindset & Ambition:** This article is a microcosm of a broader truth: the story you tell about your work determines its ceiling. If you tell yourself "I help people save money," you're optimizing for a smaller game. If you tell yourself "I help people grow," you're playing a bigger one. This applies to your product, your career, and your ambitions. Choose the frame that scales.

## Action Items
*   [ ] **Audit your positioning**: Review your landing page and sales pitch. Are you leading with "save" language? Rewrite it to lead with growth/outcome language.
*   [ ] **Calculate your growth-anchored pricing**: Instead of pricing as a percentage of cost savings, price as a percentage of the revenue/growth you enable. Compare the two numbers.
*   [ ] **Test the reframe with a real prospect**: In your next sales conversation, pitch the growth story instead of the efficiency story and observe the difference in engagement.
