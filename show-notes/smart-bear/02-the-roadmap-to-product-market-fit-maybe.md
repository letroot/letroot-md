# The Roadmap to Product/Market Fit (Maybe)

## Summary

Jason Cohen lays out the eight-step progression that took [[WP Engine]] from an idea to a unicorn, while being honest that it often doesn't work this cleanly. The steps are: Personal Fit (passion plus unique leverage), Market Fit (a working theory of why they'll buy), Customer Fit (talk to customers before building), Build and ship the SLC (Simple, Lovable, Complete), focus on Marketing and Sales over code, Retention-driven product development, ruthless prioritization, and managing your psychology through constant rejection. He contrasts Smart Bear (which skipped several steps and succeeded anyway, partly through luck) with WP Engine (which followed the roadmap precisely and became a unicorn). The core message: most founders fail because they build instead of sell, code instead of talk to customers, and prioritize what they're comfortable with over what the business actually needs.

## Key Insights

- **[[Personal Fit]] is not just passion -- it's leverage.** Passion is necessary but not sufficient. Every founder has passion. What differentiates you is unique leverage: the intersection of your above-average talents and experiences that, combined, nobody else has. The key question: "Why are you the perfect person to build this company?" Passion without an edge is just expensive motivation.
- **[[Unique Leverage]] > [[Leverage]].** Being top 25% in three different areas (like Scott Adams with illustration, humor, and tech experience) creates a unique intersection that is more defensible than being top 1% at one thing. "Different, not better" -- you should be distinctive, aligned with your calling and strengths, so the people who find your brand of different to be "better" will flock to you.
- **Most good ideas aren't good businesses.** "Product/Market Fit" means fitting into the market, not just building something fun or interesting. Most indie hacker and "AI is cool" startups fail because the genesis was a fun project, not a plausible business. You need a plausible theory of the customer, market, and business model -- using the PLEESE framework: Plausible, Self-Aware, Lucrative, Liquid, Eager, Enduring.
- **[[The Iterative-Hypothesis Customer Development Method]]**. Cohen's approach to customer interviews: create specific hypotheses about your business model, then design questions that maximize learning. When customers say yes, it's a maybe; when they say no, it's a no -- and you just saved yourself months. He used this method to invalidate one startup idea and validate WP Engine, both in 20 interviews or fewer.
- **Customers can tell you what they won't buy, which is more valuable than what they will.** You learn their language, their budgets, their current solutions, and what triggers them to say "today is the day I'm going to buy ___." But founders resist this work because building is more fun than finding and interviewing strangers.
- **[[The SLC Framework]] (Simple, Lovable, Complete) replaces [[MVP]].** Simple because complex things can't be built quickly. Lovable because crappy products are insulting. Complete because customers want v1 of something simple, not v0.1 of something broken. The learning starts only when customers are using -- or not using -- the product.
- **Marketing and Sales matter more than writing code and tweaking design.** The code is your enemy. You'll always want to build more; you need to force yourself to do so much marketing and sales that it feels like "too much." Every day, ask: what can I do today to (a) get more target-market people to the website, (b) convert more to try, or (c) convert more to buy?
- **[[Retention-Driven Product Development]]**. A customer who found you, tried you, paid you, and then cancelled means you made the right promise but didn't keep it. Low retention means fake growth -- it's not Product/Market Fit if the market decides "not a fit" after using your product. Build features that increase retention, not just attract new users.
- **[[Rocks, Pebbles, Sand]] prioritization system.** Identify the next milestone. Identify the current obstacle to achieving it. Pick one Rock that addresses both. Schedule Pebbles sparingly. Let almost everything else be "good enough." Most things you do will not determine whether the company is successful.
- **Psychology is half the battle.** Facing truth about your abilities and market realities, constant rejection (99% of website visitors do nothing), making clear decisions while uncertain, and the temptation to retreat into what you're comfortable with (coding) instead of what the business needs (selling). Perseverance is a virtue only if you're doing the right work with the right goals.

## Coach's Corner

**On Finding Your Leverage as a SaaS Founder:**

Daniel, this article is basically a roadmap for exactly where you are. You're early-stage, you're building your first product, and the biggest risk right now isn't code quality -- it's whether you're building something the market actually wants.

The Personal Fit framework is worth sitting with. Ask yourself honestly: "Why am I the perfect person to build this?" Not "why am I passionate about it" -- passion every founder has. What's your unique intersection? Your React/Tailwind background, your understanding of the SaaS founder persona, your current firsthand experience of the pain you're solving -- that's your leverage stack. Name it. Write it down. It should inform every product and marketing decision.

**On Customer Fit Before Building:**

The hardest truth in this article: "You don't want to do this work. You want to build the product because that's the fun part." Every founder falls into this trap. The article's example of Cohen getting 40 out of 50 LinkedIn prospects to agree to interviews (only one asked for payment) should embolden you. People will talk. You just have to ask.

Before you write another line of code, go have 20 conversations with people who match your ICP. Use the Iterative-Hypothesis method. You'll either validate your direction or save yourself months of building the wrong thing. Both outcomes are wins.

**On Retention Over Acquisition:**

The WP Engine story is instructive: they hit $1M ARR in 18 months, but true PMF didn't arrive until early 2012 -- roughly two years later. The initial traction came from the right promise in the right market. The hypergrowth came from actually keeping that promise (best-in-industry retention for 16 years). If people sign up and leave, you don't have a growth problem -- you have a product problem.

**On the Comfort Zone Trap:**

This is the mirror you need to hold up. When you're avoiding sales calls or customer interviews and retreating into VS Code where you know what you're doing -- that's the trap. The business doesn't need you to write better code. It needs you to sell and learn.

## Action Items

- [ ] **Define your unique leverage stack**: Write down your top 3-5 "circles of competence" and identify where they intersect -- that's your defensible advantage
- [ ] **Run the PLEESE market test**: Evaluate your current business idea against Plausible, Self-Aware, Lucrative, Liquid, Eager, Enduring. Be brutally honest.
- [ ] **Conduct 20 customer interviews** before building your next feature. Use the Iterative-Hypothesis method. Aim to learn what they won't buy, not just what they say they will.
- [ ] **Audit your time allocation**: Track how much time you spend on product vs. marketing/sales this week. If it's not close to 50/50, you're in the comfort zone trap.
- [ ] **Identify your next milestone and obstacle**: Write it down. Look at it every morning. Everything else is a distraction.
