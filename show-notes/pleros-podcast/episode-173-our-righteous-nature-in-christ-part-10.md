## The Question of Moral Objectivity

Now, having said all of these that we do have a righteous nature, I haven't pointed out that there's righteousness as a status, not just righteousness as a nature. Our concern has been how do we even explain what righteousness itself is? I would say this concern brings us into the realm of examining moral objectivity versus moral subjectivity.

And usually there's a lot of argument about whether there can even be moral objectivity at all, whether morality itself permits for objectivity. There's usually the attempt to say there is moral objectivity because of the universality of the sense of morality. Which is something I'm going to comment on later because every people, every culture, every place across all time usually have moral sensibilities. Even though the moral judgments may differ, but they do have moral sensibilities, the sense that something is right versus something being wrong.

And the very presence of moral sensibilities in humanity and the fact that moral sensibilities are universal, that is there has never been a people in a place that do not have moral sensibilities, that do not have what they would say is good or bad, even though it may differ. That presence of moral sensitivity points itself to moral objectivity. But I don't think logically that would hold.

## The Universality of Moral Sensibilities

All right, and it's true that there's a universality of morality or moral sensibilities. It just so happens everywhere. It has never just been the case that across history, across places, there are just a people without any sense of morality at all. It has never so happened. But that doesn't answer to the issue of the objectivity itself. Like what quality makes for moral objectivity? What do we witness externally to judge what makes it objective?

Now, we have pointed out that ordinarily establishing objectivity for morality is difficult because it's not a descriptive truth. Morality is not descriptive truth. Morality is prescriptive. So if we were to look at something and just describe it, that thing is white, that thing is black, that thing is big, that thing is small. Things like that or big as one might even be flexible depending on what premise you are using. But that thing is white or black or blue or red, this is objective. If someone has a different judgment, it may be because the person has a form of deficiency in their sight, but many independent parties can see and judge something descriptive and see what it objectively is.

Also, maybe even more indisputable are logical or mathematical truths. A equals A is just the case, it's just correct. A equals A or 1 + 1 = 2 is just correct. No matter who is seeing it, it's mind-independently true and right. And whenever a mind examines it, it ought to be the case.

## Descriptive vs Prescriptive Truth

But when we get to the realm of prescriptive truth, this is what ought to be done. This is what is right to be done. Then the question is, why is that right to be done? Who says it ought to be done? How do you arrive at claiming that that is what ought to be done? So, morality is interesting in that sense that universally we have moral sensibilities, yet by virtue of the kind of thing we are dealing with in morality, prescriptive truths or prescriptive realities, it doesn't readily come across as objective.

And we said, how then does Paul say there is righteousness and holiness of truth? And in Ephesians chapter 5 as well, he says we should bear the fruit of the light or the fruit of the spirit, which is in all goodness, righteousness, and truth, finding out what is acceptable to the Lord. It means that there's a truth he has in mind that helps to define moral objectivity.

## Light and Darkness: The Primary and the Subsequent

And what the Bible does in explaining the nature of God to us consistently from the beginning of the Bible to the very end is to make it clear that when we observe a contrast or contradiction in our world, the only thing that can be of God must be what is first, what is original, what is primary because he is self-existent. So, his eternal power is self-existent power. Would be pointed to the fact that his nature will not be something that is later. He cannot be a bearer of that which is subsequent, that which is secondary, that which is later. His nature will be first, will be initial, will be primary, and even that which is contradictory is only taking reference from his nature.

And the concept of what is used to describe this across very similar episodes is light and darkness as the primary concept used to describe this. Then when that concept is made more substantial, the contrast used is life and death.

All right. So, with light and darkness, we know that light is original and it's the very absence of light that is darkness. You know, if we see a non-living thing, you can never say a non-living thing is dead. ## Grounding Moral Objectivity on Existence

And this is where we ground moral objectivity. That what is objectively right is life and light. First, because that is what existence is all about. At the self-existent state, if there was no cosmos, if there was no creation, all that is would be life and it would be all that ought to be as well. And now, death and darkness, the contradiction of life, are against or anti-existence and therefore they ought not to be from an existential standpoint itself.

So, it's one thing to say this ought not to be because God doesn't want it to be. It's also another thing to say, even in almost an impersonal sense, without any subject including God commenting on it, that on an existential plane, this is anti-existence and it ought not to be. Now, whilst this still requires the introduction of an axiom, that is a self-evident truth that you can deny, which would be denying all good reason in the first place. It requires the introduction of an axiom, but we could argue that this axiom is necessary. That when we speak of morality or righteousness, it has to be that which is aligned with God or with existence as is, which is existence fundamentally and primarily is life. The presence of death, darkness, or evil is a contradiction to what it is, so it ought not to be. And it only is because of rebellion to what existence is about.

## Life is Right and Death is Wrong: A Necessary Axiom

So, we could speak in that impersonal way and still use that word ought in that sense. And introducing that axiom that life is what ought to be and death is what ought not to be is how we ground objective morality.

Now, for the believer in Christ, the axiom can be a matter of revelatory truth. But, I dare say we could argue that axiom as even necessary rational truth. And this is something we can say about the universal nature of moral sensibility. In every place where people make moral judgments, they are doing it on the premise of what is life and what is death.

Now, I can imagine one might want to point out cultures where they killed people, but you'll usually find that sometimes they kill people, like maybe they kill twins or they sacrifice people, that they are usually doing it for the sake of life, which is what they are defining as good or right.
