## Understanding Growth and the Inward Man

Growth happens on the mind of man, and this is important for a person to recognize when they are in God's purpose. You only come into God's purpose by receiving the spirit of sunship, as seen in Romans 8:15. Having the spirit means one part of the equation is solved, and we are entirely brand new with the nature and life of God. However, that doesn't account for what growth means for the believer. Growth does not happen in our spirit, which is already in a complete and divine state, but rather on our mind. 

Spiritual growth is the increase of the influence of our spirit on our mind as opposed to the influence of the flesh or the world. To understand this, we must have an appreciation of man in his entirety. 1 Thessalonians 5:23 shows us that man is spirit, soul, and body, while 2 Corinthians 4:16 describes man as an inward and outward man. The outward man is perishing while the inward man is being renewed day by day. Paul calls the outward man a tent, a building, or a cloth, while the inward man is the real man.

The Lord Jesus taught this reality in Luke 16 through the account of Lazarus and the rich man. When their outward bodies died and were buried, their inward man continued to exist with memory, reasoning, and initiative. This shows that the inward man is the spirit with the mind, while the outward man is the body with a brain. Just as the brain is the controlling center of our physical body, the mind is the controlling center of our spiritual person. 

## The Seven Aspects of the Human Mind

Because growth happens on the mind, we need to know which aspect of the mind is the determinant of our conduct. There are seven aspects of the mind to consider. Number one is reasoning, where you think about what is being said. The second aspect is memory, where information is stored long-term or short-term. The third aspect is the will, which allows us to take initiative and choose to agree or disagree with what is put before us.

If we agree with information, it becomes our conviction, which is the fourth aspect. It is one thing for something to be a conviction, and another for that conviction to become our consciousness or reckoning. This fifth aspect is what we present and stand on, which is vital for the "faith stand." The final two aspects are our emotions, which are products of our state of reckoning, and our conscience judgments, which are our reactive judgments of right and wrong.

Conscience has three things at play: the preceding consciousness, the perception at the moment, and the final judgment. The preceding consciousness is the state of your reckoning prior to a situation. At the moment of contact, there is a dual perception involving both your natural senses and your mind perceiving together with them. This "co-perception" causes you to react and pass a judgment of good, bad, or evil.

This implies that it is possible to be a believer yet still have the same worldly reasoning, reckoning, and consciousness on your mind.
