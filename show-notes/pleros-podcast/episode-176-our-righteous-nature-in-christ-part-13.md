And it's in that sense the Bible will submit about God's nature as being light or God's nature as being life. ## The Axiom of Moral Objectivity: Life is Right

Now, since we know that about God's nature, we have submitted that what makes righteousness objective is that righteousness is life or we've said and seen that if God's nature is life, which is a descriptive truth, then what ought to be done should also be that which is of the nature of God, which is life. So, that is how we arrive at an objective morality by the introduction of an axiom or by axiom, we mean a self-evident claim or truth that is necessary. It's like we say by our observation and reasoning can be trusted to discern truth. That's an axiom we have to accept. And then upon that, we do every other form of reasoning.

In this case, when we talk about morality, the fact that God's nature is life, therefore that's what ought to be done is a necessary axiom because it takes a world existing to even speak of morality being done in that world. And it takes the life of God for there to be a world existing. There will be no room for what to be done without that. What ought to be done is that very life, the sustenance of being, of life.

I hope by now that it is clear what we are pointing out, which is this axiom that says what is is what ought to be done. And upon that axiom, we have an objectivity of morality and an objectivity of righteousness. So, whatever is not of life is unrighteous.

## Righteousness as Nature vs Righteousness as Status

With all this said, there's a point that still needs to be made. How do we explain righteousness as a status versus righteousness as a nature? Everything we have studied, one thing is abundantly clear, righteousness is primarily a nature. It's an ontological thing. That is, who God is is what righteousness is. It is a nature issue. It's not a status issue.

The status issue derives from the nature one. And that's what we need to explain. How is it that we come to speak of righteousness as a status when righteousness is primarily a nature issue?

Well, there are a couple of things we need to make clear. God who made the world, he made the cosmos, is sovereign in the sense that no other person has authority above and over him. He's the one that decides whatever he wants to do. And so he has set up a world of his nature with ready consequences for things that contradict his nature. If you don't accept life, the consequence is death. If you reject life, the consequence is death. If you put out light, the consequence is darkness.

## God as Sovereign Judge

That means in his sovereignty, God plays a role as a judge. And every justice system everywhere in the world works as a matter of sovereignty. In fact, if a nation is not sovereign, you'll find that their supreme judicial offices are still subject to whichever nation is ruling over them because the issue of justice is tied to sovereignty. So if God is sovereign over his world, he's invariably automatically the judge of his world. This is not something that needs a new setup. It's automatic. The sovereign has the final say.

He could set it up as a matter of the natural order of his world so that consequences are at play. He could set it up as a personal matter where he interferes in what is at play in the world, whether ongoing or at the end. That is the prerogative of the sovereign. Now, reading God's word, knowing that he's judge, we can see generally he has set up his world. Man has the free will to do what he wants. He has set up the consequences thereof and he has also set up a final judgment at the end.

## The Law and the Judicial Sense of Righteousness

This is where the issue of righteousness as a matter of justice or as a matter of a juridical system or as a status comes in. It's not that righteousness as a status is primary. Righteousness is primarily a matter of nature, is an ontological issue. But in God's sovereignty setting up his world with consequences and being that he's going to make final judgment of the same at the end, then righteousness now starts to have judicial implications.

And that's why we now see many texts when they speak of being justified or made right, they are speaking from the standpoint of the law which makes that judicial or juridical rule of the sovereign God more obvious. Not that the ontological problem of sin and unrighteousness has not always been, but the law makes it more obvious.

But upon the law it becomes clear the sovereign God who has set up his world with consequences is judge, he has his judicial system.
