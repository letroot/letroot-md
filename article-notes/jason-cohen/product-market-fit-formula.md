# The roadmap to Product/Market Fit (PMF)… maybe
By Jason Cohen
Source: [A Smart Bear](https://longform.asmartbear.com/product-market-fit-formula/)

## Summary
Jason Cohen details the exact 8-step roadmap that took WP Engine from an idea to a billion-dollar Unicorn. While acknowledging that startups can succeed through luck or alternate paths (as his previous company, Smart Bear, did), this formula relies on repeatable processes rather than serendipity. The roadmap progresses from finding personal alignment, to validating a market theory, talking to the right customers, shipping an SLC (Simple, Lovable, Complete) product, over-indexing on sales/marketing, obsessing over retention, ruthless prioritization, and managing founder psychology.

## The 8-Step PMF Roadmap

### 1. Personal Fit: Leverage & Passion
- **Passion is necessary but insufficient:** You must be burning with an idea to survive the gauntlet of rejection, zero money, and impostor syndrome. However, passion isn't a business model.
- **Leverage gives you an edge:** You need to find the intersection of your unique talents and experiences. You don't have to be the top 1% in the world at one thing; being in the top 25% of three intersecting skills creates a unique profile. 
- **The Core Question:** *Why are you the perfect person to build this company?* Your competitive strategy stems from this personal edge.

### 2. Market Fit: A Theory of Why They Will Buy
- **Don't just build it:** "I had the problem so I built it" is a dangerous origin story. You are likely nothing like your actual paying customer.
- **Develop a specific theory:** Use Fermi estimation to ensure the market is Plausible (10M consumers or 100k businesses), Self-Aware (they know they have the problem), Lucrative (they have budget), Liquid (they can buy right now), Eager (they want to buy from *you*), and Enduring (low churn). 
- **A wrong theory is better than no theory:** A specific theory allows you to notice when reality contradicts it, enabling a clean pivot (like Slack or WhatsApp did). Walking randomly without a theory is just wandering.

### 3. Customer Fit: Talk to Ideal Customers First
- **Customers are the only source of truth:** Not advisors, not Twitter, not your gut.
- **Find your ICP (Ideal Customer Profile):** Over-specify your target. A hyper-specific target makes your messaging resonate instantly. If you target Carol perfectly, you will naturally catch the 10x people adjacent to Carol anyway.
- **Invalidation saves years:** Customers telling you "no" or showing they have no budget saves you from building the wrong thing for 18 months. Use the "Iterative-Hypothesis" interview method.

### 4. Build and Ship the SLC
- **Skip the MVP, build the SLC:** Simple, Lovable, Complete.
- **Simple:** So you can build and ship it fast before running out of money.
- **Lovable:** Because crappy, buggy v0.1 products are insulting. It must have an emotional or utility hook that makes people love it despite its simplicity.
- **Complete:** It must actually accomplish the job to be done.
- **Learning only starts at launch:** If you are building for six months in secret, you are just proving you know how to write code. Real business progress only happens when a user attempts to use the product.

### 5. Focus More on Marketing/Sales than Product
- **Code is your enemy:** Founders naturally retreat to coding because it is comfortable and they are good at it. 
- **Force the issue:** You must intentionally push yourself to do marketing and sales. Ask yourself daily: What will get more targeted traffic, convert them to try, or convert them to buy? Most of those answers don't require writing code.

### 6. Prioritize Retention-Based Product Work
- **Growth cannot outrun churn:** High churn (e.g., 7% a month) will stall a business entirely, regardless of top-of-funnel marketing.
- **Marketing = Promise, Retention = Fulfillment:** If someone clicks your ad, gives you a credit card, tries the software, and then cancels, they are saying: *You made the right promise, but your product failed to keep it.*
- **Talk to churned/struggling users:** Figure out the difference between users who succeed and users who fail. Sometimes it's a missing feature/integration; sometimes it means you need to adjust your ICP because you sold to the wrong demographic.

### 7. Ruthlessly and Systematically Prioritize
- **Rocks, Pebbles, Sand:** You only have time for one massive "Rock" at a time.
- **Identify the next milestone:** (e.g., Hit $10k MRR so you can quit your day job).
- **Identify the existential obstacle:** Face the brutal truth about what is stopping you from hitting that milestone. Don't pick the comfortable obstacle; pick the real, scary one.
- **"Good enough" is the standard for the rest:** You have taste, but perfectionism on non-critical path items will kill the startup. Let the small things go.

### 8. Manage Your Psychology
- This is the hardest part. You must navigate:
  - **Facing the truth** instead of what you wish were true.
  - **Constant rejection** from customers, investors, and recruits.
  - **Acting while uncertain** and pushing through extreme Impostor Syndrome.
- Founders fail here because they retreat to what's comfortable (coding) instead of doing what the business actually needs (selling, interviewing, facing hard truths). 

## The "Smart Bear" Exception
Cohen admits his first company, Smart Bear, violated many of these rules (he coded in isolation without interviewing). He succeeded purely by pivoting repeatedly until he stumbled into Product/Market Fit. However, relying on this method is relying on luck. The 8-step roadmap (which he used for WP Engine) is designed to systematically engineer success rather than waiting for a lucky pivot.
