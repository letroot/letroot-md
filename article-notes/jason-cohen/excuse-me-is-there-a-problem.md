# Excuse me, is there a problem?
By Jason Cohen
Source: [A Smart Bear](https://longform.asmartbear.com/problem/)

## Summary
In this article, Jason Cohen explains why simply identifying a real problem and building a product that solves it is **not enough** to build a successful startup. Startups frequently fail because the gap between "solving a problem" and "building a viable business model" is massive. 

He introduces a framework to evaluate startup viability by looking at a sequence of compounding factors—from whether enough people have the problem, to whether they know they have it, have the budget to solve it, are liquid enough to buy right now, eager to choose your specific solution, and whether they will endure as customers over time. The key insight is that these factors multiply; a failure in any single area can doom an otherwise brilliant product idea.

## Key Insights & The Viability Framework

Cohen breaks down the path from "Problem" to "Viable Business Model" using a Fermi estimation model. To build a sustainable business, all these conditions must multiply together successfully.

### 1. Plausible: Do enough people have the problem?
- **The Math:** If you rely on top-of-funnel marketing, 1% click-through rate multiplied by a 1% visitor-to-paid conversion rate means 10,000 impressions yield exactly 1 paying customer.
- **The Implication:** To get 1,000 paying customers (roughly the minimum for a sustainable small company at $30-$100/mo), you need 10 million marketing impressions. 
- **B2C vs B2B:** If selling direct to consumers, your market needs to be at least 10M people. If selling B2B, companies pay orders of magnitude more, so 100k companies might be sufficient. 

### 2. Self-Aware: Do they know & care they have the problem?
- **Ignorance vs. Apathy:** Even if they mathematically have the problem (e.g., website security), if they don't *believe* they have it, they aren't searching for a solution and they won't buy.
- **Priority List:** Customers may know they have a problem (e.g., website accessibility), but if it's the #9 priority and they only have budget/time for the top 3, they will not buy.
- **The Danger of Evangelism:** If the market doesn't know they have the problem, you must become an evangelist. Creating a market while trying to sell to it is difficult, expensive, and slow.

### 3. Lucrative: Do they have substantial budget to solve it?
- **The "College Student" Trap:** A problem might exist (e.g., college kids need cheap food), but the demographic has zero money. A business serving a broke demographic inherently lacks budget.
- **Enterprise Budgets:** Just because a company is large doesn't mean they have a budget for *your* problem. If it's not a top-3 priority, getting through compliance and internal teams (who might feel threatened by your tool) is impossible.
- **B2B is often better:** Consumers hate paying for software (complaining about $15/year apps). Businesses, however, will gladly spend money to solve problems if it helps them make more money or save substantial time.

### 4. Liquid: Are they willing and able to buy *right now*?
- **Lock-in:** A company might love your tool and have the budget, but they are locked into a 15-month enterprise contract, or the feature is bundled into their existing Workday/HR system.
- **Migration Friction:** The cost and pain of migrating data, retraining staff, or rebuilding API integrations might outweigh the benefit of your superior product.
- **Timing:** Some products are only needed briefly (e.g., wedding software, load-testing tools). If they don't need it *right now*, they won't buy. 

### 5. Eager: Do they want to buy from *you*, specifically?
- **Trust:** Will you be in business in 2 years? Are you secure? Will your servers scale with their growth?
- **Differentiation:** You might have unique features, but if the market doesn't care about those specific features, you lose to the cheaper or more established competitor. 
- **Over-serving:** If your product has 10 features but the market only wants 3, they will pick a simpler/cheaper competitor who just does the 3.

### 6. Enduring: Will they still be paying a year from now?
- **The Churn Trap:** A 7% monthly churn rate means half your customers will be gone in a year. Because growth is typically linear (or quadratic) but churn is exponential (percentage of a growing base), churn will rapidly offset all marketing efforts.
- **Growth Stalling:** If you add $2k/mo in new MRR but have a 7% churn rate, by the time you hit $27k MRR, you are losing $2k/mo in churn. Your business completely plateaus.

## Evaluating the Score (The Fermi Rubric)
Cohen offers a rubric to score a startup idea by multiplying factors across Plausible, Self-Aware, Lucrative, Liquid, Eager, and Enduring.
- If the normalized score is > 1: Viable Indie Startup.
- If the normalized score is > 2: Potential Scale-up / Unicorn.
- **What to do with a bad score:** Don't necessarily quit; pivot your target market. Instead of selling security software to "all consumers" (low budget, high churn, low awareness), pivot to "mid-sized businesses" or "high-net-worth individuals." Niche down to increase willingness to pay, budget, and awareness.

## Notes & Takeaways for Founders
- **The hardest pill to swallow:** Having a great product that genuinely solves a real problem does not guarantee a successful company.
- **Target selection is everything:** The demographic you choose to serve dictates your pricing, churn, and marketing physics. Serving a poor demographic or a low-priority problem breaks the math.
- **Evaluate ideas aggressively early:** Walk through this "Problem to Viable Business" path *before* writing code. If the math says you need 10 million impressions to survive and you have no distribution channel, the business is structurally flawed.
