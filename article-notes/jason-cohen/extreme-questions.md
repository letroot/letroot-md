# Extreme brainstorming questions to trigger new, better ideas
By Jason Cohen
Source: [A Smart Bear](https://longform.asmartbear.com/extreme-questions/)

## Summary
Brainstorming is often ineffective because we are blinded by our daily work, thinking in tiny increments based on recent customer tickets or sales calls. To break out of this "tiny thinking," Jason Cohen offers a set of extreme, reality-bending prompts. By stretching a dimension of your business to a near-nonsense extreme, you force entirely new perspectives. An idea that solves a problem in a 100% extreme hypothetical scenario might reveal a 2x or 10x strategic move in reality.

## The Extreme Prompts

### 1. 10x Prices
**Prompt:** If you were forced to increase your prices by 10x, what would you have to do to justify it?
*   **Implication:** How would the brand, positioning, and target market change? Would you need concierge human support? By exploring what justifies a massive hike, you often find things you could implement today to justify a 2x price increase.

### 2. No Customers
**Prompt:** If all our customers vanished and we had to earn growth from scratch, what would we do?
*   **Implication:** We often keep legacy features or avoid repositioning because we don't want to upset 5% of our current users, sacrificing the 95% of future users. If you had no baggage, how would your homepage, pricing, and product architecture look today?

### 3. No Tech Support
**Prompt:** If you were never allowed to provide tech support, what would have to change?
*   **Implication:** How intuitive would the onboarding need to be? What controls would the user need? Forcing extreme self-service not only cuts costs but inherently makes customers happier and more loyal.

### 4. Maximize Fun
**Prompt:** What would be the most fun thing to build? (Must improve the product/infrastructure).
*   **Implication:** When teams work on fun things, they work harder and better. Biasing towards fun is actually a highly productive, pragmatic strategy that prevents burnout and often results in delightful features. 

### 5. Complete Rip-off
**Prompt:** If our biggest competitor copied every single feature we have, how would we still win?
*   **Implication:** Competing purely on features leads to weak "bullet-point battles." If features are equal, do you win on brand, community, customer trust, ease of use, open-source philanthropy, or mission? Build the moat that code cannot copy.

### 6. No Time
**Prompt:** What if we are forced to ship a fully completed new feature in just two weeks?
*   **Implication:** Scope must be slashed to the bone. You might manually stub out an API or skip internal training, but you get something real into customers' hands to measure actual usage rather than surveying them about hypothetical usage.

### 7. Flipped Business Model
**Prompt:** What if you were forced to charge customers in a completely different manner? (e.g., flat rate to usage-based, or vice versa).
*   **Implication:** Business models dictate how you think about value creation. Flipping it forces you to rethink what features control costs or add value, sometimes revealing that a different model aligns better with customer success.

### 8. No Website
**Prompt:** If you were not allowed to have a website, how would you still grow?
*   **Implication:** Could growth be purely word-of-mouth? In-product marketing? Could the product itself be so viral or useful that it generates advocacy natively? We rely too much on "marketing sites" instead of building growth engines into the product.

### 9. No Meetings
**Prompt:** What if you had no synchronous meetings, ever again?
*   **Implication:** How would onboarding, decision-making, and socializing happen? This forces the creation of deep, asynchronous documentation (e.g., writing down the "why" behind the last 20 decisions) that scales better than endless Zoom calls.

### 10. No Customer Contact
**Prompt:** If we could never talk to our customers again, how would we figure out what to build?
*   **Implication:** Could you measure behavior perfectly? Could you use "coming soon" buttons to test demand empirically before building? Sometimes customers *say* they want a feature but don't use it. Empirical signal beats surveys.

### 11. Cost is No Object
**Prompt:** What if it didn’t matter how unprofitable you were? 
*   **Implication:** How much value could you deliver if money was free? If a human concierge could do it perfectly, can software do 30% of that job for 1/100th the cost? This often leads to ideas for high-tier pricing plans.

### 12. Sociopathic CEO
**Prompt:** What if you could change anything, regardless of what anyone thinks or feels?
*   **Implication:** Would you kill a whole product line? Pivot the company? Lay off a department? It’s inhumane in reality, but avoiding the thought entirely means avoiding necessary, hard strategic truths. 

### 13. Mortal Wound
**Prompt:** What externality has the potential to kill the entire company?
*   **Implication:** Identify the existential threats (a competitor dropping prices to zero, an economic shift, a specific security breach). Find the one mitigation that is wise to execute *anyway*, which also shores up your defenses.

### 14. Philanthropist
**Prompt:** What if our only goal were to create the most good in the world, personally for our customers?
*   **Implication:** Forget MRR. How do you help customers achieve their ultimate life/business goals? Mission-driven companies earn irrational loyalty from customers and employees alike.

### 15. Only One Thing This Year
**Prompt:** What if you could only ship *one thing* this year?
*   **Implication:** What single initiative is so differentiated and desirable that customers would stay even if you shipped nothing else? This forces you to ignore the noise and focus exclusively on the highest-leverage lever in your business.
