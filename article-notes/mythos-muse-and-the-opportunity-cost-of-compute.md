# Mythos, Muse, and the Opportunity Cost of Compute

## Summary
Ben Thompson discusses how the transition to AI and reasoning models challenges the core assumptions of Aggregation Theory, specifically the internet-era reliance on zero marginal costs. As AI drives up compute requirements, hyperscalers face significant opportunity costs rather than marginal costs—deciding whether to allocate compute to their own high-margin enterprise products or to external customers. Thompson contrasts Anthropic's enterprise focus with Meta's unique position of having zero enterprise opportunity costs, allowing Meta to freely pursue the consumer market with models like Muse Spark.

## Key Insights
*   **The End of Zero Marginal Costs?**: Doug O’Laughlin suggests that AI makes tech capital-intensive again, ending the era where internet scale meant zero marginal cost. However, Thompson argues that for hyperscalers, the real issue isn't marginal costs (since AI servers run constantly anyway) but rather **opportunity costs**. Compute used in one area cannot be used in another.
*   **Opportunity Cost vs. Marginal Cost**: Microsoft and Anthropic are dealing with opportunity costs. Microsoft missed Azure growth expectations because it allocated compute to its own higher-margin, first-party AI workloads. Anthropic faces compute constraints serving current users, leading to decisions not to broadly release models like "Mythos."
*   **Anthropic's "Mythos" and Distillation**: Anthropic focuses on security ("Project Glasswing") to justify limiting broad access to Mythos, but the real reasons likely include preserving compute for high-paying enterprise customers and preventing open-source models (especially from Chinese labs) from "distilling" their capabilities to catch up.
*   **Meta's Advantage in Consumer AI**: Meta's new model, **Muse Spark** (from Meta Superintelligence Labs), highlights Meta's unique advantage. Because Meta has no cloud or enterprise business to protect, it faces no opportunity cost in serving consumers. This makes them a fierce competitor in the consumer AI space, positioning them to win while OpenAI and Anthropic fight over enterprise compute.
*   **Owning Demand vs. Supply**: OpenAI is betting that its massive infrastructure build-out will outpace Anthropic's momentum. However, Thompson believes that Aggregation Theory's core principle—that owning demand (the customer) ultimately trumps owning supply—still holds. The winner will be the one with the best product, generating the cash flow to buy whatever compute they need.
