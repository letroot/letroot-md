**Chapter 2**

We have settled something important:

That the purpose question is really about God's intention, if we accept the truth of the Gospel \- that there is God who is Creator and has redeemed us in the work accomplished in Christ Jesus.

So what we are seeking an answer to is: What is God's purpose and specifically what is His purpose for your life as an individual.

How can we possibly find the answer to this question in a way that we are utterly sure we are not being fooled or merely being dogmatic about it?

There is the kind of confidence we have about certain things that is not a product of what someone said or how we feel at the moment.

It is a matter of following the evidence to its logical conclusion. It is the confidence we have when someone points to the green grass and asks the question "what colour is the grass". We boldly say it is green. We refuse to relent no matter the number of people who said it is red. Because we can tell by observation and reason that it is green. Our eyes and our mind have examined the evidence and we are so sure.

But when it comes to questions about someone's reason or motivation for doing something, we cannot observe and reason it out because it is on the person's heart, hidden away from our eyes and mind.

This can make it seem difficult to know in a definite and certain way what one's purpose is.

Let's see an illustration to buttress this.

Imagine for a moment that you went to a friend's house and you saw him doing some woodwork. You took your time to observe what exactly he was trying to make with the wood, and when he finishes it turns out to be a table.

So without asking him at all, you are able to know, by observation and reason, what he is making, constructing or creating.

But now, here is the challenge. You know what your friend has made and constructed by observing him. You know he made a table. But you don't know why he made the table. Is it for business, is it for personal use? Is it to be used for study or in the dining?

How do you find out?

You have to ask and he has to truthfully answer. He has to reveal his reason or intention to you. This leads to a vital conclusion:

Knowing the "why" (the reason or purpose or intention) behind something requires revelation. But knowing the "what" (the object or something made) is discernible by observation and reason.

That's the fundamental difference between "what" questions and "why" questions.

So when we ask "Why did God make us" like we can ask our friend "why did you make this table", this is a question that can only be answered by revelation.

It is not enough to look at the table to know its purpose, neither is it enough for you to look at yourself to know your purpose. You have to ask the Creator. If you say the table is for writing, you have found out one of its functions, but not its purpose.

Purpose can only be known by revelation. God's purpose can only be known if He reveals it. Otherwise it remains a mystery on His heart.

Now, we face a new challenge.

Has God revealed His purpose? Is there any trusted place or source of the revelation of God?

**Chapter 4**

There are several places in God's written revelation \- the Bible \- that we see His purpose and will revealed.

But there is arguably no place where it is more clearly stated and where the understanding and presentation is so well developed as in the book of Ephesians.

Notice how Paul presents the answer carefully.

Ephesians 1:9

The first thing to notice is the submission by Paul that God has made known His will to us. Notice he describes this will with the word "mystery" because it is not the kind of information that can be known by observation and reason. It requires revelation.

In addition to stating that God has made known His will, Paul tells us what leads up to that will. This will is according to or based on God's good pleasure. If I say A is according to B, it means there was B first, then A happened because of B.

This means there is first the reality of God having a good pleasure, which then proceeds to become His will.

The word "good pleasure" is "eudokia" in the Greek (the language that Paul and other New Testament authors wrote). And that word "eudokia" means what seems good to someone. In other words, what someone likes.

This means that God first has what He likes before it was His will, that is what He wants or craves.

It is like someone who ordinarily likes a particular food. But there would be moments they want or crave that food, right? That idea applies here.

Let's see how Ephesians 1:9 ends.

Ephesians 1:9 (quote)

God has made known His will, that is according to His good pleasure. And this is what He purposed in Himself.

Did you catch the last part?

What God purposed in Himself is the same good pleasure and will that He had.

Someone always liked the idea of being an astronaut, then they move from just liking the idea to wanting to become one. And finally, the person decides or determines to be one.

It is at that final phase we say the person has purposed to be an astronaut, right?

Now, imagine we are told that "Mr A has decided to do what they always liked and wanted",  but we are not told what exactly it is they always liked. Naturally, the question we would ask is "what exactly is it they always liked"

This applies exactly to God's purpose. If what God purposed is what He always liked (good pleasure) and wanted (will), the question we have to answer is "what is this good pleasure and will of God?"

Now, this is something Paul expressly answered in Ephesians 1:5.

Ephesians 1:5

Here, we are told that God predestined us to something called "adoption as sons" (NKJV) or "adoption of children" (KJV). We would come back to this phrase "adoption as sons" later, as it is very important to our study. But let's establish some other details first.

The same text says that God predestined us to this thing (adoption as sons) by Jesus Christ. This means that it was through the work of redemption in Christ (verse 7\) that God predestined us to this thing called "adoption as sons".

Furthermore, the same Ephesians 1:5 tells us why God predestined to that end. Notice the last part says "according to the good pleasure of His will". Remember the phrase "according to" means you did something because of or based on another thing. So God predestined us to something by redemption because of the good pleasure of His will.

Don't forget that  Ephesians 1:9 told us that God's purpose is His good pleasure and His will. This implies that we can read Ephesians 1:5 as follows: God predestined us to a particular thing because of His purpose.

If we understand that word "predestined", it will reveal exactly what God's purpose is to us from that text.

If I say "it is my purpose or will to do X", it is different from saying "I predestine to do X"

Predestination is an action I determine to do in response to something I think can possibly happen or I know for sure will happen. Predestination is predetermined response to future event that is probably or certainly going to happen. Purpose or will is not because of what will happen, it is your original intent independent of anything. Purpose is a fresh start whilst predestination is a future response.

This is why in Romans 8:29, predestination depends on and follows after foreknowledge.

What this means is that God foreknew something (man's sin) and therefore predestined the redemption in Christ to bring us to "adoption as sons" because of His good pleasure or will.

Did you catch that?

It means this "adoption as sons" is really the good pleasure or will of God, which is why He predestined the work of redemption to it despite His foreknowledge of sin. What God predestined was redemption towards "adoption as sons" because it is His good pleasure.

Now, if God's good pleasure and will is "adoption as sons", and Ephesians 1:9 says it is His very good pleasure and will that He purposed in Himself, then His purpose is "adoption as sons".

God's purpose is this thing called "adoption as sons". It is at this point that we have to explain what this phrase means.

Interestingly, the phrase "adoption of children/sons" is from a single Greek word, which is "huiothesia". Arguably the simplest, clearest, and best translation of this word is "Sonship".

This is because the word refers to being in the state or position of a son.

Two words make up this Greek word "huiothesia". The first word is "huios" that simply means "son", usually not as a male child but as one with a position of responsibility, as we would later see.

The second word is "tithemi" that means "to put" or "to place" something somewhere. So together the idea is being put or placed or stationed as a son. As a noun, the idea is the state/place/position of a son.

It is the idea of placement that leads to the common translation "adoption" but that can misrepresent how the word applied at the time the apostle wrote. It is usually not an adopted child that word is used for but mostly an actual child of a person.

For now, we have seen precisely what God's purpose is. God's purpose is Sonship.

But there is much to understand. What does Sonship mean exactly and how does this affect our purpose and pursuit on earth?

Romans 8:15

Romans 8:16

These two realities make up the concept of Sonship.

The text starts with a description or parable of an heir who cannot function in his responsibility because he is still a child (this word child here is "nepios" that refers to an infant or minor unlike the word in Romans 8:16 which is "teknon" used for child as one reproduced).

Then in Galatians 4:3, Paul compares humanity as a whole or those under the law to children (*nepios* — minor or infant) who were made to be heirs but cannot be until they pass the stage of infancy. And he says this stage was passed by the redemption that Jesus accomplished.

Galatians 4:5 submits that by the redemption Christ has done, those who had the promise of Sonship (those under the law), and then humanity by extension, have received the "adoption of sons" (the same single word "huiothesia" that means Sonship).

Notice Galatians 4:6-7, by this Spirit we cry "Abba, Father" as His Child but we are also made active and functional heirs.

Again, we see two realities that make up Sonship.

Sonship is premised on the fact that you are a person's child but extends functionally to the fact that you are the person's heir.

It is these two realities of Sonship, being God's Child and being God's Heir that define your identity and your pursuits in this world.

We must study and understand them to know what we are called to do and what our individual purpose is as a result.

**Chapter 7**

We now know what it means to be a Child of God.

This second part is so important because it is what particularly defines our own purpose in the sense of fulfilment and accomplishment of what we are here to do.

What does it mean to be an heir?

The word "heir" is "kleronomos" in the Greek. And it comes from two words "kleros" (lot, part or portion. It can be a land, property, responsibility etc) and nemo (to parcel out, to hand over, prescribe, order, instruct or assign)

So these two words refer to handing over or parcelling out a lot or portion to someone. To instruct or order a part or lot to a person. To assign a property or responsibility.

Do you understand?

The person who is assigned a lot is called heir (kleronomos). The lot that is assigned is called inheritance (kleronomia).

To make this simple and clear, we can define inheritance as that which is assigned or an assignment. The heir is one who has assignments given to them.

The concept of Sonship is becoming clearer now. It refers to the entire concept of parents bringing forth their children and giving them an inheritance or assignments.

Let's see an example. Remember there was a time where land was almost the only reasonable asset a family can have. So a father with 3 sons can assign the land he owns to his sons. He can assign a portion to each one (severed inheritance) or assign the whole property to all of them together (joint inheritance) leaving them to figure out how to manage it.

You can only assign what you own to another to inherit.

At this juncture, our question is what does God own that He assigns to His sons? This will let us know our inheritance.

There are three clear things that are referred to as our inheritance in Scripture.

1\. The very first thing God has and can give is Himself. Before anything else existed by His act of creation, there was just Him. And He can give us Himself. To be ours to have. Particularly, He can and has given us His Spirit. This is the sense in which Ephesians 1:18 uses that word inheritance, referencing the riches of His glory in us, which is His Spirit. Also notice Gal 3:14, 18\.

2\. The second thing that is obviously God's property is the created world. And He does assign it to those who are in His Family. Thus we see texts that say the meek shall inherit the Earth. Also, we are told Abraham and his seed are to inherit the world.

It is the entirety of creation that was the immediate reference of Paul in Romans 8 when he called us Heirs of God and joint heirs with Christ.

Whilst we all have the Spirit and the cosmos as our inheritance collectively, each of us has been given individual inheritance or assignments by God to fulfill.

The individual inheritance we have is of 2 kinds: there is a natural one and a spiritual one. We can simply say we have a natural assignment or inheritance as well as a spiritual assignment or inheritance.

Colossians 3:24

Colossians 4:1

What you do in the natural is God's assignment to you. We can call this part of your individual inheritance: your natural assignment.

Let's see the second part of our inheritance.

b) Our Spiritual Inheritance

There is another assignment described as an inheritance, and it is not natural. The first place we seek the inheritance language used to describe this is Acts 1\.

In this text, the responsibility of Judas in ministry is described as an inheritance using the word kleros (a lot or portion).

Acts 1:17

And this is what He does.

And this word "meros" that is an alternative or parallel word to "kleros" (lot, portion, inheritance) is used for each believer in Christ pertaining to their ministry responsibility in the body of Christ.

1 Corinthians 12:27 \- \- we are collectively the body but we are a part (meros) as members.

What is your specific Natural Assignment?

We would follow the progression of how God reveals the specific assignment He has for each individual from what He reveals in the Word to what He reveals by the Spirit.

1\. Your Starting Conditions

The very first revelation of God about natural assignment, which will be seen from God's Word, is relative to our condition at the moment we received salvation or the knowledge of His purpose.

The text that explains this vital truth is 1 Corinthians 7:17.

1 Corinthians 7:17

I would like you to notice there are two parts to this verse. In the first part, Paul makes a very important submission. Then, in the second part, he says this submission is a rule and canon for all the churches. It means the submission made in the first part is applicable to believers everywhere.

So then, what is this important submission? It can be presented as follows:

The starting conditions of our life at the point we received salvation and the knowledge of God's purpose is the very first thing God has called us to, parted to us or given us.

Let me explain.

Notice how Paul presented his thought: "as God has parted to each one and as He has called each one, let him walk". If you say "as you were at age 10..." you are pointing to the conditions of the person you are speaking of at that age.

In the same way, Paul was pointing to the conditions of people at the instance of receiving salvation, which is when we are called. (2 Thessalonians 2:13-14)

In the subsequent verses to 1 Corinthians 7:17, he will spell out the different possible conditions people can have at that point of the call of salvation.

You might have been circumcised or uncircumcised (speaking about natural or religious identity. Like being a Jew or Gentile).

Also, you might have been a slave or free. This is about one's economic status and the state of your natural pursuits.

Lastly, the direct context of 1 Corinthians 7 was about marriage. So he is also referencing the possible condition of being married or unmarried.

Whatever conditions you were in at the point of salvation (which is the primary point the rule should apply) or at the point of understanding God's purpose (which is the secondary point it would actively apply for many people) are the conditions God has allotted to you to start with.

Your starting conditions at salvation and purpose are God's allotment, division, part or call for you. That's the implication of 1 Corinthians 7:17.

So as far as the revelation of God's Word goes, your condition now is part of God's intention in His purpose for you.

Of course, the only exception is ungodly and unrighteous pursuits, as stated in Ephesians 4:28. We are called to repent from such.

So your starting conditions which may be geographical, natural identity, work status, marital status, etc. are God's starting call and allotment to you.

The reason for this is simple. By virtue of the foreknowledge of God, He knows at what point you will be saved and receive the knowledge of His will. As a result, He is able to orchestrate all He desires you to do from that very point.

Embrace your starting conditions as God's call and begin to walk with Him and in His purpose from there.

It is based on this premise that we would see Paul counselling bondservants, free men and masters to each be faithful to their natural assignment.

Colossians 3:22-4:1  
Ephesians 6:5-9

But then, does it mean all we can do in our natural assignment is whatever it is at the start upon salvation and knowledge of God's purpose?

The answer is no, and it will lead us to two vital truths about our natural assignment.

2\. Taking Advantage of Opportunities

The second thing God reveals to us in His Word about our natural assignment is that we should take advantage of opportunities that come to us.

This truth leads to a very important submission that will be examined in the third point.

For now, notice in the same 1 Corinthians 7, Paul makes a vital submission about our decisions and pursuits relative to our natural assignment in verse 21.

1 Corinthians 7:21

In the same text that tells those who are slaves not to be bothered about the starting condition in their natural assignment but treat it as God's starting call for them, they are equally told that if they have the opportunity to be free, they should use it.

Paul is giving a clear principle to choose what is better. Being free is better than being a slave. So "if you can be made free (or better), use it (take advantage of the opportunity)."

So whilst the primary wisdom of God is to be faithful to what we have at hand to see it grow, it also instructs and directs us to take advantage of opportunities that make us better.

Opportunities, in this context, are not initiatives we embark on of our own accord. There is a place for that as we will see soon.

The opportunities here are simply events or happenings that occur to us, offering us what is better than the starting conditions we have and the prospect of growth in those conditions.

It means the scriptural direction is to make wise, rational, sensible decisions on what is best in such cases except there is a clear leading to the contrary.

This is because if we are living in faith, we should primarily take opportunities that are better as God's orchestrations to us and for us, except we have a leading of the Spirit to the contrary. We will study the leading of the Spirit later.

But this leads to another very important truth we will see next.

3\. Take initiatives on your desire.

At this juncture, we make a very important submission. We can conclude from all God has revealed in His Word about our natural assignment that He has made it up to us to decide.

That is, God doesn't have a specific predetermined work, job, career, business, niche etc that He has chosen for us.

Our natural assignment is one area He wants us to freely enjoy the liberty of the free will He has given us. Stop boxing and caging yourself out of anxiety that there is something He has chosen you are not to miss out of.

Enjoy and exercise the liberty of your will when it comes to your natural assignment. We are only instructed to be constrained by the character of righteousness and the leading of the Spirit.

Notice the following texts:

1 Corinthians 7:29-31

The context of this text is Paul speaking about how he wants his audience to be undistracted in their devotion and walk with God. He had mentioned how staying single would give them greater focus but goes on to show that no matter the circumstances, they should not lose their focus.

If they are married, weeping or rejoicing, they should not get overwhelmed by it. Then, even when they buy, they are not to be attached and possessive.

Lastly, he speaks to "those who use this world" pointing to those who harness the resources and possibilities of the cosmos, that they shouldn't misuse or abuse it. The word there is about a negative use of the world, and can be understood as disregard for people or the environment of the people in the exploration and pursuit of their desires in the cosmos.

So he recognises the liberty of people to use this world, stating only the constraint of doing so righteously. Not using the world negatively.

To sum it up, most people will begin their natural assignment with the restriction of their starting conditions and they could faithfully grow it and grow in it for the rest of their lives.

Some will have better opportunities to take advantage of, and it is the wisdom of God to harness them and move forward.

But then, where one has what it takes to use this world as they desire, they are free to do so, within the constraints of what is right and the limitations of the leading of the Spirit.

You have the right to pursue your desires as long as they are righteous and there is no leading of the Spirit against it.

This brings us to the fourth thing God says about our natural assignment.

4\. Be Led by the Spirit

There are things God has already revealed in His Word about the specifics of our natural assignment.

We cannot ignore what God has already revealed on a given matter and start seeking another revelation from Him on that matter.

When we have accepted His revelation in the Word, we are able to properly situate the role of the leading of the Spirit relative to our natural assignment.

If our natural assignment is a matter of our will and choice, then the leading of the Spirit is not to tell us exactly what to do. Choosing what to do when it comes to our natural assignment is on us.

The primary role of the leading of the Spirit is to serve as a check on our decisions: approving or disapproving them.

There will be many low-stakes decisions and initiatives we would take that don't require us to get a clear approval to do them by the leading of the Spirit. If God doesn't want us making such decisions, He will call our attention to it, so long as we maintain a consistent devotion.

It is usually with high-stakes decisions, such as those that are irreversible in implication or long-term in effect, that we ought to intentionally seek the leading of the Spirit.

5\. Maximum Productivity

The final thing we know God has revealed about our natural assignment is the overwhelmingly loud instruction and exhortation to be maximally productive.

Whatever you are doing may be as your starting condition or a subsequent opportunity or a personal desire or something you have been led to do in the natural, this instruction of maximum productivity applies.

God did not call us to laziness, laxity, indifference, lethargy or indolence.

Everywhere we turn in Scripture, we don't just find examples of diligence but clear-cut instructions to be diligent.

The Epistles are even more firm and clear as to why we should be diligent. Because it is the Lord's own work which He will judge and reward.

\*  
In conclusion, the specific natural assignment God has allotted to you unfolds from your starting conditions to the opportunities you have by providence, to the pursuit of your righteous personal desires in this world. All the way, you are to be led by the Spirit and maximally productive.

**Chapter 9**

Now that we have settled what God has revealed our specific natural assignment to be, it is time to examine the second part of our inheritance as Heirs of God.

Remember, as Heirs of God, we have both a natural assignment/inheritance and a spiritual assignment/inheritance.

We have also settled that our spiritual inheritance is often described as the work of ministry.

But the real question is: how do you know precisely and exactly what God has called you as an individual to do in our spiritual assignment?

Has He called you to pastor large churches, to travel nations, serve in one role or the other? Will you know by a voice or an encounter?

To get the precise answers we need, we must examine the unfolding revelation of God on this matter, both from the Word and by His Spirit.

1\. Discipleship in the Local Church  
The very first thing God has clearly revealed to us about our spiritual assignment is the need for discipleship as preparatory to it.

Discipleship is the prerequisite for doing the work of ministry. And this discipleship is designed to happen in the local church.

This is obvious by clear-cut instructions and by examples too.

The Great Commission given by the Lord upon His resurrection is one clear instance of this.

Matthew 28:18-20

Notice, the first thing is to make disciples of those who we preach to and get saved. Then, based on this discipleship received, we are able to do everything Jesus commands including the ministry He has given to us all.

Paul will re-explain the same Great Commission in much clearer terms to the Church in Ephesus.

Ephesians 4:11-12

The Lord Jesus gave the leaders of the Church for the perfecting of the saints, which would cause the saints to do the work of ministry that edifies the body of Christ.

The word "perfecting" is descriptive of discipleship as it was used by the Lord Jesus to speak of what the teacher or master does to a disciple.

Luke 6:40

So this perfecting or discipleship is required for the work of ministry, which is our spiritual assignment.

Notice this discipleship is meant to be done by the Church. Hence, Paul lists out the leaders of the Church as those Jesus gave the responsibility to.

What Church is meant to disciple me, you may ask?

Ordinarily, the Church responsible for this should be the Church through whom the message of salvation came to you, either via her members or meeting or whatever platform.

But we must also account for the possibility, in this age and time that we live in, that discipleship may come to us through other sources apart from that from which we received salvation, and it can be God's orchestration for us.

What do we do at that juncture?

We can glean wisdom from what the Lord said in Luke 12.

Luke 12:47-48

The Lord, speaking relative to His eventual judgment, mentioned two categories of His servants: who knew His will or purpose and those who did not.

Those who didn't know His purpose will not be excused because it is the responsibility of each of us as individuals to ensure we know God's will.

However, their judgment will be less stringent.

But those who know God's purpose and do not prepare and do it will receive a much stricter judgment. Did you notice the first thing you are to do after you know His will is to prepare, that is, to be discipled.

This seems to envisage the possibility of having to make a new commitment to be prepared for God's purpose when you know it, which would ordinarily be a commitment to where the knowledge came from.

To sum it up, the first thing God reveals to you about your spiritual assignment is to commit to a local church that is committed to train you for it.

2\. Evangelism

Remember that the very reason for the discipleship or perfecting that God has called us to have is to do the work of ministry. And the very first task or phase in this ministry is evangelism, which is the publicity and proclamation of the message.

As a New Creation, you were called by God into the ministry of reconciliation where you are to preach the message of reconciliation.

2 Corinthians 5:17-19

What you are called to do is to preach that message.

Mark 16:15

The word "preach" in the instruction of the Lord Jesus is the word "kerusso", and it is really about publicity than it is about speaking. Of course, speaking is how publicity is done but that word is about the goal of getting a message publicised.

It is the word for a town crier. The goal of a town crier is not to just tell one person something but to get the news around an entire region as loudly as possible.

This means you should find ways to use all means of communication available to you to announce this message.

From one-on-one evangelism to use of tracts or via online media, always find ways to spread and proclaim the Gospel.

You are to play your own part in the publicity of the Gospel.

It is by the publicity and proclamation of the message that we would have recipients of the message toward whom we have another responsibility.

3\. Personal Discipleship for Recipients

As you receive discipleship in the local church and evangelize, it is expected that there will be those who receive the Gospel through you whom you ought to disciple.

The second phase in our spiritual assignment after getting people saved or aware of God's purpose via evangelism is discipleship to help them fulfill God's purpose.

Each of us has a responsibility to add people to the body of Christ, to strengthen them and help them fulfil God's purpose.

This is why besides reconciling people to God, Paul says the ministry of reconciliation includes reminding them not to make the grace of God on their lives in vain.

2 Corinthians 5:20 \- 6:1

As ambassador of Christ, we call men to be reconciled to God. That's salvation via evangelism.

But as workers with God, we encourage them not to receive the grace of God in vain. That's about their growth and walk via discipleship.

If you do your part in evangelism, there are those who will receive the message and be dependent on you for discipleship.

It is by faithfulness to evangelism and discipleship that the next phase in our spiritual assignment unfolds.

4\. Leadership in the Body of Christ

Even without any specific leading of the Spirit, God has already revealed in His Word the trajectory that our spiritual assignment should follow.

If we receive discipleship in the Church and proceed to participate in the work of ministry (evangelism and discipleship) as He has instructed us to, we would end up in leadership.

We can see this trajectory in a number of explanations and examples in Scripture.

The right place to start is how the Lord Jesus established the work of ministry.

From His preaching of the Gospel of the kingdom He called several people to follow Him, from whom He eventually chose 12 as leaders.

It is clear they were chosen based on definite commitment as this was the same consideration in choosing a replacement for Judas' allotment in ministry. So the twelve naturally evolved from discipleship into leadership by their faithfulness and commitment.

Besides the apostles, we see how leadership in the Church came to be by discipleship as well. The Lord gave the apostles the instruction to make disciples, which would mean replicating more of their kind.

By Acts 2:42, we see them immediately carrying out His instruction by teaching the apostles' doctrine. The fruit of their work is evident by Acts 6:1 when we are told disciples are multiplying. And it is from the disciples that leaders were chosen as the need arose in the same chapter (Acts 6:2-7).

Notice seven people were chosen to assist the apostles, which is like the responsibility of an assistant leader/pastor or cell leader or deacon today. These people will grow beyond this role  to be notable leaders, bishops or elders later.

Another clear example can be seen in the ministry of Paul. Paul brought the Gospel to certain regions (Antioch, Iconium, Lystra and Derbe) for the first time and made disciples there. It was out of the disciples made, we would see leaders emerge.

Acts 14:21-23

Notice how verse 22 is very clear that disciples had been made and that they were being confirmed and strengthened further. It was after this we were told that leaders (elders) were appointed.

We can discern how leaders were so appointed from Paul's instruction to Timothy and Titus on how to go about it.

1 Timothy 3:1

Notice how Paul says if anyone at all desires the office or responsibility of leadership (bishopric), the person desires a good thing. The idea is to commend that desire as well, not just to commend what is desired. To say that having such a desire is godly, as much as what is desired is godly.

We understand there can be ungodly motives in desiring a godly thing and Paul would address that in the qualifications for this responsibility.

Leadership can be and should be desired by every believer. They still have to qualify for it though, but it is godly to want to serve in our own part and place for the fulfilment of Christ's Mission.

The qualifications for bishopric or leadership are products of discipleship. One who has been perfected to the measure of the stature of the Fullness of Christ will be qualified to lead people.

Also, our results in evangelism and discipleship in the Church will give us lives to be responsible for. This is what Ephesians 4 was pointing to. Every part of the body of Christ is to do their own share to add others to the body.

Ephesians 4:16

We also find this reasoning in the letter to Corinth.

2 Corinthians 10:15-16

Notice again, Paul says "as your faith is increased", which is a clear reference to spiritual growth that happens by discipleship. What happens "as our faith is increased", we are enlarged to more regions. This shows again that discipleship will birth leadership of more people and places.

That is the natural trajectory of our spiritual assignment as God has revealed to us in His Word.

5\. Leading of the Spirit

It is clear that without any direct word from God, we can walk in our spiritual assignment all the way to leadership just by walking in His revealed Word.

But there is a vital place of the leading of the Spirit.

It plays some major roles:

a) To confirm or decide on that which is the natural trajectory of our spiritual assignment.

They prayed to confirm the 7 people who were naturally recommended to assist the apostles. Also, they prayed to decide between Matthias and Barnabas after a natural selection process had been done.

b) To restrain the natural trajectory of our spiritual assignment. For instance, we have been instructed to go into all the world and preach.

We have been told we can be accepted and rejected by those we preach to. But as we naturally move from one place to another based on instruction in God's Word, we can be restrained like Paul was in Acts 16\.

c) To give specific direction on initiatives to take in pursuit of our spiritual assignment.

Like Peter had to be specifically instructed to reach Cornelius and his house who were Gentiles. Or like Paul was specifically told of the special nature of his spiritual assignment. Also, when the church leaders in Antioch were specifically instructed to send forth Paul and Barnabas to a task.

One thing is clear: specific leadings are not the primary way God leads in pursuit of His Mission. We are to be in obedience on all that has already been said in His Word to have the most effective harnessing of the specific leading of God's Spirit.

It is not by waiting for specific leading that we best access and harness them. It is by obeying all of God's Word that we are rightly positioned to receive those specific leadings.

**Chapter 10**

We have examined what God's purpose is.

As He has revealed in His Word, His purpose is Sonship.

Sonship means to be His Child and His Heirs.

As His Child, we have been called to:

1\. Fellowship with the Father  
2\. Walk in His nature and character

As His Heirs, we have been called to:

1\. Natural assignment  
2\. Spiritual assignment

We have several examples of people who have run this race of God's purpose.

Hebrews 12:1

We see Abraham, given to prayer, making altars everywhere he went. Carrying out his natural assignment and yet faithful in his spiritual assignment.

The same is true of Noah, Isaac and Jacob. We all know the example of Joseph and Daniel.

But this is true of saints in the New Testament text as well.

Paul repeatedly testified to faithfully working with his hands as he carried out his spiritual assignment.

Priscilla and Aquila were his work colleagues, yet pastored churches. Philemon and Gaius were CEOs who still pastored, and we have instances of Gaius travelling for spiritual assignment sake.

You have so many witnesses and therefore so little excuse for not fully and maximally living out the life of purpose.

Now that you know precisely what you are to do, how do you fulfil it? It is not enough to know. We must desire to fulfill it. That's the mindset we see with the Lord Jesus and the saints.

John 4:34  
Acts 20:24

There is a major text that highlights how we ought to pursue and fulfill God's purpose.

1 Corinthians 9:24

We are to treat God's purpose as a race to run and win. Do not run to be busy. Rather, run to win.

How do we win?

Paul gives one answer: We must be temperate, self-controlled or disciplined in all things.

Discipline is the one word that ensures we fulfill God's purpose.

Discipline involves three things.

1\. A clear focus or vision: to win a particular race. To beat a time. To get to a destination.

2\. A definite order:

3\. Relentless drive:

The good thing is discipline is the nature of our Spirit. It is clear that by the Spirit in us, we bear the fruit of discipline. One can only bear the fruit of something from its seed. So it means the Spirit of God in us, which is the seed of God, has discipline as one of its realities.

Therefore, God has already given us all it takes to fulfill His good pleasure, will and purpose for our lives.

Philippians 2:13

God is at work in us to will/desire and to do His good pleasure or purpose.

We have no excuses. We have the nature to be focused, orderly and driven to fulfill purpose.

We should simply walk in it by faith.

The one thing that sums up how we fulfill God's purpose is prayer.

This is where we stay in fellowship with the Father, stand in faith to walk in our nature and character. It is also where we stay in faith to walk in the nature of discipline in us. That is, we set our vision, define our order and strengthen our drive to fulfill His purpose.

So we don't just know God's purpose. We know how to fulfil it.

We fulfill it by faith, standing in prayer.

Therefore, there is just one final instruction for you:

Start now. Yes, right now.

Start now in prayer. Fellowship with your Father, acknowledge His will for you. Thank Him for the new nature and character that you have. Acknowledge His assignments given to you. Set your vision, define your order, affirm the drive of God in you to fulfill your assignments in His purpose.

Start now and fulfill God's purpose for your life.
