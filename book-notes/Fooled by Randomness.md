# Fooled by Randomness

**Author:** Nassim Nicholas Taleb

## Key Highlights

### Hindsight Bias and the Illusion of Determinism

> It is as if there were two planets: the one in which we actually live and the one, considerably more deterministic, on which people are convinced we live. It is as simple as that: Past events will always look less random than they were (it is called the hindsight bias). I would listen to someone's discussion of his own past realizing that much of what he was saying was just backfit explanations concocted ex post by his deluded mind.

Taleb's central observation: humans construct deterministic narratives about past events that were actually driven by randomness. We live on a probabilistic planet but convince ourselves we inhabit a deterministic one — and we backfit explanations to maintain the illusion.

### Statements Reveal the Speaker, Not Just the Message

> Living in the real world may be painful particularly if one finds statements more informative about the people making them than the intended message: I picked up Newsweek this morning at the dentist's office and read a journalist's discussion of a prominent business figure, particularly his ability in "timing moves" and realized how I was making a list of the biases in the journalist's mind rather than getting the intended information in the article itself, which I could not possibly take seriously.

A shift in perception: rather than consuming content at face value, learn to read the *biases* of the person making the statement. The article becomes more revealing about the journalist's cognitive traps than about the subject — and less shocking once you realize that "experts" systematically fail to learn from their own predictive failures.

### The True Nature of Probability

> Probability is not a mere computation of odds on the dice or more complicated variants; it is the acceptance of the lack of certainty in our knowledge and the development of methods for dealing with our ignorance.

Probability is an epistemological stance, not just a mathematical tool. It begins with intellectual humility — accepting uncertainty — and then builds frameworks for making decisions despite it.

### Chance Favors the Prepared — But Doesn't Cause Success

> Of course chance favors the prepared! Hard work, showing up on time, wearing a clean (preferably white) shirt, using deodorant, and some such conventional things contribute to success — they are certainly necessary but may be insufficient as they do not cause success. The same applies to the conventional values of persistence, doggedness and perseverance: necessary, very necessary. One needs to go out and buy a lottery ticket in order to win. Does it mean that the work involved in the trip to the store caused the winning?

A crucial distinction: conventional virtues (hard work, persistence, punctuality) are *necessary but not sufficient*. They enable you to be in the game, but they don't *cause* the outcome in random environments. Necessary ≠ causal.

### Affirming the Consequent

> Assume that good qualities cause success; based on that assumption, even though it seems intuitively correct to think so, the fact that every intelligent, hardworking, persevering person becomes successful does not imply that every successful person is necessarily an intelligent, hardworking, persevering person (it is remarkable how such a primitive logical fallacy — affirming the consequent — can be made by otherwise very intelligent people).

The logical trap: "If A then B" does not mean "If B then A." We observe successful people, identify traits they share, and assume those traits *caused* the success — ignoring survivorship bias and the role of randomness. Even intelligent people commit this fallacy routinely.
