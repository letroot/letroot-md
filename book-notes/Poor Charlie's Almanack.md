# Poor Charlie's Almanack

**Author:** Charles T. Munger

## Foundational Principles: Focus and Frugality

Munger's grandfather instilled in him two simple but powerful habits: concentrate on the task immediately in front of you, and control your spending. These aren't glamorous insights, but they're the bedrock of both wealth accumulation and intellectual discipline.

> However, he never forgot the sound principles taught by his grandfather: to concentrate on the task immediately in front of him and to control spending.

## Do It Right the First Time

A deceptively simple maxim that reveals Munger's approach to everything — don't cut corners, don't defer quality, don't assume you'll fix it later. The cost of rework almost always exceeds the cost of doing it properly from the start.

> Do the job right the first time

## The Primacy of Personal Responsibility

Munger doesn't just advocate responsibility — he embodies it. This short highlight reads like a life philosophy compressed into three words. No excuses, no blame-shifting, no outsourcing of outcomes.

> Be responsible

## The Competitive Advantage of the Unglamorous

Most people skip the boring fundamentals — the "short game" that nobody else wants to practice. Munger's edge came not from brilliance but from his willingness to do the unglamorous work others couldn't be bothered with.

> By working on the tennis version of golf's short game, which few others could be bothered to practice, Father, as he's done throughout his life, gave himself a fair if maddening competitive advantage.

## The Power of Deep Contemplation

Munger's ability to wall off distractions — to the point where he'd miss freeway exits while thinking — was not a quirk but a core competitive advantage. In an age of constant distraction, the capacity for sustained, uninterrupted thought is rarer and more valuable than ever.

> Even when not reading, Father was often so deep in contemplation that a routine drive to take Molly and Wendy back to Pasadena could have turned into an excursion to San Bernardino without Mom calling out the correct freeway turnoffs. Whatever was on his mind, it wasn't the outcome of a football game or a botched golf shot. Father's ability to Chinese wall off the most intrusive distractions from whatever mental task he was engaged in — a practice alternately amusing and irritating if you were trying to get his attention — accounts as much as anything else for his success.

## Generous Parenting Under Constraint

Even with seven children, a small business, and modest means, Munger found ways to be generous — sending an allowance "of a much richer father" to his college-age daughter. The lesson: generosity isn't about abundance, it's about priorities.

> When I was in college, Daddy had seven other children to raise, worked in a seedy part of Spring Street, and owned but one company, a small, grimy outfit that made motor additive. But he saw these were unhinged times. He sent me the allowance of a much richer father, keeping me in professionally ironed shirts and making me feel sharp as a bandbox. From 3,000 miles away, he continued to help me keep my balance. I could go on. Suffice it to say that our father has always known what he was doing, as a parent as in so much else. I appreciated it greatly. I still do.

## A Life Defined by Books and Building

If you wanted to describe Charlie Munger in two words, "books" and "graph paper" would capture it — one hand always holding a book, the other sketching buildings. The combination of relentless reading and active creation is the Munger formula in miniature.

> No matter where he is, his hands are always holding open a volume, typically a Ben Franklin biography or the latest treatise on genetics. One might also answer "graph paper," for the buildings he's been designing.
