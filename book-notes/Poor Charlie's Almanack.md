# Poor Charlie's Almanack

**Author:** Charles T. Munger

## Key Highlights

### Concentration and Discipline

> However, he never forgot the sound principles taught by his grandfather: to concentrate on the task immediately in front of him and to control spending.

The foundational habits that shaped Munger's career — focus on the immediate task and control spending. Simple, durable principles.

### Doing Things Right and Taking Responsibility

> Do the job right the first time.

> Be responsible.

Two terse, uncompromising maxims that define Munger's operating philosophy.

### The Power of Unconventional Practice

> By working on the tennis version of golf's short game, which few others could be bothered to practice, Father, as he's done throughout his life, gave himself a fair if maddening competitive advantage.

The theme: competitive advantage often comes from doing the unglamorous work that others can't be bothered with. This echoes across business, sports, and intellectual life.

### Deep Contemplation as a Habit

> Even when not reading, Father was often so deep in contemplation that a routine drive to take Molly and Wendy back to Pasadena could have turned into an excursion to San Bernardino without Mom calling out the correct freeway turnoffs. Whatever was on his mind, it wasn't the outcome of a football game or a botched golf shot. Father's ability to Chinese wall off the most intrusive distractions from whatever mental task he was engaged in — a practice alternately amusing and irritating if you were trying to get his attention — accounts as much as anything else for his success.

Munger's capacity for deep, undistracted thinking — to the point of appearing absent-minded about mundane things — was a core driver of his intellectual output and decision quality.

### Generosity and Perspective

> When I was in college, Daddy had seven other children to raise, worked in a seedy part of Spring Street, and owned but one company, a small, grimy outfit that made motor additive. But he saw these were unhinged times. He sent me the allowance of a much richer father, keeping me in professionally ironed shirts and making me feel sharp as a bandbox. From 3,000 miles away, he continued to help me keep my balance. I could go on. Suffice it to say that our father has always known what he was doing, as a parent as in so much else. I appreciated it greatly. I still do.

Even with limited means, Munger prioritized generosity toward his children — understanding that the *appearance* of competence (sharp shirts, decent allowance) mattered for confidence and balance.

### Relentless Reading

> No matter where he is, his hands are always holding open a volume, typically a Ben Franklin biography or the latest treatise on genetics. One might also answer "graph paper," for the buildings he's been designing.

The image of Munger as a perpetually-learning machine — always reading, always absorbing. His intellectual curiosity wasn't confined to one domain.
